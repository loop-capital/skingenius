## 1. Mandarin Rind (Citrus Reticulata Peel Extract) — freq 7

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Citrus Reticulata (Tangerine) Peel Extract |
| **Category** | Botanical extract / essential oil component |
| **Mechanism** | Rich in limonene, γ-terpinene, and polymethoxyflavones (nobiletin, tangeretin). Moderate antioxidant activity via free-radical scavenging. Mild antimicrobial against C. acnes. |
| **Evidence Tier** | **C — Limited.** Strong in-vitro antioxidant and antimicrobial data. No large RCTs for skin-specific outcomes. Primarily used as fragrance/refreshing agent. |

| **Contraindications** | Known citrus allergy. Photosensitive skin conditions (lupus, porphyria). Do not use before UV exposure. |
| **Root Causes Addressed** | Oxidative stress (mild), microbial overgrowth (adjunct) |
| **Dermatologist Flag** | 🟡 AI can handle; flag phototoxicity for users with photosensitive conditions. |

## 2. Citric Acid — freq 6

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Citric Acid |
| **Category** | Alpha-hydroxy acid (AHA) / pH adjuster / chelating agent |
| **Mechanism** | Smallest AHA after glycolic acid. Exfoliates by dissolving desmosomal bonds between corneocytes. Chelates metal ions (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) that stabilize cell cohesion. Stimulates epidermal turnover and GAG synthesis. |
| **Evidence Tier** | **B — Moderate.** Extensively studied as a pH adjuster and chelator. Less robust data than glycolic/lactic acid for standalone anti-aging, but supported in combination peels. |
| **Key Findings** | Citric acid 10–20 % peels demonstrated modest improvement in photoaging and mottled pigmentation. Less irritation than glycolic at equivalent concentrations due to larger molecular size (192 vs 76 Da). |
| **Effective Concentration** | **0.5 – 10 %** (cosmetic); **10 – 20 %** (professional peels) |
| **pH Requirements** | Effective at pH 2.5 – 4.0 for exfoliation. Higher pH (4.5–6.5) when used as pH adjuster only. |
| **Conflicts** | Combine with other AHAs/BHAs for enhanced exfoliation (monitor irritation). Separate from retinoids (alternate nights). Can destabilize retinol at low pH. |
| **Side Effects** | Stinging, erythema, photosensitivity. Overuse disrupts barrier. |
| **Contraindications** | Active eczema, rosacea flares, recent facial surgery, isotretinoin use within 6 months. |
| **Root Causes Addressed** | Follicular hyperkeratinization, uneven texture, photoaging, hyperpigmentation |
| **Dermatologist Flag** | 🟢 AI can handle. Standard AHA precautions apply. |

## 3. Parsley Seed Extract (Petroselinum Crispum) — freq 5

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Petroselinum Crispum (Parsley) Seed Extract |
| **Category** | Botanical antioxidant / anti-inflammatory |
| **Mechanism** | Apigenin (flavone), apiin (flavone glycoside), luteolin, and chlorophyll derivatives. Apigenin inhibits COX-2 and iNOS expression. Mild antimicrobial and skin-brightening properties via tyrosinase inhibition. |
| **Evidence Tier** | **C — Emerging.** Robust in-vitro antioxidant data. Very limited human clinical trials for topical skin application. Primarily studied for oral antioxidant/anti-inflammatory benefits. |
| **Key Findings** | Apigenin showed photoprotective effects in UV-irradiated keratinocytes. Parsley extract demonstrated moderate tyrosinase inhibition in vitro (less potent than kojic acid or arbutin). |
| **Effective Concentration** | **0.5 – 2 %** (no established clinical range; based on formulation practices) |
| **pH Requirements** | Stable across pH 4 – 7. |
| **Conflicts** | Generally compatible. Apiogenin may theoretically interact with hormone-modulating actives (weak phytoestrogenic activity). |
| **Side Effects** | Rare contact dermatitis. Apiaceae family allergy (celery, carrot, dill) may cross-react. |
| **Contraindications** | Known Apiaceae allergy. Pregnancy: apigenin has uterotonic properties in high oral doses — topical use at cosmetic concentrations is likely safe but limited data. |
| **Root Causes Addressed** | Oxidative stress, inflammation (mild), hyperpigmentation (adjunct) |
| **Dermatologist Flag** | 🟢 AI can handle. Flag Apiaceae allergy cross-reactivity. |

## 4. Rosemary Leaf (Rosmarinus Officinalis) — freq 5

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Rosmarinus Officinalis (Rosemary) Leaf Extract / Oil |
| **Category** | Botanical antioxidant / anti-inflammatory / antimicrobial |
| **Mechanism** | Carnosic acid, rosmarinic acid, camphor, 1,8-cineole. Potent free-radical scavenging (ORAC values comparable to vitamin E). Rosmarinic acid inhibits hyaluronidase and collagenase. Carnosic acid activates Nrf2 antioxidant pathway. |
| **Evidence Tier** | **B — Moderate.** Strong in-vitro and ex-vivo data. Several small clinical trials for anti-aging and photoprotection. |
| **Key Findings** | Rosemary extract demonstrated UV-protective effects comparable to vitamin C in ex-vivo human skin models. Carnosic acid reduced MMP-1 expression in fibroblasts. |
| **Effective Concentration** | **0.1 – 2 %** extract; essential oil requires ≤ 1 % |
| **pH Requirements** | Stable across pH 4 – 7. |
| **Conflicts** | Carnosic acid is lipophilic — best delivered in emulsion or oil-based vehicles. May enhance penetration of other actives. |
| **Side Effects** | Essential oil: contact dermatitis, sensitization (EU fragrance allergen). Extract: very well tolerated. |
| **Contraindications** | Known Lamiaceae allergy (mint, basil, lavender, sage). Essential oil contraindicated in epilepsy (camphor content). Pregnancy: avoid high-concentration essential oil. |
| **Root Causes Addressed** | Photoaging, oxidative stress, inflammation, collagen degradation |
| **Dermatologist Flag** | 🟢 AI can handle. Distinguish between extract (safe) and essential oil (caution). |

## 5. Kaolin (White Clay) — freq 4

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Kaolin |
| **Category** | Clay absorbent / physical exfoliant |
| **Mechanism** | Layered aluminosilicate with high adsorptive capacity. Absorbs sebum, dirt, and impurities from skin surface. Provides gentle physical exfoliation. Mild astringent effect. |
| **Evidence Tier** | **C — Traditional use.** No large RCTs for acne or oil control. Mechanism well-understood via physical chemistry. Supported by dermatology practice for oily skin masks. |
| **Key Findings** | Kaolin masks demonstrated immediate reduction in skin surface oil (sebumeter measurements). Comparable to bentonite but less absorbent. Gentler on sensitive skin than bentonite. |
| **Effective Concentration** | **5 – 20 %** in masks and cleansers |
| **pH Requirements** | Inert; compatible with all pH ranges. |
| **Conflicts** | May be drying when combined with other absorptive agents (bentonite, charcoal). Follow with moisturizer. |
| **Side Effects** | Dryness, tightness. Overuse can disrupt barrier in dry skin types. |
| **Contraindications** | Severely dry skin, eczema flares, compromised barrier. |
| **Root Causes Addressed** | Excess sebum, clogged pores (surface-level) |
| **Dermatologist Flag** | 🟢 AI can handle. Basic OTC ingredient. |

## 6. Bisabolol — freq 4

| **Conflicts** | Synergistic with ceramides, niacinamide, and Centella asiatica for barrier repair. Enhances penetration of other actives (formulation benefit). |
| **Side Effects** | Extremely well tolerated. Rare allergic contact dermatitis. |
| **Contraindications** | Known chamomile/compositae allergy (bisabolol is derived from German chamomile). |
| **Root Causes Addressed** | Inflammation, barrier disruption, irritation from other actives |
| **Dermatologist Flag** | 🟢 AI can handle. Excellent for sensitive skin formulations. |

| **Side Effects** | Very well tolerated. Mild stinging at high concentrations. Less photosensitizing than AHAs. |
| **Contraindications** | None significant. Excellent for sensitive skin, rosacea, and eczema. |
| **Root Causes Addressed** | Photoaging, hyperpigmentation, texture, barrier dysfunction, rosacea |
