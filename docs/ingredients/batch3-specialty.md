# Batch 3 — Specialty & Protective Ingredients

_Research profiles for 5 specialty actives. Sources: training knowledge (dermatology, cosmetic chemistry)._

## 1. Licorice Root Extract (Glycyrrhiza glabra)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Glycyrrhiza Glabra (Licorice) Root Extract |
| **Category** | Skin-brightening agent / anti-inflammatory botanical |
| **Mechanism** | Contains glabridin, which inhibits tyrosinase (the rate-limiting enzyme in melanin synthesis). Liquiritin disperses existing melanin. Glycyrrhizin exhibits anti-inflammatory action via inhibition of phospholipase A₂ and COX-2. |
| **Evidence Tier** | **B — Moderate.** Multiple in-vitro and small clinical studies support brightening and anti-inflammatory effects. Fewer large RCTs than hydroquinone or vitamin C. |
| **Key Findings** | Glabridin inhibited tyrosinase activity by ~50 % in vitro. Liquiritin 1 % cream showed significant melasma improvement vs. placebo over 12 weeks. Anti-inflammatory potency comparable to low-dose hydrocortisone in some models. |
| **Effective Concentration** | 0.5 – 5 % (standardized extract; glabridin content typically 2 – 5 % of extract) |
| **pH Requirements** | Stable across pH 4 – 7; no strict optimum required. Glycyrrhizin is more stable at slightly acidic pH. |
| **Conflicts** | Generally well-tolerated with most actives. May enhance absorption of other ingredients (potential irritant risk with high-strength retinoids or AHAs). Mineral salts can complex with glycyrrhizin. |
| **Side Effects** | Rare. Contact dermatitis reported in sensitive individuals. Oral intake at high doses can cause pseudoaldosteronism (hypokalemia, hypertension) — not relevant at topical concentrations. |
| **Contraindications** | Known Glycyrrhiza allergy. Patch test recommended for rosacea-prone skin. |

## 2. Kojic Acid

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Kojic Acid (5-hydroxy-2-hydroxymethyl-4H-pyran-4-one) |
| **Category** | Tyrosinase inhibitor / skin-brightening agent |
| **Mechanism** | Chelates copper at the active site of tyrosinase, preventing the conversion of L-tyrosine → DOPA → dopaquinone. Also scavenges free radicals, reducing oxidative-stress-induced melanogenesis. |
| **Evidence Tier** | **A — Strong.** Well-established tyrosinase inhibitor with decades of in-vitro evidence and multiple RCTs demonstrating depigmenting efficacy, often used as a benchmark comparator. |
| **Key Findings** | 2 % kojic acid cream reduced melasma severity comparably to 4 % hydroquinone over 12 weeks (fewer side effects). Combination with glycolic acid enhanced penetration and results. Synergistic with arbutin and vitamin C. |
| **Effective Concentration** | 1 – 4 % (typical OTC); 2 % most common in clinical trials |
| **pH Requirements** | Most stable and effective at **pH 3 – 4**. Degrades (browns) at higher pH; significantly loses activity above pH 7. |
| **Conflicts** | Incompatible with iron salts (forms colored complexes). Oxidizes in the presence of strong oxidizers. Pair carefully with niacinamide (both address pigmentation but at different points in the pathway — generally complementary). Avoid combining with copper peptides (opposing mechanisms). |
| **Side Effects** | Contact sensitization / allergic contact dermatitis (higher rates than arbutin). Mild erythema and irritation, especially at concentrations > 4 %. Potential for paradoxical hyperpigmentation with prolonged irritation. |
| **Contraindications** | History of contact dermatitis to kojic acid or related compounds (hydroxy-pyranones). Avoid broken or inflamed skin. Not recommended during pregnancy due to limited safety data (topical). |

## 3. Astaxanthin

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Astaxanthin (Haematococcus Pluvialis Extract) |
| **Category** | Antioxidant (carotenoid) / photoprotective adjuvant |
| **Mechanism** | Potent lipid-phase antioxidant — quenches singlet oxygen (~6,000× more effective than vitamin C, ~550× more effective than vitamin E in vitro). Spans the lipid bilayer, protecting both inner and outer membrane surfaces. Inhibits matrix metalloproteinase (MMP) expression, reducing UV-induced collagen breakdown. |
| **Evidence Tier** | **C — Emerging.** Strong in-vitro and animal data; human clinical evidence is growing but still limited by small sample sizes and short durations. Not yet a mainstream dermatological recommendation. |
| **Key Findings** | Oral astaxanthin (4 mg/day) reduced wrinkle depth and improved skin elasticity over 16 weeks in a small RCT. Topical 0.001 – 0.1 % reduced UV-induced erythema (sunburn) in human studies. Demonstrated anti-inflammatory activity via NF-κB inhibition. |
| **Effective Concentration** | **Topical:** 0.001 – 0.1 % (potent at very low concentrations due to high molar efficiency). **Oral:** 4 – 6 mg/day for systemic photoprotection. |
| **pH Requirements** | Lipophilic; not water-soluble. Formulated in oil-based vehicles or encapsulated. No direct pH dependency; stable across cosmetic pH range. Degrades with light and heat exposure — opaque packaging required. |
| **Conflicts** | Compatible with most antioxidants (often synergistic with vitamin E, which it regenerates). May interfere with pro-oxidant chemotherapies (theoretical — oral use). No significant topical conflicts. |
| **Side Effects** | Excellent safety profile. Orange skin tint possible at very high topical concentrations. Oral intake: carotenodermia at very high doses (> 20 mg/day). No known toxicity at recommended doses. |
| **Contraindications** | No established topical contraindications. Oral: caution with anticoagulant therapy (theoretical antiplatelet effect). Shellfish allergy is NOT a concern for Haematococcus-derived astaxanthin, but product sourcing should be verified. |

## 4. Zinc Oxide (Sunscreen)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Zinc Oxide (ZnO) |
| **Category** | Mineral (inorganic) UV filter / physical sunscreen |
| **Mechanism** | Sits on the skin surface and reflects, scatters, and absorbs UV radiation. Primarily a **UV-A + UV-B** broad-spectrum filter (absorbs more effectively in UV-A range than TiO₂). Nano-ZnO also generates electron-hole pairs that are quickly quenched on the particle surface, minimizing free-radical release. Also exhibits mild antimicrobial and astringent properties. |
| **Evidence Tier** | **A — Strong.** FDA "GRASE" (Generally Recognized As Safe and Effective) status. Extensive clinical and epidemiological data supporting skin cancer prevention, photoaging reduction, and erythema prevention. |
| **Key Findings** | Broad-spectrum protection covering UVB (290–320 nm) and UVA (320–400 nm). Non-irritating and non-comedogenic compared to chemical filters. Nano-ZnO (< 100 nm) provides cosmetically elegant formulations with equivalent UV protection; no significant systemic absorption demonstrated. Anti-inflammatory benefit aids post-procedure healing. |
| **Effective Concentration** | **10 – 25 %** in sunscreen formulations (FDA monograph maximum: 25 %). Nano forms effective at lower percentages due to better film formation. |
| **pH Requirements** | Particulate inorganic; pH of the vehicle matters for stability but ZnO itself is stable across pH 4 – 10. Slightly acidic vehicles (pH 5 – 6) enhance skin compatibility. |
| **Conflicts** | Can interact with acidic AHAs/BHAs in the same formulation (destabilize the dispersion). Avoid formulating with high concentrations of citric acid or EDTA (can solubilize ZnO). Compatible with chemical UV filters if stabilized. Pilling risk when layered over silicone-heavy moisturizers. |
| **Side Effects** | Very low irritation potential. Mild white cast (especially on darker skin tones) — mitigated by micronized/nano forms. Rare contact sensitization. |
| **Contraindications** | No absolute contraindications for topical use. Concerns about nano-ZnO systemic penetration through damaged skin barrier (theoretical; current evidence shows negligible absorption through intact skin). Apply to intact skin only. |

## 5. Snail Mucin (Snail Secretion Filtrate)

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **INCI** | Snail Secretion Filtrate |
| **Category** | Moisturizing / wound-healing / skin-conditioning complex |
| **Mechanism** | Natural mixture of glycoproteins, glycolic acid, hyaluronic acid, copper peptides, antimicrobial peptides, and protease inhibitors. Stimulates fibroblast proliferation and collagen/elastin synthesis. Provides humectant hydration. Mild enzymatic exfoliation from glycolic acid content. Antimicrobial peptides contribute to barrier defense. |
| **Evidence Tier** | **C — Limited but promising.** Several small clinical trials and case series. In-vitro evidence for wound healing and collagen stimulation is reasonable. Lacks large-scale RCTs. Composition varies significantly by species, diet, and extraction method — standardization is a challenge. |
| **Key Findings** | Snail mucin cream improved skin hydration, fine lines, and texture over 8–12 weeks in multiple small studies. Accelerated wound re-epithelialization in vitro and in animal models. Contains allantoin (soothing), glycolic acid (exfoliating), and collagen/elastin (structural proteins). Anti-inflammatory activity demonstrated in UV-irradiated keratinocyte models. |
| **Effective Concentration** | **70 – 97 %** secretion filtrate in K-beauty products. Lower concentrations (as low as 20 %) may still offer moisturizing benefits. Optimal concentration for specific actives within the filtrate is poorly characterized. |
| **pH Requirements** | Formulated at **pH 5 – 7** (compatible with skin's acid mantle at the lower end). Glycolic acid content shifts pH slightly acidic. Stability decreases below pH 4 or above pH 8. |
| **Conflicts** | Generally compatible with most actives. Glycolic acid content means caution when layering with high-strength AHAs or retinoids (cumulative irritation). No known chemical incompatibilities with common actives. |
| **Side Effects** | Very well tolerated. Mild irritation or breakouts possible (likely due to the glycoprotein/fatty acid component). Allergic reactions are rare but documented — particularly in individuals with dust mite or shellfish cross-reactivity (tropomyosin cross-reactivity is theoretical but reported). |
| **Contraindications** | Known allergy to snail-derived products. Shellfish allergy (precautionary — cross-reactivity evidence is anecdotal but products often carry this warning). Vegan/ethical considerations — not a medical contraindication but relevant to product choice. |

---

*Compiled 2026-05-10. Evidence tiers: A = strong (RCTs, meta-analyses), B = moderate (small trials, consistent in-vitro), C = emerging (in-vitro, pilot studies, limited clinical).*
