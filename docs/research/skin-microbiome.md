# Skin Microbiome — Research Document

> The skin microbiome is the ecosystem of bacteria, fungi, viruses, and mites living on your skin. It's not an infection — it's a living shield.

---

## The Basics

Your skin hosts ~1 billion microorganisms per square centimeter. This isn't dirty — it's your immune system's front line.

### Key Players

| Organism | Role | When It Goes Wrong |
|----------|------|-------------------|
| **S. epidermidis** | Most abundant skin bacterium. Protects against pathogens, trains immune system | Usually helpful — rare issues |
| **C. acnes** | Lives in pores. Feeds on sebum | Specific strains trigger acne (not all C. acnes is bad) |
| **S. aureus** | Opportunistic pathogen | Overgrows in eczema, wounds → infection |
| **Malassezia** | Yeast on oily areas (scalp, face, chest) | Triggers seborrheic dermatitis, dandruff, fungal acne |
| **Demodex** | Microscopic mites in hair follicles | Overpopulation linked to rosacea, blepharitis |

### Microbiome Varies by Body Site
- **Oily zones** (T-zone, back): Malassezia, Cutibacterium dominate
- **Moist zones** (underarm, groin): Corynebacterium, Staphylococcus
- **Dry zones** (forearm): Most diverse, mixed community
- **Scalp:** Malassezia, Cutibacterium — unique ecosystem

### What Shapes It
- Age (less diverse after 50)
- Hormones (puberty shifts C. acnes populations)
- Geography and climate
- Antibiotic use (can take months to recover)
- Skincare routine (biggest daily influence)

## What Helps the Microbiome

### pH Matters Most
- Healthy skin pH: 4.5-5.5 (slightly acidic)
- High-pH cleansers (>7) disrupt the acid mantle → bad bacteria thrive
- Use pH-balanced cleansers (most modern face washes are fine)

### Prebiotics (Feed the Good Guys)
- Ingredients that selectively feed beneficial bacteria
- Examples: inulin, fructooligosaccharides, oat beta-glucan
- Evidence: Moderate — conceptually sound, clinical data emerging

### Probiotics (Add Good Bacteria)
- Topical live bacteria — concept is appealing, evidence is weak
- Most skincare "probiotics" are actually POSTBIOTICS (see below)
- True live probiotics are hard to formulate (preservatives kill them)
- Brands trying: Esse, Gallinee

### Postbiotics (Bacterial Byproducts)
- Lysates, ferments, metabolic products of beneficial bacteria
- More stable than live probotics, easier to formulate
- Examples: lactobacillus ferment lysate, bifida ferment lysate
- Evidence: Moderate — some studies show barrier support, anti-inflammatory
- Brands: Estée Lauder (ANR uses bifida), Manyo Factory, Missha

### Gentle Cleansing
- Don't over-wash (2x/day max for face)
- Avoid harsh surfactants (SLS/SLES can strip the microbiome)
- Oil cleansing or micellar water can be gentler alternatives
- Don't double-cleanse daily unless wearing heavy makeup/sunscreen

### Barrier Support
- Ceramides, cholesterol, fatty acids — the lipid matrix protects microbes
- Niacinamide supports barrier
- Avoid unnecessary exfoliation (destroys microbial habitat)

## What Hurts the Microbiome

### Antibiotics (Oral AND Topical)
- **Biggest disruptor.** Oral antibiotics decimate gut AND skin microbiome
- Recovery: weeks to months, sometimes incomplete
- Topical antibiotics (clindamycin) — targeted but still reduce diversity
- Benzoyl peroxide — kills C. acnes AND beneficial bacteria indiscriminately
- When necessary, use the shortest course possible

### Over-Cleansing
- More than 2x daily
- High-pH bar soaps (traditional soap pH 9-10)
- Hot water (strips lipids that house microbes)
- Scrubbing/exfoliating daily

### Harsh Actives (at high frequency)
- High-strength retinoids (>0.5% tretinoin nightly) → barrier disruption → microbiome disruption
- Daily chemical peels (AHA/BHA)
- The fix: cycle actives, don't use everything every day

### Antimicrobial Ingredients
- Triclosan (banned in many products but still in some)
- Chlorhexidine (prescription cleansers)
- Silver-based products
- Tea tree oil at high concentrations

### Steroid Overuse
- Topical corticosteroids thin the skin, reduce immune function locally
- Allows S. aureus overgrowth → steroid rosacea, perioral dermatitis
- Never use >2 weeks without medical supervision

### Environmental Damage
- UV radiation alters microbiome composition
- Pollution increases oxidative stress → shifts microbial populations
- Air conditioning/heating → dry skin → different microbial community

## Microbiome & Skin Conditions

### Acne
- **NOT just "C. acnes = bad."** Specific strains cause acne, others are protective
- Acne-prone skin has lower microbial diversity
- Phylotype IA1 (RT4, RT5) strains = inflammatory acne
- Phylotype III, IV strains = commensal, possibly protective
- **Implication:** Killing ALL C. acnes (benzoyl peroxide) may not be optimal — strain-specific treatment is the future

### Eczema / Atopic Dermatitis
- **S. aureus dominance** is the hallmark — replaces diverse community
- S. aureus produces toxins that damage barrier, trigger inflammation
- Loss of S. epidermidis (which normally keeps S. aureus in check)
- **Treatment approach:** Restore diversity, suppress S. aureus (dilute bleach baths, targeted antimicrobials)

### Rosacea
- **Demodex mites** overpopulate — present in 80-100% of rosacea patients (vs 20-50% of controls)
- Bacteria inside Demodex (Bacillus oleronius) trigger immune response
- Also linked to gut dysbiosis (H. pylori connection in some studies)
- **Treatment:** Ivermectin (Soolantra) kills Demodox, reduces inflammation

### Seborrheic Dermatitis / Dandruff
- **Malassezia** yeast overgrowth on oily areas
- Produces oleic acid → inflammation in susceptible individuals
- Antifungals (ketoconazole, zinc pyrithione) work by targeting Malassezia
- Not curable — managed, because Malassezia is normal flora that flares

### Wound Healing
- Healthy microbiome supports healing
- Biofilm formation (bacterial communities) can delay healing
- S. aureus biofilms are a major problem in chronic wounds
- Microbiome-informed wound care is emerging

## Emerging Science

### Bacteriophage Therapy
- Phages are viruses that kill specific bacteria
- Could target S. aureus in eczema without disrupting other bacteria
- More precise than antibiotics
- Status: Research phase, clinical trials ongoing

### Microbiome Transplant (Skin)
- Transferring healthy skin microbiome to damaged areas
- Used experimentally for eczema (transfer from healthy skin to affected)
- Status: Very early, mostly academic research

### Personalized Microbiome Analysis
- Companies offer skin swab analysis (similar to gut microbiome tests)
- Can identify dominant species, diversity score
- Status: Consumer products exist but clinical utility unclear
- Opportunity for SKINgenius: "Your skin microbiome score → personalized recommendations"

### Strain-Specific Probiotics
- Moving beyond "contains Lactobacillus" to specific strains with evidence
- L. rhamnosus GG (eczema prevention)
- L. reuteri (acne, anti-inflammatory)
- B. longum (barrier support)
- **Key insight:** The strain matters as much as the species
