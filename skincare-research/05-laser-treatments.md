# Laser Treatments: Comprehensive Guide

## What it is
Focused light energy delivered at specific wavelengths to target chromophores (water, melanin, hemoglobin) in the skin, creating controlled thermal injury that stimulates collagen remodeling, removes pigmentation, and resurfaces texture.

## Best for
- Fine lines and deep wrinkles
- Acne scarring (atrophic, ice pick, boxcar, rolling)
- Surgical and traumatic scars
- Hyperpigmentation (sun spots, age spots, melasma)
- Vascular lesions (spider veins, rosacea, port-wine stains)
- Skin laxity and texture irregularities
- Enlarged pores
- Photoaging and sun damage
- Tattoo removal
- Hair removal
- Actinic keratosis and precancerous lesions
- Stretch marks
- Warts and skin tags

## How it works
Laser energy is selectively absorbed by target chromophores (water, melanin, hemoglobin) based on wavelength. The absorbed energy converts to heat, causing controlled tissue injury that triggers wound healing and collagen remodeling. Key mechanisms include:
1. **Photothermolysis:** Targeted heating and destruction of specific tissue (blood vessels, pigment, hair follicles)
2. **Fractional photothermolysis:** Creating microscopic treatment zones (MTZs) surrounded by untreated tissue, enabling faster healing
3. **Photoacoustic effect:** Picosecond pulses create acoustic shock waves that shatter pigment particles (tattoo ink, melasma)
4. **Thermal collagen remodeling:** Controlled heating causes collagen denaturation and subsequent new collagen synthesis
5. **Ablative resurfacing:** Vaporization of epidermal/dermal tissue with subsequent regeneration

## Longevity/Duration
- **Ablative CO2/Er:YAG:** Results can last 3-5+ years with proper sun protection and skincare
- **Non-Ablative Fractional:** Results last 6-12 months; maintenance every 6-12 months
- **Picosecond:** Pigmentation results vary; melasma may recur without maintenance
- **IPL:** Results last 3-6 months; maintenance every 3-6 months recommended
- **Vascular:** Often long-lasting for treated vessels; new vessels may develop
- **Note:** Longevity significantly impacted by sun exposure, skincare, and aging

## Pros
1. **Dramatic results possible** - especially with ablative fractional lasers
2. **Precision targeting** - wavelength selection for specific chromophores
3. **Collagen stimulation** - long-term improvement in skin quality
4. **Versatility** - single device can address multiple concerns
5. **FDA-cleared** for numerous dermatological indications
6. **Well-established efficacy** - decades of clinical evidence
7. **Customizable depth** - adjustable parameters for individual needs
8. **Long-lasting results** - particularly for ablative treatments
9. **Single-session efficacy** - some conditions respond to one treatment
10. **Combination-friendly** - can be combined with fillers, neurotoxins, microneedling
11. **Treats precancerous lesions** - actinic keratosis management
12. **Improves skin elasticity** - thermal collagen remodeling over months
13. **Effective for stubborn pigmentation** - when topicals fail
14. **Permanent tattoo removal possible** - with picosecond technology
15. **Progressive improvement** - results continue to develop over 3-6 months

## Cons
1. **Significant downtime** - especially with ablative lasers (5-14 days)
2. **Pain during treatment** - requires topical or local anesthesia
3. **Risk of hyperpigmentation** - particularly in darker skin types (Fitzpatrick IV-VI)
4. **Risk of hypopigmentation** - more common with deeper/ablative treatments
5. **Scarring potential** - rare but possible, especially with improper settings
6. **Infection risk** - bacterial, viral (HSV), fungal if aftercare neglected
7. **Prolonged redness** - can last weeks to months with deeper treatments
8. **Acne flare** - possible post-treatment
9. **Cost** - significantly more expensive than peels or microneedling
10. **Multiple sessions often needed** - for non-ablative and pigmentation treatments
11. **Strict sun avoidance** - mandatory for weeks to months post-treatment
12. **Not suitable for all skin types** - darker skin has higher complication risk
13. **Operator skill critical** - settings, technique, and experience matter significantly
14. **Delayed results** - optimal improvement takes 3-6 months
15. **Potential for demarcation lines** - visible line between treated and untreated areas

## Downtime
- **Fractional Ablative CO2:**
  - Days 1-2: Significant redness, swelling, oozing
  - Days 3-5: Peeling, crusting, continued redness
  - Days 5-10: Healing, residual pinkness
  - Makeup: Day 5-7 (once epithelialized)
  - Full recovery: 10-14 days (pinkness may persist 2-4 weeks)
  - Activity: Light activities day 3-5, normal by day 7-10
- **Fractional Ablative Er:YAG:**
  - Days 1-2: Redness, mild swelling
  - Days 3-5: Peeling, resolving redness
  - Days 5-7: Mostly healed
  - Makeup: Day 3-5
  - Full recovery: 5-10 days
- **Non-Ablative Fractional:**
  - Day 1: Mild redness, sunburn-like sensation
  - Days 2-3: Minimal peeling/flaking
  - Makeup: Next day (mineral)
  - Full recovery: 1-3 days
  - Activity: Normal next day
- **Picosecond:**
  - Day 1: Mild redness, swelling possible
  - Days 2-3: Resolving
  - Makeup: Next day
  - Full recovery: 1-3 days
- **IPL:**
  - Day 1: Mild redness, darkening of pigmented spots
  - Days 2-5: Pigmented spots flake off
  - Makeup: Same day (mineral)
  - Full recovery: 0-2 days (pigment resolution 5-7 days)
- **Vascular Laser:**
  - Day 1: Redness, possible purpura (bruising) with certain wavelengths
  - Days 2-7: Vessels fade, purpura resolves if present
  - Makeup: Same day (mineral)
  - Full recovery: 1-7 days (depending on purpura)

## Risks + Safety Findings
**Common:** Erythema/redness (90-100% ablative, 70-90% non-ablative), edema/swelling (60-80% ablative, 30-50% non-ablative), transient hyperpigmentation (15-30%)
**Uncommon:**
- Prolonged erythema >1 month (5-15% ablative, 2-5% non-ablative)
- Acne flare (5-10%)
- Herpes simplex reactivation (2-7%, higher perioral)
- Contact dermatitis from post-treatment products (3-5%)
- Milia formation (3-8%)

**Rare but Serious:**
- **Hyperpigmentation (PIH):** 10-40% in Fitzpatrick IV-VI (lower with proper pre/post-care); can be permanent
- **Hypopigmentation:** 2-10% with ablative lasers; can be permanent and very difficult to treat
- **Scarring:** <1% with proper technique; higher risk with deep settings, poor wound care
- **Infection:** Bacterial (staph/strep), viral (HSV, VZV), fungal; <1% with prophylaxis
- **Ectropion:** Eyelid malposition with periorbital treatment (rare, requires expertise)
- **Airborne transmission:** Laser plume can contain viral DNA; proper smoke evacuation essential

**Key Safety Considerations:**
- **Skin typing crucial:** Fitzpatrick I-III lowest risk, IV-VI require specific wavelengths and conservative settings
- **Isotretinoin:** Wait minimum 6 months after discontinuation before laser treatment
- **Sun exposure:** Avoid 4-6 weeks pre and post treatment; strict SPF 30+ daily
- **Pre-treatment:** Hydroquinone/tretinoin 2-4 weeks for darker skin types to reduce PIH risk
- **Cooling essential:** Proper skin cooling during treatment prevents thermal injury
- **Test spots:** Recommended for darker skin types or new device settings
- **Eye protection:** Mandatory for both patient and provider
- **Anesthesia:** Topical, local, or nerve blocks depending on depth and area
- **Herpes prophylaxis:** Consider for perioral treatment in patients with history
- **Post-care strict:** Gentle cleansing, barrier repair, sun avoidance, no active ingredients

## When to Recommend vs Skincare Alone
**RECOMMEND LASER TREATMENTS WHEN:**
- Moderate to severe photoaging unresponsive to topicals
- Deep wrinkles and rhytides requiring significant resurfacing
- Atrophic acne scarring (particularly fractional ablative)
- Stubborn hyperpigmentation (melasma, sun spots) resistant to medical-grade skincare
- Vascular lesions (telangiectasia, rosacea) not responding to topicals
- Surgical or traumatic scars seeking improvement
- Tattoo removal requested
- Precancerous lesions (actinic keratosis) present
- Patient seeking dramatic, long-lasting improvement in skin quality
- Combination approach planned (laser + fillers + neurotoxins)
- Skin laxity not severe enough for surgical intervention
- Patient can commit to downtime and strict aftercare
- Fitzpatrick I-III skin type (lower complication risk)
- Patient has realistic expectations about results and recovery

**SKINCARE ALONE MAY BE SUFFICIENT WHEN:**
- Early photoaging with minimal texture changes (retinoids, antioxidants, sunscreen)
- Fine lines only responsive to topical retinoids
- Mild pigmentation improving with hydroquinone, vitamin C, niacinamide
- Prevention-focused approach in patients <30
- Fitzpatrick IV-VI skin types with high PIH risk (unless experienced laser provider)
- Recent isotretinoin use (<6 months)
- Active infection or inflammatory skin condition in treatment area
- History of keloid or hypertrophic scarring
- Unable to commit to strict sun avoidance post-treatment
- Budget constraints prevent proper series of treatments
- Unable to accommodate required downtime
- Pregnancy or breastfeeding
- Autoimmune conditions affecting healing
- Unrealistic expectations about degree of improvement
- Patient prefers gradual, conservative approach
- Melasma in darker skin types where laser may worsen condition

**CLINICAL PEARLS:**
- **Start conservative:** Better to under-treat and add intensity than over-treat
- **Skin typing is non-negotiable:** Always assess Fitzpatrick type before selecting laser
- **Pre-treatment priming:** 4-6 weeks of hydroquinone + tretinoin for darker skin types reduces PIH
- **Test spots:** Always perform on darker skin or new settings before full treatment
- **Combination approach:** Non-ablative fractional + IPL can address multiple concerns with less downtime
- **Interval matters:** 4-6 weeks between non-ablative sessions; 3-6 months for ablative
- **Sun protection forever:** Strict SPF use extends laser results significantly
- **Maintenance matters:** Annual or bi-annual touch-ups maintain results long-term
- **Device selection matters:** Match the laser to the chromophore and condition, not the other way around
- **Patient education critical:** Set realistic expectations for downtime, results timeline, and maintenance needs
