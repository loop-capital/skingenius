# Acne Vulgaris: Evidence-Based Treatment Guide

## Quick Summary
Acne vulgaris is a chronic inflammatory skin condition affecting the pilosebaceous unit, driven by androgens, excess sebum, *Cutibacterium acnes* colonization, and inflammation. The most effective evidence-based treatments include topical retinoids (Grade A), benzoyl peroxide (Grade A), and oral isotretinoin for severe cases (Grade A). Hormonal therapies and oral antibiotics remain valuable adjuncts.

## What It Is
Acne vulgaris is the most common skin disorder globally, affecting ~85% of adolescents and persisting into adulthood for up to 50% of individuals. It manifests as comedones (open/closed), papules, pustules, nodules, and cysts, primarily on the face, chest, and back. Severity ranges from mild comedonal to severe nodulocystic acne.

**Pathogenesis involves four key factors:**
1. Follicular hyperkeratinization (comedone formation)
2. Increased sebum production (androgen-driven)
3. *Cutibacterium acnes* proliferation
4. Inflammation and innate immune response

## Root Causes

### Primary Drivers
- **Hormonal fluctuations**: Androgens stimulate sebaceous gland activity; fluctuations during puberty, menstruation, pregnancy, and PCOS exacerbate acne
- **Genetics**: Twin studies suggest heritability of ~80% for acne severity
- **Diet**: High glycemic load diets and dairy (especially skim milk) show association in epidemiological studies
- **Microbiome dysbiosis**: *C. acnes* phylotype IA1 associated with inflammatory acne

### Exacerbating Factors
- Occlusive cosmetics and hair products ("pomade acne")
- Mechanical irritation (acne mechanica)
- Certain medications: corticosteroids, lithium, androgens, phenytoin
- Stress-induced cortisol elevation

## The Evidence-Based Approach

### Grade A Treatments (Strong Evidence)

**Topical Retinoids**
- Tretinoin, adapalene, tazarotene normalize follicular keratinization
- Reduce comedones and inflammatory lesions
- Meta-analysis: 50-80% reduction in lesion counts over 12 weeks
- Adapalene 0.1% now OTC in US; comparable efficacy to tretinoin 0.025% with better tolerability

**Benzoyl Peroxide (BPO)**
- Bactericidal against *C. acnes* without resistance development
- 2.5-10% formulations; lower concentrations often as effective with less irritation
- Evidence supports combination with topical antibiotics to prevent resistance

**Oral Isotretinoin**
- 0.5-1 mg/kg/day for 15-20 weeks
- Only therapy that targets all four pathogenic factors
- Achieves long-term remission in ~85% after single course
- Teratogenic — mandatory pregnancy prevention program (iPLEDGE)

**Topical Combination Therapy**
- BPO + adapalene (Epiduo): superior to monotherapy
- BPO + clindamycin: effective with reduced resistance risk

### Grade B Treatments (Moderate Evidence)

**Topical Antibiotics**
- Clindamycin, erythromycin (declining use due to resistance)
- Must be combined with BPO; never used as monotherapy

**Oral Antibiotics**
- Doxycycline, minocycline, sarecycline
- Limit to 3-4 months; always with BPO topical
- Sarecycline: narrower spectrum, less GI disruption

**Hormonal Therapy**
- Combined oral contraceptives (COCs): FDA-approved for acne
- Spironolactone (off-label): 50-200 mg/day; anti-androgen
- Particularly effective in adult female hormonal acne

**Azelaic Acid**
- 15-20% formulations; anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial
- Safe in pregnancy; useful for post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation

**Salicylic Acid**
- Lipophilic beta-hydroxy acid; penetrates sebaceous follicles
- 0.5-2% concentrations; modest efficacy as monotherapy

### Grade C Treatments (Limited Evidence)

**Topical Dapsone**
- 5-7.5% gel; anti-inflammatory
- Particularly helpful in adult female inflammatory acne

**Chemical Peels**
- Salicylic acid, glycolic acid, Jessner's peels
- Adjunctive; limited RCT evidence

**Light/Laser Therapy**
- Blue light (kills *C. acnes*); PDT; fractional lasers
- Expensive; variable results; limited durable evidence

**Zinc**
- Oral zinc sulfate/glucconate: modest anti-inflammatory effect
- Not first-line

### Grade D/F (Myths or Ineffective)

- **Toothpaste on pimples**: No RCT evidence; can cause irritation and contact dermatitis
- **Tanning/sun exposure**: Temporarily masks acne; UV damages skin barrier and worsens post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation
- **Excessive washing/scrubbing**: Disrupts skin barrier; does not improve acne
- **"Detox" teas/supplements**: No evidence; potential hepatotoxicity
- **Coconut oil as treatment**: Highly comedogenic; worsens acne

## Holistic Approach

### Evidence-Based Topicals
| Concern | Ingredients |
|---------|-------------|
| Active breakouts | Benzoyl peroxide 2.5-5%, adapalene 0.1% |
| Comedones | Tretinoin 0.025-0.1%, adapalene |
| Inflammation | Azelaic acid 15-20%, niacinamide 4% |
| Post-acne marks | Azelaic acid, retinoids, vitamin C |

### Supplements (Limited but Promising Evidence)
- **Zinc**: 30-50 mg elemental zinc daily; anti-inflammatory
- **Omega-3 fatty acids**: EPA/DHA 1-2g daily; may reduce inflammation
- **Probiotics**: *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* strains; emerging evidence for gut-skin axis
- **Vitamin D**: Supplement if deficient; some association with severity
- **Spearmint tea**: Small RCT showed reduction in androgen levels in women

### Lifestyle
- **Low glycemic load diet**: Reduces insulin/IGF-1 signaling
- **Dairy reduction**: Especially skim milk; consider elimination trial
- **Stress management**: Cortisol exacerbates acne
- **Sleep hygiene**: Sleep deprivation increases inflammatory markers
- **Non-comedogenic products**: Check ingredient lists

## When to See a Dermatologist

- Nodulocystic or severe acne
- Scarring (atrophic or hypertrophic) developing
- Significant psychosocial impact
- Failed 8-12 weeks of appropriate OTC therapy
- Signs of hyperandrogenism (irregular periods, hirsutism)
- Acne fulminans (sudden severe onset with systemic symptoms)
- Need for isotretinoin (requires prescriber in iPLEDGE program)

### Beginner Routine
| Step | AM | PM |
|------|-----|-----|
| Cleanse | Gentle non-foaming cleanser | Gentle cleanser |
| Treat | — | Adapalene 0.1% (start 2-3x/week) |
| Moisturize | Non-comedogenic moisturizer | Non-comedogenic moisturizer |
| Protect | SPF 30+ mineral or chemical | — |

Add benzoyl peroxide 2.5% in AM if needed after 2 weeks.

### Advanced Routine
| Step | AM | PM |
|------|-----|-----|
| Cleanse | Salicylic acid 0.5-2% cleanser or gentle cleanser | Oil cleanse + gentle cleanser |
| Treat | Benzoyl peroxide 2.5-5% or azelaic acid 15% | Tretinoin 0.05% or adapalene 0.3% |
| Treat 2 | Vitamin C 10-15% (for marks) | Niacinamide 4-10% |
| Moisturize | Gel-based moisturizer | Richer moisturizer |
| Protect | SPF 50+ | — |

**Notes**: Introduce actives slowly (one every 2 weeks). Tretinoin: apply to dry skin (20 min after cleansing) to reduce irritation. Buffer with moisturizer if sensitive.

## Common Myths vs Facts

| Myth | Fact |
|------|------|
| "Acne means dirty skin" | Acne is not caused by poor hygiene; over-washing worsens it |
| "You need to dry out acne" | Over-drying triggers rebound oil production and barrier damage |
| "Adults don't get acne" | Adult acne affects 12-22% of women; increasingly common |
| "Makeup always causes acne" | Non-comedogenic formulas are generally safe; remove thoroughly |
| "Isotretinoin causes depression" | Evidence is mixed; screen for mood changes but causal link unproven |
| "Diet doesn't matter" | High glycemic load and dairy show association in studies |
| "Popping pimples helps" | Increases inflammation, infection risk, and scarring |
| "Natural is always better" | Tea tree oil has modest evidence; many "natural" remedies irritate |
| "Retinoids thin the skin" | They thin the stratum corneum but thicken the dermis over time |
