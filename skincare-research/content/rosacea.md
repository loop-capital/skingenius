# Rosacea: Evidence-Based Treatment Guide

## Quick Summary
Rosacea is a chronic inflammatory skin condition characterized by facial erythema, papules, pustules, telangiectasias, and phymatous changes. It affects ~5% of the global population, with higher prevalence in fair-skinned individuals. Evidence-based treatments include topical ivermectin (Grade A), metronidazole (Grade A), azelaic acid (Grade A), and oral doxycycline (Grade A). Trigger avoidance and gentle skincare are foundational. Laser therapy addresses persistent erythema and telangiectasias.

## What It Is
Rosacea is a chronic inflammatory disorder primarily affecting the convexities of the central face (cheeks, nose, chin, forehead). It is classified into four subtypes per the National Rosacea Society:

1. **Erythematotelangiectatic rosacea (ETR)**: Persistent facial erythema, flushing, telangiectasias
2. **Papulopustular rosacea (PPR)**: Papules, pustules on background of erythema
3. **Phymatous rosacea**: Skin thickening, nodularities, sebaceous hyperplasia (rhinophyma)
4. **Ocular rosacea**: Blepharitis, conjunctival hyperemia, keratitis, meibomian gland dysfunction

**Pathophysiology**: Multifactorial — neurovascular dysregulation, innate immune dysfunction, altered skin microbiome (*Demodex* mites, *Bacillus oleronius*), and cathelicidin LL-37 overexpression.

## Root Causes

### Primary Factors
- **Neurovascular dysregulation**: Enhanced flushing response; abnormal vascular reactivity
- **Innate immune activation**: Elevated LL-37 (cathelicidin antimicrobial peptide) levels
- **Microbiome alterations**: *Demodex folliculorum* overpopulation; *Bacillus oleronius* from *Demodex* may trigger inflammation
- **Genetics**: Family history present in 30-40% of patients
- **UV exposure**: Chronic UV damage to dermal vasculature

### Contributing Factors
- **Skin barrier dysfunction**: Increased transepidermal water loss (TEWL)
- **Helicobacter pylori**: Controversial association; some studies link to rosacea
- **Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO)**: Limited evidence; some patients improve with rifaximin
- **Menopause**: Hormonal fluctuations can exacerbate flushing

## The Evidence-Based Approach

### Grade A Treatments (Strong Evidence)

**Topical Ivermectin 1% Cream (Soolantra)**
- Targets *Demodex* mites and reduces inflammation
- Superior to metronidazole 0.75% gel in head-to-head RCTs
- Significant reduction in inflammatory lesions at 12 weeks
- Once-daily application; well-tolerated

**Topical Metronidazole**
- 0.75% gel/cream/lotion, 1% cream
- First-line for papulopustular rosacea for decades
- Anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties
- Multiple RCTs confirm efficacy; safe for long-term maintenance

**Topical Azelaic Acid 15% Gel**
- Anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and normalizes keratinization
- FDA-approved for PPR; comparable efficacy to metronidazole
- Also addresses post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation
- Twice-daily application

**Oral Doxycycline**
- 40mg modified-release (sub-antimicrobial dose): Oracea
- Anti-inflammatory at this dose (does not act as antibiotic)
- FDA-approved for inflammatory lesions of rosacea
- Efficacy for papules/pustules; limited effect on erythema
- Limit duration; can use intermittently

**Brimonidine 0.33% Gel (Mirvaso)**
- Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist; topical vasoconstrictor
- FDA-approved for persistent facial erythema
- Onset within 30 minutes; lasts up to 12 hours
- Rebound erythema reported in some patients; paradoxical worsening possible

**Oxymetazoline 1% Cream (Rhofade)**
- Alpha-1 adrenergic agonist; vasoconstrictor
- Similar efficacy to brimonidine for persistent erythema
- Once-daily application; longer duration of action

**Oral Isotretinoin**
- Low-dose: 0.1-0.3 mg/kg/day or intermittent dosing
- Reserved for severe, refractory PPR or phymatous rosacea
- Dramatic efficacy; requires monitoring for side effects
- Not FDA-approved for rosacea but widely used off-label

### Grade B Treatments (Moderate Evidence)

**Topical Sodium Sulfacetamide 10% + Sulfur 5%**
- Anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial
- RCT evidence supports efficacy; less commonly used now
- Can be drying; sulfur odor

**Topical Clindamycin**
- 1% gel/lotion; anti-inflammatory
- Generally reserved for cases not responding to first-line agents
- Should not be used as monotherapy (resistance concern less relevant in rosacea)

**Topical Tacrolimus / Pimecrolimus**
- Calcineurin inhibitors; anti-inflammatory without steroid side effects
- Useful for steroid-induced rosacea or when steroids must be avoided
- Limited RCTs specifically for rosacea

**Oral Tetracyclines (Minocycline, Tetracycline)**
- 50-100mg daily or sub-antimicrobial dosing
- Doxycycline preferred due to MR formulation; minocycline alternative

**Topical Minocycline (FMX102)**
- 1.5% foam; FDA-approved for PPR
- Emerging evidence; convenient formulation

**Laser and Light Therapy**
- **Pulsed dye laser (PDL)**: Gold-standard for erythema and telangiectasias
- **Nd:YAG 1064nm**: Deeper vessels; suitable for darker skin types
- **IPL**: Broad improvement in erythema, telangiectasias, and inflammatory lesions
- Series of 3-6 treatments; maintenance required

### Grade C Treatments (Limited Evidence)

**Topical Permethrin 5%**
- *Demodex*-targeting; some small studies show benefit
- Off-label; can be irritating

**Oral Ivermectin**
- 200μg/kg single dose or short course
- Case reports and small series for refractory rosacea
- Off-label; potential neurotoxicity in high doses

**Topical Niacinamide**
- Anti-inflammatory; improves barrier function
- May reduce erythema; limited specific rosacea RCTs
- Well-tolerated in rosacea-prone skin

**Topical Zinc / Zinc Sulfate**
- Anti-inflammatory; limited clinical evidence
- Some patients report benefit

**Probiotics**
- Gut-skin axis; limited RCTs specifically for rosacea
- *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* strains; emerging evidence

**Dietary Modifications**
- Low-histamine or anti-inflammatory diets: anecdotal; limited evidence
- Omega-3 supplementation: anti-inflammatory; indirect support

### Grade D/F (Myths or Ineffective)

- **Topical corticosteroids**: Cause steroid-induced rosacea/perioral dermatitis; absolutely contraindicated on face for rosacea
- **Alcohol-based toners / astringents**: Worsen barrier dysfunction and erythema
- **Harsh exfoliants (scrubs, high-concentration AHAs/BHAs)**: Damage barrier; worsen inflammation
- **"Curing" rosacea with detox/cleanses**: No evidence; potentially harmful
- **Essential oils undiluted on face**: Common irritants and allergens; worsen rosacea
- **Antifungal shampoos on face**: No evidence for fungal cause of rosacea

## Holistic Approach

### Evidence-Based Topicals
| Concern | Ingredients |
|---------|-------------|
| Papules/pustules | Ivermectin 1%, metronidazole 0.75%, azelaic acid 15% |
| Persistent erythema | Brimonidine 0.33%, oxymetazoline 1% |
| Telangiectasias | Laser (PDL, Nd:YAG); no topical cures |
| Rhinophyma | Oral isotretinoin; surgical/laser debulking |
| Barrier repair | Niacinamide, ceramides, hyaluronic acid |

### Skincare for Rosacea-Prone Skin
- **Cleanser**: Gentle, non-foaming, pH-balanced; avoid sulfates
- **Moisturizer**: Ceramide-rich, fragrance-free; repair barrier
- **Sunscreen**: Mineral (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide); chemical sunscreens may sting
- **Avoid**: Fragrance, alcohol, menthol, peppermint, eucalyptus, witch hazel, harsh acids
- **Temperature**: Lukewarm water only; no hot showers on face

### Lifestyle and Trigger Management
- **Sun protection**: Broad-spectrum SPF 30+ daily; hats; shade
- **Temperature management**: Avoid saunas, hot tubs, very hot beverages
- **Diet**: Identify personal triggers; consider low-histamine trial
- **Alcohol**: Reduce or eliminate; especially red wine (high histamine)
- **Stress**: Mindfulness, yoga, CBT for stress-induced flushing
- **Exercise**: Low-intensity preferred; cool down gradually
- **Skincare minimalism**: Fewer products = fewer potential irritants

### Supplements
- **Omega-3 fatty acids**: 1-2g EPA/DHA; anti-inflammatory
- **Zinc**: 25-50mg elemental zinc; modest anti-inflammatory benefit
- **Probiotics**: Mixed evidence; consider trial with *Lactobacillus* strains
- **Nicotinamide**: 500mg twice daily; anti-inflammatory; evidence for non-melanoma skin cancer prevention

## When to See a Dermatologist

- New-onset facial redness not explained by triggers
- Development of papules, pustules, or eye symptoms
- Persistent erythema not responding to OTC/basic measures
- Suspicion of steroid-induced rosacea (history of topical steroid use on face)
- Phymatous changes (nose thickening)
- Ocular symptoms: dry eyes, gritty sensation, blurred vision (urgent — refer to ophthalmology)
- Failed 8-12 weeks of appropriate topical therapy
- Interest in laser therapy for telangiectasias or persistent redness
- Significant psychosocial impact or quality of life impairment

### Advanced Routine (PPR + Erythema)
| Step | AM | PM |
|------|-----|-----|
| Cleanse | Gentle cleanser | Gentle cleanser |
| Treat 1 | Brimonidine 0.33% (for erythema) or — | Ivermectin 1% |
| Treat 2 | Azelaic acid 15% (if tolerating) | Metronidazole 0.75% (alternate nights) |
| Moisturize | Barrier-repair moisturizer | Rich night cream |
| Protect | Mineral SPF 50 | — |
| Weekly | — | Soothing mask (colloidal oatmeal) |

### Sample Weekly Routine for Papulopustular Rosacea
| Day | AM | PM |
|-----|-----|-----|
| Mon | Mineral SPF | Ivermectin 1% |
| Tue | Azelaic acid 15% + SPF | Metronidazole 0.75% |
| Wed | Mineral SPF | Ivermectin 1% |
| Thu | Azelaic acid 15% + SPF | Metronidazole 0.75% |
| Fri | Mineral SPF | Ivermectin 1% |
| Sat | Rest + SPF | Azelaic acid 15% |
| Sun | Brimonidine (if event) + SPF | Recovery night |

**Notes**: Introduce one active at a time. If using brimonidine, apply to small test area first (paradoxical erythema in ~5-10%). Moisturizer can be applied before actives ("moisture sandwich") to reduce irritation. Avoid introducing new products during flares.

## Common Myths vs Facts

| Myth | Fact |
|------|------|
| "Rosacea is just adult acne" | Different pathology; acne has comedones, rosacea does not; treatments differ |
| "Rosacea is caused by poor hygiene" | Not an infection or hygiene issue; inflammatory and vascular disorder |
| "Alcohol causes rosacea" | Alcohol is a trigger, not the cause; many rosacea patients do not drink |
| "Rosacea can be cured" | No cure; excellent control achievable with consistent treatment and trigger management |
| "Topical steroids help rosacea redness" | Steroids cause rebound worsening and steroid-induced rosacea; absolutely contraindicated |
| "You should exfoliate rosacea skin" | Harsh exfoliation damages barrier and worsens inflammation; gentle only |
| "Rosacea only affects the face" | Can affect neck, chest, scalp; ocular rosacea affects eyes |
| "Makeup worsens rosacea" | Mineral makeup (titanium dioxide, zinc oxide) can actually soothe and protect |
| "Only fair-skinned people get rosacea" | Most common in Fitzpatrick I-III but occurs in all skin types; often underdiagnosed in darker skin |
| "Dermatologists always recommend antibiotics" | Sub-antimicrobial doxycycline (40mg MR) is anti-inflammatory, not antibiotic |
