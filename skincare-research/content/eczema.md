# Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis): Evidence-Based Treatment Guide

## Quick Summary
Atopic dermatitis (AD) is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin condition characterized by pruritus, erythema, and barrier dysfunction. It affects up to 20% of children and 3-10% of adults. First-line treatment includes liberal emollient use and topical corticosteroids (Grade A). Newer targeted therapies — topical calcineurin inhibitors, crisaborole, and biologics (dupilumab, tralokinumab) — have transformed management of moderate-to-severe disease. Identifying and avoiding triggers is foundational.

## What It Is
Atopic dermatitis is the most common chronic inflammatory skin disease. It is part of the "atopic triad" (with asthma and allergic rhinitis). Key features:

- **Pruritus**: The hallmark symptom; "the itch that rashes"
- **Erythema, scaling, lichenification**: Varies by age and chronicity
- **Xerosis**: Dry skin from barrier dysfunction
- **Flexural predominance in children/adults**: Antecubital and popliteal fossae
- **Facial/extensor involvement in infants**

**Pathophysiology**: Type 2 inflammation driven by IL-4, IL-13, IL-31; filaggrin gene mutations impair barrier function; dysbiosis of skin microbiome (*Staphylococcus aureus* colonization in 90% of flares).

## Root Causes

### Genetic Factors
- **Filaggrin (FLG) mutations**: Most significant genetic risk; impair cornified envelope
- **Atopic family history**: Strong predictor; ~70% have personal/family atopy
- **Genome-wide association studies**: IL4, IL13, STAT6, OX40L, TSLP variants

### Immune Dysregulation
- **Th2 skewing**: IL-4, IL-13 drive IgE class switching and barrier disruption
- **IL-31**: "Itch cytokine"; directly activates sensory nerves
- **Th17/Th22**: Contribute in acute flares and chronic disease

### Environmental Triggers
| Trigger | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Soaps/detergents | Strip natural lipids; raise skin pH |
| Fragrances | Common allergens; irritants |
| Wool/synthetic fabrics | Mechanical irritation; heat retention |
| Temperature extremes | Heat increases pruritus; cold dries skin |
| Sweat | Irritant; contains proteases |
| Stress | Neurogenic inflammation; cortisol effects |
| Allergens | Pollen, dust mites, pet dander (IgE-mediated) |
| Food allergens | Eggs, milk, wheat, soy, peanuts, fish (especially in children) |
| Hard water | Higher calcium/magnesium; may worsen barrier |
| *S. aureus* colonization | Superantigens trigger inflammation |

## The Evidence-Based Approach

### Grade A Treatments (Strong Evidence)

**Emollients (Moisturizers)**
- First-line and ongoing maintenance for ALL AD patients
- Apply within 3 minutes of bathing ("soak and seal")
- Ointments > creams > lotions for barrier repair
- Ceramide-containing formulations restore lipid matrix
- RCT evidence: regular emollient use reduces flares and steroid need

**Topical Corticosteroids (TCS)**
- Cornerstone of acute flare management; anti-inflammatory
- Potency ladder: hydrocortisone (low) → triamcinolone (medium) → betamethasone/mometasone (high) → clobetasol (super-high)
- Apply to active lesions 1-2x daily during flares
- "Weekend therapy" (twice weekly on previously affected skin): proven flare prevention
- Fear of side effects (skin atrophy, striae) leads to under-treatment; use appropriate potency for body site and duration
- Ointment vehicle preferred for AD (more occlusive, less burning)

**Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors (TCI)**
- Tacrolimus 0.03% (children)/0.1% (adults); pimecrolimus 1%
- Steroid-sparing for sensitive areas (face, eyelids, skin folds, genitals)
- Black box warning (theoretical lymphoma risk) — no causal evidence in over 20 years of use
- Approved for maintenance (twice weekly) to prevent flares
- Burning/stinging upon application is common initially

**Dupilumab (Dupixent)**
- IL-4Rα antagonist; blocks IL-4 and IL-13
- FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe AD (≥6 months old)
- Subcutaneous injection every 2 weeks (after loading)
- Phase 3 RCTs: 75% improvement (EASI-75) in ~50-60% at 16 weeks
- Side effects: conjunctivitis (10-20%), injection site reactions
- Transforms lives of patients with recalcitrant AD

**Wet Wrap Therapy**
- Apply emollient or TCS, then damp tubular bandage or gauze, then dry layer
- Dramatically increases hydration and steroid efficacy
- Especially effective for acute flares in children
- Can be left on for several hours or overnight

### Grade B Treatments (Moderate Evidence)

**Crisaborole 2% Ointment (Eucrisa)**
- PDE4 inhibitor; anti-inflammatory
- FDA-approved for mild-to-moderate AD (≥3 months)
- Non-steroidal; suitable for sensitive areas
- Burning/stinging common; efficacy modest compared to TCS

**Topical Ruxolitinib 1.5% Cream (Opzelura)**
- JAK1/JAK2 inhibitor
- FDA-approved for short-term use in non-immunocompromised adults/children ≥12
- Rapid itch relief; efficacy comparable to mid-potency TCS
- Label limits continuous use to 8 weeks per treatment area

**Phototherapy**
- Narrowband UVB (311nm): First-line for widespread AD not controlled with topicals
- UVA1 for acute, severe flares
- Requires 2-3x weekly treatments for 6-12 weeks
- Risk of photodamage with long-term use

**Systemic Corticosteroids**
- Short bursts (e.g., prednisone 0.5-1mg/kg x 5-7 days) for severe acute flares
- NOT for chronic maintenance (rebound flares, side effects)

**Systemic Immunosuppressants**
- Cyclosporine: Most evidence; rapid onset; nephrotoxicity limits long-term use
- Methotrexate: Effective; slower onset; hepatotoxicity, teratogenicity
- Azathioprine: TPMT testing required; myelosuppression risk
- Mycophenolate mofetil: Alternative; GI side effects

**Biologics (Emerging)**
- **Tralokinumab**: IL-13 antagonist; FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe AD
- **Nemolizumab**: IL-31RA antagonist; in Phase 3; dramatic itch relief
- **Lebrikizumab**: IL-13 antagonist; Phase 3 completed

**JAK Inhibitors (Oral)**
- **Upadacitinib**: JAK1 selective; FDA-approved for refractory AD
- **Abrocitinib**: JAK1 selective; FDA-approved
- Rapid itch relief; efficacy comparable to dupilumab
- Side effects: acne, headache, lab abnormalities; need monitoring

### Grade C Treatments (Limited Evidence)

**Antihistamines**
- First-generation (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine): Sedating; may help sleep but do not treat AD inflammation
- Second-generation (cetirizine, loratadine): Minimal evidence for pruritus in AD
- Not first-line for AD specifically

**Coal Tar**
- Anti-inflammatory and antipruritic; reduces *S. aureus*
- Messy, malodorous; limited modern RCTs
- 2-5% preparations available OTC

**Bleach Baths**
- 0.005% sodium hypochlorite (1/4 cup household bleach in half-full standard tub)
- Reduces *S. aureus* colonization; RCT evidence shows modest benefit
- 10-minute soak 2x weekly; rinse off; moisturize immediately after

**Probiotics**
- Mixed evidence; some RCTs show modest benefit in children when started prenatally or early infancy
- *Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG* most studied
- Not a standalone treatment

**Elimination Diets**
- ONLY if immediate-type food allergy confirmed (positive SPT/serum IgE + history)
- Broad elimination diets without evidence are harmful (nutritional deficiency, anxiety)
- Consider referral to allergist for testing if food-trigger suspicion

**Acupuncture / Traditional Chinese Medicine**
- Some RCTs show benefit; heterogeneity in interventions
- May be adjunctive; not first-line

### Grade D/F (Myths or Ineffective)

- **"Steroid-free is always better"**: TCS are safe when used appropriately; under-treatment leads to suffering and complications
- **Topical antihistamines (calamine, diphenhydramine cream)**: Sensitizing; can cause contact dermatitis; not recommended
- **Essential oils for eczema**: Tea tree, lavender common allergens; risk of sensitization and contact dermatitis
- "Detox" regimens: No evidence; potentially harmful
- Homeopathy: No evidence beyond placebo
- Topical antibiotic monotherapy: Does not treat inflammation; resistance risk

## Holistic Approach

### Evidence-Based Topicals
| Concern | Treatment |
|---------|-----------|
| Acute flare (mild) | Hydrocortisone 1% + thick emollient |
| Acute flare (moderate) | Triamcinolone 0.1% ointment + wet wraps |
| Acute flare (severe) | Clobetasol 0.05% short course; consider phototherapy/systemic |
| Maintenance | Tacrolimus or pimecrolimus twice weekly; emollients daily |
| Itch (breakthrough) | Cool compresses; wet wraps; oral JAK inhibitor if severe |
| Infection | Dilute bleach baths; culture if weeping/crusted; oral antibiotics if impetiginized |

### Bathing
- **Lukewarm water** (not hot); 5-10 minutes
- **Gentle, fragrance-free cleansers** only
- **Soak and seal**: Apply moisturizer within 3 minutes
- **Bleach baths** for frequent infective flares

### Clothing and Environment
- **Soft cotton**; avoid wool and synthetics directly on skin
- **Layer clothing** for temperature control
- **Humidifier** in dry climates (target 40-60% humidity)
- **Keep fingernails short**; cotton gloves at night to prevent scratching

### Diet
- **No broad elimination without evidence**: Risk of nutritional deficiency and food aversion
- **Food allergy testing**: If history of immediate reactions (hives, vomiting, anaphylaxis) within 2 hours
- **Anti-inflammatory diet**: Omega-3 rich foods (fatty fish), colorful vegetables; limited direct AD evidence

### Stress and Sleep
- **Stress management**: CBT, mindfulness, yoga; stress triggers flares
- **Sleep hygiene**: Pruritus disrupts sleep; sedating antihistamines occasionally for sleep (not AD treatment)
- **Scratching habit reversal**: Behavioral techniques; keep hands busy

### Supplements
- **Vitamin D**: Supplement if deficient; some association with AD severity
- **Omega-3 fatty acids**: 1-2g EPA/DHA; anti-inflammatory; mixed evidence
- **Probiotics**: As above; modest potential benefit
- **Evening primrose oil / borage oil**: GLA content; limited and mixed evidence

## When to See a Dermatologist

- AD not controlled with appropriate topical therapy after 2-4 weeks
- Widespread disease affecting quality of life, sleep, or school/work
- Frequent infections (impetigo, eczema herpeticum — URGENT if suspected)
- Suspected contact dermatitis complicating picture (patch testing needed)
- Eyelid or facial AD not responding to low-potency TCS/TCI
- Interest in phototherapy, biologics, or systemic immunosuppressants
- Significant psychosocial impact or signs of anxiety/depression
- Diagnostic uncertainty (could be psoriasis, cutaneous T-cell lymphoma, etc.)

## Building Your Routine

### Beginner Routine (Mild AD)
| Step | AM | PM |
|------|-----|-----|
| Cleanse | Lukewarm water or gentle cleanser | Lukewarm bath (5-10 min) |
| Treat | Hydrocortisone 1% on active areas | Hydrocortisone 1% on active areas |
| Moisturize | Thick ceramide cream (all over) | Thick ointment (all over) |
| Protect | Soft cotton clothing | Cotton gloves/socks if scratching |

### Advanced Routine (Moderate-Severe AD)
| Step | AM | PM |
|------|-----|-----|
| Cleanse | Gentle cleanser or water | Bleach bath 2x/week; lukewarm otherwise |
| Treat | Tacrolimus 0.1% on sensitive areas | Triamcinolone 0.1% ointment on body flares |
| Moisturize | Ceramide cream (generous) | Thick ointment; wet wraps on severe areas |
| Medication | — | Dupilumab injection (if prescribed; every 2 weeks) |

### Sample Weekly Routine for Moderate AD
| Day | AM | PM |
|-----|-----|-----|
| Mon | Emollient + tacrolimus on face | Steroid ointment on body + wet wraps |
| Tue | Emollient | Emollient only (maintenance) |
| Wed | Emollient + tacrolimus on face | Steroid ointment on body + wet wraps |
| Thu | Emollient | Emollient only |
| Fri | Emollient + tacrolimus on face | Steroid ointment + wet wraps |
| Sat | Emollient | Bleach bath + emollient |
| Sun | Emollient | Emollient only (recovery) |

**Notes**: Adjust steroid potency by body site (low for face/folds, medium-high for trunk/extremities). Wet wraps: apply steroid/emollient, then damp layer, then dry layer; leave on 2 hours or overnight. Introduce one new product at a time.

## Common Myths vs Facts

| Myth | Fact |
|------|------|
| "Eczema is contagious" | Not contagious; genetic and inflammatory, not infectious |
| "You can cure eczema with diet alone" | Food triggers are real for some but not all; elimination without evidence is harmful |
| "Topical steroids are dangerous and should be avoided" | Safe when used correctly; under-treatment causes more harm than appropriate use |
| "Natural skincare is better for eczema" | Many natural ingredients are allergens; fragrance-free, hypoallergenic products are safest |
| "Eczema is just dry skin" | It's an inflammatory disease; moisturizers treat symptoms but inflammation needs anti-inflammatories |
| "Children outgrow all eczema" | ~60% clear by adolescence; 40% persist into adulthood |
| "You shouldn't bathe with eczema" | Lukewarm bathing with immediate moisturizing is beneficial |
| "Eczema means poor hygiene" | Not related to cleanliness; actually, harsh soaps worsen it |
| "Antihistamines treat eczema" | They help sleep but do not treat the underlying inflammation |
| "Eczema is just a skin problem" | Associated with asthma, allergic rhinitis, food allergies, anxiety, depression, sleep disturbance |
