## 1. ACNE VULGARIS

**Guideline:** American Academy of Dermatology (AAD) — Acne Vulgaris Clinical Practice Guideline
**Year:** 2016 (major update); 2024 updates anticipated
**Grading System:** Strength of Recommendations (A/B/C) based on evidence quality

### Grading/Scoring System
- **IGA (Investigator Global Assessment):** 0 (clear) to 4 (severe)
- **Leeds scoring:** For inflammatory lesions
- **Global Acne Grading System (GAGS):** Comprehensive scoring
- **Lesion count:** Comedones, papules, pustules, nodules

### First-Line Treatments

#### Mild Acne (Comedonal)
1. **Topical retinoids** — **Strength: A**
   - Tretinoin 0.025-0.1%
   - Adapalene 0.1-0.3% (OTC available 0.1%)
   - Tazarotene 0.045-0.1%
   - Apply nightly; pea-sized amount for entire face

2. **Topical combination therapy** — **Strength: A**
   - Retinoid + Benzoyl peroxide (BPO)
   - Retinoid + Topical antibiotic

3. **Benzoyl peroxide 2.5-5%** — **Strength: A**
   - OTC; antimicrobial + keratolytic
   - BPO 2.5% as effective as higher concentrations with less irritation

#### Moderate Acne (Papulopustular)
1. **Topical retinoid + BPO ± topical antibiotic** — **Strength: A**
   - Fixed combinations preferred: adapalene-BPO, clindamycin-BPO
   - **Topical antibiotic must be paired with BPO** (prevents resistance)

2. **Oral antibiotics** — **Strength: A**
   - Doxycycline 50-100 mg daily or BID
   - Minocycline 50-100 mg BID
   - Sarecycline 1.5 mg/kg (narrow-spectrum, less GI upset)
   - **Maximum 3-4 months** (limit antibiotic exposure)
   - Always with topical retinoid; always with BPO if topical antibiotic used

3. **Hormonal therapy (females)** — **Strength: A**
   - Combined oral contraceptives (COCs) — FDA-approved: norgestimate/ethinyl estradiol, drospirenone/ethinyl estradiol, norethindrone acetate/ethinyl estradiol
   - Spironolactone 50-200 mg daily (off-label but well-supported)

#### Severe Acne (Nodulocystic)
1. **Oral isotretinoin** — **Strength: A**
   - 0.5-1 mg/kg/day
   - Cumulative dose 120-150 mg/kg
   - iPLEDGE program (US) — mandatory for prescribing
   - Single course often curative (65-85% long-term remission)

### Second-Line Treatments
- **Azelaic acid 15-20%:** Good for PIH, pregnancy-safe
- **Topical dapsone 5-7.5% gel:** Especially for adult females
- **Clascoterone 1% cream:** Androgen receptor inhibitor (newer option)
- **Chemical peels:** Salicylic acid, glycolic acid
- **Light/laser therapy:** Photodynamic therapy, blue/red light

### Third-Line / Adjunctive
- **Intralesional corticosteroids:** For nodules/cysts (triamcinolone 2.5-5 mg/mL)
- **Excision/drainage:** Large cysts
- **Comedo extraction:** Physical removal

### When to Refer to Specialist (Dermatologist)
- **Immediate/urgent:** Acne fulminans, severe scarring, significant psychosocial impact
- **Soon (within weeks):** Moderate-severe acne not responding to 6-8 weeks of appropriate therapy
- **Consider referral:** Need for isotretinoin, suspicion of endocrine disorder (PCOS, CAH), severe scarring risk
- **Hormonal workup:** Signs of androgen excess (hirsutism, irregular periods, virilization)

### Red Flags Requiring Urgent Referral
- Acne fulminans (ulcerative, systemic symptoms)
- Rapid onset severe acne
- Signs of underlying endocrinopathy
- Severe cystic acne with scarring
- Significant mental health impact (depression, suicidal ideation — especially important with isotretinoin)

### Special Populations

#### Pregnancy
- **SAFE:** Azelaic acid, topical erythromycin, clindamycin (oral also generally safe), BPO
- **CONTRAINDICATED:** Isotretinoin (Category X — absolute), tazarotene, oral tetracyclines, spironolactone
- **CAUTION:** Tretinoin (avoid if possible; limited systemic absorption but concern)

#### Pediatrics
- **Neonatal acne:** Self-limited; no treatment usually needed
- **Infantile acne:** May indicate androgen abnormality; consider workup if severe/persistent
- **Adolescent:** Start with topical therapy; early intervention prevents scarring
- **Isotretinoin:** Consider for severe scarring acne; requires close monitoring

#### Skin of Color
- **Higher risk:** PIH (post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation)
- **Preferred:** Retinoids (prevent PIH), azelaic acid, avoid irritants
- **Avoid:** Aggressive topical therapies that trigger inflammation
- **Important:** Treat PIH concurrently; sun protection essential

### SKINgenius Implementation
- **Recommendation logic:** Severity-based algorithm; start conservative, escalate
- **User assessment:** Lesion counting, IGA self-assessment, impact on QoL
- **Follow-up:** Reassess at 6-8 weeks; adjust therapy
- **Monitoring:** Track lesion counts, side effects, adherence
- **Education:** Set expectations (12 weeks for full response); sun protection; avoid picking
- **Red flag alerts:** Automated flags for severe symptoms, suicidal ideation screening if isotretinoin discussed

### Key References
- Zaenglein AL, et al. "Guidelines of Care for the Management of Acne Vulgaris." *J Am Acad Dermatol*. 2016;74(5):945-973.
- Thiboutot D, et al. "Practical Management of Acne for Clinicians." *Dermatol Clin*. 2022.
- Eichenfield DZ, et al. "Management of Acne Vulgaris: A Review." *JAMA*. 2021.

## 2. ATOPIC DERMATITIS

**Guideline:** AAD — Guidelines of Care for the Management of Atopic Dermatitis
**Year:** 2014 (Section 1-3); 2023-2024 updates
**Grading System:** Evidence-based recommendations (A/B/C)

### Grading/Scoring System
- **SCORAD (SCORing Atopic Dermatitis):** 0-103; <25 mild, 25-50 moderate, >50 severe
- **EASI (Eczema Area and Severity Index):** 0-72; <7 mild, 7-21 moderate, >21 severe
- **POEM (Patient-Oriented Eczema Measure):** Patient-reported
- **DLQI:** Dermatology Life Quality Index
- **IGA:** Investigator Global Assessment (0-4)

### First-Line Treatments (Step Therapy)

#### Step 1: Foundation (ALL patients)
1. **Emollients/Moisturizers** — **Strength: A**
   - Apply within 3 minutes of bathing ("soak and seal")
   - Ointments > creams > lotions (for dry skin)
   - Frequency: 2-3x daily minimum; more during flares
   - Occlusive agents: Petrolatum, mineral oil
   - Ceramide-containing: Support barrier repair

2. **Trigger avoidance** — **Strength: B**
   - Irritants: Fragrances, harsh soaps, wool
   - Allergens: Dust mites (encasings), pet dander, pollen
   - Temperature: Excessive heat, sweating
   - Stress management

3. **Bathing practices** — **Strength: B**
   - Lukewarm water, 5-10 minutes
   - Gentle cleansers (pH 5.5)
   - Immediate moisturization after

#### Step 2: Mild Flares (Add to foundation)
1. **Topical corticosteroids (TCS)** — **Strength: A**
   - Mild (hydrocortisone 1-2.5%) for face/folds
   - Medium (triamcinolone 0.1%) for trunk/extremities
   - Potent (betamethasone dipropionate 0.05%) for thick plaques (short-term)
   - **Apply once or twice daily during flares**

2. **Topical calcineurin inhibitors (TCI)** — **Strength: A**
   - Tacrolimus 0.03% (children) / 0.1% (adults)
   - Pimecrolimus 1%
   - **Steroid-sparing:** Face, folds, long-term maintenance
   - Black box warning (theoretical malignancy risk — controversial; FDA requires warning)

#### Step 3: Moderate-Severe (Add to above)
1. **Phototherapy** — **Strength: A**
   - Narrowband UVB (NB-UVB): First-line phototherapy
   - UVA1: For acute flares
   - Frequency: 2-3x weekly

2. **Systemic therapies** — **Strength: A**
   - **Dupilumab (Dupixent®):** IL-4Rα inhibitor — FDA-approved, biweekly injection
   - **Tralokinumab (Adbry®):** IL-13 inhibitor — FDA-approved
   - **Nemolizumab:** IL-31 inhibitor — FDA-approved 2024
   - **Upadacitinib (Rinvoq®):** JAK inhibitor — FDA-approved for AD
   - **Abrocitinib (Cibinqo®):** JAK inhibitor — FDA-approved
   - **Baricitinib (Olumiant®):** JAK inhibitor (AD indication varies by region)

### Second-Line Treatments
- **Crisaborole 2% ointment (Eucrisa®):** PDE4 inhibitor — mild-moderate AD
- **Ruxolitinib 1.5% cream (Opzelura®):** Topical JAK inhibitor — short-term use
- **Wet wrap therapy:** For severe flares
- **Systemic corticosteroids:** Avoid if possible; short bridge only if needed
- **Cyclosporine:** Off-label; effective but nephrotoxic
- **Methotrexate:** Off-label; monitor liver

### Third-Line / Experimental
- **Biologics:** Lebrikizumab (IL-13, pending)
- **JAK inhibitors:** Oral options above
- **Allergen immunotherapy:** Controversial; may help selected patients
- **Probiotics:** Evidence mixed; may help prevention when started early

### When to Refer to Specialist
- **Immediate:** Severe AD not controlled with topicals, eczema herpeticum (HSV superinfection), widespread infection
- **Soon:** Moderate-severe AD requiring phototherapy or systemic therapy, significant QoL impact, suspicion of allergic contact dermatitis
- **Consider:** Diagnostic uncertainty, failure of standard therapy, need for patch testing

### Red Flags Requiring Urgent Referral
- **Eczema herpeticum:** Painful, monomorphic punched-out erosions with fever, malaise — EMERGENCY
- **Widespread bacterial infection:** Purulent exudate, honey-colored crusts, fever
- **Sudden severe worsening:** Possible contact allergen or systemic issue

### Special Populations

#### Infants/Children
- **Foundation critical:** Emollients from birth may prevent AD in high-risk infants
- **TCS:** Use appropriate potency for age/location; hydrocortisone for face
- **TCI:** Tacrolimus 0.03% approved for children ≥2 years
- **Dupilumab:** Approved for children ≥6 months (weight-based dosing)

#### Pregnancy/Breastfeeding
- **Safe:** Most topical therapies (minimal systemic absorption)
- **Avoid:** Systemic immunosuppressants, JAK inhibitors
- **Dupilumab:** Limited pregnancy data; generally considered safe
- **TCS:** Safe; use lowest effective potency

#### Skin of Color
- **Presentation differences:** Papular/follicular pattern more common; may appear hyperpigmented rather than erythematous
- **PIH:** Higher risk; prioritize TCI over TCS on face
- **Keloid risk:** Consider with any skin injury

### SKINgenius Implementation
- **Assessment:** SCORAD/EASI self-assessment tools; trigger tracking; QoL impact
- **Step therapy algorithm:** Based on severity, age, body site
- **Steroid safety:** Potency guide, duration limits, face/fold warnings
- **TCI education:** Black box warning explanation; benefits vs risks
- **Biologic matching:** IL-4/IL-13/IL-31/JAK pathway selection
- **Phototherapy access:** Referral network, home NB-UVB options
- **Itch management:** "Itch-scratch cycle" education; nighttime strategies
- **Flare action plan:** Pre-written plan for patients

## 3. PSORIASIS

**Guideline:** AAD — Guidelines of Care for the Management of Psoriasis and Psoriatic Arthritis
**Year:** 2019 (major update); ongoing updates
**Grading System:** Evidence-based recommendations

### Grading/Scoring System
- **PASI (Psoriasis Area and Severity Index):** 0-72; <3 mild, 3-10 moderate, >10 severe
- **BSA (Body Surface Area):** <3% mild, 3-10% moderate, >10% severe
- **DLQI:** Quality of life measure
- **PGA (Physician Global Assessment):** 0-5 scale

### First-Line Treatments (Severity-Based)

#### Mild (<3% BSA or limited involvement)
1. **Topical corticosteroids** — **Strength: A**
   - Potency matched to location
   - Vitamin D analog combinations: Calcipotriene-betamethasone dipropionate

2. **Vitamin D analogs** — **Strength: A**
   - Calcipotriene (calcipotriol) 0.005%
   - Calcitriol 0.0003%
   - Slower onset than steroids; good maintenance

3. **Topical retinoids** — **Strength: A**
   - Tazarotene 0.05-0.1%
   - Irritation common; steroid combination helps

4. **Coal tar** — **Strength: B**
   - Traditional; messy; less commonly used now
   - 1-5% preparations

#### Moderate (3-10% BSA or significant QoL impact)
1. **Phototherapy** — **Strength: A**
   - **NB-UVB:** First-line phototherapy
   - **PUVA (psoralen + UVA):** Second-line phototherapy; carcinogenic risk
   - Frequency: 2-3x weekly

2. **Systemic non-biologics** — **Strength: A**
   - **Methotrexate:** 7.5-25 mg weekly; monitor liver
   - **Cyclosporine:** 2.5-5 mg/kg/day; rapid onset; nephrotoxic
   - **Acitretin:** 25-50 mg daily; teratogenic; useful for pustular psoriasis
   - **Apremilast (Otezla®):** PDE4 inhibitor; oral; weight loss side effect

#### Severe (>10% BSA or refractory)
1. **Biologic agents** — **Strength: A**
   - **TNF inhibitors:** Adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab, certolizumab
   - **IL-17 inhibitors:** Secukinumab, ixekizumab, brodalumab, bimekizumab
   - **IL-23 inhibitors:** Guselkumab, risankizumab, tildrakizumab, mirikizumab
   - **IL-12/23 inhibitor:** Ustekinumab

### Second-Line Treatments
- **Combination therapy:** Biologic + methotrexate (enhances efficacy, reduces immunogenicity)
- **Targeted phototherapy:** Excimer laser for limited plaques
- **Roflumilast 0.3% cream:** PDE4 topical — intertriginous psoriasis
- **Tapinarof 1% cream:** Aryl hydrocarbon receptor agonist — novel mechanism

### Third-Line / Adjunctive
- **Systemic corticosteroids:** Generally AVOID (rebound risk)
- **Goeckerman regimen:** Coal tar + UVB (specialized centers)
- **Climate therapy:** Dead Sea climatotherapy
- **Dietary modification:** Weight loss if obese; Mediterranean diet; reduce alcohol

### When to Refer to Specialist
- **Immediate:** Erythrodermic psoriasis, generalized pustular psoriasis (von Zumbusch), psoriatic arthritis signs
- **Soon:** Moderate-severe psoriasis, any patient requiring systemic or biologic therapy, diagnostic uncertainty
- **Consider:** Failure of topical therapy, significant nail/scalp involvement, comorbidity management

### Red Flags Requiring Urgent Referral
- **Erythrodermic psoriasis:** >90% BSA erythema; dehydration, hypothermia, cardiac stress — EMERGENCY
- **Generalized pustular psoriasis (GPP):** Fever, widespread pustules, systemic illness — EMERGENCY
- **Acute guttate flares:** Possible streptococcal trigger
- **Psoriatic arthritis:** Joint pain, swelling, morning stiffness — early referral prevents joint damage

### Special Populations

#### Pregnancy
- **Safe:** Topical therapy, NB-UVB phototherapy
- **Avoid:** Methotrexate, acitretin (teratogenic), TNF inhibitors (avoid 3rd trimester), oral psoralens
- **Cyclosporine:** May be used if necessary
- **Ustekinumab:** Limited data; may be lowest risk biologic

#### Pediatrics
- **Topicals:** First-line
- **Phototherapy:** NB-UVB safe
- **Biologics:** Etanercept, adalimumab, ustekinumab approved for pediatric psoriasis

#### Skin of Color
- **Presentation:** May appear violaceous, hyperpigmented, or with gray scale rather than classic erythema
- **PIH risk:** Higher; treat gently
- **Scalp psoriasis:** Common; may be misdiagnosed as seborrheic dermatitis
- **Nail psoriasis:** Higher prevalence; may be only sign

### Comorbidities (Important in SKINgenius)
- **Metabolic syndrome:** Higher risk; screen BMI, blood pressure, lipids, glucose
- **Cardiovascular disease:** Increased risk; manage modifiable risk factors
- **Psoriatic arthritis:** Screen all patients (joint pain, nail changes, dactylitis)
- **Crohn's disease/UC:** Increased incidence
- **Depression/anxiety:** Common; screen mental health
- **NAFLD:** Increased risk with metabolic syndrome

### SKINgenius Implementation
- **Severity assessment:** PASI calculator, BSA estimator, DLQI integration
- **Comorbidity screening:** Automated flags for metabolic syndrome, arthritis screening
- **Biologic selection:** Based on comorbidities, patient preference, insurance
- **Treatment targets:** PASI 90 (90% improvement) as goal for biologics
- **Phototherapy tracking:** Visit schedule, cumulative dose monitoring
- **Lifestyle interventions:** Weight management, smoking cessation, alcohol reduction
- **Mental health:** Depression screening; QoL assessment

### Key References
- Menter A, et al. "Guidelines of Care for the Management of Psoriasis and Psoriatic Arthritis." *J Am Acad Dermatol*. 2019.
- Elmets CA, et al. "Joint AAD-NPF Guidelines of Care for the Management and Treatment of Psoriasis." *J Am Acad Dermatol*. 2019.

## 4. ROSACEA

**Guideline:** AAD — Rosacea Clinical Practice Guideline
**Year:** 2017; updates ongoing
**Grading System:** Evidence-based recommendations

### Grading/Scoring System
- **IGA (Investigator Global Assessment):** 0 (clear) to 4 (severe)
- **PSS (Physician Static Score):** Erythema, papules, pustules, telangiectasia
- **Self-assessment scales:** Patient-reported flushing, impact

### Subtype-Specific Treatment

#### Erythematotelangiectatic Rosacea (ETR)
**Features:** Persistent facial erythema, flushing, telangiectasia

1. **Trigger avoidance** — **Strength: B**
   - Sun exposure, heat, spicy foods, alcohol, hot beverages, stress
   - Skin care: Gentle, non-irritating products

2. **Topical brimonidine 0.33% gel (Mirvaso®)** — **Strength: A**
   - Alpha-agonist; constricts vessels
   - Daily use for erythema; rebound erythema possible

3. **Topical oxymetazoline 1% cream (Rhofade®)** — **Strength: A**
   - Alpha-agonist; longer-lasting than brimonidine

4. **Laser/IPL** — **Strength: A**
   - Pulsed dye laser (PDL), Nd:YAG, IPL
   - For persistent erythema and telangiectasia

#### Papulopustular Rosacea (PPR)
**Features:** Papules, pustules on background of erythema

1. **Topical metronidazole 0.75-1%** — **Strength: A**
   - Gel, cream, lotion; BID

2. **Topical ivermectin 1% cream (Soolantra®)** — **Strength: A**
   - Once daily; anti-inflammatory and anti-Demodex
   - Often superior to metronidazole

3. **Topical azelaic acid 15%** — **Strength: A**
   - Gel; BID; anti-inflammatory, helps PIH

4. **Oral doxycycline 40 mg modified-release (Oracea®)** — **Strength: A**
   - Sub-antimicrobial dose; anti-inflammatory only
   - Avoid standard antimicrobial doses (resistance, side effects)

5. **Oral minocycline/isotretinoin** — **Strength: B**
   - For refractory cases

#### Phymatous Rosacea
**Features:** Skin thickening, nodularity (rhinophyma)

1. **Medical therapy:** Limited efficacy
2. **Surgical/laser:** CO2 laser, electrosurgery, dermabrasion, scalpel sculpting
3. **Isotretinoin:** May slow progression

#### Ocular Rosacea
**Features:** Blepharitis, conjunctivitis, dry eye, meibomian gland dysfunction

1. **Lid hygiene:** Warm compresses, lid scrubs
2. **Artificial tears:** Preservative-free
3. **Oral doxycycline:** 40 mg modified-release or 50-100 mg
4. **Ophthalmology referral:** **MANDATORY** — corneal involvement risk

### When to Refer to Specialist
- **Immediate:** Ocular rosacea (ophthalmology), severe phymatous changes
- **Soon:** Refractory PPR, need for laser/phototherapy, diagnostic uncertainty
- **Consider:** If no response to topical therapy in 8-12 weeks

### Red Flags Requiring Urgent Referral
- **Ocular involvement:** Vision changes, severe pain, photophobia — ophthalmology emergency
- **Severe phymatous changes:** Airway compromise (rare with rhinophyma)

### Special Populations

#### Skin of Color
- **Underdiagnosed:** Erythema may be subtle; look for warmth, skin sensitivity, papules
- **PIH risk:** Higher; prioritize anti-inflammatory therapies
- **Telangiectasia:** Harder to visualize; dermoscopy helpful

#### Men vs Women
- **Men:** More likely phymatous subtype
- **Women:** More likely ETR and PPR subtypes

### SKINgenius Implementation
- **Subtype identification:** Guided questionnaire for proper classification
- **Trigger diary:** Digital tracking with AI pattern recognition
- **Treatment matching:** Subtype-specific algorithm
- **Ocular screening:** Automated questions; referral prompts
- **Skincare guidance:** Gentle routine; avoid irritants (retinoids, acids, scrubs initially)
- **Expectation setting:** Chronic condition; manage, not cure

### Key References
- Del Rosso JQ, et al. "Management of Rosacea." *J Am Acad Dermatol*. 2017.
- Gallo RL, et al. "Standard Classification and Pathophysiology of Rosacea." *J Am Acad Dermatol*. 2017.

## 5. SKIN CANCER SCREENING

**Guideline:** AAD — Skin Cancer Screening Guidelines; USPSTF Recommendations
**Year:** 2023-2024
**Grading System:** USPSTF Grade (A/B/C/D/I)

### ABCDE Criteria for Melanoma Detection
- **A - Asymmetry:** One half unlike the other
- **B - Border:** Irregular, scalloped, poorly defined edges
- **C - Color:** Varied color (tan, brown, black, red, white, blue)
- **D - Diameter:** >6mm (pencil eraser) — but can be smaller
- **E - Evolving:** Changing in size, shape, color, symptoms

### Ugly Duckling Sign
- Lesion that looks different from patient's other moles
- May be the only clue to melanoma in patients with multiple atypical nevi
- **Highly sensitive** for melanoma detection

### High-Risk Populations
- **Personal history:** Prior skin cancer (melanoma, SCC, BCC)
- **Family history:** Melanoma in first-degree relative
- **Skin type:** Fair skin, light eyes, red/blonde hair, freckles
- **Sun exposure:** History of blistering sunburns, indoor tanning
- **Immunosuppression:** Organ transplant, immunosuppressive therapy
- **Genetic syndromes:** Xeroderma pigmentosum, familial atypical mole syndrome
- **Age:** >65 years
- **Gender:** Men higher risk for melanoma and SCC

### Screening Intervals
- **Average risk:** No routine population screening recommended (USPSTF I — insufficient evidence)
- **High risk:** Annual total body skin examination (TBSE) by dermatologist
- **Very high risk (transplant):** Every 6 months
- **Self-examination:** Monthly for high-risk individuals

### When to Refer to Specialist (Dermatologist)
- **Immediate:** Any lesion suspicious for melanoma (ABCDE, Ugly Duckling, changing)
- **Soon:** New or changing lesions, non-healing sores, rough scaly patches
- **Annual:** High-risk individuals for routine TBSE

### Red Flags Requiring Urgent Referral
- **Possible melanoma:** Changing mole, new "ugly duckling," bleeding lesion, non-healing >3 weeks
- **Possible SCC:** Rapidly growing nodule, non-healing ulcer, horn-like projection
- **Possible BCC:** Pearly papule with telangiectasia, non-healing sore, scar-like lesion
- **Any lesion on immunosuppressed patient:** Higher risk of aggressive disease

### Special Populations

#### Skin of Color
- **Lower risk but NOT no risk:** Melanoma often diagnosed at later stage
- **Acral lentiginous melanoma:** Palms, soles, nail beds — higher incidence in skin of color
- **BCC/SCC:** Can occur; often pigmented BCCs
- **Screening:** Still important; focus on acral sites, mucosal surfaces

#### Transplant/Immunosuppressed
- **100x higher risk** of SCC
- **Aggressive disease:** Metastatic potential of SCC in immunosuppressed
- **Screening:** Every 6 months minimum; aggressive management

#### Pediatric
- **Congenital nevi:** Risk correlates with size; giant nevi need specialist care
- **Pediatric melanoma:** Rare but increasing; spitzoid lesions challenging
- **Xeroderma pigmentosum:** Extreme sun avoidance; frequent screening

### SKINgenius Implementation
- **Skin check tool:** AI-assisted lesion documentation, comparison over time
- **ABCDE education:** Interactive learning module
- **Ugly duckling identification:** Pattern recognition across user's documented lesions
- **Risk assessment:** Algorithm based on skin type, history, exposures
- **Photo documentation:** Serial photography with change detection
- **Referral prompts:** Automated dermatologist referral for suspicious lesions
- **Screening reminders:** For high-risk users

### Key References
- US Preventive Services Task Force. "Screening for Skin Cancer." *JAMA*. 2023.
- AAD. "Melanoma Screening Guidelines." 2023.
- Marchetti MA, et al. "Results of a Randomized Trial on Screening for Melanoma." *J Am Acad Dermatol*. 2023.

## 6. MELASMA

**Guideline:** AAD — Evidence-Based Treatment of Melasma; International Consensus
**Year:** 2017; ongoing updates
**Grading System:** Evidence-based recommendations

### Grading/Scoring System
- **mMASI (modified Melasma Area and Severity Index):** 0-24
- **MASI:** Area + darkness + homogeneity scoring
- **Colorimetry:** Objective measurement
- **Photography:** Standardized serial photography

### Treatment Ladder (Evidence-Based)

#### First-Line
1. **Triple combination cream (TCC)** — **Strength: A**
   - Hydroquinone 4% + Tretinoin 0.05% + Fluocinolone acetonide 0.01%
   - (e.g., Tri-Luma®)
   - **Most effective topical** for melasma
   - Use up to 8 weeks; then maintenance
   - Risk: Exogenous ochronosis with prolonged HQ use

2. **Sun protection** — **Strength: A**
   - **Broad-spectrum SPF 50+:** Daily, reapply every 2 hours
   - **Iron oxide-tinted sunscreen:** Blocks visible light (also triggers melasma)
   - **Physical blockers:** Zinc oxide, titanium dioxide preferred
   - **Behavioral:** Hats, shade, avoid midday sun

3. **Hydroquinone 2-4%** — **Strength: A**
   - Gold standard depigmenting agent
   - Monotherapy or in combination
   - Limit to 3-4 months continuous; then maintenance
   - OTC 2% (US); prescription 4%

#### Second-Line
1. **Topical azelaic acid 20%** — **Strength: A**
   - Safe in pregnancy (Category B)
   - Less irritating than HQ
   - Good for maintenance

2. **Topical vitamin C** — **Strength: B**
   - L-ascorbic acid 15-20%
   - Antioxidant, tyrosinase inhibitor
   - Unstable; needs proper formulation

3. **Topical kojic acid** — **Strength: B**
   - Tyrosinase inhibitor
   - Often combined with HQ
   - Potential sensitization

4. **Topical niacinamide 4-5%** — **Strength: B**
   - Inhibits melanosome transfer
   - Well-tolerated; good adjunct

5. **Topical retinoids** — **Strength: B**
   - Tretinoin, adapalene
   - Promotes epidermal turnover
   - Combination with HQ enhances efficacy

6. **Oral tranexamic acid** — **Strength: B**
   - 250 mg BID or 500 mg daily
   - **Off-label** but growing evidence
   - See safety profile in supplement section
   - Consider for refractory cases

#### Third-Line / Procedure Adjuncts
1. **Chemical peels** — **Strength: B**
   - Glycolic acid 20-70%
   - Salicylic acid 20-30%
   - Trichloroacetic acid (TCA) — caution in darker skin
   - **Risk of PIH in skin of color — experienced provider essential**

2. **Microneedling** — **Strength: B**
   - Enhances topical penetration
   - Lower PIH risk than lasers
   - Combine with tranexamic acid, vitamin C

3. **Lasers (caution)** — **Strength: C**
   - **Q-switched Nd:YAG:** Picosecond lasers (755nm, 1064nm)
   - **High PIH risk in darker skin types**
   - Must be experienced provider
   - Often worsens melasma if not done properly

4. **Oral Polypodium leucotomos** — **Strength: C**
   - 240 mg before sun exposure
   - Adjunct to prevent UV-triggered darkening

### Maintenance Therapy
- **Essential:** Melasma is chronic; maintenance prevents relapse
- **Options:** Azelaic acid, niacinamide, vitamin C, retinoids, iron oxide sunscreen
- **Continue sun protection lifelong**

### When to Refer to Specialist
- **Soon:** Refractory melasma, considering procedures, diagnostic uncertainty
- **Consider:** Need for oral tranexamic acid (medical supervision required)
- **All procedures:** Should be performed by experienced dermatologist

### Red Flags
- **Rapid darkening:** Rule out underlying condition (Addison's, medication-induced)
- **Melanoma mimic:** Atypical pigmentation pattern
- **Exogenous ochronosis:** Blue-black pigmentation from prolonged HQ use

### Special Populations

#### Pregnancy (Chloasma)
- **Very common:** 50-70% of pregnant women
- **Often resolves postpartum**
- **Safe treatments:** Sun protection (physical), azelaic acid, vitamin C
- **Avoid:** Hydroquinone, retinoids, oral tranexamic acid
- **Postpartum:** Can treat if persists

#### Hormonal Influence
- **OCPs:** Can trigger or worsen; consider non-hormonal alternatives
- **HRT:** May worsen; discuss with gynecologist

#### Skin of Color (Fitzpatrick III-VI)
- **Higher prevalence and severity**
- **Higher PIH risk:** All treatments must be gentler
- **Laser caution:** Higher risk of worsening pigmentation
- **Preferred:** Topical therapies, microneedling over lasers
- **Iron oxide sunscreen:** Essential (blocks visible light)

### SKINgenius Implementation
- **Sun protection emphasis:** Non-negotiable foundation; iron oxide sunscreen recommendation
- **Treatment algorithm:** Step-up based on severity, skin type, pregnancy status
- **Pregnancy mode:** Auto-filter safe treatments
- **Hormonal assessment:** Flag potential triggers (OCPs, pregnancy, HRT)
- **Maintenance tracking:** Relapse prevention protocol
- **Procedure warnings:** Strong cautions about laser risks in darker skin
- **Expectation management:** Realistic timeline (12-24 weeks for improvement); chronic condition

### Key References
- Kimbrough-Green CK, et al. "Topical Retinoic Acid (Tretinoin) for Melasma." *Arch Dermatol*. 1994.
- Rivas S, Pandya AG. "Treatment of Melasma with Topical Agents, Peels, and Lasers." *Am J Clin Dermatol*. 2013.
- Mansouri P, et al. "Evaluation of the Efficacy of Cysteamine 5% Cream in the Treatment of Epidermal Melasma." *J Cosmet Dermatol*. 2015.

## 7. ACTINIC KERATOSIS

**Guideline:** AAD — Guidelines of Care for the Management of Actinic Keratosis
**Year:** 2021 update
**Grading System:** Evidence-based recommendations

### Clinical Presentation
- Rough, scaly patches on sun-exposed skin
- Erythematous base with adherent scale
- Multiple lesions common ("field cancerization")
- Risk of progression to cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (cSCC): 0.1-10% per lesion per year

### Treatment Approach: Field Therapy vs Lesion-Directed

#### Lesion-Directed Treatments
1. **Cryotherapy** — **Strength: A**
   - Liquid nitrogen; freeze-thaw cycle
   - Good for limited number of lesions (fewer than 5-10)
   - Cure rate: ~85-90%
   - Side effects: Pain, blistering, hypopigmentation (especially skin of color)

2. **Curettage** — **Strength: B**
   - With or without electrodessication
   - For thicker, suspicious lesions

3. **Excision** — **Strength: B**
   - If suspicion for invasive SCC

#### Field Therapy (For Multiple Lesions / Field Cancerization)
1. **Topical 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) 5%** — **Strength: A**
   - Cream or solution; BID for 2-4 weeks
   - Inflammatory response expected (redness, erosion)
   - Highly effective; long track record

2. **Topical imiquimod 2.5% or 3.75%** — **Strength: A**
   - Immune response modifier
   - 2.5% daily for 2 weeks; 3.75% daily for 2 weeks × 2 cycles
   - More convenient than 5-FU; less severe reaction

3. **Topical diclofenac 3% gel** — **Strength: A**
   - BID for 60-90 days
   - Less inflammatory response; better tolerated
   - Lower efficacy than 5-FU or imiquimod

4. **Topical tirbanibulin 1% ointment (Klisyri®)** — **Strength: A**
   - Once daily for 5 days
   - Newest option; convenient short course
   - Similar efficacy to 5-FU with shorter duration

5. **Photodynamic therapy (PDT)** — **Strength: A**
   - Aminolevulinic acid (ALA) or methyl aminolevulinate (MAL) + red/blue light
   - Excellent for field treatment
   - Cosmetically superior for large areas (face, scalp)

### Progression Risk Factors
- **High risk:** Immunosuppression, multiple lesions, prior SCC, location (lip, ear)
- **Hypertrophic AKs:** Higher risk; may need biopsy
- **Pigmented AKs:** Can mimic melanoma; biopsy if uncertain

### When to Refer to Specialist
- **Immediate:** Suspicion for invasive SCC, rapidly growing lesion, ulceration, immunosuppressed patient
- **Soon:** Multiple lesions requiring field therapy, diagnostic uncertainty
- **Consider:** Failure of topical therapy, cosmetic concerns

### Red Flags
- **Induration/nodularity:** Possible invasive SCC
- **Ulceration/bleeding:** Possible SCC transformation
- **Rapid growth:** Requires biopsy
- **Lip involvement:** Higher risk SCC; specialist management

### Special Populations

#### Immunosuppressed (Transplant Patients)
- **Much higher risk:** 65-250x increased SCC risk
- **Aggressive management:** Preventive field therapy; frequent screening
- **Field therapy:** Consider routine prophylactic 5-FU or imiquimod
- **Screening:** Every 3-6 months

#### Skin of Color
- **Less common:** But occurs with significant sun exposure or albinism
- **Pigmented AKs:** Can mimic seborrheic keratosis or melanoma
- **Hypopigmentation risk:** Post-cryotherapy; discuss with patient

### SKINgenius Implementation
- **Risk stratification:** Number of lesions, location, immunosuppression, prior skin cancer
- **Treatment matching:** Lesion count → lesion-directed vs field therapy
- **Patient preference:** Consider treatment duration, tolerability, cosmetic concerns
- **Follow-up:** Recurrence monitoring; field cancerization management
- **Prevention education:** Sun protection; regular self-examination
- **Immunosuppressed alerts:** Flag high-risk patients for aggressive management

### Key References
- Lebwohl M, et al. "Actinic Keratosis: Natural History and Risk of Malignant Transformation." *J Am Acad Dermatol*. 2021.
- Dirschka T, et al. "Real-World Effectiveness and Safety of Ingenol Mebutate." *J Eur Acad Dermatol Venereol*. 2016.

## 8. SEBORRHEIC DERMATITIS

**Guideline:** AAD — Guidelines of Care for the Management of Seborrheic Dermatitis
**Year:** 2018; ongoing updates
**Grading System:** Evidence-based recommendations

### Clinical Presentation
- Erythematous patches with greasy yellow scale
- **Common locations:** Scalp, eyebrows, nasolabial folds, glabella, chest, ears
- Dandruff = mild seborrheic dermatitis of scalp
- Associated with Malassezia yeast overgrowth
- Chronic, relapsing condition

### Treatment Ladder

#### Scalp
1. **Antifungal shampoos** — **Strength: A**
   - **Ketoconazole 2% shampoo:** 2-3x weekly; leave 3-5 minutes
   - **Selenium sulfide 1-2.5%:** 2-3x weekly
   - **Zinc pyrithione 1-2%:** Daily or 2-3x weekly
   - **Ciclopirox 1.5% shampoo:** Effective alternative
   - **Coal tar:** Anti-inflammatory; may stain, odor

2. **Topical corticosteroids** — **Strength: A**
   - Solutions/foams for scalp: Betamethasone valerate, clobetasol
   - Short-term for flares
   - **Limit potent steroids** to avoid skin atrophy

3. **Calcineurin inhibitors** — **Strength: B**
   - Tacrolimus solution 0.1%
   - Steroid-sparing; good for maintenance

#### Face/Body
1. **Topical antifungals** — **Strength: A**
   - Ketoconazole 2% cream
   - Ciclopirox 0.77% cream/gel
   - BID during flares

2. **Topical corticosteroids** — **Strength: A**
   - Low-potency: Hydrocortisone 1-2.5% (face)
   - Medium-potency: Triamcinolone 0.1% (body)
   - Short-term; steroid-sparing preferred for maintenance

3. **Calcineurin inhibitors** — **Strength: A**
   - Tacrolimus 0.1% ointment, pimecrolimus 1% cream
   - Excellent for facial SD; steroid-sparing

4. **Topical lithium succinate/gluconate** — **Strength: B**
   - Anti-inflammatory; antifungal
   - Good for facial SD

#### Severe/Refractory
- **Oral itraconazole:** 200mg daily for 7 days — **Strength: B**
- **Oral fluconazole:** 150-300mg weekly — **Strength: C**
- **Oral terbinafine:** Less effective for SD
- **Phototherapy:** NB-UVB for refractory cases
- **Isotretinoin:** Low-dose for very severe, refractory cases

### Maintenance Therapy
- **Essential:** SD is chronic and relapsing
- **Rotate antifungal shampoos:** Prevents resistance; maintains efficacy
- **Weekly maintenance:** Antifungal shampoo 1x weekly minimum
- **Intermittent topical steroids:** For breakthrough flares

### When to Refer to Specialist
- **Soon:** Refractory to standard therapy, diagnostic uncertainty (psoriasis mimic), severe/widespread disease
- **Consider:** Need for systemic therapy, associated conditions

### Red Flags
- **Sudden severe onset:** Possible underlying condition (HIV, Parkinson's, immunosuppression)
- **Treatment failure:** Consider alternate diagnosis (psoriasis, cutaneous T-cell lymphoma)
- **Infantile SD:** Usually self-limited; severe/widespread may indicate Langerhans cell histiocytosis

### Special Populations

#### HIV/AIDS
- **Much more severe and widespread**
- **May indicate immune decline**
- Often first sign of HIV
- Requires aggressive antifungal therapy

#### Parkinson's Disease
- **Higher prevalence and severity**
- Related to sebum changes and immobility
- May require more intensive therapy

#### Infants (Cradle Cap)
- Usually self-limited (resolves by 6-12 months)
- Gentle emollients + soft brushing
- Mild baby shampoo
- Avoid potent steroids
- Refer if severe, widespread, or atypical

#### Skin of Color
- **May present with hypopigmentation** rather than erythema
- **Hypopigmentation** may persist after inflammation resolves
- Post-inflammatory pigmentary changes common

### SKINgenius Implementation
- **Location-specific guidance:** Scalp vs face vs body protocols
- **Product recommendations:** Antifungal shampoo rotation; formulation guidance
- **Flare management:** Quick-action plan
- **Maintenance scheduling:** Automated reminders for preventive use
- **Associated conditions:** Flag HIV, Parkinson's, immunosuppression

### Key References
- Borda LJ, Wikramanayake TC. "Seborrheic Dermatitis and Dandruff." *J Clin Investig*. 2015.
- Dessinioti C, Katsambas A. "Seborrheic Dermatitis: Etiology, Risk Factors, and Treatments." *J Dtsch Dermatol Ges*. 2013.

## 9. CONTACT DERMATITIS

**Guideline:** AAD — Contact Dermatitis Clinical Practice Guideline
**Year:** 2019; ongoing updates
**Grading System:** Evidence-based recommendations

### Classification
- **Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD):** Type IV delayed hypersensitivity; requires prior sensitization
- **Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD):** Direct chemical/physical damage; no prior sensitization needed

### Common Allergens (ACD)
- **Nickel:** #1 allergen; jewelry, belt buckles, cell phones
- **Fragrance mixes:** Perfumes, personal care products
- **Preservatives:** Formaldehyde, methylisothiazolinone (MI/MCI), quaternium-15
- **Hair dyes:** p-Phenylenediamine (PPD)
- **Rubber chemicals:** Thiurams, carbamates (gloves)
- **Topical antibiotics:** Neomycin, bacitracin
- **Corticosteroids:** Allergic to the steroid itself (rare but real)
- **Poison ivy/oak/sumac:** Urushiol

### Common Irritants (ICD)
- **Soaps/detergents:** Harsh surfactants (SLS/SLES)
- **Solvents:** Alcohol, acetone
- **Water:** Prolonged wet work
- **Acids/alkalis:** Industrial; some personal care
- **Friction:** Repetitive mechanical irritation

### Treatment

#### Acute Phase
1. **Discontinue/avoid trigger** — **Strength: A**
   - Identify and eliminate causative agent
   - May require detailed history, product review

2. **Topical corticosteroids** — **Strength: A**
   - Potent (clobetasol, betamethasone) for acute, severe cases (short-term)
   - Medium (triamcinolone) for moderate
   - Low (hydrocortisone) for face/folds/maintenance

3. **Oral corticosteroids** — **Strength: A**
   - For severe, widespread, acute flares (e.g., poison ivy)
   - Prednisone 40-60 mg taper over 2-3 weeks
   - **Avoid short 5-day bursts** (rebound common)

4. **Wet dressings** — **Strength: B**
   - Burow's solution (aluminum acetate) for weeping lesions

#### Chronic Phase
1. **Emollients** — **Strength: A**
   - Barrier repair critical

2. **Calcineurin inhibitors** — **Strength: A**
   - Steroid-sparing for chronic ACD

3. **Phototherapy** — **Strength: B**
   - For chronic, refractory cases

### Patch Testing
- **Indications:** Chronic/recurrent dermatitis, treatment failure, occupational exposure, facial/hand dermatitis
- **Method:** Standardized series (TRUE Test, or comprehensive series)
- **Extended series:** For specific exposures (cosmetics, hairdressing, plants, corticosteroids)
- **Timing:** Apply Monday, remove Wednesday, read Wednesday and Friday
- **Interpretation:** Requires trained specialist

### Allergen Avoidance
- **Education critical:** Patient must know what to avoid
- **Cross-reactivity education:** e.g., nickel cross-reacts with cobalt, chromium
- **Product substitution:** Provide safe alternatives
- **Occupational modifications:** Protective equipment, job modification

### When to Refer to Specialist
- **Immediate:** Severe/widespread acute reaction, anaphylaxis risk (latex)
- **Soon:** Chronic/recurrent dermatitis, need for patch testing, diagnostic uncertainty
- **Consider:** Occupational dermatitis, complex exposures

### Red Flags
- **Severe widespread reaction:** Possible systemic contact dermatitis
- **Latex allergy:** Risk of anaphylaxis; medical alert needed
- **Photo-contact dermatitis:** Reactions only in sun-exposed areas
- **Erythema multiforme:** Target lesions; may indicate severe reaction
- **Infection:** Purulent exudate, spreading erythema, fever

### Special Populations

#### Occupational
- **Irritant contact dermatitis:** Most common occupational skin disease
- **Prevention:** Barrier creams, protective gloves, moisturizers
- **Vinyl/nitrile gloves:** For rubber allergy; cotton liners for sweat
- **Job modification:** May be necessary in severe cases

#### Pediatric
- **Common allergens:** Nickel, fragrance, preservatives, topical medications
- **Diaper dermatitis:** Often irritant; rule out Candida, psoriasis
- **Atopic dermatitis + ACD:** High risk; patch testing may be needed early

#### Skin of Color
- **May present with hyperpigmentation** rather than erythema
- **Post-inflammatory pigmentary changes** common and persistent
- **Patch test reactions** may be subtler

### SKINgenius Implementation
- **Exposure history:** Detailed questionnaire for common allergens
- **Patch testing referral:** Clear criteria and referral network
- **Allergen database:** Product recommendations avoiding specific allergens
- **Occupational guidance:** Protective strategies, safe alternatives
- **Education materials:** Visual guides for allergen avoidance
- **Poison ivy protocol:** Specific guidance for plant exposures

### Key References
- DeKoven JG, et al. "North American Contact Dermatitis Group Patch Test Results." *Dermatitis*. 2023.
- Warshaw EM, et al. "Contact Dermatitis: A Practice Parameter Update." *J Allergy Clin Immunol Pract*. 2021.

## 10. VITILIGO

**Guideline:** AAD — Vitiligo Clinical Practice Guideline
**Year:** 2021; European guidelines (VETF, EDF) also referenced
**Grading System:** Evidence-based recommendations

### Clinical Presentation
- Depigmented macules/patches with sharp borders
- Wood's lamp accentuation (chalk-white fluorescence)
- Types: Segmental, non-segmental (most common), mixed, universal
- Koebner phenomenon (lesions at sites of trauma)
- Associated: Thyroid disease, other autoimmune conditions

### Grading/Scoring
- **VASI (Vitiligo Area Scoring Index):** Body surface area + depigmentation extent
- **VETF (Vitiligo European Task Force):** Extent, staging, progression
- **DLQI:** Quality of life measure
- **Photography:** Serial standardized photos

### Treatment Approach

#### First-Line: Active/Progressing Disease
1. **Topical corticosteroids** — **Strength: A**
   - Medium to potent potency
   - BID for 3-6 months
   - **Facial lesions:** Lower potency to avoid atrophy
   - Best for recent-onset, limited disease

2. **Topical calcineurin inhibitors** — **Strength: A**
   - Tacrolimus 0.1% ointment, pimecrolimus 1% cream
   - **Preferred for face, neck, folds** (no atrophy risk)
   - BID for 3-6 months
   - Similar efficacy to TCS without side effects

3. **Narrowband UVB (NB-UVB) phototherapy** — **Strength: A**
   - **First-line for widespread disease (>5-10% BSA)**
   - 2-3x weekly
   - 30-40% repigmentation expected (face/neck respond best)
   - Requires 6-12 months for full assessment

#### Second-Line
1. **Excimer laser (308nm)** — **Strength: A**
   - For localized, stable lesions
   - 2-3x weekly
   - Face/neck respond best; hands/feet poorly

2. **Combination therapy:** TCI + phototherapy — **Strength: A**
   - Superior to either alone
   - Tacrolimus applied before UVB

3. **Systemic corticosteroids** — **Strength: B**
   - Mini-pulse: Dexamethasone 2.5mg on weekends
   - Stabilizes rapidly progressive disease
   - Short-term only

4. **Topical JAK inhibitors** — **Strength: A (emerging)**
   - Ruxolitinib 1.5% cream (Opzelura®): FDA-approved for non-segmental vitiligo
   - Excellent efficacy for face; ongoing studies

#### Third-Line / Surgical
1. **Autologous melanocyte transplantation** — **Strength: B**
   - For stable disease (>1 year stable)
   - Punch grafting, blister grafting, cellular grafting

2. **Depigmentation** — **Strength: C**
   - Monobenzone 20% cream
   - For extensive (>50% BSA), refractory disease
   - Permanent; irreversible decision

3. **Camouflage:** Makeup, self-tanners, permanent tattoo

### Prognostic Factors
- **Good prognosis:** Recent onset, facial lesions, younger age, darker skin
- **Poor prognosis:** Long-standing, acral lesions, segmental, older age, leukotrichia (white hair in lesion)

### Associated Conditions to Screen
- **Thyroid disease:** Hashimoto's, Graves' (test TSH, antibodies)
- **Other autoimmune:** Pernicious anemia, Addison's, diabetes, alopecia areata
- **Ocular:** Uveitis (especially in segmental vitiligo)
- **Auditory:** Sensorineural hearing loss (rare)

### When to Refer to Specialist
- **Soon:** All vitiligo patients should have dermatologist involvement
- **Consider:** Widespread disease, rapidly progressive, need for phototherapy, surgical options
- **Pediatric:** Early referral; family support important

### Red Flags
- **Rapid progression:** May need systemic therapy to stabilize
- **Universal vitiligo:** Consider depigmentation; ocular/auditory monitoring
- **Associated autoimmune symptoms:** Fatigue, weight changes, vision changes

### Psychological Support
- **Major impact:** Quality of life often severely affected
- **Depression/anxiety:** Screen and refer
- **Support groups:** Vitiligo community resources
- **Camouflage therapy:** Dermatography, cosmetic counseling

### Special Populations

#### Pediatric
- **Favorable response:** Better repigmentation than adults
- **Phototherapy challenges:** Compliance, school schedules
- **Psychological impact:** School-age children especially vulnerable
- **Family support:** Essential for adherence

#### Skin of Color
- **More noticeable:** Greater psychosocial impact
- **Treatment response:** Often excellent (more melanocyte reservoir)
- **PIH risk:** Treatments may cause hyperpigmentation in surrounding skin
- **Camouflage:** May be more challenging

### SKINgenius Implementation
- **Assessment tools:** VASI calculator, progression tracking
- **Treatment matching:** BSA-based algorithm; facial vs non-facial protocols
- **Phototherapy access:** Referral network; home NB-UVB options
- **JAK inhibitor pathway:** New option tracking; insurance access
- **Psychological screening:** QoL assessment; mental health resources
- **Autoimmune screening:** Thyroid, other conditions
- **Stability assessment:** Important for surgical candidacy
- **Camouflage guidance:** Product recommendations, technique videos

### Key References
- Rodrigues M, et al. "Current and Emerging Treatments for Vitiligo." *J Am Acad Dermatol*. 2021.
- Ezzedine K, et al. "Vitiligo Guidelines from the Vitiligo European Task Force." *Eur J Dermatol*. 2021.
- Harris JE. "Chemical-Induced Vitiligo." *Dermatol Clin*. 2017.

## 11. EUROPEAN GUIDELINES COMPARISON

### European Dermatology Forum (EDF) / European Academy of Dermatology and Venereology (EADV)

#### General Approach Differences
1. **Emphasis on severity scoring:** More systematic use of validated scores (PASI, SCORAD, EASI)
2. **Access to biologics:** Often broader access to biologics earlier in treatment algorithm
3. **Phototherapy:** More widely available and used as first-line for moderate disease
4. **Cost-effectiveness:** More explicit consideration of cost in treatment selection

#### Key Condition-Specific Differences

**Acne:**
- **Similar to AAD** in overall approach
- **May emphasize hormonal workup** more in women
- **European products:** Different topical combinations available

**Atopic Dermatitis:**
- **Earlier biologic use:** Dupilumab approved earlier in some European countries
- **Phototherapy:** More prominent role before systemic therapy
- **Wet wrap therapy:** More commonly used in Europe

**Psoriasis:**
- **PASI 75/90 targets:** Explicit treatment targets
- **Biologic sequencing:** More guidance on switching between biologics
- **Apremilast:** Used more as bridge to biologics

**Melanoma Screening:**
- **More structured population screening** in some countries (Germany)
- **Dermoscopy emphasis:** More emphasis on dermoscopy training for primary care

### NICE Guidelines (UK) — Key Differences from AAD

#### Access and Cost Focus
- **Explicit cost-effectiveness analysis:** NICE evaluates cost per QALY
- **Restricted access:** Some treatments not approved due to cost (e.g., some biologics)
- **Step therapy mandated:** More rigid step-up requirements

#### Specific Differences

**Acne:**
- **Oral isotretinoin:** Restricted access; dermatologist-only
- **Spironolactone:** Off-label; not formally recommended but used

**Atopic Dermatitis:**
- **Dupilumab:** Approved for moderate-severe; but requires specific severity thresholds
- **Crisaborole:** Not recommended (cost-effectiveness)

**Psoriasis:**
- **Biologics:** PASI 10 + DLQI >10 required for NHS access
- **Apremilast:** Approved but with restrictions
- **Biosimilars:** Strongly encouraged due to cost savings

**Skin Cancer:**
- **Melanoma:** 2-week wait referral for suspected melanoma
- **Teledermatology:** More advanced integration

### SKINgenius Implications
- **Multi-guideline approach:** Present AAD as primary (US market), acknowledge European alternatives
- **Access considerations:** Note when treatments may have regional availability differences
- **Cost transparency:** Where relevant, note cost considerations
- **Global applicability:** Ensure recommendations work across healthcare systems

## 12. SKINgenius IMPLEMENTATION FRAMEWORK

### How SKINgenius Should Implement Clinical Guidelines

#### 1. Severity Assessment Algorithms
```
User Input → Symptom Checker → Severity Scoring → Treatment Recommendation
```

#### 2. Recommendation Logic

**Conservative First Principle:**
- Always start with least invasive, safest option
- Step-up based on severity and response
- Consider user preferences (topical vs oral, cost, convenience)

**Special Population Filtering:**
- Pregnancy mode: Filter unsafe treatments automatically
- Pediatric mode: Age-appropriate dosing and options
- Skin of color: PIH warnings, tailored recommendations

**Red Flag Detection:**
- Automated screening for urgent symptoms
- Immediate referral prompts
- Emergency guidance when appropriate

#### 3. Evidence Grading Integration

| Grade | Description | User Display |
|-------|-------------|--------------|

- Medical conditions
- Prior adverse reactions

#### 7. User Education Integration

**Condition-Specific Education:**
- Pathophysiology (simplified)
- Treatment expectations (timeline to results)

- Most skin conditions: 6-12 weeks for visible improvement
