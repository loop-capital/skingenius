# SKINgenius Melanin Assessment
## Ingredient & Product Appropriateness for Higher Melanin Skin (Fitzpatrick IV-VI)

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-05-14  
**Classification:** Clinical Research — Evidence-Based Guidelines  
**Source Authority:** Dermatology literature, Skin of Color Society guidelines, clinical consensus  
**⚠️ Medical Disclaimer:** This assessment is for educational purposes only. Always consult a board-certified dermatologist for personalized treatment, especially for persistent hyperpigmentation or uncertain skin conditions.

---

## Executive Summary

Skin of color (Fitzpatrick phototypes IV–VI) has unique physiological characteristics that demand tailored skincare approaches:

- **Melanocyte activity:** Greater baseline melanin production and more reactive melanocytes
- **Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) risk:** 65%+ higher than Fitzpatrick I–III after equivalent inflammatory insult
- **Trans-epidermal water loss (TEWL):** Variable by body site; cheek/jaw often drier than forehead
- **Sebum production:** Generally lower overall, with regional variation (often lower on cheeks, normal on T-zone)
- **Photoaging patterns:** Less wrinkling, more mottled pigmentation and seborrheic keratosis
- **Structural differences:** Stratum corneum equal in layers but corneocytes are more compact; some studies show increased desquamation challenges

These differences mean ingredient selection, concentration, formulation, and treatment sequencing must be adapted. What is safe and effective for lighter skin may trigger or worsen PIH, cause irritation, or be cosmetically unacceptable (e.g., white cast) in melanin-rich skin.

## 1. Melanin-Reactive Ingredient Categories

### 1.1 HIGH PIH-RISK INGREDIENTS — Use With Extreme Caution or Avoid

These ingredients carry elevated risk of triggering post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation in Fitzpatrick IV–VI. If used, they require dermatologist supervision, strict photoprotection, and often pre-/post-treatment melanocyte-stabilizing regimens.

| Ingredient | Risk Level | Mechanism of Concern | Mitigation if Used |
|------------|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **High-strength AHAs** (glycolic >8%, lactic >10%, mandelic >15%) | HIGH | Excessive exfoliation → inflammation → melanocyte activation | Reduce concentration; limit frequency; pair with barrier repair |
| **High-strength BHAs** (salicylic >2%) | HIGH | Deep keratolysis, drying → irritation cascade | Use ≤2%; avoid layering with retinoids; buffer with moisturizer |
| **High-strength retinoids** (tretinoin >0.05%, adapalene >0.3%) | HIGH | Rapid cell turnover → retinization irritation → PIH | Start at lowest concentration; introduce 2–3×/week; sandwich method |
| **Benzoyl peroxide >2.5%** | HIGH | Oxidative stress, bleaching of fabric/skin, paradoxical darkening | Use 2.5% or lower; short contact therapy; never leave-on overnight initially |
| **Hydroquinone >2%** | VERY HIGH | Cytotoxicity to melanocytes; exogenous ochronosis with prolonged use; paradoxical darkening | **Avoid >2% without dermatologist supervision**; limit duration to 3–4 months; mandatory breaks |
| **TCA peels >10%** | VERY HIGH | Deep chemical injury → intense inflammatory response → severe PIH | **Avoid in Fitzpatrick V–VI**; Fitzpatrick IV only with physician; test spot mandatory |
| **Strong physical scrubs** (walnut shell, apricot pit, salt crystals) | HIGH | Micro-tears in stratum corneum → trauma-induced melanogenesis | **Avoid entirely**; use enzymatic or very mild chemical exfoliation instead |
| **High-potency topical steroids** | MODERATE–HIGH | Skin atrophy, steroid-induced acne, telangiectasia, rebound pigmentation | Short courses only; never on face >2 weeks without supervision |

**Key Clinical Principle:** In skin of color, the threshold between "therapeutic exfoliation" and "inflammation triggering PIH" is narrower. Conservative dosing wins.

### 1.2 SAFE FOR DARK SKIN — Evidence-Based Recommendations

These ingredients have strong safety profiles in Fitzpatrick IV–VI and may actively reduce PIH risk or improve existing hyperpigmentation.

| Ingredient | Strength of Evidence | Mechanism | Notes for Skin of Color |
|------------|---------------------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Azelaic acid 15–20%** | Strong (Level A) | Tyrosinase inhibition; anti-inflammatory; antimicrobial | Excellent for acne + PIH; pregnancy-safe; well-tolerated across all Fitzpatrick types |
| **Kojic acid 1–4%** | Moderate–Strong | Copper chelation → tyrosinase inhibition | Effective for melasma; can cause irritation in some—start low; often combined with other agents |
| **L-Ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) 10–20%** | Strong | Antioxidant; tyrosinase inhibition; collagen synthesis | Photoprotective; stabilizes melanocytes; all skin types; best in morning routine |
| **Niacinamide 2–5%** | Strong | Melanosome transfer inhibition; anti-inflammatory; barrier repair | Very well tolerated; excellent for barrier-compromised skin; reduces PIH formation |
| **Tranexamic acid 2–5% (topical)** | Moderate–Strong | Plasmin inhibition → reduced arachidonic acid → less melanocyte stimulation | Emerging gold standard for melasma; orally also used (prescription); very safe topically |
| **Mandelic acid 5–10%** | Moderate | AHA with large molecular weight → slower penetration → less irritation | **Preferred AHA for skin of color**; antibacterial properties help acne; good introduction AHA |
| **Lactic acid 5–8%** | Moderate | Gentle AHA; also hydrates (humectant properties) | Good for dry, sensitive skin of color; promotes healthy desquamation |
| **Polyhydroxy acids (PHAs): Gluconolactone, lactobionic acid** | Moderate | Very gentle exfoliation; antioxidant; humectant | Excellent for sensitive or reactive skin of color; minimal PIH risk |
| **Peptides (copper peptides, signal peptides)** | Emerging | Collagen signaling; anti-inflammatory | No PIH risk; good for anti-aging in skin of color where retinoids may be too irritating |
| **Centella asiatica (Madecassoside)** | Moderate | Wound healing; anti-inflammatory; collagen synthesis | Traditional remedy; modern evidence supports scar and PIH reduction |
| **Licorice root extract (glabridin)** | Moderate | Tyrosinase inhibition; anti-inflammatory | Gentle brightener; suitable for daily use; often in combination products |
| **Arbutin (alpha-arbutin preferred)** | Moderate | Slow-release hydroquinone derivative; tyrosinase inhibition | Safer alternative to hydroquinone; stable; well-tolerated |
| **Thiamidol (isobutylamido thiazolyl resorcinol)** | Moderate–Strong | Tyrosinase inhibition; clinical trials show melasma improvement | Developed specifically for hyperpigmentation; very gentle |

### 1.3 CAUTION NEEDED — Context-Dependent Safety

| Ingredient | Concern | When It's OK | When to Avoid |
|------------|---------|-------------|---------------|
| **Glycolic acid** | Small molecule = deep penetration; high irritation potential | ≤5% for daily use; 8–10% as weekly treatment in Fitzpatrick IV with established tolerance | >10% without supervision; during active breakout; when barrier compromised |
| **Salicylic acid** | Can over-dry; drying → irritation → PIH | ≤2% leave-on; ≤0.5% in cleansers; short-contact therapy | Dry, flaky skin; during retinoid introduction; Fitzpatrick VI with sensitive subtype |
| **Certain essential oils** (citrus, bergamot, lavender undiluted) | Phototoxicity; allergic contact dermatitis; irritation | Properly diluted in formulations; non-phototoxic varieties | Direct application; before sun exposure; if any history of contact dermatitis |
| **Vitamin A/retinol (OTC)** | Retinization can trigger PIH if introduced too aggressively | Encapsulated/time-release formulas; 0.25% or lower to start; buffered with moisturizer | High concentrations (>0.5%) without ramp-up; during summer without photoprotection |
| **Chemical sunscreens** (oxybenzone, avobenzone, octinoxate) | Higher irritation/allergy rates; some data suggesting hormonal concerns; can sting | Well-formulated modern filters (Tinosorb S/M, Uvinul A Plus) in stable formulations | Sensitive or reactive skin of color; if any stinging/burning reported |
| **Alpha-arbutin vs. beta-arbutin** | Beta-arbutin can degrade to hydroquinone in skin | Alpha-arbutin is stable and safe | Beta-arbutin in high concentrations or unstable formulations |
| **Mequinol (4-HA)** | Derivative of hydroquinone; similar concerns | Prescription only; short-term use | Without dermatologist supervision; long-term use |

| Ingredient Category | Standard Concentration | Frequency | Notes |
|--------------------|----------------------|-----------|-------|
| Glycolic acid | 8–12% | 2–3×/week | Well-tolerated |
| Lactic acid | 10–15% | 3–4×/week | Gentle; can use higher |
| Salicylic acid | 2% | Daily or 3–4×/week | Standard acne treatment |
| Retinol (OTC) | 0.3–1% | Nightly | Can titrate up relatively quickly |
| Tretinoin | 0.025–0.1% | Nightly | Standard prescription range |
| Benzoyl peroxide | 2.5–5% | Daily | Standard for acne |
| Vitamin C (L-AA) | 15–20% | Daily AM | Well-tolerated |
| Niacinamide | 5–10% | Daily | Very safe |
| Azelaic acid | 15–20% | Daily | Standard prescription strength |

### 2.2 Fitzpatrick IV (Medium-Dark: Olive to Light Brown)

| Ingredient Category | Recommended Concentration | Frequency | Rationale |
|--------------------|---------------------------|-----------|-----------|
| Glycolic acid | **5–8%** | 1–2×/week max | Reduce by ~30–40%; melanocytes more reactive |
| Lactic acid | **8–12%** | 2–3×/week | Gentler AHA; still effective at lower concentrations |
| Mandelic acid | **5–10%** | 2–3×/week | Preferred AHA; large molecule = slower penetration |
| Salicylic acid | **≤2%** | 2–3×/week | Monitor for dryness; reduce if any tightness |
| Retinol (OTC) | **0.25–0.5%** | 3×/week initially | Slower titration; watch for retinization signs |
| Tretinoin | **0.025–0.05%** | 2–3×/week initially | Prescription; start low; consider short-contact |
| Benzoyl peroxide | **≤2.5%** | 2–3×/week or short-contact | Higher concentrations risk paradoxical darkening |
| Vitamin C (L-AA) | **10–15%** | Daily AM | Slightly reduced to minimize potential irritation |
| Niacinamide | **2–5%** | Daily | Effective at lower concentrations; excellent safety |
| Azelaic acid | **15–20%** | Daily | Generally well-tolerated; can start at 15% |
| Tranexamic acid | **2–5%** | Daily | Excellent safety profile; no concentration adjustment needed |
| Kojic acid | **1–2%** | Daily or 3–4×/week | Start low; some find irritating |

**Fitzpatrick IV Special Considerations:**
- **Patch test mandatory** for all new active ingredients
- **Introduction rule:** One new active every 2–3 weeks
- **Photoprotection:** Physical sunscreen SPF 30–50, reapply every 2 hours outdoors
- **Watch for early PIH signs:** Any darkening around acne, irritation, or procedure sites = stop active and reassess

### 2.3 Fitzpatrick V (Brown to Dark Brown)

| Ingredient Category | Recommended Concentration | Frequency | Rationale |
|--------------------|---------------------------|-----------|-----------|
| Glycolic acid | **3–5%** | 1×/week max | Significant reduction; consider PHAs instead |
| Lactic acid | **5–8%** | 1–2×/week | Very gentle approach |
| Mandelic acid | **5–8%** | 2×/week | Preferred AHA at this Fitzpatrick type |
| PHAs (gluconolactone) | **5–10%** | 3–4×/week | **Strongly preferred over traditional AHAs** |
| Salicylic acid | **≤1% (leave-on); ≤0.5% (cleanser)** | 2×/week | Higher risk of dryness and PIH |
| Retinol (OTC) | **0.1–0.25%** | 2×/week initially | Very slow titration; consider encapsulated retinol |
| Tretinoin | **0.025%** | 1–2×/week initially | Dermatologist-supervised only at introduction |
| Benzoyl peroxide | **≤2.5% short-contact only** | 2×/week | Significant bleaching/darkening risk |
| Vitamin C (L-AA) | **10%** | Daily AM | Reduced from standard; derivatives (MAP, SAP) may be better tolerated |
| Niacinamide | **2–4%** | Daily | Barrier support + anti-PIH; ideal concentration |
| Azelaic acid | **15%** | Daily | Generally well-tolerated; start at 10% if sensitive |
| Tranexamic acid | **2–5%** | Daily | No adjustment needed; excellent candidate |
| Kojic acid | **1%** | 3×/week | Monitor closely for irritation |

**Fitzpatrick V Special Considerations:**
- **Patch test + test spot on jawline for 1 week** before facial use
- **Consider "skin cycling":** 4-day cycles (Exfoliation → Retinoid → Recovery ×2) reduces cumulative irritation
- **Physical sunscreen mandatory:** Tinted zinc oxide to avoid white cast
- **Barrier-first approach:** Ensure robust barrier before introducing any active
- **Professional treatments:** Any peel, laser, or microneedling requires provider experienced with skin of color

### 2.4 Fitzpatrick VI (Deep Brown to Black)

| Ingredient Category | Recommended Concentration | Frequency | Rationale |
|--------------------|---------------------------|-----------|-----------|
| Glycolic acid | **Avoid or ≤3%** | Rarely | **PHAs or enzymatic exfoliation strongly preferred** |
| Lactic acid | **≤5%** | 1×/week | If used at all; very cautious approach |
| Mandelic acid | **3–5%** | 1–2×/week | Only if PHAs insufficient |
| PHAs (gluconolactone, lactobionic) | **5–10%** | 3–4×/week | **First-line chemical exfoliant** |
| Enzymatic exfoliants (papain, bromelain) | Product-dependent | 1–2×/week | Very gentle; minimal PIH risk |
| Salicylic acid | **≤0.5% in cleanser only** | 2–3×/week | Leave-on formulations generally not recommended |
| Retinol (OTC) | **0.1% or lower; encapsulated/time-release** | 1–2×/week initially | Slowest titration; may take 3–6 months to reach nightly |
| Tretinoin | **0.025% or lower; microsphere formulation** | 1×/week initially | Dermatologist-managed; consider adapalene 0.1% as gentler alternative |
| Benzoyl peroxide | **≤2.5% short-contact; or avoid** | 2×/week max | High risk of paradoxical darkening and bleaching |
| Vitamin C | **5–10%; derivatives preferred (MAP, SAP, THD)** | Daily AM | L-AA may sting; derivatives better tolerated |
| Niacinamide | **2–4%** | Daily | Cornerstone ingredient; barrier + anti-PIH |
| Azelaic acid | **10–15%** | Daily | Excellent safety; start at 10% |
| Tranexamic acid | **2–5%** | Daily | **Highly recommended**; oral TXA may be considered by dermatologist |
| Kojic acid | **≤1%** | 2×/week | Very cautious; discontinue if any irritation |
| Thiamidol | **Product-dependent (usually 0.2%)** | Daily | Specifically developed for sensitive/hyperpigmentation-prone skin |
| Arbutin (alpha) | **Product-dependent** | Daily | Safer alternative to hydroquinone |

**Fitzpatrick VI Special Considerations:**
- **Mandatory dermatologist consultation before:** Any prescription retinoid, hydroquinone, or professional procedure
- **Barrier is everything:** Compromised barrier = guaranteed PIH risk; prioritize ceramides, fatty acids, cholesterol
- **White cast is a real problem:** Untinted zinc oxide can appear ashy/grey; tinted sunscreens are essential for compliance
- **Hair removal caution:** Shaving bumps (pseudofolliculitis barbae) very common; see Cultural Considerations section
- **Anti-inflammatory diet and lifestyle:** Systemic inflammation worsens PIH; address sleep, stress, diet

## 3. Product Formulation Considerations by Category

### 3.1 SUNSCREEN — THE NON-NEGOTIABLE FOUNDATION

**Why it matters most for skin of color:**
- UV exposure is the #1 driver of PIH and melasma in melanin-rich skin
- Visible light and infrared also contribute to pigmentation in darker skin
- Sunscreen non-compliance is high in skin of color due to cosmetic concerns (white cast)

| Formulation Type | Recommendation for Fitzpatrick IV–VI | Rationale |
|-----------------|--------------------------------------|-----------|
| **Physical (mineral): Zinc oxide 10–25%** | **PREFERRED** | Broadest spectrum; sits on skin; less irritation; anti-inflammatory |
| **Physical: Titanium dioxide** | Acceptable but less preferred than ZnO alone | Less UVA-I coverage; can be more whitening |
| **Tinted mineral sunscreen** | **STRONGLY RECOMMENDED for V–VI** | Iron oxides in tint block visible light (melasma driver); eliminates white cast |
| **Chemical/organic filters** | Modern stable filters acceptable; older filters less ideal | Tinosorb S/M, Uvinul A Plus, Uvinul T 150 have better safety profiles; avoid oxybenzone if sensitive |
| **Hybrid (physical + chemical)** | Good compromise for texture | Often more elegant than pure physical; still needs to be tested for irritation |
| **Spray/mist formats** | **Avoid as primary application** | Uneven coverage; inhalation concerns; insufficient quantity applied |
| **Powder sunscreens** | Supplement only, not primary | Insufficient quantity for stated SPF; good for reapplication over makeup |

**SPF Guidelines:**
- **Daily (incidental exposure):** SPF 30 minimum
- **Extended outdoor exposure:** SPF 50; reapply every 2 hours
- **For melasma/PIH management:** SPF 50+ with iron oxides (tinted) for visible light protection
- **Quantity:** 2 mg/cm² (approximately ¼ teaspoon for face alone)

**Application Tips for Skin of Color:**
- Apply in thin layers, allowing each to dry (reduces white cast)
- Tinted formulas in "tan," "deep," or "rich" shades eliminate cast concern
- Some brands with excellent tinted options for deep skin: Black Girl Sunscreen, Melé, Bolden, Unsun, Supergoop (some)
- Reapplication: Tinted powder or spray can help; or reapply liquid and blend

### 3.2 MOISTURIZERS — BARRIER REPAIR IS PIH PREVENTION

**Physiological Basis:**
- Transepidermal water loss (TEWL) varies by facial region in skin of color
- Cheeks and perioral areas often drier; barrier compromise → inflammation → PIH
- Ashiness when dry is a visible sign of compromised barrier in dark skin

| Formulation Type | Fitzpatrick IV | Fitzpatrick V | Fitzpatrick VI | Key Ingredients to Seek |
|-----------------|---------------|---------------|----------------|--------------------------|
| **Cream (rich)** | Good for dry areas | Preferred for most | **Strongly preferred** | Ceramides, fatty acids, cholesterol (3:1:1 ratio), shea butter, squalane |
| **Lotion (medium)** | Good for normal/combo | Good for less dry areas | May be insufficient alone | Lightweight ceramides, hyaluronic acid, glycerin |
| **Gel-cream** | Good for oily areas | Good for oily skin | Use as layer under richer cream | Humectants first, then seal with cream |
| **Ointment/balm** | For very dry/eczema-prone | For very dry/eczema-prone | For very dry/eczema-prone | Petrolatum, lanolin, thick butters (use at night) |
| **Facial oils** | Good sealant layer | Good sealant layer | Good sealant layer | Jojoba (similar to sebum), squalane, rosehip (vitamin A gently) |

**Ingredients to Prioritize in Moisturizers for Skin of Color:**
- **Ceramides (NP, AP, EOP):** Barrier lipids; essential for all skin of color
- **Niacinamide (2–5%):** Barrier repair + anti-inflammatory + melanocyte-stabilizing
- **Hyaluronic acid:** Humectant; holds water without heaviness
- **Glycerin:** Classic humectant; well-tolerated
- **Shea butter:** Emollient; rich without being pore-clogging for most
- **Squalane:** Non-comedogenic oil; mimics natural sebum
- **Centella asiatica:** Wound healing; anti-inflammatory
- **Allantoin:** Soothing; promotes healthy cell turnover
- **Colloidal oatmeal:** Anti-inflammatory; barrier repair

**Ingredients to Avoid in Moisturizers:**
- **Denatured alcohol high in ingredient list:** Drying, barrier-disrupting
- **Fragrance (if sensitive):** Common irritant; can trigger PIH cascade
- **Essential oils high in ingredient list:** Potential phototoxicity and irritation
- **Menthol, peppermint, eucalyptus:** Sensory irritants

### 3.3 CLEANSERS — pH-BALANCED & NON-STRIPPING

**Why it matters:**
- Cleanser pH affects skin barrier integrity (optimal ~5.5)
- High pH (alkaline) cleansers disrupt acid mantle → barrier compromise → PIH risk
- Over-cleansing or stripping cleansers cause dryness → ashy appearance in dark skin

| Cleanser Type | Fitzpatrick IV | Fitzpatrick V | Fitzpatrick VI | Notes |
|--------------|---------------|---------------|----------------|-------|
| **Cream cleanser** | Good | Good | **Preferred** | Non-foaming; lipid-rich; minimal disruption |
| **Oil cleanser / balm** | Good | Good | **Preferred** | Dissolves sebum and sunscreen; emulsifies off; barrier-friendly |
| **Micellar water** | Good (AM or light cleanse) | Good | Good | Rinse after use; surfactants can remain on skin |
| **Gel cleanser (pH-balanced)** | Good for oily skin | Good for oily skin | OK if very gentle | Must be pH 5.0–5.5; avoid SLS/SLES |
| **Foaming cleanser** | OK if gentle | Caution | **Avoid or very occasional** | Often too stripping; SLS/SLES common |
| **Bar soap** | **Avoid** | **Avoid** | **Avoid** | pH 9–10; strips lipids; promotes dysbiosis |

**Cleansing Guidelines for Skin of Color:**
- **Double cleanse (PM):** Oil/balm first (removes SPF/makeup), then cream or gentle gel
- **Water temperature:** Lukewarm; hot water strips lipids
- **Frequency:** 2×/day max; morning may be just water or micellar if not oily
- **Drying:** Pat, don't rub; immediate moisturizer application (within 3 minutes)

### 3.4 SERUMS — TARGETED TREATMENT DELIVERY

**Layering Order (Critical for Efficacy and Safety):**

| Order | Product Type | Examples | Wait Time |
|-------|-------------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | **Water-based, pH-dependent actives** | Vitamin C (L-AA), azelaic acid | 1–2 min |
| 2 | **Water-based, non-pH-dependent** | Niacinamide, hyaluronic acid, peptides | 1 min |
| 3 | **Light oils / oil-based serums** | Squalane, rosehip oil | 1 min |
| 4 | **Emulsions / gel-creams** | Lightweight moisturizers | — |
| 5 | **Rich creams / ointments** | Barrier repair moisturizers | — |
| 6 | **Oil (if used as occlusive)** | Facial oil as last step | — |
| 7 | **AM only: Sunscreen** | Physical/tinted SPF | — |

**Serum-Specific Recommendations by Fitzpatrick:**

| Serum Type | Fitzpatrick IV | Fitzpatrick V | Fitzpatrick VI |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Vitamin C (brightening/antioxidant)** | L-AA 10–15% or derivatives | L-AA 10% or derivatives preferred | Derivatives (MAP, SAP, THD ascorbate) strongly preferred |
| **Niacinamide (barrier/anti-PIH)** | 4–5% | 2–4% | 2–4% |
| **Azelaic acid (acne/PIH/rosacea)** | 15–20% | 15% | 10–15% |
| **Tranexamic acid (melasma/PIH)** | 2–5% | 2–5% | 2–5% |
| **Kojic acid (brightening)** | 1–2% | 1% | ≤1% |
| **Arbutin (brightening)** | Product standard | Product standard | Product standard |
| **Retinol/retinoid (anti-aging/acne)** | 0.25–0.5% | 0.1–0.25% | ≤0.1% or encapsulated |
| **AHA exfoliating serum** | Mandelic 5–10% or lactic 8–12% | Mandelic 5–8% or PHA 5–10% | PHA 5–10% or enzymatic |
| **BHA exfoliating serum** | Salicylic 1–2% | Salicylic ≤1% | Avoid leave-on; 0.5% in cleanser |
| **Peptide serums** | Standard | Standard | Standard (no adjustment needed) |

**Safety Rules for Serum Layering in Skin of Color:**
1. **Never layer two potent actives at the same time** when introducing (e.g., no AHA + retinol same night)
2. **"Skin cycling" approach:** Night 1 = Exfoliation; Night 2 = Retinoid; Nights 3–4 = Recovery (barrier focus)
3. **If irritation occurs:** Stop all actives for 3–5 days; barrier repair only; reintroduce one at a time
4. **Morning = protection; Evening = treatment** (with exceptions: vitamin C AM, some peptides AM+PM)

### 3.5 EXFOLIANTS — CHEMICAL PREFERRED, PHYSICAL AVOIDED

**Why Chemical Over Physical for Skin of Color:**
- Physical scrubs create micro-tears → trauma → melanocyte activation → PIH
- Chemical exfoliants work uniformly; controlled depth based on type/concentration
- Enzymatic exfoliation (papain from papaya, bromelain from pineapple) is ultra-gentle option

| Exfoliant Type | Fitzpatrick IV | Fitzpatrick V | Fitzpatrick VI | Frequency |
|--------------|---------------|---------------|----------------|-----------|
| **PHA (gluconolactone)** | 5–10% | 5–10% | 5–10% | 3–4×/week |
| **Lactobionic acid** | 5–10% | 5–10% | 5–10% | 3–4×/week |
| **Mandelic acid** | 5–10% | 5–8% | 3–5% | 1–3×/week |
| **Lactic acid** | 8–12% | 5–8% | ≤5% | 1–3×/week |
| **Glycolic acid** | 5–8% | 3–5% | ≤3% or avoid | 1×/week max |
| **Salicylic acid (leave-on)** | 1–2% | ≤1% | Avoid | 2–3×/week |
| **Salicylic acid (cleanser)** | 0.5–2% | 0.5% | 0.5% | Daily to 3×/week |
| **Enzymatic (papain/bromelain)** | Good | Good | **Preferred** | 1–2×/week |
| **Physical scrubs** | **Avoid** | **Avoid** | **Avoid** | Never |
| **Microdermabrasion** | Caution | **Avoid** | **Avoid** | — |
| **Professional chemical peels** | Physician only | **Dermatologist experienced in skin of color** | **Dermatologist experienced in skin of color** | — |

**The "Gentle Exfoliation Hierarchy" for Skin of Color:**
1. **First choice:** PHAs (gluconolactone, lactobionic acid)
2. **Second choice:** Enzymatic exfoliants
3. **Third choice:** Mandelic acid (largest AHA molecule = gentlest penetration)
4. **Fourth choice:** Lactic acid (hydrating AHA)
5. **Fifth choice (Fitzpatrick IV only):** Low-strength glycolic
6. **Avoid:** Physical scrubs, microdermabrasion, high-strength peels without supervision

## 4. Safety Warnings by Skin Tone

### 4.1 HIGH RISK — Requires Dermatologist Supervision or Avoidance

| Risk Factor | Ingredient/Procedure | Specific Danger in Skin of Color | Action |
|------------|----------------------|----------------------------------|--------|
| **Exogenous ochronosis** | Hydroquinone >2%, prolonged use | Blue-black irreversible pigmentation; collagen degeneration | **Avoid >2%; limit to 3–4 months; mandatory 2–3 month breaks** |
| **Severe PIH** | TCA peels >10%, phenol peels | Deep injury → intense inflammation → permanent or long-lasting dark patches | **Avoid TCA >10% in V–VI; Fitzpatrick IV physician only** |
| **Paradoxical darkening** | Benzoyl peroxide >2.5%, unsupervised | Oxidative damage to melanocytes; uneven bleaching/darkening | **Use ≤2.5%; short-contact; discontinue if darkening occurs** |
| **Laser complications** | Non-Q-switched lasers, IPL on dark skin | Target melanin in epidermis → burns, blistering, severe PIH | **Only Q-switched Nd:YAG 1064nm or 755nm; provider MUST have skin of color experience** |
| **Microneedling complications** | Deep microneedling (>1.5mm) at home | Deep trauma → extensive PIH; infection risk | **Professional only; 0.5mm max at home** |
| **Steroid-induced changes** | High-potency topical steroids >2 weeks | Skin atrophy, telangiectasia, rebound hyperpigmentation, steroid acne | **Face: never >2 weeks without supervision; use lowest potency** |
| **Mercury toxicity** | Skin lightening products from unregulated markets | Mercury poisoning; nephrotic syndrome; permanent neurological damage | **Avoid all unregulated "lightening" products; check for mercury ingredients** |

### 4.2 MEDIUM RISK — Use With Caution and Close Monitoring

| Risk Factor | Ingredient/Procedure | Warning Signs to Watch | Mitigation |
|------------|----------------------|----------------------|------------|
| **Retinoid-induced PIH** | Tretinoin >0.025%, aggressive introduction | Redness, peeling, stinging, new dark patches around irritated areas | Start 0.025% 1–2×/week; "sandwich" method (moisturizer-retinoid-moisturizer); 3-month ramp-up |
| **AHA over-exfoliation** | Glycolic >5%, lactic >8% in Fitzpatrick V–VI | Tightness, shiny but dry skin, increased sensitivity, new PIH | Reduce concentration; switch to PHA; increase recovery days |
| **Benzoyl peroxide bleaching** | Any concentration on dark fabric/skin | Orange/brown stains on pillowcases; temporary lightening of skin areas | White linens only; moisturize after; reduce concentration if skin lightening occurs |
| **Salicylic acid over-drying** | Leave-on >1% in Fitzpatrick V–VI | Flaking, ashiness, increased PIH around acne | Switch to cleanser format; increase moisturizer; reduce frequency |
| **Vitamin C oxidation** | L-AA >15% or unstable formula | Orange-brown discoloration of product; stinging; no efficacy | Use stable formulations; store properly; reduce concentration if stinging |
| **Essential oil phototoxicity** | Bergamot, lime, lemon (undiluted or before sun) | Blistering, severe PIH in sun-exposed areas | Avoid before sun; use only in PM; ensure proper dilution in formulations |

### 4.3 LOW RISK — Generally Safe Across All Fitzpatrick Types

| Ingredient | Why It's Safe | Best Practices |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|
| **Niacinamide 2–5%** | Anti-inflammatory; barrier repair; inhibits melanosome transfer without cytotoxicity | Can be used daily AM and PM; layer-friendly |
| **Vitamin C derivatives (MAP, SAP, THD)** | Antioxidant; gradual tyrosinase inhibition; less irritating than L-AA | Daily AM; stable; good for sensitive skin of color |
| **Azelaic acid 10–20%** | Anti-inflammatory; antimicrobial; tyrosinase inhibition; pregnancy-safe | Daily; can use with most other ingredients; minimal resistance |
| **Tranexamic acid 2–5%** | Plasmin inhibition; no direct melanocyte interaction; systemic safety profile known | Daily; excellent for melasma; topical very safe |
| **Peptides** | Signaling molecules; no irritation; anti-aging support | Daily; layer-friendly; good retinoid alternative for sensitive skin |
| **Ceramides / fatty acids / cholesterol** | Barrier lipids; identical to skin's natural composition | Daily; essential for all skin of color; use in moisturizer |
| **Hyaluronic acid** | Humectant; non-irritating; holds 1000× weight in water | Daily; all layers; good for plumping and hydration |
| **Centella asiatica** | Traditional wound healing; modern anti-inflammatory evidence | Daily; soothing; supports PIH resolution |
| **Zinc oxide (sunscreen)** | Inert mineral; anti-inflammatory; broadest UV protection | Daily AM; reapply; tinted versions for V–VI |

## 6. Cultural & Practical Considerations

### 6.1 Hair Removal & Pseudofolliculitis Barbae

**The Problem:**
- Very common in Fitzpatrick IV–VI, especially men (shaving bumps)
- Curved hair follicles + close shaving = hair re-enters skin → inflammation → PIH
- Can lead to keloid scarring in predisposed individuals

**Safe Approaches:**
| Method | Safety in Skin of Color | Notes |
|--------|------------------------|-------|
| **Chemical depilatories** | Moderate | Test spot first; some cause irritation; don't leave on longer than directed |
| **Waxing (professional)** | Moderate | Ensure technician experienced with skin of color; risk of lifting skin if technique wrong |
| **Sugaring** | Moderate–Good | Generally gentler than waxing; less traumatic |
| **Laser hair removal** | **Provider-dependent** | **Nd:YAG 1064nm only** for dark skin; IPL and diode can burn; requires experienced provider |
| **Electrolysis** | Good | Permanent; slower; safe for all skin types; each follicle treated individually |
| **Shaving (modified technique)** | Good with modifications | Use single-blade razor; shave with grain; don't stretch skin; use lubricating shave gel; electric clippers (not foil) leave slight stubble = less ingrown |

**Post-Hair Removal Care (Critical for PIH Prevention):**
- Apply soothing, anti-inflammatory product immediately (aloe, centella, niacinamide)
- No actives (retinoids, AHAs) for 24–48 hours after
- Physical sunscreen mandatory on treated areas
- If bumps occur: Treat with topical antibiotic (clindamycin) or azelaic acid; don't pick/squeeze

### 6.2 Skin Lightening & "Brightening" — Addressing Safely

**The Cultural Context:**
- Colorism and societal pressure lead some to seek skin lightening
- Unregulated products (especially from certain markets) contain mercury, steroids, or high-dose hydroquinone
- Safe approach: Even skin tone ≠ lighter skin tone

**SKINgenius Position:**
- **We do not support skin lightening for cosmetic color change**
- **We DO support:**
  - Treatment of hyperpigmentation disorders (PIH, melasma, solar lentigines)
  - Even skin tone where patches exist
  - Brightening = radiance, not shade change
  - Addressing underlying conditions (acne, eczema) that cause PIH

**Safe Brightening Ingredients (not lightening):**
- Azelaic acid
- Vitamin C and derivatives
- Niacinamide
- Tranexamic acid
- Alpha-arbutin
- Kojic acid (safe concentrations)
- Licorice extract
- Thiamidol

**Red Flags in Products (Avoid):**
- Mercury (calomel, mercuric, mercurio)
- High-dose hydroquinone without prescription
- Unknown "herbal" lightening products
- Products promising "3 shades lighter"
- Corticosteroids not disclosed on label

### 6.3 Climate & Geographic Factors

| Climate | Impact on Skin of Color | Adaptation |
|---------|------------------------|------------|
| **Tropical / High humidity** | Increased sweating → potential irritation; higher UV index; fungal acne risk | Lightweight, non-comedogenic moisturizers; antifungal body wash if needed; very diligent sunscreen; blotting papers |
| **Tropical / High altitude** | Intense UV; rapid PIH development | SPF 50+ mandatory; reapply every 90 min outdoors; wide-brim hat; seek shade |
| **Temperate / 4 seasons** | Winter dryness → barrier compromise; summer UV peaks | Richer moisturizer in winter; maintain sunscreen year-round (UVA penetrates clouds) |
| **Arid / Desert** | Extreme TEWL; ashiness prominent; wind irritation | Very rich moisturizers; occlusive layer at night; gentle everything; avoid over-cleansing |
| **Polluted urban** | Oxidative stress; particulate matter inflammation; accelerated aging | Antioxidant serums (vitamin C, ferulic acid); thorough but gentle PM cleansing; anti-pollution skincare if available |
| **Cold / Northern latitudes** | Low humidity indoors; windburn; seasonal affective → cortisol → skin impact | Humidifier indoors; richest moisturizers; don't skip sunscreen (UVA present); manage stress/sleep |

### 6.4 Diet & Lifestyle Considerations

| Factor | Relevance to Skin of Color | Recommendation |
|--------|-------------------------|--------------|
| **Vitamin D** | Higher melanin = less UVB vitamin D synthesis; supplementation often needed | Check 25-OH vitamin D levels; supplement if <30 ng/mL; especially important in northern latitudes or if very sun-protective |
| **Anti-inflammatory diet** | Systemic inflammation worsens PIH and acne | Omega-3 fatty acids; colorful vegetables; reduce refined sugar; adequate protein for collagen synthesis |
| **Stress management** | Cortisol triggers melanocyte activity; stress worsens many skin conditions | Sleep 7–9 hours; mindfulness practice; exercise; address mental health |
| **Hormonal factors** | Melasma strongly hormone-related; pregnancy; oral contraceptives; HRT | Discuss melasma history with OCP prescribing; pregnancy requires ingredient safety review (avoid retinoids, hydroquinone) |
| **Smoking** | Oxidative stress; impaired wound healing; worsened PIH | Cessation; nicotine itself may affect skin healing |

## 7. When to See a Dermatologist

**Urgent / Soon:**
- Any changing mole (possible melanoma — **melanoma in skin of color often appears on palms, soles, nail beds, mucous membranes**)
- Rapidly spreading pigmentation
- Pigmentation with pain, bleeding, or ulceration
- Severe acne with significant PIH
- Suspected exogenous ochronosis (blue-black patches from hydroquinone)

**Routine Consultation Recommended:**
- Persistent melasma despite OTC treatment for 3+ months
- Extensive PIH affecting quality of life
- Keloid tendency (affects treatment options)
- Considering prescription treatments (tretinoin, hydroquinone, oral TXA)
- Interested in professional procedures (chemical peels, lasers, microneedling)
- Unclear diagnosis (is it PIH, melasma, or something else?)

**Dermatologist Selection for Skin of Color:**
- Seek board-certified dermatologists
- Prefer those with stated interest/expertise in skin of color
- Skin of Color Society membership is a positive indicator
- Ask about experience with your specific concern in your skin type
- Before any procedure: "How many patients with Fitzpatrick V–VI skin have you treated with this specific procedure?"

## Appendices

### Appendix A: Fitzpatrick Skin Type Classification

| Type | Skin Color | Characteristics | UV Sensitivity |
|------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| I | Very fair; always burns, never tans | Pale white skin, blue/hazel eyes, blond/red hair | Extremely sensitive |
| II | Fair; usually burns, tans minimally | White skin, fair; any eye/hair color | Very sensitive |
| III | Medium; sometimes burns, tans gradually | Cream white skin; any eye/hair color | Moderately sensitive |
| IV | Olive/light brown; rarely burns, tans well | Moderate brown skin; Mediterranean, Latin, Asian heritage | Minimally sensitive |
| V | Brown; very rarely burns, tans very easily | Dark brown skin; Middle Eastern, some Hispanic, some African heritage | Very minimally sensitive |
| VI | Dark brown/black; never burns, tans deeply | Deeply pigmented skin; African, Aboriginal, Melanesian heritage | Least sensitive |

**Note:** Fitzpatrick typing is a guide, not absolute. Many individuals have mixed ancestry and may not fit neatly into one category. Assess based on:
- Genetic background (all known ancestry)
- Sun reaction history
- Tanning response
- Clinical observation

### Appendix B: Patch Testing Protocol

Before introducing any new active ingredient in Fitzpatrick IV–VI:

1. **Cleanse** inner forearm with mild cleanser; pat dry
2. **Apply** small amount of product to 1-inch area
3. **Cover** with bandage (optional, but increases sensitivity)
4. **Wait** 48–72 hours
5. **Assess:**
   - Redness = mild irritation; proceed with caution
   - Itching, swelling, blistering = allergic reaction; do not use
   - No reaction = proceed with facial introduction

For professional procedures:
- **Test spot** behind ear or along jawline
- **Wait** 1–2 weeks to assess PIH response
- **Document** with photographs

### Appendix C: Photographic Documentation Guide

For tracking PIH, melasma, or treatment response in skin of color:

- **Lighting:** Natural indirect daylight or color-correct LED (5500K)
- **Background:** Neutral gray or white
- **Distance:** 12–18 inches, perpendicular to face
- **Angles:** Front, both profiles (45°), close-up of affected areas
- **Consistency:** Same time of day, same location, same camera
- **Frequency:** Baseline, then monthly during treatment
- **Note:** Darker skin can be challenging to photograph accurately; avoid warm/yellow lighting which distorts color
