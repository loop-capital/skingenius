# Acne Spectrum

## 1. Acne Vulgaris

### Clinical Definition

Acne vulgaris is a chronic inflammatory disease of the pilosebaceous unit characterized by comedones, papules, pustules, nodules, and cysts. It is the most common skin condition in the United States, affecting up to 50 million Americans annually.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L70.9 (Acne, unspecified)
- **Subclassification codes:**
  - L70.0 — Acne vulgaris
  - L70.1 — Acne conglobata
  - L70.2 — Acne varioliformis
  - L70.3 — Acne tropica
  - L70.4 — Infantile acne
  - L70.5 — Acne excoriée des jeunes filles
  - L70.8 — Other acne
  - L70.9 — Acne, unspecified

### Diagnostic Criteria

Primary lesions include:
- **Comedones** (non-inflammatory): open (blackheads) and closed (whiteheads)
- **Papules** (inflammatory): small, raised, red bumps (< 5mm)
- **Pustules** (inflammatory): papules with visible purulent exudate
- **Nodules** (severe inflammatory): larger, deeper, painful lesions (> 5mm)
- **Cysts** (severe): deep, pus-filled lesions capable of scarring

Distribution pattern typically affects face, neck, chest, upper back, and shoulders — areas with highest density of sebaceous glands.

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Severity | Clinical Features | Lesion Count (Approximate) |
|-------|----------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| I (Mild) | Comedonal | Predominantly open/closed comedones; rare papules | < 20 total lesions |
| II (Mild-Moderate) | Papulopustular | Comedones + multiple papules/pustules | 20–30 lesions |
| III (Moderate) | Nodular | Numerous papules/pustules + occasional nodules | 30–50 lesions |
| IV (Severe) | Nodulocystic/Conglobata | Numerous nodules and cysts with scarring | > 50 lesions |

**Leeds Revised Acne Grading System:** Uses photographic standards for global acne assessment.

**Global Acne Grading System (GAGS):** Assigns severity factor × site factor for face, chest, and back.

### Root Causes

The pathogenesis involves four primary mechanisms:

1. **Follicular hyperkeratinization** — abnormal desquamation of keratinocytes leading to microcomedone formation
2. **Increased sebum production** — androgen-driven sebaceous gland hyperplasia
3. **Cutibacterium acnes (C. acnes) colonization** — formerly *Propionibacterium acnes*; triggers inflammatory cascade
4. **Inflammation** — innate immune response to C. acnes and biofilm formation

**Evidence-based contributing factors:**

| Factor | Evidence Strength | Mechanism |
|--------|------------------|-----------|
| **Genetics** | Strong | Family history increases risk 3–4x; twin studies show 80% heritability |
| **Androgens** | Strong | Stimulate sebaceous gland activity; DHT is primary mediator |
| **Diet** | Moderate | High glycemic load and dairy (especially skim milk) associated with worsening |
| **Gut microbiome** | Emerging | Gut-skin axis; dysbiosis may drive systemic inflammation |
| **Stress** | Moderate | CRH/ACTH axis increases cortisol and androgens, stimulating sebum |
| **Hormonal fluctuations** | Strong | Menstrual cycle, puberty, PCOS directly influence severity |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

- **Cosmetics:** Occlusive or comedogenic products (check for "non-comedogenic" labeling)
- **Mechanical irritation:** Helmets, tight collars, chin straps (see Acne Mechanica)
- **Medications:** Lithium, corticosteroids, testosterone, phenytoin, isoniazid
- **Occupational exposures:** Cutting oils, chlorinated hydrocarbons, tar
- **Climate:** High humidity and heat can worsen symptoms
- **Picking/squeezing:** Mechanical irritation increases inflammation and scarring risk

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Presentation Differences | Treatment Considerations |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–II** | Erythema highly visible; inflammatory lesions appear bright red; post-inflammatory erythema (PIE) common | Can tolerate higher-strength retinoids; careful with PIE management |
| **Fitzpatrick III–IV** | Mixed presentation; both PIH and PIE concerns; keloid scarring risk moderate | Balanced approach needed; introduce actives gradually |
| **Fitzpatrick V–VI** | Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) is primary concern; acne may present with less visible erythea; keloid risk higher | Gentle approach mandatory; prioritize PIH prevention; avoid aggressive mechanical exfoliation |

### Treatment Pathways

#### Over-the-Counter (OTC)

| Ingredient | Concentration | Mechanism | Efficacy |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|----------|
| **Benzoyl Peroxide (BPO)** | 2.5%–10% | Antimicrobial against C. acnes; keratolytic; oxidizes bacterial proteins | First-line; 2.5% as effective as higher concentrations with less irritation |
| **Salicylic Acid** | 0.5%–2% | Lipophilic beta-hydroxy acid; penetrates follicles; keratolytic | Effective for comedonal acne; anti-inflammatory properties |
| **Adapalene (Differin®)** | 0.1%–0.3% (0.1% OTC in US) | Third-generation retinoid; normalizes keratinization; anti-inflammatory | OTC retinoid of choice; less irritating than tretinoin |
| **Azelaic Acid** | 10%–20% | Antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, anti-keratinizing; tyrosinase inhibitor | Good for acne + PIH; safe in pregnancy (20% Rx) |
| **Niacinamide** | 2%–10% | Anti-inflammatory; sebum regulation; barrier support | Adjunctive; particularly good for sensitive skin |
| **Sulfur** | 3%–10% | Keratolytic; mild antimicrobial; reduces sebum | Traditional ingredient; good for sensitive skin |

#### Prescription (Topical)

| Medication | Class | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **Tretinoin** | First-generation retinoid | Gold standard; photosensitivity; start 0.025%–0.05% |
| **Tazarotene** | Third-generation retinoid | Most potent topical retinoid; teratogenic |
| **Clindamycin** | Topical antibiotic | Never as monotherapy (resistance risk); combine with BPO |
| **Erythromycin** | Topamic antibiotic | Resistance concerns; declining use |
| **Dapsone gel** | Sulfone | Anti-inflammatory; useful in adult females |
| **Minocycline foam** | Topical antibiotic | 1.5% foam; lower resistance risk than erythromycin |
| **Clascoterone** | Androgen receptor inhibitor | First topical hormonal acne treatment; blocks androgen receptors in skin |

#### Prescription (Systemic)

| Medication | Indication | Monitoring |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Doxycycline** | Moderate inflammatory acne | Photosensitivity; take with food; not for children < 8 years |
| **Minocycline** | Moderate inflammatory acne | Vestibular side effects; skin pigmentation (rare) |
| **Sarecycline** | Moderate to severe acne | Narrow-spectrum tetracycline; reduced GI side effects |
| **Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole** | Resistant cases | Sulfa allergy; blood dyscrasias (rare) |
| **Combined Oral Contraceptives** | Hormonal acne in females | FDA-approved: norgestimate/ethinyl estradiol, drospirenone/ethinyl estradiol |
| **Spironolactone** | Hormonal acne in females | Monitor potassium; anti-androgen effects |
| **Isotretinoin** | Severe nodular acne; treatment-resistant acne | iPLEDGE program; teratogenic; monthly labs (lipids, LFTs, pregnancy) |

#### Professional Procedures

- **Chemical peels:** Salicylic acid 20%–30%, glycolic acid 30%–70%, Jessner's solution
- **Comedone extraction:** Manual extraction by trained professional
- **Corticosteroid injection:** Intralesional triamcinolone for cysts (2.5–5 mg/mL)
- **Light/laser therapy:** Blue light (415 nm), red light (630 nm), PDT with ALA
- **Microneedling:** For acne scarring (not active acne)

#### Lifestyle & Supplements

| Intervention | Evidence | Notes |
|-------------|----------|-------|
| **Low glycemic index diet** | Moderate | Reduce refined carbohydrates; may decrease IGF-1 |
| **Dairy reduction** | Moderate | Particularly skim milk; mechanism unclear |
| **Probiotics** | Emerging | Lactobacillus strains show promise for gut-skin axis |
| **Zinc** | Moderate | 30–50 mg elemental zinc; anti-inflammatory; compare to minocycline in some studies |
| **Omega-3 fatty acids** | Limited | Anti-inflammatory potential |
| **Stress management** | Moderate | Meditation, adequate sleep, exercise |
| **Consistent cleansing** | Strong | Twice daily with gentle cleanser; avoid over-washing |

### Red Flags — Urgent Dermatologist Referral

- [ ] **Nodulocystic lesions** with risk of permanent scarring
- [ ] **Acne fulminans:** Sudden onset ulcerative acne with systemic symptoms (fever, arthralgia)
- [ ] **Significant psychological distress** — depression, anxiety, social withdrawal
- [ ] **Treatment failure** after 3 months of appropriate topical therapy
- [ ] **Signs of androgen excess** in females (hirsutism, irregular menses, virilization) — evaluate for PCOS
- [ ] **Medication-induced acne** requiring management
- [ ] **Any lesion suspicious for skin cancer** (atypical, changing, bleeding)

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Acne is caused by dirty skin" | Acne is not a hygiene issue; over-washing can worsen it |
| "Chocolate causes acne" | Evidence is mixed; high glycemic load is more relevant than chocolate specifically |
| "You should let acne run its course" | Untreated acne leads to scarring and PIH; early treatment prevents this |
| "Toothpaste dries out pimples" | Can cause chemical burns and contact dermatitis; not recommended |
| "Sun exposure clears acne" | Temporary drying effect; UV damages skin, increases PIH, and causes photoaging |
| "Popping pimples helps them heal faster" | Increases inflammation, scarring, and PIH risk |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Acne Vulgaris:**

1. **Initial Assessment:**
   - Photo analysis for lesion type, distribution, and severity
   - User questionnaire: duration, prior treatments, menstrual correlation, scarring history
   - Fitzpatrick skin type determination

2. **Severity Stratification:**
   - **Mild (Grade I-II):** OTC regimen with adapalene + BPO or salicylic acid
   - **Moderate (Grade III):** Add topical antibiotic (clindamycin) + consider oral doxycycline; recommend dermatologist
   - **Severe (Grade IV):** Immediate dermatologist referral; discuss isotretinoin

3. **Personalization:**
   - Fitzpatrick V-VI: Emphasize gentle approach, PIH prevention, azelaic acid inclusion
   - Hormonal pattern (jawline, cyclical): Flag for hormonal evaluation
   - Sensitive skin: Niacinamide, azelaic acid; avoid high-concentration BPO initially

4. **Progress Tracking:**
   - 8–12 week assessment intervals (retinoid response time)
   - Photo comparison for objective improvement
   - Flag worsening or new nodules for escalation

5. **Safety Guardrails:**
   - Pregnancy screening before recommending retinoids, spironolactone, or isotretinoin
   - Photosensitivity warnings for doxycycline and tretinoin
   - Antibiotic stewardship: never recommend topical antibiotic monotherapy

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **First-line** | Azelaic acid 20%, glycolic acid (low %), topical erythromycin, benzoyl peroxide (limited data but generally considered safe) | Retinoids (tretinoin, adapalene, tazarotene), salicylic acid (high concentrations/oral), isotretinoin |
| **Systemic** | Erythromycin (if needed), cephalexin | Tetracyclines (doxycycline, minocycline), spironolactone, isotretinoin (absolute contraindication) |
| **Hormonal** | — | Combined oral contraceptives (stop when trying to conceive) |

**Isotretinoin:** Absolute contraindication in pregnancy; requires iPLEDGE enrollment, two forms of contraception, and monthly pregnancy testing.

## 2. Hormonal Acne

### Clinical Definition

Hormonal acne is a pattern of acne vulgaris driven by fluctuations or excesses in androgens, typically presenting in adult females ("adult female acne"). It is characterized by deep, tender papules and nodules along the jawline, chin, and neck, often correlating with the menstrual cycle.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L70.9 (Acne, unspecified) — no specific hormonal acne code; use with additional codes for underlying conditions (e.g., E28.2 for PCOS)
- **Related codes:**
  - E28.2 — Polycystic ovarian syndrome
  - L73.0 — Acne keloidalis nuchae
  - N91.2 — Amenorrhea, unspecified

### Diagnostic Criteria

- Onset or persistence beyond age 25 (late-onset acne)
- Distribution primarily lower face (jawline, chin, perioral), neck, and sometimes trunk
- Deep, tender, inflammatory lesions (papules, nodules) rather than superficial comedones
- Clear correlation with menstrual cycle (flare in luteal phase, days 14–28)
- Often refractory to conventional topical therapy
- May be accompanied by signs of androgen excess

### Grading Scale

Uses same Leeds/GAGS grading as acne vulgaris, but note:

| Feature | Hormonal Acne Typical Pattern |
|---------|---------------------------|
| **Lesion type** | Predominantly inflammatory (papules, nodules); fewer comedones |
| **Distribution** | Lower third of face, jawline, neck, periarial |
| **Cyclical pattern** | Worsens 7–10 days before menses |
| **Severity** | Often moderate; persistent rather than explosive |
| **Scarring risk** | Higher due to depth of inflammatory lesions |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Androgen excess** | Strong | DHT stimulates sebaceous glands; source can be ovaries (PCOS), adrenals, or peripheral conversion |
| **Estrogen/progesterone imbalance** | Strong | Relative androgen dominance in luteal phase; progesterone can stimulate sebum |
| **PCOS** | Strong | 70–80% of women with PCOS have acne; insulin resistance drives ovarian androgen production |
| **Menstrual cycle fluctuations** | Strong | Premenstrual flare in up to 70% of female acne patients |
| **Stress-induced cortisol** | Moderate | Cortisol increases androgens and sebum production |
| **Postpartum hormonal shifts** | Strong | Androgen rebound after delivery can trigger flares |
| **Discontinuation of OCPs** | Strong | "Post-pill acne" — rebound androgen activity |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

- **Menstrual cycle:** Luteal phase (days 14–28) is high-risk period
- **PCOS:** Oligomenorrhea, hirsutism, weight gain, insulin resistance
- **Stress:** Chronic stress elevates cortisol and CRH
- **Diet:** High glycemic load increases insulin and IGF-1, which stimulate ovarian androgens
- **Dairy:** Possible IGF-1 contribution
- **Medications:** Testosterone, DHEA, certain progestins in contraceptives
- **Endocrine disorders:** Cushing's syndrome, congenital adrenal hyperplasia (rare)

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

- **All skin types:** PHH risk is universal but most pronounced in Fitzpatrick IV–VI
- **Darker skin tones:** Jawline/neck PIH can be particularly persistent; keloid scarring risk higher on neck
- **Lighter skin tones:** Post-inflammatory erythema (PIE) more visible; may benefit from vascular lasers

### Treatment Pathways

#### OTC & Cosmeceuticals

| Ingredient | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Adapalene 0.1%** | Normalizes follicular keratinization; anti-inflammatory |
| **Azelaic Acid 15–20%** | Anti-inflammatory; addresses PIH; some anti-androgen data |
| **Niacinamide 4–5%** | Sebum regulation; anti-inflammatory |
| **BPO 2.5–5%** | Antimicrobial; reduces C. acnes load |
| **Salicylic Acid 2%** | Follicular penetration; keratolytic |

#### Prescription (Topical)

| Medication | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| **Clascoterone 1%** | First topical androgen receptor inhibitor; directly blocks hormonal pathway in skin |
| **Spironolactone 2–5% topical** | Compounded; limited evidence but used off-label |
| **Retinoids** (tretinoin, tazarotene) | Standard care; tazarotene most potent |
| **Dapsone 5% gel** | Useful in adult females; anti-inflammatory |

#### Prescription (Systemic — Hormonal)

| Medication | Dose | Mechanism | Monitoring |
|-----------|------|-----------|------------|
| **Spironolactone** | 25–200 mg/day | Androgen receptor antagonist; inhibits androgen biosynthesis | Potassium levels (especially > 50 mg or with ACE inhibitors/ARBs); menstrual irregularities; breast tenderness |
| **Combined Oral Contraceptives** | Various | Suppress ovarian androgen production; increase SHBG | BP, thromboembolism risk; contraindicated in smokers > 35, migraine with aura |
| **Cyproterone acetate** | 2 mg (with EE) | Potent anti-androgen; not available in US | Liver function; depression |
| **Flutamide** | 250 mg/day | Androgen receptor antagonist; rarely used | Hepatotoxicity monitoring required |
| **Metformin** | 500–2000 mg/day | For PCOS-related insulin resistance; indirectly reduces androgens | Renal function; GI side effects |

#### Prescription (Systemic — Non-Hormonal)

- Same antibiotics as acne vulgaris (doxycycline, minocycline, sarecycline) for inflammatory component
- Isotretinoin for severe, treatment-resistant cases

#### Professional Procedures

- **Hormonal evaluation:** DHEAS, total/free testosterone, LH/FSH ratio (for PCOS), 17-OH progesterone (for CAH)
- **Chemical peels:** Salicylic acid, Jessner's
- **Intralesional corticosteroids:** For nodules
- **Laser/light:** Blue/red light, PDT for inflammatory component

#### Lifestyle & Supplements

| Intervention | Evidence | Rationale |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| **Low glycemic index diet** | Moderate | Reduces insulin and IGF-1; decreases ovarian androgen production |
| **Spearmint tea** | Limited | Two cups daily may reduce free testosterone |
| **Inositol (myo- or D-chiro-)** | Moderate | Improves insulin sensitivity in PCOS; may reduce androgens |
| **Zinc** | Moderate | Anti-androgen and anti-inflammatory effects |
| **Omega-3** | Limited | May reduce inflammation |
| **Stress reduction** | Moderate | Lowers cortisol; reduces androgen stimulation |
| **Regular exercise** | Moderate | Improves insulin sensitivity |

### Red Flags — Urgent Dermatologist Referral

- [ ] Signs of virilization (deepening voice, clitoromegaly, male-pattern baldness) — evaluate for adrenal/ovarian tumor
- [ ] Rapid onset of severe acne with hirsutism
- [ ] Irregular menses or amenorrhea with acne — PCOS workup indicated
- [ ] Treatment failure with spironolactone 100 mg + topical regimen after 3–6 months
- [ ] Severe depression or anxiety related to acne
- [ ] Desire for pregnancy while on hormonal therapy — requires medication adjustment

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Hormonal acne only happens in teenagers" | 50% of women in 20s, 25% in 30s, 12% in 40s have acne; hormonal pattern is common in adults |
| "You can't treat hormonal acne topically" | Clascoterone (topical androgen receptor inhibitor) is FDA-approved; retinoids and azelaic acid also help |
| "Birth control always helps acne" | Some progestins (levonorgestrel, nongestrel) are androgenic and can worsen acne |
| "Spironolactone is only a diuretic" | At dermatologic doses (50–200 mg), it's a potent anti-androgen; diurete effect is minimal |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Hormonal Acne:**

1. **Pattern Recognition:**
   - Ask about menstrual correlation (calendar tracking integration)
   - Distribution analysis: jawline/chin/neck emphasis
   - Age and prior OCP history
   - Screen for PCOS symptoms (irregular periods, weight gain, hirsutism)

2. **Treatment Pathway:**
   - **Mild:** Adapalene + azelaic acid + lifestyle (low GI diet)
   - **Moderate:** Add spironolactone 50 mg (in females) + topical clascoterone if available
   - **Severe/PCOS suspected:** Recommend dermatologist/endocrinologist; suggest hormonal workup

3. **Cycle-Aware Recommendations:**
   - Luteal phase: Increase BPO use, spot treatment
   - Follicular phase: Focus on repair, PIH management

4. **Safety Checks:**
   - Pregnancy screening before any hormonal therapy
   - Contraception counseling if prescribing spironolactone or isotretinoin
   - Flag need for potassium monitoring on spironolactone

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Azelaic acid, benzoyl peroxide (low %), glycolic acid | Retinoids, clascoterone |
| **Systemic** | Erythromycin, cephalexin | Spironolactone, cyproterone, flutamide, isotretinoin, tetracyclines |
| **Hormonal** | — | All hormonal therapies; stop OCPs when trying to conceive |

**Note:** Postpartum acne flares are common due to androgen rebound; breastfeeding limits systemic options.

## 3. Acne Mechanica

### Clinical Definition

Acne mechanica is a form of acne triggered by mechanical irritation, friction, pressure, heat, or occlusion of the skin. It was first described in athletes wearing helmets and padding but affects anyone with repetitive skin friction.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L70.8 (Other acne) or L70.9 (Acne, unspecified)
- **Historical synonym:** "Sports acne," "friction acne"

### Diagnostic Criteria

- History of mechanical irritation (helmet, mask, tight clothing, musical instrument)
- Lesions appear at sites of friction/pressure
- Comedones, papules, and pustules in distribution matching the offending object
- Often monomorphic (similar-sized lesions)
- Improvement when irritant is removed

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mild** | Few comedones and papules at friction site |
| **Moderate** | Numerous papules and pustules; may extend beyond direct contact area |
| **Severe** | Deep nodules and cysts; risk of scarring; "acne miliaris necrotica" variant |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Physical occlusion** | Strong | Blocks follicular opening; traps sebum and sweat |
| **Friction/pressure** | Strong | Microtrauma to follicle; rupture of microcomedones |
| **Heat and humidity** | Strong | Increases sweating and sebum fluidity; expands follicular ostia |
| **Sweat retention** | Strong | Sweat contains antimicrobial peptides and salts that irritate when trapped |
| **C. acnes proliferation** | Moderate | Occlusion creates anaerobic environment favorable to bacterial growth |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

- **Sports equipment:** Football helmets, hockey masks, shoulder pads, wrestling headgear
- **Clothing:** Tight uniforms, synthetic fabrics, bra straps, tight collars
- **Occupational:** Chin straps, respirators, surgical masks ("maskne"), headbands
- **Musical instruments:** Violin chin rest, flute chin contact
- **Backpacks:** Shoulder straps
- **Wheelchair use:** Constant pressure on back and buttocks
- **Surgical masks/N95 respirators:** Increased prevalence since 2020 ("maskne")

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

- **All types:** PHH risk proportional to Fitzpatrick type; darker skin more prone to post-inflammatory pigment changes
- **Fitzpatrick V–VI:** Higher keloid risk on areas of repeated trauma (chest, back, jawline)
- **Post-inflammatory changes** may persist long after acne resolves

### Treatment Pathways

#### Prevention (Primary)

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Barrier protection** | Wear moisture-wicking, breathable fabrics; apply thin barrier (petroleum jelly or dimethicone) to friction points |
| **Equipment hygiene** | Clean helmet pads, chin straps, and mask surfaces regularly |
| **Break periods** | Remove equipment when possible; allow skin to dry |
| **Proper fit** | Adjust equipment to minimize pressure points |
| **Cotton liners** | Use absorbent cotton under helmets/pads |

#### OTC Treatment

| Ingredient | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Benzoyl Peroxide 2.5–5%** | Apply to affected areas after activity; wash off before re-equipping |
| **Salicylic Acid 0.5–2%** | Cleanser or leave-on; penetrates follicles |
| **Glycolic Acid** | Exfoliant; helps clear follicular occlusion |
| **Niacinamide** | Anti-inflammatory; reduces redness |

#### Prescription

- Same as acne vulgaris for severity-appropriate treatment
- **Intralesional corticosteroids** for persistent nodules
- **Doxycycline** for inflammatory component if widespread

#### Lifestyle & Modifications

- Shower immediately after sweating
- Change out of wet/sweaty clothing promptly
- Use clean towels and pillowcases
- Consider "acne-fighting" laundry detergents (fragrance-free, hypoallergenic)

### Red Flags

- [ ] Deep nodules or cysts with scarring potential
- [ ] Secondary infection (increasing water, warmth, pus)
- [ ] No improvement after removing/mechanical trigger
- [ ] Widespread involvement suggesting additional acne vulgaris

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "It's just sweat causing it" | Sweat alone doesn't cause acne; occlusion + friction + heat are the triad |
| "Only athletes get it" | Anyone with repeated friction can develop it — musicians, healthcare workers, drivers |
| "You have to stop the activity" | Usually manageable with proper hygiene, barriers, and treatment |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Acne Mechanica:**

1. **Trigger Identification:**
   - Ask about sports, occupation, equipment use, mask-wearing habits
   - Photo analysis of distribution pattern (does it match mask strap line? helmet edge?)

2. **Prevention-First Approach:**
   - Recommend barrier strategies specific to identified trigger
   - Equipment hygiene checklists
   - Breathable fabric recommendations

3. **Treatment:**
   - Mild: BPO wash + salicylic acid
   - Moderate: Add adapalene; consider doxycycline if inflammatory
   - Emphasize that prevention is as important as treatment

4. **Occupational/Special Circumstances:**
   - Healthcare workers: Gentle approach due to mask-wearing; niacinamide and azelaic acid preferred
   - Athletes: Schedule-aware recommendations (apply treatment post-practice)

### Pregnancy Considerations

Same as acne vulgaris; emphasize prevention and gentle topical options.

## 4. Fungal Acne (Malassezia Folliculitis)

### Clinical Definition

Malassezia folliculitis (formerly Pityrosporum folliculitis) is a fungal infection of hair follicles caused by overgrowth of *Malassezia* yeast (lipophilic yeast, part of normal skin flora). It is frequently misdiagnosed as bacterial acne vulgaris.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** B36.0 (Pityriasis versicolor) — use L73.8 (Other specified follicular disorders) or B36.9 for Malassezia folliculitis specifically
- **Organism:** *Malassezia furfur* (formerly *Pityrosporum ovale*), *M. globosa*, *M. restricta*

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Monomorphic papules and pustules** — all lesions look similar (unlike bacterial acne which is polymorphic)
- **Intense itching** — hallmark feature; bacterial acne is typically not itchy
- **Distribution:** Chest, back, upper arms, forehead, forehead, forehead, hairline, neck (areas rich in sebaceous glands)
- **Worsens with:** Heat, humidity, sweating, topical/systemic antibiotics, occlusive clothing
- **Fails to respond to:** Standard acne antibiotics and retinoids
- **KOH preparation:** Reveals budding yeast ("spaghetti and meatballs" pattern with tinea, but Malassezia shows round/oval yeast forms)

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mild** | Scattered monomorphic papules; mild pruritus |
| **Moderate** | Numerous papules and pustules; moderate to severe itching |
| **Severe** | Confluent lesions; significant pruritus; widespread distribution; may have secondary bacterial infection |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Malassezia overgrowth** | Strong | Yeast proliferates in lipid-rich environment; requires fatty acids for growth |
| **Sebum overproduction** | Strong | Provides substrate for yeast proliferation |
| **Immunological factors** | Moderate | Host immune response to yeast drives inflammation |
| **Antibiotic use** | Strong | Oral/topical antibiotics alter skin flora, allowing yeast overgrowth |
| **Hot/humid climate** | Strong | Promotes sweating and yeast proliferation |
| **Occlusion** | Moderate | Trapped sweat and sebum create favorable environment |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

- **Oral antibiotics** (especially tetracyclines for acne) — disrupts bacterial competition
- **Topical corticosteroids** — immunosuppressive; can worsen fungal infections
- **Immunosuppression** — HIV/AIDS, organ transplant, chemotherapy
- **Diabetes mellitus** — altered immune function
- **Pregnancy** — hormonal changes increase sebum production
- **High humidity and heat**
- **Oily skin products** — provide lipid substrate

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

- **All types:** May cause hypopigmentation or hyperpigmentation post-inflammation
- **Fitzpatrick V–VI:** PIH is common after resolution; may be mistaken for persistent fungal acne
- **Differential diagnosis:** Must distinguish from bacterial acne, pseudofolliculitis barbae, and acneiform drug eruptions

### Treatment Pathways

#### First-Line (Topical Antifungals)

| Medication | Concentration | Application | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|-------|
| **Ketoconazole shampoo** | 2% | Use as body wash on affected areas; leave on 3–5 minutes | First-line; safe, effective |
| **Selenium sulfide** | 2.5% (Rx) or 1% (OTC) | Same as ketoconazole shampoo | Anti-yeast; also used for seborrheic dermatitis |
| **Zinc pyrithione** | 1–2% | Shampoo or soap | OTC option; antimicrobial and antifungal |
| **Ciclopirox shampoo** | 1.5% | Prescription | Broad-spectrum antifungal |
| **Ketoconazole cream** | 2% | Apply BID | For facial involvement |
| **Clotrimazole cream** | 1% | Apply BID | OTC alternative |

#### Oral Antifungals (Severe or Recalcitrant)

| Medication | Dose | Duration | Notes |
|-----------|------|----------|-------|
| **Itraconazole** | 200 mg daily | 7 days | Preferred; lipophilic; concentrates in sebum |
| **Fluconazole** | 150–300 mg weekly | 2–4 weeks | Alternative; less lipophilic |
| **Ketoconazole** | 200 mg daily | — | Hepatotoxicity risk; generally avoided |

**Note:** Oral antifungals require physician oversight; check liver function and drug interactions.

#### What NOT to Use

| Treatment | Why to Avoid |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Oral antibiotics** (doxycycline, etc.) | Worsen condition by disrupting bacterial flora |
| **Topical corticosteroids** | Immunosuppressive; can cause tinea incognito |
| **Retinoids as monotherapy** | May help comedones but won't address yeast |
| **Benzoyl Peroxide** | Antibacterial but not antifungal; limited efficacy |

#### Adjunctive Measures

- **Discontinue antibiotics** if currently on them for misdiagnosed "acne"
- **Loose, breathable clothing**
- **Shower after sweating**
- **Avoid oily skincare products**
- **Laundry hygiene:** Hot wash towels and clothing

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Misdiagnosis as bacterial acne** — leading to prolonged inappropriate antibiotic use
- [ ] **Immunosuppression** — more severe, widespread disease; may need systemic workup
- [ ] **Secondary bacterial infection** — increasing pain, warmth, purulent drainage
- [ ] **Treatment failure** after 2 weeks of appropriate antifungal therapy — reconsider diagnosis

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Fungal acne isn't real acne" | Correct — it's not acne at all; it's a folliculitis caused by yeast |
| "It should respond to acne antibiotics" | No — antibiotics worsen it by killing competing bacteria |
| "It only happens on the face" | Most common on trunk (chest, back); can affect face |
| "Over-the-counter antifungals don't work" | Ketoconazole shampoo is first-line and highly effective |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Fungal Acne:**

1. **Diagnostic Screening:**
   - Ask: Is it itchy? Are all bumps the same size? Worse with heat/sweat?
   - Ask: Are you on antibiotics? Using steroid creams?
   - Flag monomorphic appearance vs. polymorphic bacterial acne

2. **Differential Flag:**
   - If user has been on doxycycline for "acne" with no improvement → HIGH suspicion for Malassezia folliculitis
   - If lesions are intensely itchy → Fungal acne likely

3. **Treatment Recommendation:**
   - **Mild:** Ketoconazole 2% shampoo as body wash; leave on 3–5 minutes; 3x weekly
   - **Moderate:** Daily ketoconazole shampoo + ketoconazole cream BID
   - **Severe/Widespread:** Refer to dermatologist for oral itraconazole

4. **Follow-up:**
   - Reassess at 2 weeks; should show improvement
   - If no improvement, flag for dermatologist — possible misdiagnosis or mixed infection

5. **Prevention:**
   - Weekly maintenance ketoconazole shampoo for recurrent cases
   - Avoid unnecessary antibiotics
   - Manage sweating and humidity

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Ketoconazole shampoo (limited exposure), zinc pyrithione | Oral antifungals |
| **Systemic** | — | Oral azoles (teratogenic risk, especially first trimester) |

## 5. Steroid Acne

### Clinical Definition

Steroid acne (corticosteroid-induced acne or steroid folliculitis) is an acneiform eruption caused by systemic or topical corticosteroid use. It typically presents as sudden-onset, monomorphic inflammatory papules.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L70.8 (Other acne) or L70.4 (Infantile acne for neonatal cases)
- **Related:** Topical steroid-induced acne, iatrogenic acne

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **History of corticosteroid use:** Systemic (oral, IV, IM) or potent topical steroids
- **Sudden onset:** Appears days to weeks after steroid initiation
- **Monomorphic lesions:** Uniform papules and pustules (similar in size and stage)
- **Distribution:** Face, chest, back, upper arms — areas with high sebaceous gland density
- **Timing:** Onset typically 2–6 weeks after starting steroid systems; sooner with potent topical steroids
- **Resolution:** Clears after steroid discontinuation (may take weeks to months)

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mild** | Few scattered papules; minimal symptoms |
| **Moderate** | Numerous papules and pustules; may cover large areas |
| **Severe** | Confluent lesions; significant inflammation; possible scarring |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Androgenic effect of corticosteroids** | Strong | Glucocorticoids have weak androgenic activity; stimulate sebaceous glands |
| **Follicular inflammation** | Strong | Steroids induce neutrophilic infiltration of follicles |
| **Immunosuppression** | Moderate | Altered skin immune response may allow C. acnes proliferation |
| **Steroid withdrawal** | Strong | Can trigger rebound inflammatory response |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

- **Systemic corticosteroids:** Prednisone, methylprednisolone, dexamethasone (dose-dependent; > 10 mg prednisone equivalent more likely)
- **Topical corticosteroids:** Especially potent classes (I–III) used on face or occluded areas
- **Intralesional corticosteroids:** Can cause local atrophy and acneiform eruptions
- **Inhaled corticosteroids:** Less common but reported, especially around mouth/nose
- **Anabolic steroids:** Also cause acneiform eruptions (steroid acne)

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

- **All types:** PIH risk after resolution, especially in darker skin
- **Fitzpatrick V–VI:** Higher risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation; may take months to fade
- **Topical steroid misuse** in darker skin: Risk of hypopigmentation in addition to acne

### Treatment Pathways

#### Primary Approach

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **1. Discontinue steroid** | Work with prescribing physician to taper; do not abruptly stop steroid |
| **2. Topical acne treatment** | Adapalene, BPO, salicylic acid as tolerated |
| **3. Anti-inflammatory** | Doxycycline or minocycline if inflammatory component significant |
| **4. Taper support** | Address underlying condition with non-steroid alternatives when possible |

#### Specific Treatments

| Medication | Role |
|-----------|------|
| **Benzoyl Peroxide 2.5–5%** | Antimicrobial; reduces inflammation |
| **Adapalene 0.1%** | Normalizes keratinization |
| **Doxycycline 100 mg daily** | If inflammatory; short course |
| **Tretinoin** | If retinoid-tolerant; helps with post-steroid texture |
| **Azelaic acid** | Anti-inflammatory; helps PIH |

#### What NOT to Do

| Action | Why |
|--------|-----|
| **Continue topical steroids on face** | Perpetuates the problem |
| **Use steroid-antibiotic combinations** | Masking effect; worsens long-term |
| **Abrupt systemic steroid cessation** | Can cause adrenal crisis; must taper under medical supervision |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Adrenal suppression** from abrupt steroid withdrawal — medical emergency
- [ ] **Severe inflammatory response** on tapering
- [ ] **Signs of Cushing's syndrome** (moon face, striae, weight gain) with systemic use
- [ ] **Topical steroid addiction/withdrawal** (red skin syndrome) — especially on face

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Steroid acne is the same as regular acne" | No — it's monomorphic, sudden onset, and directly linked to steroid use |
| "You can treat it the same as bacterial acne" | Similar treatments work, but discontinuing the steroid is essential |
| "Topical steroids are safe for the face" | Potent topical steroids on the face commonly cause steroid acne, rosacea, and atrophy |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Steroid Acne:**

1. **Medication History:**
   - Ask about recent steroid use (prescription, OTC, supplements, inhalers)
   - Ask about topical steroid use on face
   - Flag anabolic steroid use

2. **Pattern Recognition:**
   - Sudden onset + monomorphic papules + steroid history = high probability
   - Distribution matching areas of topical steroid application

3. **Management:**
   - **Critical:** Advise discussing steroid taper/modification with prescribing physician
   - Recommend standard acne treatment for residual lesions
   - Emphasize never stopping systemic steroids without medical supervision

4. **Education:**
   - Explain steroid-induced nature to reduce anxiety
   - Set expectations: improvement follows steroid discontinuation (weeks to months)
   - Warn against "steroid skin lightening" products or compounded creams with unknown ingredients

### Pregnancy Considerations

- Systemic corticosteroids in pregnancy require obstetric oversight
- Treatment of steroid acne in pregnancy: same pregnancy-safe options as acne vulgaris
- Topical steroids: generally safe in pregnancy (classes VI–VII preferred for face)

# Inflammatory Conditions

## 6. Rosacea

### Clinical Definition

Rosacea is a chronic inflammatory skin condition primarily affecting the central face, characterized by recurrent flushing, persistent erythema, papules, pustules, telangiectasias, and phymatous changes. It is most common in fair-skinned individuals aged 30–50.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L71.9 (Rosacea, unspecified)
- **Subclassification codes:**
  - L71.0 — Perioral dermatitis
  - L71.1 — Rhinophyma
  - L71.8 — Other rosacea
  - L71.9 — Rosacea, unspecified

### Diagnostic Criteria (National Rosacea Society Expert Committee)

**Primary features** (one or more required for diagnosis):
1. **Flushing (transient erythema)**
2. **Persistent erythema**
3. **Papules and pustules**
4. **Telangiectasias**

**Secondary features:**
- Burning or stinging
- Plaque formation
- Dry appearance
- Edema
- Ocular manifestations
- Phymatous changes

### Subtypes (Phenotypes)

| Subtype | Features | Common Triggers |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| **Erythematotelangiectatic (ETR)** | Central facial erythema, flushing, telangiectasias; skin may be rough, scaly | Heat, sun, alcohol, spicy foods, emotional stress |
| **Papulopustular (PPR)** | Persistent erythema with papules, pustules; may resemble acne but no comedones | Similar to ETR; also skincare irritants |
| **Phymatous** | Skin thickening, irregular nodularities; rhinophyma (nose), gnathophyma (chin), metophyma (forehead), otophyma (ears) | Chronic inflammation; androgens may play role |
| **Ocular** | Blepharitis, conjunctivitis, dry eye, photophobia, recurrent chalazia; may precede skin signs | Not applicable |

### Grading Scale

**IGA (Investigator Global Assessment) for Rosacea:**

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Clear (0)** | No inflammatory lesions; no erythema |
| **Almost clear (1)** | Very few small papules/pustules; very mild erythema |
| **Mild (2)** | Few small papules/pustules; very mild erythema |
| **Moderate (3)** | Several small papues/pustules or few large; moderate erythema |
| **Severe (4)** | Many small and/or large papules/pustules; severe erythema |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Neurovascular dysregulation** | Strong | Abnormal vascular responsiveness; dysregulation of TRPV1 and TRPA1 channels |
| **Innate immune dysfunction** | Strong | Elevated cathelicidin (LL-37) and kallikrein 5 (KLK5); abnormal antimicrobial peptide processing |
| **Demodex folliculorum overgrowth** | Moderate | Higher mite density in rosacea skin; may trigger immune response or act as vector for bacteria |
| **Genetic predisposition** | Moderate | Family history; Northern European ancestry; Celtic origin |
| **Cutaneous microbiome alterations** | Emerging | Dysbiosis; role of *Bacillus oleronius* (associated with Demodex) |
| **UV damage** | Strong | Chronic sun exposure damages dermal matrix and vasculature |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Trigger | Evidence | Mechanism |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Sun exposure** | Strong | UV radiation directly damages skin; most common trigger |
| **Heat/hot beverages** | Strong | Thermally activates TRPV1 channels; vasodilation |
| **Alcohol** | Moderate | Vasodilation; red wine particularly triggering |
| **Spicy foods** | Moderate | Capsaicin activates TRPV1 |
| **Emotional stress** | Moderate | Neurovascular activation |
| **Exercise** | Moderate | Heat and flushing; not a reason to avoid exercise |
| **Skincare irritants** | Strong | Harsh cleansers, scrubs, alcohol-based products, fragrance |
| **Hot baths/saunas** | Moderate | Thermal triggering |
| **Cold weather/wind** | Moderate | Vascular reactivity |
| **Certain medications** | Moderate | Niacin, vasodilators, topical corticosteroids |
| **Hormonal changes** | Limited | Menopause; hormonal fluctuations |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Presentation | Challenges |
|-----------|--------------|------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–III** | Classic presentation; erythema and flushing highly visible; telangiectasias prominent | Easier to diagnose; photodamage often present |
| **Fitzpatrick IV–VI** | Erythema harder to detect; may present with papulopustular lesions without obvious background redness; PIH after papules | Underdiagnosed; "skin of color rosacea" often missed; requires careful examination for subtle erythema, warmth, history of flushing |

**Important:** Rosacea in skin of color is underdiagnosed and underreported. Look for:
- History of facial warmth/flushing
- Papules and pustules without comedones (to distinguish from acne)
- Dry, sensitive skin
- Ocular symptoms

### Treatment Pathways

#### Erythematotelangiectatic Rosacea (ETR)

| Treatment | Mechanism | Evidence |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Brimonidine 0.33% gel** | Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist; vasoconstricts superficial vessels | FDA-approved; onset 30 minutes; lasts 12 hours |
| **Oxymetazoline 1% cream** | Alpha-1 adrenergic agonist; vasoconstriction | FDA-approved; once daily |
| **Laser/IPL** | Targets hemoglobin; reduces erythema and telangiectasias | Pulsed dye laser (PDL), KTP, IPL; gold standard for vascular component |
| **Sunscreen (broad-spectrum)** | Prevents UV-triggered flushing and vascular damage | Essential; mineral sunscreens often better tolerated |
| **Barrier repair moisturizers** | Reduces irritation; improves tolerance to treatments | Ceramides, niacinamide, hyaluronic acid |

#### Papulopustular Rosacea (PPR)

| Treatment | Dose/Concentration | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| **Metronidazole cream/gel** | 0.75% | First-line topical; anti-inflammatory |
| **Azelaic acid gel** | 15% | FDA-approved; anti-inflammatory; also helps PIH |
| **Ivermectin cream** | 1% | Targets Demodex; FDA-approved; anti-inflammatory |
| **Sodium sulfacetamide 10% + sulfur 5%** | — | Alternative; antimicrobial |
| **Doxycycline 40 mg delayed-release** | 40 mg daily | Sub-antimicrobial dose; anti-inflammatory; FDA-approved |
| **Minocycline 1.5% foam** | Once daily; approved for PPR |
| **Clindamycin 1% gel** | — | Less preferred; resistance concerns |
| **Oral isotretinoin** | Low dose (10–20 mg) | Severe, refractory cases; off-label |

#### Phymatous Rosacea

| Treatment | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| **Isotretinoin** | May halt progression; limited efficacy for established phymatous changes |
| **Surgical intervention** | CO2 laser, scalpel sculpting, dermabrasion, electrosurgery for rhinophyma |
| **Oral anti-androgens** | Limited data; 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride) |

#### Ocular Rosacea

| Treatment | Application |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Lid hygiene** | Warm compresses, lid scrubs |
| **Artificial tears** | For dry eye |
| **Cyclosporine 0.05% ophthalmic emulsion** | Anti-inflammatory |
| **Oral doxycycline/metronidazole** | For more severe cases |
| **Ophthalmology referral** | For corneal involvement, vision changes |

#### General Skincare for Rosacea

| Principle | Recommendations |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Cleansing** | Gentle, non-foaming, pH-balanced; no scrubs or brushes |
| **Moisturizing** | Fragrance-free; barrier-supporting ingredients |
| **Sun protection** | Broad-spectrum SPF 30+; mineral (zinc oxide, titanium dioxide) often better tolerated |
| **Avoid** | Alcohol, witch hazel, fragrance, menthol, peppermint, eucalyptus, eucalyptus, harsh exfoliants |
| **Patch test** | All new products |

#### Lifestyle & Trigger Management

| Trigger | Management Strategy |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Sun** | Daily SPF; seek shade; wide-brimmed hats |
| **Heat** | Room temperature beverages; avoid hot showers; cool environments |
| **Alcohol** | Limit; especially red wine; consider avoidance |
| **Spicy foods** | Identify personal triggers; reduce capsaicin intake |
| **Stress** | Mindfulness, CBT, adequate sleep |
| **Exercise** | Cool down gradually; swim (cooler); avoid overheating |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Ocular involvement** — requires ophthalmology referral; risk of corneal damage
- [ ] **Rhinophyma** — may require surgical intervention
- [ ] **Severe refractory disease** — consider underlying causes
- [ ] **Signs of lupus** (malar rash, photosensitivity, joint pain) — rosacea can mimic lupus; differential diagnosis needed
- [ ] **Granulomatous rosacea** — firm yellow-brown papules; biopsy may be needed
- [ ] **Steroid-induced rosacea** — from topical steroid misuse; requires withdrawal

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Rosacea is just adult acne" | Different disease; no comedones; different pathophysiology |
| "It's caused by alcoholism" | Alcohol is a trigger, not a cause; many rosacea patients don't drink |
| "It will go away on its own" | Chronic condition; requires ongoing management |
| "You can't exercise with rosacea" | Exercise is fine; manage heat exposure and cool down properly |
| "Only fair-skinned people get it" | More common in fair skin but occurs in all skin types; underdiagnosed in skin of color |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Rosacea:**

1. **Diagnostic Screening:**
   - Ask: Do you flush? Is the redness persistent? Any eye symptoms?
   - Ask about triggers: heat, sun, alcohol, spicy foods, stress
   - Photo analysis: look for central facial distribution, absence of comedones
   - Determine Fitzpatrick type for detection sensitivity

2. **Subtype Stratification:**
   - **ETR:** Emphasize trigger avoidance, brimonidine/oxymetazoline, laser referral
   - **PPR:** Topical metronidazole/azelaic acid/ivermectin; consider doxycycline 40 mg
   - **Phymatous:** Dermatologist referral; discuss surgical options
   - **Ocular:** Ophthalmology referral; lid hygiene education

3. **Trigger Management:**
   - Personalized trigger diary with tracking
   - Weather-based alerts (high UV, heat)
   - Lifestyle modification coaching

4. **Skincare Guidance:**
   - Sensitive-skin product recommendations only
   - Flag common irritants in user-submitted products
   - Emphasize barrier repair

5. **Safety Checks:**
   - Never recommend topical corticosteroids for rosacea
   - Screen for ocular symptoms; automatic ophthalmology referral flag
   - Pregnancy considerations for doxycycline and isotretinoin

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Azelaic acid 15%, metronidazole, gentle skincare | Brimonidine (limited data; potential systemic absorption), retinoids |
| **Systemic** | — | Doxycycline (tetracycline), isotretinoin (absolute contraindication), minocycline |
| **Procedures** | — | Most lasers (limited safety data); defer elective procedures |

**Note:** Pregnancy can worsen rosacea due to hormonal changes; emphasize trigger management and safe topicals.

## 7. Atopic Dermatitis / Eczema

### Clinical Definition

Atopic dermatitis (AD) is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin disease characterized by pruritus, eczematous lesions, xerosis (dry skin), and a dysfunctional skin barrier. It is the most common inflammatory skin disease, affecting up to 20% of children and 10% of adults.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L20.9 (Atopic dermatitis, unspecified)
- **Subclassification codes:**
  - L20.0 — Besnier's prurigo
  - L20.8 — Other atopic dermatitis
  - L20.9 — Atopic dermatitis, unspecified
  - L20.81 — Atopic neurodermatitis
  - L20.82 — Flexural eczema
  - L20.83 — Infantile (acute) eczema
  - L20.84 — Intrinsic (allergic) eczema
  - L20.85 — Extrinsic (allergic) eczema
  - L20.89 — Other atopic dermatitis

### Diagnostic Criteria (Hanifin-Rajka / UK Working Party)

**Hanifin-Rajka Criteria (must have 3+ major + 3+ minor):**

**Major features:**
- Pruritus
- Typical morphology and distribution (flexural lichenification in adults; facial/extensor in infants)
- Chronic or relapsing course
- Personal or family history of atopy (asthma, hay fever, AD)

**Minor features (selected):**
- Xerosis
- Ichthyosis/palmar hyperlinearity/keratosis pilaris
- Immediate skin test reactivity
- Elevated serum IgE
- Early age of onset
- Tendency toward cutaneous infections
- Dennie-Morgan infraorbital folds
- Perioral pallor
- Facial erythema/sweating

### Grading Scale

| Grade | SCORAD (Severity Scoring of Atopic Dermatitis) | Description |
|-------|-----------------------------------------------|-------------|
| **Mild** | < 25 | Limited areas; occasional itching; minimal impact on sleep/activities |
| **Moderate** | 25–50 | Multiple areas; frequent itching; some sleep disturbance |
| **Severe** | > 50 | Widespread; constant itching; significant sleep loss and functional impairment |

**EASI (Eczema Area and Severity Index):** Used in clinical trials; scores 0–72.

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Filaggrin (FLG) gene mutations** | Strong | Loss-of-function mutations in FLG are the strongest genetic risk factor; impair barrier function |
| **Skin barrier dysfunction** | Strong | Increased transeptic water loss (TEWL); reduced ceramides; altered lipid matrix |
| **Th2 immune skewing** | Strong | IL-4, IL-5, IL-13 drive inflammation; elevated IgE; eosinophilia |
| **Type 2 inflammation** | Strong | IL-4 and IL-13 central to pathophysiology; targeted by biologics |
| **Microbiome dysbiosis** | Strong | *Staphylococcus aureus* colonization (> 90% of lesional skin); reduced diversity |
| **Environmental factors** | Moderate | Hard water, urban environment, climate, pollutants |
| **Itch-scratch cycle** | Strong | Mechanical irritation perpetuates inflammation |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Trigger | Evidence | Management |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **Irritants** | Strong | Soaps, detergents, fragrances, wool, chemicals |
| **Allergens** | Moderate | Dust mites, pollen, pet dander, mold (role varies by individual) |
| **Food allergies** | Moderate (children) | Eggs, milk, wheat, soy, peanuts, tree nuts, fish, shellfish; role less clear in adults |
| **Heat/sweating** | Strong | Overheating triggers itching |
| **Stress** | Moderate | Psychological stress exacerbates AD via neuroimmune pathways |
| **Infections** | Strong | *S. aureus*, herpes simplex (eczema herpeticum), molluscum |
| **Hormonal changes** | Limited | Pregnancy, menstrual cycle |
| **Climate** | Moderate | Winter (low humidity), extreme temperatures |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Presentation | Considerations |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–III** | Erythema prominent; classic "red, inflamed" appearance; post-inflammatory erythema | Easier to assess severity visually |
| **Fitzpatrick IV–VI** | Erythema may be subtle or violaceous; lichenification and scaling more prominent; follicular pattern may dominate; perifollicular accentuation | Higher risk of misdiagnosis; PHH is major concern; may need to assess warmth and induration rather than color alone |

**Atopic dermatitis in skin of color:**
- More likely to present with follicular prominence
- Can have "dirty neck" appearance (accentuated skin lines with post-inflammatory pigment)
- PHH can be more distressing than the eczema itself
- Lichenified plaques may appear gray or violaceous rather than red

### Treatment Pathways

#### Foundational Skincare (All Severities)

| Component | Recommendation | Rationale |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Bathing** | Lukewarm water; 5–10 minutes; gentle, fragrance-free cleanser | Removes irritants; hydrates skin |
| **Moisturizing** | Apply within 3 minutes of bathing; liberal amounts; at least twice daily | Restores barrier; reduces TEWL; prevents flares |
| **Emollient selection** | Ointments (petrolatum) for severe; creams for moderate; lotions for mild | Higher lipid content = better barrier repair |
| **Wet wraps** | For moderate-severe flares; apply moisturizer + topical steroid, then wet gauze + dry layer | Increases steroid efficacy; enhances hydration |

#### OTC & Barrier Repair

| Ingredient/Approach | Notes |
|--------------------|-------|
| **Petrolatum (Vaseline®)** | Most effective occlusive; restores barrier |
| **Ceramide-containing moisturizers** | Physiologic lipid replacement; improves barrier function |
| **Colloidal oatmeal** | Anti-inflammatory; soothes itching; FDA-approved skin protectant |
| **Glycerin, hyaralonic acid** | Humectants; draw moisture into skin |
| **Niacinamide** | Anti-inflammatory; barrier support; reduces TEWL |
| **Anti-itch lotions** | Pramoxine, menthol, camphor (limited efficacy) |

#### Prescription (Topical)

| Medication | Class | Indication | Notes |
|-----------|-------|------------|-------|
| **Hydrocortisone 1–2.5%** | Low-potency corticosteroid | Face, skin folds, infants; maintenance | Safest for thin skin areas |
| **Triamcinolone 0.025–0.1%** | Medium-potency corticosteroid | Body, trunk, extremities | First-line for most body flares |
| **Betamethasone dipropionate 0.05%** | High-potency corticosteroid | Thick plaques on palms/soles | Limited duration; avoid face |
| **Clobetasol propionate 0.05%** | Super-high-potency | Severe, lichenified plaques | Short-term only; highest potency |
| **Tacrolimus 0.03–0.1% ointment** | Topical calcineurin inhibitor | Face, skin folds, maintenance; steroid-sparing | Black box warning (theoretical cancer risk); stinging on application |
| **Pimecrolimus 1% cream** | Topical calcineurin inhibitor | Mild-moderate; face; maintenance | Similar to tacrolimus; less potent |
| **Crisaborole 2% ointment** | PDE4 inhibitor | Mild-moderate AD; all ages | Non-steroidal; stinging common |
| **Ruxolitinib 1.5% cream** | JAK inhibitor | Short-term for mild-moderate | Newest topical; rapid improvement |
| **Topical antibiotics** | Mupirocin, retapamulin | Secondary *S. aureus* infection | Short course only |

#### Prescription (Systemic)

| Medication | Class | Indication | Monitoring |
|-----------|-------|------------|------------|
| **Dupilumab (Dupixent®)** | IL-4Rα antagonist (biologic) | Moderate-severe AD; first-line biologic | Conjunctivitis (common); avoid live vaccines |
| **Tralokinumab (Adbry®)** | IL-13 antagonist (biologic) | Moderate-severe AD | Similar to dupilumab; less conjunctivitis |
| **Lebrikizumab** | IL-13 antagonist | Moderate-severe AD | Emerging |
| **Nemolizumab** | IL-31RA antagonist | Moderate-severe AD | Targets itch specifically |
| **Upadacitinib (Rinvoq®)** | JAK1 inhibitor (oral) | Severe AD; refractory to biologics | Lipids, CBC, LFTs; thrombosis risk; shingles vaccination |
| **Abrocitinib (Cibinqo®)** | JAK1 inhibitor (oral) | Severe AD | Similar to upadacitinib |
| **Cyclosporine** | Calcineurin inhibitor | Short-term for severe flares | BP, renal function, lipids; nephrotoxicity |
| **Methotrexate** | Antimetabolite | Severe, refractory AD | LFTs, CBC; folate supplementation |
| **Azathioprine** | Antimetabolite | Severe AD | TPMT testing required; myelosuppression |
| **Oral corticosteroids** | Systemic steroid | Short-term bridge only | NEVER for maintenance; severe rebound |
| **Antihistamines** | H1 antagonists | Adjunct for sleep (sedating) | Limited efficacy for AD itch specifically |

#### Professional Procedures

- **Phototherapy:** NB-UVB, UVA1 — for widespread AD; steroid-sparing
- **Diluted bleach baths:** 0.005% sodium hypochlorite (½ cup household bleach in full tub); reduces *S. aureus* colonization
- **Wet wrap therapy:** Intensive hydration + topical treatment
- **Allergy testing:** Patch testing for contact dermatitis; specific IgE or skin prick for food/environmental allergies in select cases

#### Lifestyle & Adjunctive

| Intervention | Evidence | Notes |
|-------------|----------|-------|
| **Bleach baths** | Moderate | 2x weekly for colonized patients; reduces *S. aureus* |
| **Probiotics** | Mixed | Some evidence for Lactobacillus in prevention; less clear for treatment |
| **Vitamin D supplementation** | Moderate | Many AD patients are deficient; supplementation may help |
| **Stress reduction** | Moderate | CBT, mindfulness, sleep hygiene |
| **Elimination diets** | Variable | Only if true food allergy documented; avoid empiric elimination |
| **Hydrocolloid bandages** | Limited | For weeping lesions; protects and hydrates |

### Red Flags — Urgent Referral

- [ ] **Eczema herpeticum:** Painful, monomorphic punched-out erosions; fever; malaise — EMERGENCY; requires IV acyclovir
- [ ] **Widespread bacterial infection:** Increasing pain, warmth, pus, crusting, fever — oral antibiotics needed
- [ ] **Severe disease not responding to topical therapy + phototherapy**
- [ ] **Significant impact on quality of life:** Sleep deprivation, school/work absence, depression
- [ ] **Growth failure in children** from severe disease or inappropriate steroid use
- [ ] **Cataracts** with prolonged systemic/topical steroid use near eyes
- [ ] **Signs of topical steroid withdrawal** — rebound redness, burning on previously treated skin

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Eczema is contagious" | Not contagious; not an infection |
| "It's caused by poor hygiene" | Over-washing actually worsens it; barrier dysfunction is the issue |
| "You can cure eczema with diet" | Food allergies trigger some cases (especially children), but most AD is not diet-responsive |
| "Topical steroids are dangerous and should be avoided" | When used appropriately, they are safe and essential; "steroid phobia" leads to under-treatment |
| "Natural/organic products are always better" | Many botanical ingredients are irritants and allergens; fragrance-free, hypoallergenic is key |
| "Children outgrow all eczema" | 60% outgrow by adolescence; 40% persist into adulthood |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Atopic Dermatitis:**

1. **Severity Assessment:**
   - Body surface area involvement
   - Itch severity (numerical rating scale)
   - Sleep disturbance
   - Quality of life impact
   - Signs of infection

2. **Treatment Ladder:**
   - **Mild:** Intensive emollients + low-potency steroid or crisaborole for flares
   - **Moderate:** Medium-potency steroid + calcineurin inhibitor (maintenance) + wet wraps for flares
   - **Severe:** Dermatologist referral; biologic (dupilumab) or JAK inhibitor consideration; phototherapy

3. **Barrier Repair Emphasis:**
   - Daily moisturizing regimen education
   - Product recommendations with ceramides, petrolatum, colloidal oatmeal
   - Bathing technique guidance

4. **Infection Monitoring:**
   - Flag signs of secondary infection (*S. aureus*)
   - Educate on eczema herpeticum symptoms (emergency)
   - Bleach bath recommendation for recurrent infection

5. **Trigger Management:**
   - Personalized trigger tracking
   - Weather-based recommendations (humidity, temperature)
   - Stress management resources

6. **Safety:**
   - Steroid potency guidance by body area
   - Duration limits for topical steroids
   - TCI (tacrolimus/pimecrolimus) as steroid-sparing for face/folds
   - Pregnancy considerations for systemic agents

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Caution/Avoid |
|----------|------|---------------|
| **Topical** | Low-potency hydrocortisone (short-term), emollients, crisaborole (limited data) | High-potency steroids (especially large area/long term), tacrolimus/pimecrolimus (theoretical risk, limited data) |
| **Systemic** | — | Most systemic agents limited; cyclosporine may be used in severe cases with close monitoring |
| **Biologics** | Dupilumab appears safe in pregnancy (limited data) | Discuss risk/benefit with specialist |
| **Phototherapy** | Generally considered safe | — |

## 8. Contact Dermatitis

### Clinical Definition

Contact dermatitis is an inflammatory skin condition resulting from direct contact with an irritant or allergen. It is divided into two main types: irritant contact dermatitis (ICD, 80% of cases) and allergic contact dermatitis (ACD, 20% of cases).

- **ICD-10-CM Codes:**
  - L24.9 — Irritant contact dermatitis, unspecified cause
  - L25.9 — Unspecified contact dermatitis
  - L23.9 — Allergic contact dermatitis, unspecified cause
  - Specific codes by causative agent (L24.0–L24.9, L23.0–L23.9)

### Diagnostic Criteria

**Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD):**
- Direct chemical/physical injury to skin
- Anyone exposed to sufficient concentration will react
- Immediate or delayed (hours) onset
- Well-demarcated erythema, scaling, fissuring, burning > itching
- Confined to site of contact
- History of exposure to known irritant

**Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD):**
- Type IV delayed hypersensitivity (T-cell mediated)
- Requires prior sensitization
- Intense pruritus
- Erythema, edema, vesicles, weeping
- May spread beyond contact site
- Patch testing required for definitive diagnosis

### Grading Scale

| Grade | ICD Presentation | ACD Presentation |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Mild** | Dryness, mild erythema, tightness | Mild erythema, slight itching |
| **Moderate** | Erythema, scaling, fissures, burning | Erythema, edema, vesicles, intense pruritus |
| **Severe** | Bullae, ulceration, necrosis (chemical burns) | Widespread vesiculation, edema, secondary infection |

### Root Causes

**Irritant Contact Dermatitis:**

| Irritant Category | Examples |
|-------------------|----------|
| **Soaps/detergents** | Sodium lauryl sulfate, hand soaps, dishwashing liquid |
| **Solvents** | Alcohols, acetone, gasoline |
| **Oils/cutting fluids** | Machine oils, petroleum distillates |
| **Acids/alkalis** | Hydrochloric acid, sodium hydroxide |
| **Physical irritants** | Friction, heat, cold, UV radiation |
| **Water** | Prolonged wet work (wet hands) |

**Allergic Contact Dermatitis (Common Allergens):**

| Allergen | Source | Prevalence |
|----------|--------|------------|
| **Nickel** | Jewelry, belt buckles, cell phones, eyeglasses | Most common; 10–20% sensitive |
| **Fragrance mix** | Perfumes, cosmetics, personal care | 1–3% |
| **Balsam of Peru** | Fragrance, flavoring, topical medications | 1–2% |
| **Formaldehyde/releasers** | Preservatives in cosmetics, fabrics, cleaning products | 1–2% |
| **Cobalt** | Metal alloys, dyes, cement | 1–2% |
| **Potassium dichromate** | Leather, cement, matches | 1–2% |
| **Thiurams** | Rubber, pesticides | 1% |
| **Neomycin/bacitracin** | Topical antibiotics | 1% |
| **MCI/MI** | Preservatives (Kathon CG) | Increasing; 1–3% |
| **PPD (p-phenylenediamine)** | Hair dye, temporary tattoos, textiles | 1% |
| **Cocamidopropyl betaine** | Surfactant in shampoos, body washes | 1% |
| **Lanolin/wool alcohol** | Emollients, cosmetics | 1–3% |
| **Methylisothiazolinone (MI)** | Preservative | 1–2% |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

- **Occupational exposure:** Healthcare workers (hand irritants), hairdressers (dyes), construction workers (cement, chromate), cleaners, food handlers
- **Atopic dermatitis:** Damaged barrier increases risk of both ICD and ACD
- **Frequent hand washing:** COVID-19 pandemic increased ICD incidence dramatically
- **Product accumulation:** Multiple products with same allergen
- **Skin trauma:** Damaged skin more susceptible
- **Age:** Children and elderly have more permeable barriers

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

- **All types:** Acute phase shows erythema in all skin types; chronic phase shows lichenification
- **Fitzpatrick V–VI:** Erythema may be subtle; look for warmth, edema, scale, and distribution pattern; PIH is common after resolution
- **Hair dye reactions (PPD):** More common and severe in darker skin tones due to higher use of chemical relaxers and dyes
- **Post-inflammatory pigment changes:** Often the primary concern after acute dermatitis resolves in darker skin

### Treatment Pathways

#### Acute Management

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **1. Identify and remove trigger** | Discontinue suspected product; review all personal care, occupational exposures |
| **2. Gentle cleansing** | Lukewarm water; mild, fragrance-free cleanser |
| **3. Barrier repair** | Petrolatum or ceramide moisturizer liberally and frequently |
| **4. Wet compresses** | For weeping/vesicular lesions; Burrow's solution (aluminum acetate) |
| **5. Topical corticosteroid** | Medium to high potency for body; low potency for face |

#### Medications

| Medication | Indication | Duration |
|-----------|------------|----------|
| **Hydrocortisone 2.5%** | Face, skin folds, mild ICD | 1–2 weeks |
| **Triamcinolone 0.1%** | Body, moderate severity | 2–3 weeks |
| **Betamethasone dipropionate 0.05%** | Severe, lichenified, palms/soles | 1–2 weeks |
| **Tacrolimus 0.1% / Pimecrolimus 1%** | Face, maintenance, steroid-sparing | Long-term |
| **Oral corticosteroids** | Severe, widespread ACD (e.g., poison ivy) | Short taper (2–3 weeks); never abrupt stop |
| **Oral antihistamines** | Pruritus (ACD); sedating for sleep | As needed |

#### Patch Testing (for ACD)

- **Indication:** Chronic or recurrent dermatitis; occupational exposures; treatment failure
- **Method:** Standard series (80 allergens) + extended series based on exposure history
- **Timing:** Apply patches Monday, read Wednesday and Friday (48 and 96 hours)
- **Interpretation:** Erythema, papules, vesicles at patch site = positive
- **Referral:** Dermatologist or allergist with patch testing expertise

#### Occupational/Prevention

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Protective equipment** | Gloves (nitrile for most chemicals; avoid latex if allergic); barrier creams |
| **Gloves selection** | Match glove material to chemical; change frequently |
| **Moisturizing** | Apply after washing; "pre-work" creams for barrier protection |
| **Product review** | Read ingredient lists; avoid known allergens |
| **Nickel avoidance** | Jewelry labeled "nickel-free"; apply clear nail polish to metal contacts |
| **Fragrance-free** | Choose fragrance-free products |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Severe widespread reaction** — requires oral steroids
- [ ] **Involvement of eyes, mouth, genitals** — urgent care
- [ ] **Signs of infection** — increasing pain, warmth, purulent drainage
- [ ] **Occupational contact dermatitis** — may require workplace modifications; workers' compensation
- [ ] **Systemic contact dermatitis** — from ingested allergen (e.g., balsam of Peru → cola, citrus, tomatoes)
- [ ] **Photoallergic contact dermatitis** — reaction only in sun-exposed areas; requires photo-patch testing

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Natural products can't cause allergies" | Many natural ingredients are potent allergens (lanolin, essential oils, fragrance) |
| "If I didn't react before, I'm not allergic" | Sensitization requires initial exposure; reaction may develop after years of use |
| "Hypoallergenic means no allergy risk" | Unregulated term; can still contain allergens |
| "Irritant and allergic are the same" | Different mechanisms; ACD requires patch testing for diagnosis |
| "Patch testing is only for severe cases" | Should be considered for any chronic/recurrent contact dermatitis |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Contact Dermatitis:**

1. **Pattern Recognition:**
   - Ask about new products (skincare, hair care, cosmetics, detergents, occupational exposures)
   - Distribution analysis: hands (occupational), eyelids (cosmetics/nail products), face (skincare), neck (fragrance), earlobes (jewelry)
   - Timing: when did it start? New product introduction?

2. **ICD vs. ACD Differentiation:**
   - ICD: Anyone can get it; burning > itching; immediate
   - ACD: Requires prior exposure; intense itching; delayed 24–72 hours; may spread

3. **Initial Management:**
   - Stop all new/suspected products
   - Gentle skincare routine
   - OTC hydrocortisone for mild cases
   - Flag for patch testing if chronic/recurrent

4. **Product Safety Database:**
   - Flag common allergens in user-submitted products
   - Cross-reference known allergies with product ingredients
   - "Safe for sensitive skin" product recommendations

5. **Referral Triggers:**
   - Severe or widespread reactions
   - Occupational exposure
   - Failure to improve after removing suspected trigger
   - Need for patch testing

### Pregnancy Considerations

- Topical corticosteroids: low to medium potency generally safe; limit high potency
- Patch testing: generally safe in pregnancy; some allergens may be deferred
- Oral corticosteroids: use only if absolutely necessary; lowest effective dose

## 9. Seborrheic Dermatitis

### Clinical Definition

Seborrheic dermatitis is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin condition affecting sebum-rich areas of the body. It is characterized by erythema and greasy scaling, most commonly on the scalp, face (eyebrows, nasolabial folds), and chest.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L21.9 (Seborrheic dermatitis, unspecified)
- **Subclassification codes:**
  - L21.0 — Seborrhea capitis (cradle cap)
  - L21.1 — Seborrheic infantile dermatitis
  - L21.8 — Other seborrheic dermatitis
  - L21.9 — Seborrheic dermatitis, unspecified

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Distribution:** Scalp, eyebrows, glabella, nasolabial folds, ears (external auditory canal, postauricular), presternal chest, intertriginous areas
- **Morphology:** Erythematous patches/plaques with greasy, yellowish scale
- **Symptoms:** Mild pruritus; can be asymptomatic
- **Dandruff (pityriasis capitis):** Mild form limited to scalp with scaling only
- **Infantile form:** "Cradle cap" — thick, yellow, greasy scales on scalp; usually resolves by 12 months

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mild** | Dandruff only; minimal erythema; no significant symptoms |
| **Moderate** | Visible erythema and scaling; mild pruritus; social concern |
| **Severe** | Thick, adherent scale; significant erythema; pruritus; secondary infection; widespread |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Malassezia yeast overgrowth** | Strong | Lipophilic yeast (*M. restricta*, *M. globosa*) proliferates in sebum-rich environment |
| **Immune response to Malassezia** | Strong | Host inflammatory reaction to yeast metabolites (oleic acid) |
| **Sebum composition** | Moderate | Altered sebum lipids may promote yeast growth |
| **Genetic factors** | Moderate | Family history; HLA associations |
| **Neurogenic factors** | Moderate | Parkinson's disease, stroke, spinal injury increase severity |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Factor | Evidence | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **HIV/AIDS** | Strong | Prevalence up to 80–90% in advanced HIV; often severe and refractory |
| **Parkinson's disease** | Strong | Increased sebum production; immobility |
| **Immunosuppression** | Strong | Organ transplant recipients; chemotherapy |
| **Stress** | Moderate | Can exacerbate |
| **Seasonal variation** | Moderate | Often worse in winter |
| **Infancy** | Strong | "Cradle cap" due to maternal androgens stimulating infant sebaceous glands |
| **Obesity** | Limited | May contribute via skin folds and metabolic factors |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

- **All types:** Scale is typically yellow/white; erythema may be less visible in darker skin
- **Fitzpatrick V–VI:** May present with hypopigmentation or hyperpigmentation rather than obvious erythema; scale may be more prominent than color change
- **Scalp involvement in darker skin:** May be associated with central centrifugal cicatricial alopeca (CCCA) — important to assess for hair loss
- **Post-inflammatory changes** common after flares in darker skin

### Treatment Pathways

#### Scalp (Dandruff to Severe SD)

| Treatment | Application | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **Zinc pyrithione shampoo** | 2–3x weekly | OTC; antifungal and antimicrobial |
| **Selenium sulfide shampoo** | 2x weekly | OTC (1%) or Rx (2.5%); antifungal; may discolor hair |
| **Ketoconazole shampoo** | 2x weekly | OTC (1%) or Rx (2%); antifungal; first-line |
| **Coal tar shampoo** | 2–3x weekly | OTC; reduces scaling and inflammation; odor; photosensitivity |
| **Salicylic acid shampoo** | 2–3x weekly | Keratolytic; removes scale |
| **Ciclopirox shampoo** | 2x weekly | Rx; broad-spectrum antifungal |
| **Fluocinolone acetonide 0.01% shampoo** | Daily then taper | Rx; steroid shampoo for inflammation |
| **Betamethasone valerate 0.12% foam** | Apply to scalp | Rx; steroid foam for severe inflammation |

**Application technique:** Leave shampoo on scalp 3–5 minutes before rinsing for optimal efficacy.

#### Face and Body

| Treatment | Application | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **Ketoconazole cream 2%** | BID | First-line topical antifungal |
| **Ciclopirox cream 0.77%** | BID | Alternative antifungal |
| **Hydrocortisone 1–2.5%** | BID for flares | Low-potency steroid; short term |
| **Desonide 0.05%** | BID | Low-potency steroid for face |
| **Tacrolimus 0.1% / Pimecrolimus 1%** | BID | Steroid-sparing; good for maintenance |
| **Metronidazole 0.75% gel** | BID | Anti-inflammatory |
| **Lithium succinate 8% ointment** | BID | Alternative; anti-inflammatory and antifungal |

#### Severe or Recalcitrant

| Treatment | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| **Oral itraconazole** | 200 mg daily for 7 days; for severe or widespread |
| **Oral fluconazole** | 150–300 mg weekly; alternative |
| **Phototherapy** | NB-UVB for refractory cases |
| **Isotretinoin** | Very low dose (5–10 mg); reduces sebum; off-label |

#### Infantile (Cradle Cap)

| Treatment | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| **Mineral oil/olive oil** | Apply to scalp, let sit 15 minutes, gently comb out scales |
| **Gentle baby shampoo** | Daily washing |
| **Ketoconazole 2% shampoo** | For persistent cases; safe in infants > 2 months |
| **Low-potency hydrocortisone** | Short course for inflamed areas |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Severe, refractory disease** — consider HIV testing if risk factors present
- [ ] **Secondary infection** — bacterial or fungal superinfection
- [ ] **Psoriasis overlap** — sebopsoriasis; may need different treatment approach
- [ ] **Tinea capitis** — must rule out in children with scalp scaling and hair loss
- [ ] **Langerhans cell histiocytosis** — severe, refractory infantile seborrheic dermatitis with systemic symptoms
- [ ] **Erythroderma** — widespread involvement; systemic symptoms

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "It's caused by poor hygiene" | Not related to cleanliness; yeast overgrowth is the driver |
| "It's contagious" | Not contagious |
| "It can be cured" | Chronic, relapsing condition; manageable but not curable |
| "Dandruff shampoo damages hair" | Modern formulations are safe; leaving on scalp (not hair) for 3–5 minutes is key |
| "Cradle cap is dangerous" | Benign; usually resolves spontaneously; treat for comfort |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Seborrheic Dermatitis:**

1. **Distribution Analysis:**
   - Scalp: Dandruff, itching, scaling
   - Face: Eyebrows, nasolabial folds, glabella
   - Body: Chest, intertriginous areas
   - Flag HIV risk if severe and refractory

2. **Severity Stratification:**
   - **Mild (dandruff):** OTC antifungal shampoo (ketoconazole, selenium sulfide, zinc pyrithione)
   - **Moderate:** Prescription ketoconazole shampoo + ketoconazole cream for face; low-potency steroid for flares
   - **Severe:** Oral antifungal referral; consider underlying conditions

3. **Maintenance Plan:**
   - Weekly antifungal shampoo for prevention
   - Steroid-sparing maintenance (tacrolimus/pimecrolimus)
   - Seasonal awareness (winter flares)

4. **Product Recommendations:**
   - Avoid heavy, oily products on affected areas
   - Non-comedogenic, non-greazy moisturizers
   - Gentle cleansers

5. **Safety Checks:**
   - Limit topical steroid use on face
   - Screen for immunosuppression if severe
   - Flag for HIV testing if severe + risk factors

### Pregnancy Considerations

- Topical antifungals (ketoconazole, ciclopirox): generally considered safe
- Selenium sulfide and coal tar: limited systemic absorption; use with caution
- Oral antifungals: avoid in pregnancy
- Low-potency hydrocortisone: safe for short-term use

## 10. Psoriasis

### Clinical Definition

Psoriasis is a chronic, immune-mediated inflammatory disease characterized by well-demarcated erythematous plaques with silvery scale. It affects approximately 2–3% of the global population and is associated with significant comorbidities.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L40.9 (Psoriasis, unspecified)
- **Subclassification codes:**
  - L40.0 — Psoriasis vulgaris
  - L40.1 — Generalized pustular psoriasis
  - L40.2 — Acrodermatitis continua of Hallopeau
  - L40.3 — Pustulosis palmaris et palmaris
  - L40.4 — Guttate psoriasis
  - L40.5 — Arthropathic psoriasis
  - L40.8 — Other psoriasis
  - L40.9 — Psoriasis, unspecified
  - L40.50 — Psoriatic arthropathy, unspecified
  - L40.51 — Distal interphalangeal psoriatic arthropathy
  - L40.52 — Psoriatic arthritis mutilans
  - L40.53 — Psoriatic spondylitis
  - L40.54 — Psoriatic juvenile arthropathy
  - L40.59 — Other psoriatic arthropathy

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Well-demarcated erythematous plaques** with silvery-white scale
- **Auspitz sign:** Pinpoint bleeding when scale is removed
- **Koebner phenomenon:** New lesions at sites of trauma
- **Symmetrical distribution:** Extensor surfaces (elbows, knees), scalp, lumbosacral area
- **Nail changes:** Pitting, onycholysis, oil spots, subungual hyperkeratosis
- **Pruritus:** Variable; can be severe

### Clinical Subtypes

| Subtype | Features | Distribution |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **Plaque (Vulgaris)** | > 80% of cases; well-demarcated red plaques with silvery scale | Extensors, scalp, trunk, lumbosacral |
| **Guttate** | Sudden onset; "drop-like" small papules with scale; often post-streptococcal | Trunk, proximal extremities |
| **Inverse (Intertrigonous)** | Smooth, red, non-scaly plaques in folds | Axillae, groin, inframammary, inframmary, gluteal cleft |
| **Pustular** | Sterile pustules on erythematous base; localized (palms/soles) or generalized | Localized or generalized; can be life-threatening |
| **Erythrodermic** | Generalized erythema and scaling covering > 90% BSA | Universal; dermatologic emergency |
| **Nail psoriasis** | Pitting, oil spots, onycholysis, subungual hyperkeratosis | Fingernails and toenails |
| **Psoriatic arthritis** | Joint inflammation; dactylitis, enthesitis, axial involvement | Asymmetric oligoarthritis pattern |

### Grading Scale

**PASI (Psoriasis Area and Severity Index):**

| Score | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| 0 | Clear |
| 1–10 | Mild |
| 10–20 | Moderate |
| > 20 | Severe |

**BSA (Body Surface Area):**
- One palm (including fingers) = approximately 1% BSA
- < 3% BSA = mild
- 3–10% BSA = moderate
- > 10% BSA = severe

**DLQI (Dermatology Life Quality Index):** Assesses impact on quality of life; scores 0–30.

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Genetic predisposition** | Strong | 30% family history; PSORS1 locus (HLA-Cw6) major susceptibility locus; polygenic |
| **Immune dysregulation** | Strong | Th1/Th17/IL-23 pathway central; TNF-α, IL-17, IL-23 key cytokines |
| **Environmental triggers** | Strong | Infection (strep throat for guttate), trauma (Koebner), stress, medications |
| **Skin barrier dysfunction** | Moderate | Keratinocyte hyperproliferation (turnover 3–5 days vs. 28 days normal) |
| **Autoinflammation** | Emerging | NLRP3 inflammasome; innate immune activation |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Trigger | Evidence | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Stress** | Strong | Psychological and physical stress can initiate and exacerbate flares |
| **Streptococcal infection** | Strong | Gutate psoriasis commonly follows strep throat (1–3 weeks) |
| **Skin trauma** | Strong | Koebner phenomenon — new lesions at injury sites |
| **Medications** | Strong | Beta-blockers, lithium, antimalarials, NSAIDs, corticosteroid withdrawal, interferon |
| **Alcohol** | Moderate | Exacerbates psoriasis; reduces treatment response |
| **Smoking** | Strong | Risk factor for development and severity; particularly pustular palmoplantar |
| **Obesity** | Moderate | Increases severity; adipokines promote inflammation |
| **HIV infection** | Strong | Can worsen existing psoriasis or trigger new onset |
| **Pregnancy** | Variable | Often improves during pregnancy; postpartum flares common |
| **Climate** | Moderate | Cold, dry weather worsens; UV exposure improves |

### Comorbidities

| Comorbidity | Prevalence | Screening |
|-------------|------------|-----------|
| **Psoriatic arthritis** | 20–30% | Early rheumatology referral; screen for joint pain, morning stiffness, dactylitis |
| **Cardiovascular disease** | Increased | BP, lipids, smoking cessation, weight management |
| **Metabolic syndrome** | Increased | BMI, waist circumference, glucose, lipids |
| **Type 2 diabetes** | Increased | Fasting glucose/HbA1c |
| **Obesity** | Increased | Weight counseling |
| **Depression/anxiety** | Increased | Mental health screening; psoriasis strongly impacts QoL |
| **Crohn's disease/UC** | Increased | GI symptoms screen |
| **NAFLD** | Increased | Liver enzymes; metabolic assessment |
| **Lymphoma** | Slightly increased | (long-term severe disease) |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Presentation | Considerations |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–III** | Classic bright red plaques with silvery scale; erythema prominent | Easier to assess severity |
| **Fitzpatrick IV–VI** | Plaques may be violaceous, purple, or dark brown; scale may appear gray; erythema less visible; post-inflammatory hyper/hypopigmentation common | Higher risk of misdiagnosis; may be mistaken for lichen planus or eczema; assess plaque thickness and distribution rather than color alone |

**Psoriasis in skin of color:**
- Underdiagnosed due to atypical color presentation
- Dyspigmentation (both hyper- and hypo-) can be more distressing than the plaques
- Scalp psoriasis may be mistaken for seborrheic dermatitis or traction alopecia
- Lower treatment satisfaction reported; may be due to pigmentary concerns

### Treatment Pathways

#### Topical Therapy (Mild to Moderate, < 5% BSA)

| Medication | Class | Application | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------------|----n|
| **Corticosteroids** | Anti-inflammatory | BID | Potency by body area: low (face/folds), medium (trunk/extremities), high (scalp/palms/soles/thick plaques) |
| **Calcipotriene (Dovonex®)** | Vitamin D analog | BID | Slow onset (8–12 weeks); steroid-sparing; don't use > 100 g/week |

- Consider malignancy history

#### Combination Therapy

- **Rotational therapy:** Reduces cumulative toxicity (e.g., methotrexate + phototherapy)
- **Sequential therapy:** Clear with cyclosporine, then maintain with methotrexate or biologic
- **Concomitant:** Topicals + systemic for optimal results

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Erythrodermic psoriasis** — dermatologic emergency; dehydration, temperature dysregulation
- [ ] **Generalized pustular psoriasis** — life-threatening; fever, leukocytosis; requires hospitalization
- [ ] **Psoriatic arthritis** — joint pain, morning stiffness, dactylitis → rheumatology referral
- [ ] **Signs of infection** — pustules with fever, malaise
- [ ] **Medication-induced flare** — evaluate recent medication changes
- [ ] **Significant depression/suicidal ideation** — psoriasis strongly associated with mental health burden
- [ ] **Treatment failure with 2+ systemic agents** — consider clinical trial or alternative diagnosis

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Psoriasis is contagious" | Not contagious; autoimmune/immune-mediated |
| "It's just a skin condition" | Systemic inflammatory disease with cardiovascular, metabolic, and joint comorbidities |
| "It can be cured with diet" | No evidence-based diet cures psoriasis; weight loss can improve severity |
| "Biologics are dangerous and should be last resort" | Biologics are highly effective and generally safe with proper screening; earlier use improves outcomes |
| "Psoriasis doesn't affect children" | Can occur at any age; guttate often in children/young adults |
| "Natural sunlight is always good" | While UV helps, sunburn can trigger Koebner phenomenon; controlled phototherapy is safer |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Psoriasis:**

1. **Initial Assessment:**
   - BSA estimation (palm method)
   - Subtype identification (plaque, guttate, inverse, pustular, nail)
   - Quality of life impact (DLQI)
   - Joint symptom screening (psoriatic arthritis)
   - Trigger identification (stress, infections, medications)

2. **Severity Stratification:**
   - **Mild (< 3% BSA):** Topical therapy (steroid + calcipotriene combination preferred)
   - **Moderate (3–10% BSA):** Phototherapy or systemic (apremilast, methotrexate) or biologic
   - **Severe (> 10% BSA):** Biologic or systemic; dermatology referral

3. **Comorbidity Screening:**
   - Cardiovascular risk factors
   - Joint symptoms (psoriatic arthritis)
   - Mental health screening
   - Metabolic syndrome assessment

4. **Treatment Selection:**
   - Consider patient preferences (topical vs. injection vs. pill)
   - Lifestyle factors (ability to attend phototherapy)
   - Comorbidities (avoid methotrexate in liver disease; avoid cyclosporine in renal disease)
   - Pregnancy plans

5. **Safety Monitoring:**
   - Biologic screening requirements
   - Systemic medication lab monitoring
   - Infection risk education
   - Live vaccine completion before biologics

6. **Special Populations:**
   - **Pediatric:** Limited options; topical, phototherapy, some biologics approved
   - **Pregnancy:** Avoid methotrexate, acitretin; biologics (TNF inhibitors) may be safest option in severe disease
   - **Skin of color:** Address dyspigmentation concerns; ensure adequate treatment for plaque clearance

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe/Caution | Avoid |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| **Topical** | Low-potency steroids, emollients | Tazarotene (teratogenic), anthralin, high-potency steroids large area |
| **Phototherapy** | NB-UVB (preferred) | PUVA |
| **Systemic** | Cyclosporine (if needed), apremilast (limited data) | Methotrexate (absolute), acitretin (absolute; 3-year contraception post) |
| **Biologics** | TNF inhibitors (most data) appear safe | IL-17, IL-23 inhibitors (limited data) |

**Note:** Pregnancy often improves psoriasis; postpartum flares are common. Breastfeeding considerations for systemic agents.

# Pigmentation Disorders

## 11. Melasma

### Clinical Definition

Melasma is an acquired hyperpigmentary disorder characterized by symmetric, brown to brown-brown patches on sun-exposed areas, primarily the face. It is most common in women (90% of cases), particularly those with darker skin types (Fitzpatrick III–VI), and is strongly associated with hormonal factors and UV exposure.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L81.1 (Melasma)
- **Synonyms:** Chloasma, "mask of pregnancy"

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Symmetric distribution:** Patches on cheeks, forehead, upper lip, nose, chin (centrofacial pattern); or cheeks and nose (malar pattern); or mandibular pattern
- **Color:** Light to dark brown or gray-brown; color is uniform within each patch
- **Shape:** Irregular, patchy, confluent macules and patches
- **Distribution:** Sun-exposed facial areas; occasionally neck, forearms
- **History:** Often associated with pregnancy, oral contraceptives, hormone replacement therapy, or sun exposure
- **Wood's lamp examination:** Enhances epidermal pigment; dermal melasma does not enhance

### Clinical Patterns

| Pattern | Distribution |
|---------|------------|
| **Centrofacial** | Forehead, nose, cheeks, upper lip, chin (most common: 50–80%) |
| **Malar** | Cheeks and nose (less common) |
| **Mandibular** | Jawline and chin (more refractory; often epidermal-dermal) |

### Histological Classification

| Type | Wood's Lamp | Location | Treatment Response |
|------|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Epidermal** | Enhances (contrast increases) | Epidermis | Best; responds to topical lightening |
| **Dermal** | No enhancement | Dermis | Poorest; resistant to topical therapy |
| **Mixed** | Partial enhancement | Both layers | Intermediate response |
| **Indeterminate** | Unclear in dark skin | — | Difficult to predict |

### Grading Scale

**MASI (Melasma Area and Severity Index):**

| Score | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| 0 | Clear |
| 1–10 | Mild |
| 11–20 | Moderate |
| > 20 | Severe |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **UV radiation** | Strong | Stimulates melanocyte activity; primary exacerbating factor |
| **Hormonal factors** | Strong | Estrogen, progesterone stimulate melanogenesis; pregnancy, OCPs, HRT |
| **Genetic predisposition** | Strong | Family history in 40–60%; higher prevalence in Fitzpatrick III–VI |
| **Melanocyte hyperactivity** | Strong | Functional melanocytes with increased melanin production; not increased melanocyte number |
| **Vascular component** | Emerging | Increased vascularity in melasma skin; VEGF may play role |
| **Inflammation** | Emerging | Mast cells, solar elastosis contribute |
| **Thyroid disease** | Moderate | Association with autoimmune thyroid disease |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Factor | Evidence | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Sun exposure** | Strong | Primary trigger; even brief exposure can worsen; UVA, UVB, and visible light all contribute |
| **Pregnancy** | Strong | "Chloasma gravidarum"; typically resolves postpartum but may persist |
| **Oral contraceptives** | Strong | Estrogen-progestin combinations |
| **Hormone replacement therapy** | Strong | Systemic estrogens |
| **Phototoxic medications** | Moderate | Tetracyclines, NSAIDs, phenothiazines, amiodarone, some cosmetics |
| **LED screens** | Emerging | Visible light/blue light may contribute in susceptible individuals |
| **Heat** | Emerging | Infrared radiation may stimulate melanocytes |
| **Cosmetics/fragrances** | Moderate | Phototoxic/photoallergic reactions |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Prevalence | Presentation | Treatment Considerations |
|-----------|------------|--------------|------------------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–II** | Lower | Brown patches; background erythema may be visible | Good response to topical therapy; strict photoprotection |
| **Fitzpatrick III–IV** | High | Brown to gray-brown patches | Mixed melasma common; combination therapy needed |
| **Fitzpatrick V–VI** | Very high | Gray-brown, blue-gray, or "dirty" patches; often harder to distinguish from background skin tone | Dermal melasma more common; slower response; higher risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation from aggressive treatments; PIH from treatment is major concern |

**Critical consideration in darker skin:**
- Response to treatment is slower
- Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation from procedures is a significant risk
- Conservative approach mandatory
- Visible light (not just UV) protection is essential

### Treatment Pathways

#### First-Line: Topical Combination Therapy (Triple Combination)

| Component | Concentration | Role |
|-----------|--------------|------|
| **Hydroquinone (HQ)** | 2–4% (OTC 2%; Rx up to 4%) | Tyrosinase inhibitor; gold standard depigmenting agent |
| **Tretinoin** | 0.05–0.1% | Increases epidermal turnover; enhances HQ penetration |
| **Fluocinolone acetonide** | 0.01% | Low-potency steroid; reduces irritation from tretinoin and HQ |

**Trade name:** Tri-Luma® (fluocinolone 0.01%, HQ 4%, tretinoin 0.05%)

**Use:** Once daily at night; limited to 8 weeks continuous use; then maintenance with non-HQ regimen

#### Alternative Topical Lightening Agents

| Agent | Mechanism | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| **Azelaic acid 15–20%** | Tyrosinase inhibitor; anti-inflammatory | Safe in pregnancy; good for sensitive skin |
| **Kojic acid 1–4%** | Tyrosinase inhibitor by chelating copper | Effective; can cause irritation and contact dermatitis |
| **Arbutin (alpha/beta)** | Hydroquinone derivative; tyrosinase inhibitor | Gentler than HQ; less effective |
| **Vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid)** | Antioxidant; inhibits tyrosinase | Unstable; best in serum form; also provides photoprotection |
| **Niacinamide 2–5%** | Inhibits melanosome transfer | Well-tolerated; anti-inflammatory; good for maintenance |
| **Tranexamic acid (TXA)** | 2–5% topical; inhibits plasminogen activation | Emerging; good evidence for topical and oral |
| **Cysteamine 5%** | Antioxidant; inhibits melanogenesis | New; strong evidence; cysteamine HCl 5% cream |
| **Retinoids** (tretinoin, adpalen) | Increase cell turnover | Adjunctive; helps penetration of other agents |
| **Mequinol 2%** | Phenolic compound; melanocyte toxicity | Alternative to HQ |
| **Soy, licorice extract, mulberry, bearberry** | Natural tyrosinase inhibitors | Variable evidence; generally milder |

#### Oral Therapy

| Medication | Dose | Evidence | Notes |
|-----------|------|----------|-------|
| **Tranexamic acid** | 250 mg BID–TID or 500 mg daily | Strong | Off-label; significant improvement; thromboembolism risk; contraindicated in personal history of thrombosis |
| **Polypodium leucotomos** | 240 mg BID | Moderate | Oral photoprotectant; fern extract; antioxidant |
| **Glutathione** | 500 mg daily | Limited | Antioxidant; mixed evidence |

#### Procedures (Caution in Darker Skin)

| Procedure | Indication | Caution |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| **Chemical peels** | Epidermal melasma; adjunctive | Glycolic acid 20–70%, Jessner's, salicylic acid; MUST be conservative in Fitzpatrick IV–VI; test spot essential |
| **Microneedling** | Mixed melasma; enhances topical penetration | Risk of PIH in darker skin; professional only |
| **Laser/IPL** | Refractory cases; dermal melasma | High risk of PIH in darker skin; Q-switched Nd:YAG (low fluence, large spot) or fractional lasers; expert required |
| **Mesotherapy with TXA** | Intradermal tranexamic acid | Emerging; mixed evidence |

#### Photoprotection (Critical)

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Broad-spectrum sunscreen** | SPF 30+ daily; SPF 50+ for outdoor activity; reapply every 2 hours |
| **Physical blockers** | Zinc oxide, titanium dioxide — best for visible light protection |
| **Iron oxides** | Added to tinted sunscreens; blocks visible light (important for darker skin) |
| **Protective clothing** | Wide-brimmed hats, UV-protective clothing |
| **Behavioral** | Seek shade; avoid peak sun (10 AM–4 PM) |

**Iron oxide-containing tinted sunscreens** are particularly important for Fitzpatrick IV–VI because visible light (which iron oxides block) contributes to melasma in darker skin.

### Maintenance Therapy

After achieving clearance:
- Continue strict photoprotection indefinitely
- Maintenance with non-HQ lightening agents (azelaic acid, niacinamide, vitamin C)
- Intermittent HQ (e.g., 2x weekly) if needed
- Consider oral polypodium leucotomos

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Rapidly changing pigmented lesions** — must rule out melanoma; dermatoscopy or biopsy
- [ ] **Unilateral melasma** — atypical; consider other diagnoses (e.g., frictional hyperpigmentation, Riehl's melanosis)
- [ ] **Treatment-induced PIH** — especially after procedures in darker skin
- [ ] **Hydroquinone exogenous ochronosis** — blue-black pigmentation from prolonged HQ use (> 6 months continuous); more common in darker skin
- [ ] **Underlying endocrine disorder** — screen for thyroid disease if new onset

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Melasma can be cured" | No cure; chronic condition requiring lifelong management; goal is control |
| "Sunscreen alone will clear it" | Necessary but not sufficient; requires combination therapy |
| "Hydroquinone is dangerous and banned" | 2% is OTC in US; 4% is Rx; safe when used as directed; 8-week limit + maintenance |
| "Natural lighteners are as effective as hydroquinone" | Generally less potent; may be useful for maintenance |
| "Lasers are the best treatment for melasma" | High risk of worsening melasma (PIH); only for refractory cases by experts |
| "It will go away after pregnancy" | Often persists; early treatment prevents chronicity |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Melasma:**

1. **Diagnostic Screening:**
   - Distribution pattern (centrofacial, malar, mandibular)
   - Trigger history: pregnancy, OCPs, HRT, sun exposure, recent procedures
   - Wood's lamp guidance (if available) or clinical assessment of epidermal vs. dermal
   - Fitzpatrick type for treatment planning

2. **Treatment Ladder:**
   - **Mild/First-line:** Strict photoprotection + azelaic acid 20% or HQ 2%
   - **Moderate:** Triple combination (HQ 4% + tretinoin + steroid) for 8 weeks → maintenance
   - **Severe/Refractory:** Add oral tranexamic acid; consider conservative peels; laser only as last resort with expert

3. **Photoprotection Emphasis:**
   - Daily sunscreen reminder with reapplication alerts
   - Recommend iron oxide-containing tinted sunscreen for Fitzpatrick IV–VI
   - UV index-based alerts

4. **Safety Guardrails:**
   - HQ duration limits (8 weeks on, then off)
   - Pregnancy screening (avoid HQ, retinoids)
   - Thrombosis history before recommending oral TXA
   - Conservative procedural recommendations for darker skin
   - PIH prevention education

5. **Expectation Setting:**
   - Improvement takes 8–12 weeks minimum
   - Relapse is common; maintenance is essential
   - No cure; ongoing management required

6. **Follow-up:**
   - Photo tracking at 4, 8, 12 weeks
   - Adjust regimen based on response
   - Flag for dermatologist if no improvement at 12 weeks

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Azelaic acid, vitamin C, niacinamide, zinc oxide/titanium dioxide sunscreen | Hydroquinone (systemic absorption concerns), tretinoin/retinoids (teratogenic), chemical sunscreens (some) |
| **Systemic** | — | Oral tranexamic acid, oral retinoids, isotretinoin |
| **Procedures** | — | All elective procedures; peels, lasers |

**Note:** Melasma often worsens during pregnancy; reassure that treatment can begin postpartum and after breastfeeding.

## 12. Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation (PIH)

### Clinical Definition

Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) is an acquired hyperpigmentation resulting from increased melanin deposition following cutaneous inflammation or injury. It represents the skin's response to trauma, with melanin "spilling" into the dermis or being overproduced in the epidermis.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L81.0 (Disorders of pigmentation, unspecified) or L81.8 (Other specified disorders of pigmentation)
- **Related codes:** By underlying cause (e.g., L70.5 for acne excoriée with PIH)

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **History of preceding inflammation or injury:** Acne, eczema, psoriasis, insect bites, burns, procedures, friction
- **Distribution:** Matches pattern of preceding inflammation
- **Color:** Tan, brown, dark brown, or gray-brown; gray-brown if dermal melanin
- **Morphology:** Macules or patches following shape of original lesions
- **No active inflammation:** Lesion is flat; if raised or changing, reconsider diagnosis
- **Wood's lamp:** Epidermal PIH enhances; dermal does not

### Classification by Depth

| Type | Wood's Lamp | Color | Resolution Time |
|-------|-------------|-------|-----------------|
| **Epidermal PIH** | Enhances | Tan to brown | 3–6 months (sometimes longer) |
| **Dermal PIH** | No enhancement | Gray to blue-gray | 6–12+ months; may be permanent |
| **Mixed** | Partial | Variable | Intermediate |

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mild** | Focal, light brown macules; limited number |
| **Moderate** | Multiple brown patches; moderate contrast with surrounding skin |
| **Severe** | Widespread, dark brown to gray patches; significant cosmetic concern |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Inflammatory mediator release** | Strong | Cytokines (IL-1, TNF-α, GM-CSF) stimulate melanocytes |
| **Melanocyte activation** | Strong | Inflammation triggers melanin production and transfer to keratinocytes |
| **Melanin incontinence** | Strong | Inflammation damages basal layer; melanin falls into dermis (dermal PIH) |
| **Genetic predisposition** | Strong | Fitzpatrick III–VI at much higher risk due to larger, more active melanocytes |
| **UV exposure** | Strong | Post-inflammatory skin is photosensitive; UV worsens and prolongs PIH |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Preceding Condition | Common PIH Pattern |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| **Acne vulgaris** | Dark spots at sites of previous papules/pustules; face, jawline, back |
| **Atopic dermatitis** | Diffuse patches in areas of previous eczema; periorbital, neck, flexures |
| **Contact dermatitis** | Pattern matching irritant/allergen exposure |
| **Psoriasis** | Residual pigmentation at plaque sites; often "inverse" (hypopigmented center, hypepigmented border) |
| **Insect bites** | Scattered dark spots on exposed areas |
| **Burns (thermal, chemical, UV)** | Pattern matching burn distribution |
| **Procedures** | Peels, lasers, microneedling, waxing, electrolysis — operator-induced |
| **Friction** | Chronic rubbing (acne excoriée, tight clothing, undergarments) |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | PIH Risk | Presentation | Treatment Approach |
|-----------|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–II** | Low | Post-inflammatory erythema (PIE) more common than PIH; pink/red marks | Focus on vascular treatment (niacinamide, vitamin C, vascular lasers); PIH less common |
| **Fitzpatrick III–IV** | Moderate-High | Both PIE and PIH; brown patches common | Combination approach; early intervention |
| **Fitzpatrick V–VI** | Very high | PIH is the rule rather than exception after inflammation; dark brown to gray patches; can be more distressing than original condition | **Prevention is paramount;** conservative treatment; high risk of worsening with aggressive procedures |

**Critical principle:** In Fitzpatrick V–VI, preventing PIH by treating the underlying inflammatory condition promptly and avoiding manipulation is more effective than treating established PIH.

### Treatment Pathways

#### Prevention (Most Important)

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Treat underlying inflammation promptly** | Control acne, eczema, psoriasis to prevent new PIH |
| **Do not pick/squeeze lesions** | Mechanical irritation worsens PIH |
| **Strict photoprotection** | SPF 30+ daily; prevents darkening of existing PIH and formation of new |
| **Gentle skincase** | Avoid harsh treatments that cause additional inflammation |
| **Early anti-inflammatory treatment** | Topical steroids for eczema; prompt acne treatment |

#### Topical Lightening Agents

| Agent | Concentration | Notes |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| **Hydroquinone** | 2–4% | Gold standard; 8–12 weeks; then maintenance or rotation |
| **Azelaic acid** | 15–20% | Safe for long-term; anti-inflammatory; good for acne-related PIH |
| **Retinoids** (tretinoin, adpalen) | 0.025–0.1% | Increase cell turnover; enhance HQ penetration; prevent new acne |
| **Vitamin C** (L-ascorbic acid) | 10–20% | Antioxidant; tyrosinase inhibitor; photoprotection |
| **Niacinamide** | 2–5% | Inhibits melanosome transfer; anti-inflammatory; well-tolerated |
| **Kojic acid** | 1–4% | Tyrosinase inhibitor |
| **Arbutin** | 1–2% | HQ derivative; gentler |
| **Tranexamic acid** | 2–5% | Topical; inhibits plasminogen |
| **Cysteamine** | 5% | Strong depigmenting agent |
| **Azelaic acid + tretinoin** | Combination | Synergistic |

#### Combination Approaches

| Combination | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Morning:** Vitamin C + niacinamide + sunscreen | Antioxidant + melanosome transfer inhibition + photoprotection |
| **Evening:** Hydroquinone or azelaic acid + retinoid | Depigmentation + cell turnover |

#### Chemical Peels (Conservative)

| Peel | Concentration | Indication | Caution |
|------|--------------|------------|---------|
| **Glycolic acid** | 20–70% | Epidermal PIH; series of 6+ | Higher risk of PIH in darker skin; start low |
| **Salicylic acid** | 20–30% | Acne + PIH; oil-soluble | Self-neutralizing; safer in darker skin |
| **Jessner's solution** | — | Epidermal PIH | Resorcinol component can cause PIH |
| **Lactic acid** | 10–30% | Gentle; good for sensitive skin | Slower results |
| **Mandelic acid** | 20–40% | Large molecular weight; slower penetration; safer for darker skin | Good option for Fitzpatrick IV–VI |

#### Microdermabrasion

- Gentle mechanical exfoliation
- Low risk of PIH
- Multiple sessions needed
- Good for epidermal PIH

#### Laser/IPL (Caution)

| Modality | Indication | Notes |
|----------|------------|-------|
| **Q-switched Nd:YAG 1064 nm** | Dermal PIH | Low fluence, large spot; multiple sessions; expert only |
| **Fractional lasers** | Resistant PIH | Higher risk; test spot mandatory |
| **IPL** | Epidermal PIH | Risk of worsening PIH in darker skin; conservative settings |
| **Picosecond lasers** | Emerging | Shorter pulse duration; theoretically safer |

**Mandel rule for darker skin:** Start with lowest settings, test spot, and prioritize melanin-specific wavelengths with longer pulse durations.

#### Time and Natural Resolution

- Epidermal PIH: 3–6 months with photoprotection
- Dermal PIH: 6–12+ months; may not fully resolve
- Consistent treatment accelerates but does not eliminate the natural timeline

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Active inflammation alongside PIH** — treat active disease first
- [ ] **Lesions that are changing, irregular, or multifocal** — rule out melanoma
- [ ] **Worsening with treatment** — likely post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation from the treatment itself; stop and reassess
- [ ] **Blue-gray color** — suggests dermal melanin; topical therapy less effective
- [ ] **Frustration leading to over-treatment** — user may layer multiple actives, causing irritation and worsening PIH

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "PIH is permanent" | Epidermal PIH resolves with time and treatment; dermal PIH may be long-lasting |
| "Exfoliating aggressively will fade it faster" | Over-exfoliation causes more inflammation and worsens PIH |
| "Natural remedies are safer" | Lemon juice, baking soda, undiluted essential oils can cause chemical irritation and worsen PIH |
| "If one lightening agent doesn't work, use them all" | Layering multiple actives increases irritation risk; use one or two consistently |
| "Once the acne is gone, PIH will fade quickly" | PIH takes months to fade; ongoing photoprotection is essential |
| "PIH and PIE are the same" | PIH = excess melanin (brown/dark); PIE = damaged capillaries (pink/red); treatments differ |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for PIH:**

1. **Identify Source:**
   - Ask about preceding conditions (acne, eczema, procedures, burns)
   - Assess if active inflammation is still present
   - Determine if acne-related (most common) or other cause

2. **Prevention Emphasis:**
   - If acne-related: Prioritize acne control to prevent new PIH
   - If eczema-related: Optimize eczema management
   - Universal: "Hands off" education; no picking/squeezing

3. **Severity & Type Assessment:**
   - Epidermal vs. dermal (clinical assessment)
   - Fitzpatrick type for risk stratification
   - Color: brown (epidermal) vs. gray/blue (dermal)

4. **Treatment Planning:**
   - **Mild/Epidermal:** Vitamin C + niacinamide AM; retinoid + azelaic acid PM; strict SPF
   - **Moderate:** Add hydroquinone 2–4% for 8–12 weeks; continue retinoid
   - **Severe/Refractory:** Dermatologist referral; consider oral tranexamic acid, conservative peels, or laser

5. **Fitzpatrick V–VI Specific:**
   - Ultra-conservative approach
   - Emphasize prevention over treatment
   - Avoid aggressive peels and lasers unless by expert
   - Iron oxide sunscreen recommendation
   - Realistic timeline (6–12+ months)

6. **Follow-up:**
   - Photo tracking monthly
   - Adjust if irritation occurs
   - Celebrate small improvements to maintain adherence

7. **Safety Checks:**
   - Pregnancy screening before retinoids or HQ
   - Irritation monitoring (redness, peeling, stinging)
   - Screen for active acne that needs simultaneous treatment

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Azelaic acid, vitamin C, niacinamide, glycolic acid (low %), physical sunscreen | Hydroquinone (absorption concerns), retinoids (teratogenic), salicylic acid (high concentrations), chemical pees |
| **Systemic** | — | Oral tranexamic acid |
| **Procedures** | — | All elective procedures |

# Pigmentation Disorders

## 13. Post-Inflammatory Erythema (PIE)

### Clinical Definition

Post-inflammatory erythema (PIE) is a vascular response to cutaneous inflammation, presenting as persistent pink-to-red macules and patches at the site of prior inflammatory skin lesions. Unlike post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH), PIE results from capillary dilation and damage rather than excess melanin deposition. It is particularly common after acne vulgaris in lighter skin types.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L93.1 (Erythema, not elsewhere classified) or by underlying cause (e.g., L70.5 for acne excoriée)
- **Nature:** Vascular — not pigmentary

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **History of preceding inflammation:** Acne (most common), atopic dermatitis, contact dermatitis, insect bites, procedures
- **Color:** Pink, red, or purple macules/patches; color persists with digital pressure (unlike active inflammation)
- **Distribution:** Matches pattern of preceding inflammation; face (cheeks, jawline), chest, back after acne
- **Diascopy:** Blanches with glass slide pressure — confirms vascular nature (distinguishes from PIH, which does NOT blanch)
- **Wood's lamp:** May enhance erythema due to vascular fluorescence
- **Evolution:** Persists weeks to months after inflammation resolves

### Diascopy Technique

| Step | Procedure | Interpretation |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| 1 | Apply clear glass slide or spatula firmly to lesion | Press firmly to expel blood from capillaries |
| 2 | Observe color change under good lighting | PIE blanches (turns white/pale) |
| 3 | Compare to surrounding skin | Confirms vascular etiology |
| 4 | Distinguish from PIH | PIH does NOT blanch (melanin is fixed in tissue) |

**Clinical Pearl:** Diascopy is the single most useful bedside test to differentiate PIE from PIH. PIE blanches; PIH does not.

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mild** | Faint pink macules; limited number; subtle |
| **Moderate** | Distinct red patches; multiple lesions; cosmetically noticeable |
| **Severe** | Deep red to violaceous patches; widespread; persistent > 6 months |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Capillary dilation** | Strong | Inflammation triggers local vasodilation; vessels remain dilated after inflammation subsides |
| **Vascular endothelial damage** | Moderate | Inflammation damages capillary walls; increased permeability |
| **Neovascularization** | Moderate | Chronic inflammation stimulates new vessel formation |
| **Delayed wound healing** | Moderate | Inadequate resolution of inflammatory vascular response |
| **Genetic predisposition** | Moderate | Fitzpatrick I–III much more susceptible; lighter skin has less melanin camouflage |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Preceding Condition | PIE Pattern |
|--------------------|-------------|
| **Acne vulgaris** | Most common cause; red marks at sites of papules/pustules |
| **Atopic dermatitis** | Diffuse facial redness after flares |
| **Contact dermatitis** | Pattern matching allergen/irritant exposure |
| **Procedures** | Peels, microneedling, laser (over-treatment) |
| **Insect bites** | Scattered red spots |
| **Trauma/picking** | Mechanical irritation of healing lesions |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | PIE Risk | Presentation | Treatment Approach |
|-----------|----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–II** | Very high | PIE is the predominant post-inflammatory mark; bright pink/red; highly visible | Focus on vascular treatment; lasers highly effective |
| **Fitzpatrick III–IV** | Moderate | Mix of PIE and PIH; PIE may fade to brown (PIH) over time | Treat PIE first, then address PIH if needed |
| **Fitzpatrick V–VI** | Low | PIE is uncommon; PIH predominates; if PIE occurs, it often transitions quickly to PIH | Address underlying inflammation; PIH prevention |

### Treatment Pathways

#### Key Distinction: PIE vs. PIH Treatment

| Feature | PIE (Vascular) | PIH (Pigmentary) |
|---------|----------------|------------------|
| **Nature** | Dilated capillaries | Excess melanin |
| **Color** | Pink, red, purple | Brown, tan, gray-brown |
| **Diascopy** | Blanches | Does NOT blanch |
| **Best treatments** | Vascular lasers, niacinamide, time | Hydroquinone, retinoids, azelaic acid |
| **UV effect** | Minimal darkening (but UV can prolong) | Darkens significantly with UV |

#### First-Line: Topical (Conservative)

| Ingredient | Concentration | Mechanism | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|-------|
| **Niacinamide** | 4–5% | Anti-inflammatory; reduces vascular dilation; barrier repair | First-line; well-tolerated; good for all skin types |
| **Vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid)** | 10–20% | Antioxidant; strengthens vessel walls; anti-inflammatory | Also helps prevent PIH transition |
| **Azelaic acid** | 15–20% | Anti-inflammatory; some vascular benefit | Good for acne-related PIE |
| **Centella asiatica** | Variable | Promotes wound healing; anti-inflammatory | Adjunctive |

#### Vascular Lasers (Gold Standard for Moderate-Severe PIE)

| Laser | Wavelength | Mechanism | Sessions | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Pulsed Dye Laser (PDL)** | 585–595 nm | Targets oxyhemoglobin; selective photothermolysis of vessels | 2–4 sessions, 4–6 weeks apart | Gold standard for PIE; minimal downtime |
| **KTP Laser** | 532 nm | Targets superficial vessels | 2–4 sessions | Good for fine vessels; risk of PIH in darker skin |
| **Nd:YAG 1064 nm** | 1064 nm | Deeper vascular target | 2–4 sessions | Safer for darker skin types |
| **IPL** | 500–1200 nm | Broad spectrum; targets hemoglobin and melanin | 3–5 sessions | Less specific than lasers; good for mixed PIE + PIH |

#### Adjunctive Measures

| Intervention | Rationale |
|-------------|-----------|
| **Strict photoprotection** | Prevents PIE from transitioning to PIH (especially in Fitzpatrick III–IV) |
| **Time** | Most PIE resolves spontaneously over 3–6 months |
| **Avoid picking/squeezing** | Mechanical irritation worsens vascular damage |
| **Gentle skincare** | Anti-inflammatory approach; avoid harsh actives that prolong inflammation |

#### What NOT to Use for PIE

| Treatment | Why to Avoid |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Hydroquinone** | No melanin to target; ineffective and unnecessary irritation |
| **High-strength retinoids initially** | Can cause irritation and prolong erythema |
| **Aggressive exfoliation** | Increases inflammation |
| **Bleaching agents** | PIE is not a pigment problem |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **PIE persisting > 12 months** — consider vascular malformation or rosacea overlap
- [ ] **Transition to PIH** — indicates need for pigment-directed therapy
- [ ] **Worsening with laser treatment** — incorrect settings or wrong wavelength; possible burn/PIH
- [ ] **Active acne alongside PIE** — must treat acne first to prevent new PIE
- [ ] **Signs of rosacea** — persistent facial redness with flushing triggers; may be rosacea, not PIE

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "PIE is just another name for PIH" | Different pathophysiology; different treatments; dIscopy distinguishes them |
| "Hydroquinone will fade red marks" | HQ targets melanin, not vessels; ineffective for PIE |
| "PIE is permanent" | Most PIE fades over 3–6 months; vascular lasers can accelerate |
| "Sun exposure will help PIE fade" | UV can worsen inflammation and cause PIH; always use SPF |
| "Exfoliation will fade red marks" | Over-exfoliation increases inflammation and prolongs PIE |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for PIE:**

1. **Diagnostic Differentiation:**
   - Ask: What color are the marks? Pink/red = PIE; Brown/dark = PIH
   - Perform "virtual dIscopy" education: press on mark — does it blanch?
   - Determine Fitzpatrick type (PIE more common in I–III)

2. **Treatment Matching:**
   - **Mild:** Niacinamide 5% + vitamin C + strict SPF
   - **Moderate:** Add azelaic acid; consider professional vascular laser consultation
   - **Severe/Persistent:** PDL or IPL referral; 2–4 sessions expected

3. **Prevention:**
   - Emphasize treating active acne/inflammation promptly
   - "Hands off" education
   - Photoprotection to prevent PIE → PIH transition

4. **Expectation Setting:**
   - Natural resolution: 3–6 months
   - Laser: 2–4 sessions, 4–6 weeks apart
   - Avoid "bleaching" or "lightening" language — PIE is vascular, not pigment

5. **Safety Checks:**
   - Screen for active acne requiring simultaneous treatment
   - Pregnancy: no laser in pregnancy; niacinamide and azelaic acid safe
   - Darker skin: caution with KTP laser; prefer Nd:YAG or PDL with conservative settings

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Caution |
|----------|------|---------|
| **Topical** | Niacinamide, vitamin C, azelaic acid | Retinoids |
| **Procedures** | — | Vascular lasers generally deferred |

## 14. Vitiligo

### Clinical Definition

Vitiligo is an acquired, chronic autoimmune disorder characterized by selective destruction of melanocytes, resulting in depigmented (white) macules and patches. It affects approximately 1–2% of the global population, with onset typically before age 30. The disease has profound psychological and social impact due to visible disfigurement.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L80 (Vitiligo)
- **Subclassification codes:**
  - L80.0 — Vitiligo vulgaris
  - L80.1 — Segmental vitiligo
  - L80.2 — Vitiligo, unspecified
  - L80.3 — Vitiligo, other specified

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Depigmented macules/patches:** Milk-white, well-demarcated, often symmetrical
- **Distribution:** Focal, segmental, generalized (vulgaris), acrofacial, universal
- **Wood's lamp examination:** Bright blue-white fluorescence (enhances detection of subtle lesions)
- **Trichrome vitiligo:** Three zones — white (complete loss), intermediate (partial loss), and normal skin
- **Koebner phenomenon:** New lesions at sites of trauma
- **Associated conditions:** Autoimmune thyroid disease, pernicious anemia, Addison's disease, type 1 diabetes, alopecia areata

### Classification

| Type | Features | Prognosis |
|------|----------|-----------|
| **Non-segmental (NSV)** | Bilateral, symmetrical; most common (85–90%); often progressive | Variable; repigmentation possible |
| **Segmental (SV)** | Unilateral, follows dermatomal pattern; onset early (often childhood); rapid progression then stabilization | Less responsive to treatment; stable after initial spread |
| **Focal** | One or few patches; not segmental; may evolve to NSV | Uncertain |
| **Acrofacial** | Distal extremities and facial orifices (lips, fingertips) | Difficult to treat |
| **Universal** | > 80% BSA depigmented | Consider depigmentation therapy |
| **Mucosal** | Limited to mucous membranes | Resistant to treatment |

### Grading Scale

**VASI (Vitiligo Area Scoring Index):**

| Score | Extent |
|-------|--------|
| 0 | Clear |
| 1–25 | Mild |
| 26–50 | Moderate |
| 51–75 | Severe |
| 76–100 | Very severe |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Autoimmune destruction** | Strong | CD8+ T-cells target melanocytes; Th1/Th17 response; interferon-gamma signaling |
| **Genetic predisposition** | Strong | Polygenic; 30% family history; associated with HLA-A2, HLA-DR4, HLA-DRB1*04 |
| **Oxidative stress** | Strong | Excessive hydrogen peroxide in epidermis; defective catalase activity |
| **Melanocyte intrinsic defects** | Moderate | Abnormal melanocyte adhesion, migration, and survival |
| **Neural theory** | Moderate | Neurochemical mediators destroy melanocytes (more relevant for segmental type) |
| **Viral trigger** | Emerging | Possible role in initiating autoimmunity |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Trigger | Evidence | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| **Koebner phenomenon** | Strong | Trauma, friction, burns, surgical scars initiate new lesions |
| **Sunburn** | Strong | UV damage triggers melanocyte destruction |
| **Stress** | Moderate | Psychological and physical stress can precipitate or worsen |
| **Hormonal changes** | Limited | Pregnancy, puberty may trigger |
| **Chemical exposure** | Moderate | Phenols, catechols, sulfhydryl compounds (industrial, hair dyes) |
| **Thyroid disease** | Strong | 20–30% have autoimmune thyroid dysfunction; screen all patients |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Visibility | Treatment Considerations |
|-----------|------------|------------------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–II** | Less visible; easier to camouflage; sunburn risk high in depigmented areas | Phototherapy highly effective; sun protection critical |
| **Fitzpatrick III–IV** | Clearly visible; significant psychosocial impact | Balanced approach; camouflage important |
| **Fitzpatrick V–VI** | Highly visible; stark contrast; profound psychosocial impact | Treatment urgency higher; phototherapy may be less effective; camouflage and depigmentation options important |

### Treatment Pathways

#### Topical Therapy (Limited/Early Disease)

| Medication | Concentration | Mechanism | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|-------|
| **Mid-potency corticosteroids** | Betamethasone valerate 0.1%, mometasone furoate 0.1% | Anti-inflammatory; immunosuppressive | First-line for limited disease; avoid face/folds long-term; 2–3 months max |
| **Calcineurin inhibitors** | Tacrolimus 0.03–0.1%, pimecrolimus 1% | T-cell suppression; no atrophy | Face, folds, eyelids; safe for prolonged use |
| **Topical JAK inhibitors** | Ruxolitinib 1.5% cream | JAK1/2 inhibition; blocks IFN-gamma signaling | FDA-approved (2022) for non-segmental vitiligo; face and body; significant repigmentation |
| **Vitamin D analogs** | Calcipotriene 0.005% | Immunomodulatory; stimulates melanocytes | Adjunct; often combined with steroids |
| **Prostaglandin analogs** | Latanoprost 0.005% | Melanocyte stimulation | Limited evidence; adjunctive |

#### Phototherapy (Moderate to Widespread)

| Modality | Mechanism | Sessions | Response |
|----------|-----------|----------|----------|
| **NB-UVB (311 nm)** | Immunosuppression; melanocyte stimulation | 2–3x weekly; 20–40+ sessions | First-line for widespread; 50–70% achieve > 75% repigmentation |
| **Excimer laser (308 nm)** | Targeted UVB | 2x weekly; 24+ sessions | For limited, focal lesions; higher doses than NB-UVB |
| **PUVA** | Psoralen + UVA | 2–3x weekly | Older; less favored than NB-UVB due to toxicity (phototoxicity, nausea, skin cancer risk) |
| **Home NB-UVB** | — | Prescription units | Maintenance; convenience |

**Phototherapy pearls:**
- Face and neck respond best; hands and feet respond poorly
- Segmental vitiligo less responsive to phototherapy
- Response may take 3–6 months
- Maintenance therapy may be needed

#### Systemic Therapy (Rapidly Progressive or Extensive)

| Medication | Dose | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| **Oral mini-pulse steroids** | Dexamethasone 2.5–5 mg or betamethasone 2.5 mg, 2 consecutive days/week | Stabilizes progressive disease; "weekend therapy"; monitor for steroid side effects |
| **Oral JAK inhibitors** | Tofacitinib 5 mg BID, ruxolitinib 20 mg BID | Off-label; promising results with NB-UVB combination; requires monitoring |

#### Surgical Options (Stable Disease > 1 Year)

| Procedure | Indication | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| **Miniature punch grafting** | Small, stable lesions | Transfer of pigmented skin to depigmented area |
| **Suction blister grafting** | Stable segmental or focal lesions | Epidermal grafting; good cosmetic results |
| **Melanocyte-keratinocyte transplantation** | Stable, refractory cases | Cultured or non-cultured cellular grafts; requires expertise |
| **Tattooing (micropigmentation)** | Lip vitiligo, stable disease | Permanent; color matching challenges |

#### Depigmentation Therapy (Universal Vitiligo > 50% BSA)

| Agent | Application | Notes |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| **Monobenzone 20% cream** | BID to remaining pigmented areas | Permanent depigmentation; irreversible; > 12 months to achieve |
| **Hydroquinone 4%** | Similar to monobenzone | Less permanent; may repigment |
| **Q-switched ruby laser (694 nm)** | Destroys remaining melanocytes | Adjunct to topical depigmentation |

**Important:** Depigmentation is permanent and irreversible. Requires extensive counseling.

#### Camouflage and Cosmetic

| Option | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Cosmetic camouflage** | High-coverage waterproof makeup (Dermablend, Covermark) |
| **Self-tanners** | Dihydroxyacetone (DHA) stains unaffected skin darker; minimizes contrast |
| **Micropigmentation** | Permanent cosmetic tattooing for eyebrows, lips |
| **Sunless tanning** | Temporary bronzing of surrounding skin |

#### Supplements and Adjuncts

| Supplement | Evidence | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Polypodium leucotomos** | Moderate | Oral photoprotectant; may enhance repigmentation with phototherapy |
| **Ginkgo biloba** | Limited | Some studies show stabilization and repigmentation |
| **Alpha-lipoic acid** | Limited | Antioxidant; adjunctive |
| **Vitamin D** | Limited | Supplementation if deficient |
| **Catalase** | Limited | Topical; addresses oxidative stress |

### Psychological Impact

Vitiligo has profound psychosocial effects:
- Depression and anxiety prevalence: 25–35%
- Social stigma, especially in certain cultures
- Body image disturbance
- Quality of life impairment comparable to chronic diseases like diabetes
- Children may experience bullying and social isolation

**Support resources:** Vitiligo support groups, psychological counseling, patient education organizations (Vitiligo Research Foundation, Global Vitiligo Foundation).

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Rapid progression** — requires systemic stabilization (mini-pulse steroids)
- [ ] **Universal/extensive vitiligo** — consider depigmentation vs. treatment
- [ ] **Associated autoimmune disease** — screen thyroid (TSH), B12, fasting glucose
- [ ] **Significant psychological distress** — mental health referral
- [ ] **Treatment failure after 6 months of appropriate therapy** — reconsider diagnosis or treatment plan
- [ ] **Unilateral, dermatomal pattern** — segmental vitiligo; different prognosis and treatment approach

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Vitiligo is contagious" | Not contagious; autoimmune, not infectious |
| "It's caused by eating certain foods" | No dietary cause; no evidence for food avoidance |
| "Vitiligo can be cured with diet or supplements alone" | No cure; some supplements may help but are not curative |
| "It's just a cosmetic problem" | Significant autoimmune and psychological burden; associated with thyroid disease and other autoimmune conditions |
| "Treatment never works" | Many patients achieve significant repigmentation, especially on face/neck with early treatment |
| "Depigmentation is 'giving up'" | For universal vitiligo, depigmentation can be a valid, life-improving choice |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Vitiligo:**

1. **Initial Assessment:**
   - Distribution and extent (BSA estimate)
   - Classification: segmental vs. non-segmental
   - Activity: stable vs. progressive (new lesions in past 6 months?)
   - Associated symptoms: thyroid, autoimmune history
   - Psychological impact assessment

2. **Dermatologist Referral:**
   - All vitiligo patients should be evaluated by dermatology
   - Early treatment is more effective
   - Need for autoimmune screening

3. **Treatment Guidance:**
   - **Limited/Stable:** Topical corticosteroid or calcineurin inhibitor; consider ruxolitinib cream
   - **Widespread/Active:** NB-UVB phototherapy referral; systemic stabilization
   - **Rapidly progressive:** Urgent dermatology for mini-pulse steroids
   - **Universal:** Discuss depigmentation vs. continued treatment

4. **Support Resources:**
   - Connect to support groups and counseling
   - Camouflage education and product recommendations
   - Psychosocial support integration

5. **Safety Checks:**
   - Screen for thyroid disease (TSH recommendation)
   - Pregnancy: avoid systemic treatments; topical tacrolimus/pimecrolimus preferred
   - Sun protection: depigmented skin has no melanin protection; SPF 50+ mandatory

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Tacrolimus, pimecrolimus, low-potency steroids (short-term) | Systemic agents |
| **Phototherapy** | NB-UVB may be continued with caution | PUVA |
| **Systemic** | — | Oral steroids, JAK inhibitors, methotrexate |
| **Surgical** | — | Generally deferred |

## 15. Solar Lentigines

### Clinical Definition

Solar lentigines (senile lentigines, "age spots," "liver spots") are benign, well-circumscribed, hyperpigmented macules caused by chronic ultraviolet radiation exposure. They represent localized proliferation of melanocytes with increased melanin deposition in the basal layer. Despite the name "liver spots," they have no relation to liver disease.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L81.4 (Other melanin hyperpigmentation) or L81.0 (Disorders of pigmentation, unspecified)
- **Synonyms:** Senile lentigo, age spot, sun spot, liver spot

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Color:** Uniform light brown to dark brown; may have variegated color
- **Size:** 3 mm to 2+ cm; typically 5–15 mm
- **Shape:** Oval, round, or irregular; well-demarcated borders
- **Surface:** Flat (macular); no palpable elevation
- **Distribution:** Sun-exposed areas — face (malar area, temples), dorsal hands, forearms, upper chest, shoulders
- **Number:** Usually multiple; may be solitary
- **Age:** Typically > 40 years; earlier with significant sun exposure
- **Wood's lamp:** Enhances contrast; helps distinguish from freckles and seborrheic keratoses

### Differential Diagnosis: Solar Lentigo vs. Freckle

| Feature | Solar Lentigo | Freckle (Ephelis) |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Onset** | Adulthood (> 40) | Childhood |
| **Size** | Larger (5–15 mm) | Smaller (1–3 mm) |
| **Border** | Well-defined, sharp | Fuzzy, indistinct |
| **Color** | Uniform tan to dark brown | Light brown; fades in winter |
| **Sun exposure effect** | Permanent; does not fade | Darkens in summer; fades in winter |
| **Histology** | Elongated rete ridges with increased melanocytes | Normal melanocyte number; increased melanin only |
| **Malignant potential** | Very low; can evolve to lentigo maligna | None |

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mild** | Few (< 5) small lesions; subtle |
| **Moderate** | Multiple (5–20) visible lesions; cosmetically noticeable |
| **Severe** | Numerous (> 20) or large, confluent lesions; "weather-beaten" appearance |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Chronic UV exposure** | Strong | Cumulative sun damage; UVA primarily responsible for melanocyte stimulation |
| **Melanocyte proliferation** | Strong | Increased melanocyte number (unlike freckles) with increased melanin |
| **Epidermal hyperplasia** | Strong | Elongation of rete ridges (sawtooth pattern on histology) |
| **DNA damage** | Strong | UV-induced p53 mutations in keratinocytes; senescence-associated changes |
| **Genetic predisposition** | Moderate | Fitzpatrick I–III at higher risk; family history |
| **Immunosuppression** | Moderate | Organ transplant recipients have increased prevalence |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Factor | Evidence | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Lifetime sun exposure** | Strong | Cumulative dose; outdoor occupations, recreation |
| **Sunburns** | Moderate | Accelerate formation |
| **Tanning beds** | Strong | Artificial UV exposure; dose-dependent |
| **Age** | Strong | Prevalence increases with age; "age spots" |
| **Fitzpatrick I–III** | Strong | Higher risk due to less melanin protection |
| **Immunosuppression** | Moderate | Post-transplant; UV carcinogenesis |
| **Phototoxic medications** | Limited | May enhance UV damage |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Presentation | Treatment Considerations |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–II** | Early onset; numerous lesions; sun damage may include actinic keratoses | Good response to treatment; high skin cancer risk; need for skin cancer surveillance |
| **Fitzpatrick III–IV** | Fewer lesions; later onset; may be mistaken for melasma | Careful diagnosis; treatment response good |
| **Fitzpatrick V–VI** | Less common; may present as darker brown or gray-brown patches; higher risk of post-procedural dyspigmentation | Conservative treatment; high PIH risk from aggressive lasers/peels |

### Dermoscopy Patterns

| Pattern | Description | Significance |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| **Jellyfish pattern** | Central homogenous pigmentation with "fingers" extending outward | Classic solar lentigo |
| **Moth-eaten borders** | Geographic, sharply demarcated borders | Benign; characteristic |
| **Fingerprint pattern** | Fine parallel lines | Early solar lentigo |
| **Reticular pattern** | Fine pigment network | Common; benign |
| **Rhomboidal structures** | Gray-brown structures forming rhomboids | Indicates significant photodamage |

### Treatment Pathways

#### Prevention (Essential)

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Broad-spectrum sunscreen** | SPF 30+ daily; SPF 50+ for outdoor activity |
| **Physical blockers** | Zinc oxide, titanium dioxide |
| **Protective clothing** | Wide-brimmed hats, long sleeves, UV-protective fabrics |
| **Sun avoidance** | Peak hours (10 AM–4 PM) |
| **Regular skin checks** | Annual dermatology exam; self-examination monthly |

#### Topical Therapy (Mild Lesions)

| Agent | Concentration | Notes |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| **Hydroquinone** | 2–4% | First-line; 8–12 weeks; limit to 5–6 months |
| **Tretinoin** | 0.05–0.1% | Increases cell turnover; used with HQ |
| **Triple combination** | HQ + tretinoin + steroid | Fastest topical results |
| **Azelaic acid** | 15–20% | Alternative; safer for long-term |
| **Kojic acid** | 2–4% | Adjunctive |
| **Vitamin C** | 10–20% | Antioxidant; mild lightening |
| **Niacinamide** | 4–5% | Maintenance; prevents new lesions |
| **Cryosurgery** | Liquid nitrogen 5–10 seconds | Effective for individual lesions; blistering and PIH risk |

#### Professional Procedures (Moderate-Severe)

| Procedure | Mechanism | Sessions | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Q-switched Nd:YAG 1064 nm** | Selective photothermolysis of melanin | 1–3 sessions | Gold standard; effective for superficial and deep pigment |
| **Q-switched ruby 694 nm** | Targets melanin | 1–3 sessions | Very effective; risk of PIH in darker skin |
| **Q-switched alexandrite 755 nm** | Targets melanin | 1–3 sessions | Good for superficial lesions |
| **Intense Pulsed Light (IPL)** | Broad spectrum; targets melanin | 2–4 sessions | Good for diffuse photodamage; "photofacial" |
| **Fractional lasers** | Creates microthermal zones | 3–5 sessions | For associated photodamage; dual benefit |
| **Chemical peels** | Glycolic 30–70%, TCA 10–25% | Series of 4–6 | Adjunctive; superficial pigment |

**Laser/pearl selection by skin type:**
- Fitzpatrick I–III: Q-switched ruby, alexandrite, Nd:YAG, IPL
- Fitzpatrick IV: Q-switched Nd:YAG preferred; conservative settings
- Fitzpatrick V–VI: Q-switched Nd:YAG 1064 nm only; test spot mandatory; high PIH risk

#### Maintenance

- Daily broad-spectrum sunscreen (non-negotiable)
- Niacinamide or vitamin C serum
- Tretinoin for ongoing cell turnover and prevention
- Annual dermatology skin check

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Irregular borders, asymmetry, color variation** — must rule out lentigo maligna (melanoma in situ)
- [ ] **Rapid change in size, color, or texture** — biopsy indicated
- [ ] **Bleeding, ulceration, or itching** — concerning for malignancy
- [ ] **New solitary lesion in elderly** — lentigo maligna risk; dermoscopy or biopsy
- [ ] **Treatment resistance** — reassess diagnosis
- [ ] **Post-procedural PIH** — more common in darker skin; may require treatment

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Liver spots mean liver disease" | No relation to liver function; purely UV-related |
| "They're a normal part of aging you can't prevent" | Primarily sun-induced; strict photoprotection from youth prevents them |
| "Lasers remove them permanently" | Existing lesions clear, but new ones will form with continued sun exposure |
| "If you have many, you have a higher skin cancer risk" | True — extensive solar lentigines indicate significant photodamage; regular skin checks needed |
| "They can be scrubbed or peeled off at home" | Aggressive home peeling causes PIH and scarring; professional treatment needed |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Solar Lentigines:**

1. **Assessment:**
   - Distribution and number of lesions
   - Photodamage extent (solar elastosis, rhytides)
   - Fitzpatrick type for treatment safety
   - Distinguish from melasma, seborrheic keratosis, lentigo maligna

2. **Prevention Emphasis:**
   - Daily sunscreen education and tracking
   - Hat and protective clothing recommendations
   - UV index alerts

3. **Treatment Planning:**
   - **Mild/Few lesions:** Topical HQ 2% + tretinoin + strict SPF
   - **Moderate/Multiple:** Professional Q-switched laser or IPL referral
   - **Severe/Diffuse:** Combination laser + topical maintenance

4. **Safety Checks:**
   - Screen for skin cancer history and risk factors
   - Flag suspicious lesions for dermatology evaluation
   - Pregnancy: avoid retinoids, HQ; use azelaic acid and physical sunscreen
   - Darker skin: conservative laser settings; PIH prevention

5. **Maintenance:**
   - Emphasize that sun protection prevents new lesions
   - Retinoid maintenance for ongoing prevention
   - Annual skin check reminders

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Azelaic acid, vitamin C, niacinamide, physical sunscreen | Hydroquinone, tretinoin, retinoids |
| **Procedures** | — | All elective laser and peel procedures |

# Aging & Vascular Conditions

## 16. Photoaging

### Clinical Definition

Photoaging (dermatoheliosis, extrinsic aging) is premature skin aging caused by chronic ultraviolet radiation exposure. It clinically and histologically differs from intrinsic aging, with UV radiation responsible for up to 80% of visible facial aging signs.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L57.8 (Other skin changes due to chronic exposure to nonionizing radiation)
- **Related codes:**
  - L57.0 — Actinic keratosis
  - L57.3 — Polymorphic light eruption
  - L57.4 — Cutis rhomboidalis nuchae

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Distribution:** Sun-exposed areas — face, neck ("V" of chest), dorsal hands, forearms, lower legs
- **Rhytides:** Fine and deep wrinkles; "crow's feet," glabellar lines, perioral lines
- **Pigmentation:** Solar lentigines, mottled hyperpigmentation, guttate hypomelanosis
- **Vascular:** Telangiectasias, cherry angiomas, purpura
- **Skin texture:** Roughness, dryness, scaling, actinic keratoses
- **Dermal changes:** Solar elastosis (yellow, thickened, leathery skin), loss of elasticity, solar comedones
- **Nail changes:** Horizontal ridges, discoloration

### Glogau Classification of Photoaging

| Type | Classification | Typical Age | Skin Characteristics | Wrinkles |
|------|---------------|-------------|----------------------|----------|
| **I** | Mild | 20s–30s | Minimal wrinkles; early pigment changes; no keratoses | Wrinkles only with facial expression |
| **II** | Moderate | 30s–40s | Early smile lines; visible solar lentigines; palpable but not visible keratoses | Wrinkles present at rest and with expression |
| **III** | Advanced | 50s–60s | Persistent wrinkling; obvious discoloration; visible actinic keratoses | Wrinkles present at rest; no wrinkles absent |
| **IV** | Severe | 60s–70s+ | Yellow-gray skin color; prior skin malignancies; extensive actinic damage | Wrinkling throughout; no normal skin visible |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **UVA (320–400 nm)** | Strong | Penetrates deep into dermis; primary driver of photoaging; generates ROS; breaks down collagen and elastin |
| **UVB (280–320 nm)** | Strong | Epidermal damage; DNA mutations; sunburn; contributes to photocarcinogenesis |
| **UVC (100–280 nm)** | Minimal | Absorbed by ozone layer; minimal terrestrial exposure |
| **Infrared (IR) and visible light** | Moderate | Contributes to collagen degradation; generates heat-induced ROS |
| **Reactive oxygen species (ROS)** | Strong | Oxidative damage to DNA, proteins, lipids; matrix metalloproteinase (MMP) activation |
| **Matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)** | Strong | UV upregulates MMP-1, MMP-3, MMP-9; degrades collagen and elastin |
| **DNA damage** | Strong | Pyrimidine dimers; mitochondrial DNA mutations; p53 tumor suppressor pathway activation |
| **Advanced glycation end-products (AGEs)** | Moderate | Accumulate with age and UV exposure; cross-link collagen |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Factor | Evidence | Impact |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **Lifetime cumulative UV exposure** | Strong | Primary driver; outdoor occupations, recreation, residence near equator |
| **Tanning beds** | Strong | Artificial UV; accelerates photoaging; carcinogenic |
| **Smoking** | Strong | Independent risk factor; synergistic with UV; reduces blood flow; generates ROS |
| **Fitzpatrick I–III** | Moderate | Higher risk due to less melanin; more visible damage |
| **Altitude** | Moderate | Higher UV intensity at altitude |
| **Occupational exposure** | Strong | Outdoor workers, pilots, sailors |
| **Medications** | Moderate | Photosensitizing drugs (tetracyclines, thiazides, amiodarone, retinoids) |
| **Alcohol** | Moderate | Dehydration; nutritional depletion; vasodilation |
| **Poor nutrition** | Moderate | Antioxidant deficiency; impaired repair |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Photoaging Presentation | Treatment Considerations |
|-----------|------------------------|------------------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–II** | Early wrinkling (30s); prominent solar lentigines; telangiectasias; actinic keratoses; skin cancer risk very high | Aggressive photoprotection; regular skin checks; good response to lasers and peels |
| **Fitzpatrick III–IV** | Later onset (40s–50s); solar lentigines; mild wrinkling; less actinic keratosis | Moderate approach; good response to most treatments |
| **Fitzpatrick V–VI** | "Ashy" appearance; mottled pigmentation; solar lentigines; wrinkling less prominent until older age; pseudoacanthosis nigricans on neck | PIH risk from aggressive treatments; focus on pigment and texture; conservative energy settings |

### Treatment Pathways

#### Prevention (Primary)

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Broad-spectrum sunscreen** | SPF 30+ daily; SPF 50+ for extended outdoor activity; reapply every 2 hours |
| **Antioxidant serums** | Vitamin C, vitamin E, ferulic acid — applied in morning under sunscreen |
| **Retinoids** | Tretinoin 0.025–0.1% nightly — prevents and reverses photoaging |
| **Protective clothing** | UPF-rated fabrics, wide-brimmed hats, sunglasses |
| **Avoid tanning beds** | Complete avoidance |
| **Smoking cessation** | Critical for skin health |
| **Diet** | Antioxidant-rich foods; adequate protein for collagen synthesis |

#### Topical Treatments

| Ingredient | Concentration | Mechanism | Indication |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|------------|
| **Tretinoin** | 0.025–0.1% | Increases collagen synthesis; normalizes keratinization; reduces MMPs | Gold standard for photoaging; all severities |
| **Tazarotene** | 0.1% | Similar to tretinoin; more potent | Moderate-severe photodamage |
| **Adapalene** | 0.3% | Retinoid; less irritating; some anti-aging benefit | Mild-moderate; good for sensitive skin |
| **Vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid)** | 10–20% | Antioxidant; stimulates collagen; inhibits tyrosinase | Photoprotection; pigment; fine lines |
| **Niacinamide** | 4–5% | Increases ceramide synthesis; reduces TEWL; anti-inflammatory | Barrier repair; pigment; texture |
| **Alpha-hydroxy acids (AHA)** | Glycolic 8–15% | Exfoliation; collagen stimulation | Texture; fine lines; pigment |
| **Growth factors** | Variable | Stimulate cell turnover and repair | Adjunctive; emerging evidence |
| **Peptides** | Variable | Signal collagen production | Adjunctive; limited independent evidence |

#### Professional Procedures

| Procedure | Mechanism | Sessions | Indication |
|-----------|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Botulinum toxin** | Neuromuscular blockade; reduces dynamic wrinkles | Every 3–4 months | Forehead lines, crow's feet, glabellar lines |
| **Dermal fillers** | Volume restoration; stimulates collagen | 6–18 months | Nasolabial folds, marionette lines, temples, cheeks |
| **Chemical peels** | Controlled wounding; collagen remodeling | Series of 3–6 | Superficial pigment; fine lines; texture |
| **Microneedling** | Percutaneous collagen induction | 3–6 sessions | Fine lines; scars; texture; all skin types |
| **Fractional lasers (ablative)** | CO2, Er:YAG — vaporize tissue columns | 1–3 sessions | Deep wrinkles; significant photodamage |
| **Fractional lasers (non-ablative)** | 1540 nm, 1440 nm — thermal injury without vaporization | 3–6 sessions | Mild-moderate wrinkles; less downtime |
| **Radiofrequency (RF)** | Thermal collagen remodeling | 3–6 sessions | Skin laxity; jowls; neck |
| **Ultrasound (HIFU)** | Deep thermal injury to SMAS layer | 1 session/year | Skin lifting; brow, neck, jawline |
| **IPL/photofacial** | Broad spectrum; targets pigment and vessels | 3–6 sessions | Solar lentigines; telangiectasias; diffuse redness |
| **Thread lifts** | Mechanical lifting with dissolvable threads | 1–2 years | Mid-face lifting; jawline contouring |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Changing or suspicious lesions** — rule out skin cancer (BCC, SCC, melanoma)
- [ ] **Rapidly enlarging solar lentigo** — lentigo maligna risk
- [ ] **Non-healing ulcer or persistent scab** — possible SCC
- [ ] **Treatment complications** — burns, PIH, scarring from procedures
- [ ] **Unrealistic expectations** — photoaging cannot be fully reversed; goal is improvement and prevention

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Photoaging is just about wrinkles" | Includes pigment changes, vascular lesions, texture, and skin cancer risk |
| "You can't reverse sun damage" | Significant improvement possible with retinoids, procedures, and strict photoprotection |
| "A base tan protects against photoaging" | No safe tan; any UV exposure contributes to photoaging |
| "Sunscreen is only needed on sunny days" | UVA penetrates clouds; daily use is essential |
| "Expensive creams can replace sunscreen" | No topical can replace photoprotection; sunscreen is foundational |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Photoaging:**

1. **Assessment:**
   - Glogau classification
   - Photodamage extent (solar lentigines, wrinkling, texture)
   - Skin cancer risk factors
   - Fitzpatrick type
   - Lifestyle factors (smoking, occupation, sun habits)

2. **Prevention Emphasis:**
   - Daily sunscreen compliance tracking
   - UV index-based alerts
   - Antioxidant and retinoid education

3. **Treatment Ladder:**
   - **Type I (Mild):** Sunscreen + vitamin C + retinoid introduction
   - **Type II (Moderate):** Add professional treatments (peels, IPL, botulinum toxin)
   - **Type III–IV (Advanced/Severe):** Comprehensive approach; dermatology/plastic surgery referral for procedural options

4. **Safety Checks:**
   - Skin cancer screening reminders
   - Pregnancy: avoid retinoids, botulinum toxin, fillers
   - Darker skin: conservative procedural settings; PIH prevention

5. **Realistic Expectations:**
   - Improvement, not reversal
   - Prevention is more effective than treatment
   - Consistency matters more than intensity

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Vitamin C, niacinamide, peptides, hyaluronic acid, physical sunscreen | Retinoids (tretinoin, tazarotene, adapalene), high-dose salicylic acid |
| **Procedures** | — | Botulinum toxin, fillers, lasers, deep peels, RF, ultrasound |
| **Sunscreen** | Zinc oxide, titanium dioxide | Chemical sunscreens (some concerns) |

## 17. Intrinsic Aging

### Clinical Definition

Intrinsic aging (chronological aging, natural aging) is the genetically programmed, time-dependent deterioration of skin structure and function that occurs independent of environmental factors. It represents the baseline aging process affecting all skin, including sun-protected areas.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L98.9 (Disorder of the skin and subcutaneous tissue, unspecified) or R23.8 (Other skin changes)
- **Nature:** Physiological, universal, unavoidable

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Distribution:** Universal; affects sun-protected areas (buttocks, abdomen, inner arm) equally with sun-exposed areas
- **Skin thinning:** Fine, translucent skin; decreased epidermal and dermal thickness
- **Dryness (xerosis):** Reduced sebum production; decreased skin hydration
- **Fine wrinkles:** Shallow, superficial lines; not deep furrows
- **Loss of elasticity:** Laxity; poor recoil
- **Hair changes:** Graying (canities); thinning; decreased density
- **Nail changes:** Slow growth; ridging; brittleness
- **Vascular:** Easy bruising (senile purpura); fragile capillaries
- **Fat redistribution:** Atrophy of facial fat pads; hollowing of temples, cheeks, orbits

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Features |
|-------|----------|
| **Mild (30s–40s)** | Fine lines; subtle dryness; early texture changes; minimal volume loss |
| **Moderate (50s–60s)** | Visible fine wrinkles; moderate dryness; noticeable thinning; early jowling; graying hair |
| **Severe (70s+)** | Deep fine wrinkling; significant thinning; marked laxity; prominent volume loss; easy bruising; pronounced hair thinning |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Telomere shortening** | Strong | With each cell division, telomeres shorten; replicative senescence occurs when critically short |
| **Cellular senescence** | Strong | Accumulation of senescent cells that secrete SASP (senescence-associated secretory phenotype) pro-inflammatory factors |
| **Declining estrogen/androgens** | Strong | Post-menopausal collagen loss accelerates; decreased skin thickness, elasticity, and hydration |
| **Decreased collagen synthesis** | Strong | Type I and III collagen production declines ~1% per year after age 20 |
| **Elastin degradation** | Moderate | Elastin fibers fragment and degrade; loss of recoil |
| **Reduced subcutaneous fat** | Moderate | Facial fat atrophy leads to hollowing and sagging |
| **Bone resorption** | Moderate | Maxillary and mandibular bone loss contributes to facial volume loss |
| **Decreased blood supply** | Moderate | Reduced dermal vascularity impairs nutrient delivery |
| **Mitochondrial dysfunction** | Moderate | Accumulation of mitochondrial DNA mutations; reduced ATP production |
| **Proteostasis decline** | Emerging | Impaired protein quality control; accumulation of damaged proteins |
| **Hormonal changes (menopause)** | Strong | Estrogen decline causes rapid collagen loss (up to 30% in first 5 years post-menopause) |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Factor | Evidence | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Genetics** | Strong | Determines rate of intrinsic aging; family patterns |
| **Hormonal status** | Strong | Menopause accelerates skin aging in women; andropause in men |
| **Chronic diseases** | Moderate | Diabetes, vascular disease impair skin health |
| **Nutrition** | Moderate | Protein, antioxidant, and micronutrient deficiency |
| **Sleep quality** | Moderate | Poor sleep accelerates aging markers |
| **Chronic stress** | Moderate | Cortisol elevation degrades collagen |
| **Gravity** | Moderate | Contributes to ptosis over decades |

### Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic (Photo) Aging

| Feature | Intrinsic Aging | Photoaging (Extrinsic) |
|---------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Cause** | Time, genetics, hormones | UV radiation |
| **Distribution** | Universal; sun-protected = sun-exposed | Sun-exposed areas predominant |
| **Wrinkles** | Fine, superficial, uniform | Coarse, deep, "leathery" |
| **Pigmentation** | Uniform fading; guttate hypomelanosis | Solar lentigines; mottled pigmentation |
| **Skin texture** | Thin, dry, smooth | Thickened, leathery, rough, solar elastosis |
| **Vascular** | Easy bruising, senile purpura | Telangiectasias, cherry angiomas |
| **Neoplasia risk** | Baseline | Significantly increased |
| **Reversibility** | Limited | Partially reversible with photoprotection and treatment |

### Treatment Pathways

#### Topical Approaches

| Ingredient | Concentration | Mechanism | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|-------|
| **Retinoids** | Tretinoin 0.025–0.1% | Stimulate collagen; increase cell turnover | Gold standard; addresses both intrinsic and photoaging |
| **Estrogen/progesterone (topical)** | Variable | Increases collagen, hyaluronic acid, skin thickness | Limited availability; systemic HRT has skin benefits |
| **Growth factors** | Variable | Signal repair and regeneration | Emerging; mixed evidence |
| **Peptides** | Variable | Collagen signaling | Adjunctive |
| **Hyaluronic acid** | Various molecular weights | Hydration; plumping | Immediate cosmetic benefit |
| **Ceramides** | Variable | Barrier repair; reduce TEWL | Essential for dry, aging skin |
| **Niacinamide** | 4–5% | Ceramide synthesis; anti-inflammatory; barrier support | Well-tolerated; good for all skin types |
| **Alpha-hydroxy acids** | Lactic acid 5–12% | Gentle exfoliation; hydration | Good for dry, sensitive aging skin |
| **Antioxidants** | Vitamin C, E, CoQ10, resveratrol | Combat oxidative stress | Preventive; adjunctive |

#### Systemic Hormonal Therapy (for Post-Menopausal Women)

| Therapy | Skin Benefits | Risks |
|---------|--------------|-------|
| **Systemic HRT** | Increases collagen, skin thickness, elasticity; reduces wrinkles | Thromboembolism, breast cancer, cardiovascular risk (depends on timing and formulation) |
| **Tibolone** | Similar to HRT | Androgenic side effects; stroke risk |
| **Phytoestrogens** | Modest skin benefits | Limited evidence; variable |

**Note:** HRT decisions require individualized risk-benefit analysis with gynecologist.

#### Professional Procedures

| Procedure | Indication | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| **Dermal fillers (HA)** | Volume loss; nasolabial folds; temples; lips | Immediate results; temporary (6–18 months) |
| **Sculptra (PLLA)** | Global facial volume restoration | Stimulates collagen; gradual (3–6 months); lasts 2+ years |
| **Radiesse (CaHA)** | Volume; jawline; hands | Immediate + collagen stimulation |
| **Skin boosters** | Micro-injections of hyaluronic acid for hydration | Fine lines; texture; glow |
| **Thread lifts** | Mild-moderate laxity | Mechanical lifting; 1–2 year duration |
| **Radiofrequency microneedling** | Collagen remodeling; laxity; texture | All skin types; minimal downtime |
| **Laser resurfacing** | Wrinkles; texture; pigment | Ablative or fractional options |
| **Fat grafting** | Severe volume loss | Autologous; longer lasting; surgical |

#### Lifestyle & Nutrition

| Intervention | Evidence | Rationale |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| **Adequate protein intake** | Moderate | Collagen synthesis requires amino acids |
| **Collagen peptides** | Moderate | Oral supplementation may improve skin elasticity and hydration |
| **Omega-3 fatty acids** | Moderate | Anti-inflammatory; supports cell membrane integrity |
| **Antioxidant-rich diet** | Moderate | Combats oxidative stress |
| **Adequate hydration** | Limited | Maintains skin turgor |
| **Sleep optimization** | Moderate | Growth hormone release during sleep supports repair |
| **Exercise** | Moderate | Improves circulation; supports mitochondrial health |
| **Stress management** | Moderate | Reduces cortisol-mediated collagen degradation |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Sudden accelerated aging** — consider underlying disease (Cushing's, hypothyroidism, malnutrition)
- [ ] **Easy bruising + other symptoms** — rule out coagulopathy, platelet dysfunction, medication side effects
- [ ] **Skin fragility with minimal trauma** — dermatoporosis; increased wound healing risk
- [ ] **Unintentional weight loss + skin changes** — malignancy workup

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Intrinsic aging is inevitable and can't be slowed" | While unavoidable, lifestyle, skincare, and hormonal optimization can significantly improve appearance and skin health |
| "Expensive anti-aging creams reverse aging" | No cream can fully reverse intrinsic aging; retinoids and procedures offer modest improvement |
| "Collagen supplements replace lost collagen" | Oral collagen peptides may stimulate synthesis but don't directly replace dermal collagen |
| "Intrinsic aging is separate from photoaging" | They interact synergistically; photoprotection benefits both |
| "Men don't experience hormonal skin aging" | Andropause causes decreased testosterone, affecting skin thickness and sebum |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Intrinsic Aging:**

1. **Assessment:**
   - Age and menopausal status
   - Skin thickness, elasticity, hydration
   - Volume loss assessment
   - Lifestyle factors (nutrition, sleep, stress, smoking)
   - Distinguish intrinsic vs. photoaging components

2. **Foundational Skincare:**
   - Retinoid introduction (tretinoin or adapalene)
   - Hydration: hyaluronic acid, ceramides
   - Antioxidants: vitamin C, niacinamide
   - Gentle cleansing (avoid over-drying)

3. **Volume and Laxity:**
   - Education on filler options for volume loss
   - RF microneedling and ultrasound for laxity
   - Thread lifts for appropriate candidates

4. **Hormonal Considerations:**
   - For post-menopausal women: discuss HRT skin benefits with physician
   - Men: andropause considerations

5. **Lifestyle Integration:**
   - Nutrition guidance (protein, antioxidants)
   - Sleep optimization
   - Stress management
   - Exercise recommendations

6. **Realistic Expectations:**
   - Intrinsic aging cannot be reversed; goal is optimization
   - Consistency over intensity
   - Combine topical, procedural, and lifestyle approaches

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Hyaluronic acid, ceramides, niacinamide, peptides, vitamin C (stable forms) | Retinoids, high-dose salicylic acid, growth factors (limited data) |
| **Procedures** | — | Fillers, toxins, lasers, deep peels, RF, ultrasound |
| **Supplements** | Standard prenatal vitamins | Collagen supplements (limited data), high-dose vitamin A |

## 18. Telangiectasia

### Clinical Definition

Telangiectasias (spider veins, broken capillaries) are permanently dilated, superficial blood vessels visible on the skin surface. They represent venous or capillary ectasia and can be primary (idiopathic) or secondary to various skin conditions or environmental factors.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** I78.1 (Nevus, non-neoplastic) or L93.1 (Erythema, not elsewhere classified) — use I78.1 for vascular telangiectasia
- **Synonyms:** Spider veins, broken capillaries, vascular ectasia, angioectasia

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Morphology:** Fine, linear, arborizing (branching), or punctate red-to-purple vessels
- **Color:** Red (capillaries), blue (venules), purple (mixture)
- **Size:** 0.1–1.0 mm in diameter
- **Distribution:** Face (cheeks, nose, chin), legs (spider veins), chest, hands
- **Blanching:** Blanches with diascopy (pressure), then refills
- **Types:**
  - **Simple/linear:** Straight, single vessel
  - **Arborizing:** Branching pattern like a tree
  - **Spider (starburst):** Central punctum with radiating vessels (associated with liver disease, pregnancy, or normal variant)
  - **Papular:** Raised, small vascular papules (e.g., angioma)

### Classification by Cause

| Type | Features | Associations |
|------|----------|------------|
| **Primary/idiopathic** | No underlying cause; genetic predisposition | Family history; fair skin |
| **Rosacea-associated** | Central face; persistent background erythema | Erythematotelangiectatic rosacea |
| **Sun damage-related** | Cheeks, nose, temples; solar elastosis background | Photoaging |
| **Pregnancy-related** | Facial or lower extremity; often resolve postpartum | Hormonal vasodilation |
| **Liver disease** | Spider angiomas; central punctum with radiating vessels | Cirrhosis, elevated estrogen |
| **Corticosteroid-induced** | Diffuse, fine vessels; atrophic skin | Long-term topical or systemic steroid use |
| **Radiation-induced** | Within radiation field | Prior radiation therapy |
| **Connective tissue disease** | Periungual, facial mat-like telangiectasias | Scleroderma, CREST syndrome, lupus |
| **Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT)** | Multiple mucocutaneous and visceral telangiectasias | Autosomal dominant; GI bleeding, AVMs |
| **Chronic venous insufficiency** | Lower extremity spider veins; varicose veins | Venous hypertension |

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mild** | Few fine vessels; subtle; requires close inspection |
| **Moderate** | Multiple visible vessels; cosmetically noticeable; background erythema |
| **Severe** | Dense, confluent network; prominent; associated with background redness or varicosities |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Vascular wall weakness** | Strong | Structural weakness of vessel walls; loss of elastic tissue |
| **Chronic vasodilation** | Strong | Persistent dilation from heat, alcohol, UV, hormones |
| **UV radiation damage** | Strong | Degrades dermal collagen and elastin; vessel support structures weaken |
| **Genetic predisposition** | Strong | Family history; HHT (autosomal dominant; ENG or ACVRL1 mutations) |
| **Venous hypertension** | Strong | Valve incompetence increases pressure in superficial veins |
| **Hormonal factors** | Moderate | Estrogen causes vasodilation; pregnancy, oral contraceptives |
| **Inflammation** | Moderate | Chronic skin inflammation (rosacea) damages vessel walls |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Factor | Evidence | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Sun exposure** | Strong | Primary preventable cause; UVA damages vessel support |
| **Alcohol** | Moderate | Vasodilation; associated with spider angiomas in liver disease |
| **Heat/hot beverages** | Moderate | Temporary dilation; may contribute over time |
| **Rosacea** | Strong | Chronic inflammation; neurovascular dysregulation |
| **Pregnancy** | Moderate | Estrogen-mediated; often improve postpartum |
| **Corticosteroids** | Moderate | Topical and systemic cause vessel fragility |
| **Aging** | Strong | Natural loss of dermal support structures |
| **Trauma** | Moderate | Injuries can cause localized telangiectasia |
| **Occupational** | Moderate | Prolonged standing (leg veins); extreme temperatures |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Visibility | Treatment Considerations |
|-----------|------------|------------------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–II** | Highly visible; bright red; may be mistaken for flushing or rosacea | Excellent laser response; less PIH risk |
| **Fitzpatrick III–IV** | Visible; may appear more purple | Good laser response; moderate PIH risk |
| **Fitzpatrick V–VI** | Less visible against darker skin; may be underdiagnosed | Laser selection critical (Nd:YAG preferred); higher PIH risk; ensure treatment is desired by patient |

### Treatment Pathways

#### Topical (Limited Efficacy)

| Ingredient | Mechanism | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Brimonidine 0.33% gel** | Alpha-2 agonist; vasoconstriction | FDA-approved for rosacea erythema; temporary (12 hours); not for vessel destruction |
| **Oxymetazoline 1% cream** | Alpha-1 agonist; vasoconstriction | Similar to brimonidine; temporary effect |
| **Vitamin K cream** | Theoretical vessel strengthening | Limited evidence |
| **Horse chestnut extract** | Venotonic | Limited evidence for leg veins |

**Note:** Topicals constrict vessels temporarily but do not eliminate telangiectasias. Destructive treatment is needed for permanent clearance.

#### Laser and Light Therapy (Gold Standard)

| Modality | Wavelength | Target | Indication | Sessions |
|----------|-----------|--------|------------|----------|
| **Pulsed Dye Laser (PDL)** | 585–595 nm | Oxyhemoglobin | Facial telangiectasias; rosacea; port wine stains | 1–3 |
| **KTP Laser** | 532 nm | Superficial oxyhemoglobin | Fine facial vessels; small lesions | 1–3 |
| **Nd:YAG 1064 nm** | 1064 nm | Deeper vessels; deoxygenated blood | Leg veins; deeper facial vessels; darker skin | 1–4 |
| **IPL** | 500–1200 nm | Hemoglobin + melanin | Diffuse redness; multiple small vessels; photodamage | 3–5 |
| **Long-pulse Alexandrite** | 755 nm | Vessels | Leg veins; larger vessels |

**Treatment pearls:**
- PDL is gold standard for facial telangiectasias (purpura setting) or subpurpuric (no bruising)
- Nd:YAG preferred for darker skin and leg veins
- KTP good for very fine, superficial vessels
- IPL best for diffuse redness with multiple vessels
- Leg veins may require sclerotherapy

#### Sclerotherapy (Leg Telangiectasias and Varicose Veins)

| Sclerosant | Concentration | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-------|
| **Polidocanol** | 0.5–1% | FDA-approved; anesthetic; low allergy risk |
| **Sodium tetradecyl sulfate (STS)** | 0.1–0.5% | Effective; higher irritation/allergy risk |
| **Hypertonic saline** | 11.7–23.4% | No allergy risk; painful; skin necrosis risk |
| **Glycerin** | 72% | For small vessels; low side effect profile |

**Mechanism:** Injection of sclerosant into vessel causes endothelial damage, fibrosis, and vessel obliteration.

#### Electrocautery/Electrodesiccation

- Direct destruction of vessels with electric current
- Good for isolated, small facial vessels
- Risk of scarring if too aggressive
- Requires skill

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Multiple spider angiomas + signs of liver disease** — jaundice, ascites, palmar erythema; hepatology referral
- [ ] **Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT)** — multiple mucocutaneous lesions + family history; screen for visceral AVMs and GI bleeding
- [ ] **CREST syndrome** — calcinosis, Raynaud's, esophageal dysmotility, sclerodactyly, telangiectasia; rheumatology referral
- [ ] **Sudden onset with systemic symptoms** — rule out underlying systemic disease
- [ ] **Associated with varicose veins, leg swelling, or skin changes** — chronic venous insufficiency; vascular surgery referral
- [ ] **Treatment failure or paradoxical worsening** — reconsider diagnosis (e.g., angiosarcoma, amelanotic melanoma)

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Spider veins are broken" | The vessels are dilated and visible, not broken; blood still flows through them |
| "Crossing your legs causes spider veins" | Not directly; prolonged standing and genetics are bigger factors; leg crossing may slightly impede venous return |
| "Topical creams can eliminate spider veins" | No topical cream permanently removes established telangiectasias; lasers or sclerotherapy required |
| "Spider veins are only a cosmetic concern" | Leg telangiectasias can indicate venous insufficiency; facial spider angiomas can indicate liver disease |
| "Laser treatment is permanent" | Existing vessels are destroyed, but new ones can form with continued sun exposure, aging, or underlying conditions |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Telangiectasia:**

1. **Assessment:**
   - Distribution (face vs. legs vs. generalized)
   - Associated conditions (rosacea, pregnancy, liver disease)
   - Triggers (heat, alcohol, sun)
   - Fitzpatrick type
   - Family history

2. **Cause Identification:**
   - Isolated facial: likely photoaging or rosacea
   - Central face + flushing: possible rosacea
   - Multiple spider angiomas: screen for liver disease
   - Leg veins: venous insufficiency?
   - Diffuse + family history: HHT?

3. **Treatment Matching:**
   - **Mild/Few facial:** Concealing makeup; brimonidine for temporary improvement
   - **Moderate facial:** PDL or KTP laser referral; 1–3 sessions
   - **Diffuse facial redness + vessels:** IPL or PDL
   - **Leg veins:** Sclerotherapy (gold standard) or Nd:YAG laser
   - **Darker skin:** Nd:YAG 1064 nm preferred; conservative settings

4. **Prevention:**
   - Daily sunscreen (UV is primary preventable cause)
   - Avoid excessive heat and alcohol
   - Rosacea trigger management
   - Leg vein prevention: compression stockings if standing occupation; elevate legs

5. **Safety Checks:**
   - Screen for liver disease if multiple spider angiomas
   - Pregnancy: defer elective treatments; vessels often improve postpartum
   - History of blood clots: caution with leg sclerotherapy
   - Diabetes: caution with leg procedures; wound healing risk

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Limited; brimonidine/oxymetazoline have limited data | — |
| **Procedures** | — | All elective laser, sclerotherapy, and electrocautery deferred |
| **Compression** | Compression stockings for leg symptoms | — |

**Note:** Pregnancy commonly causes new facial and leg telangiectasias; most improve within 3–6 months postpartum. Reassure and defer treatment.

# Pre-Cancerous & Keratinization Conditions

## 19. Actinic Keratosis

### Clinical Definition

Actinic keratosis (AK, solar keratosis) is a pre-malignant lesion caused by chronic ultraviolet radiation exposure. It represents intraepidermal keratinocyte dysplasia with potential to progress to invasive squamous cell carcinoma (SCC). AKs are the most common pre-malignant skin lesions, affecting millions of adults.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L57.0 (Actinic keratosis)
- **Synonyms:** Solar keratosis, senile keratosis (outdated term)
- **Malignant potential:** 0.025–20% per lesion per year may progress to SCC; risk increases with number of lesions and immunosuppression

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **History:** Chronic sun exposure; typically age > 40; outdoor occupation/recreation
- **Morphology:** Rough, scaly, sandpaper-like patch or thin plaque
- **Color:** Skin-colored, pink, red, or pigmented
- **Size:** 2–6 mm; can coalesce into larger patches
- **Texture:** Palpable scale or roughness more important than visible changes
- **Distribution:** Sun-exposed areas — face (forehead, cheeks, nose, ears), scalp (bald men), dorsal hands, forearms, chest, lower legs (women)
- **Tenderness:** May be tender or asymptomatic
- **Subtypes:**
  - **Classic/ Hypertrophic:** Thick, scaly plaque
  - **Atrophic:** Smooth, red, thin plaque
  - **Atypical/Pigmented:** Brown or gray; can mimic melanoma
  - **Bowenoid:** Large, thick; higher SCC risk
  - **Cutaneous horn:** Hyperkeratotic projection from AK base

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Grade I (Mild)** | Slightly palpable, barely visible; better felt than seen |
| **Grade II (Moderate)** | Moderately thick hyperkeratosis; easily visible and palpable |
| **Grade III (Severe)** | Very thick, hyperkeratotic; confluent; may be tender or ulcerated |

**AK Field Cancerization:**

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Clinical field** | Visible AKs + surrounding sun-damaged skin |
| **Clinical-cytological field** | Visible AKs + subclinical lesions detectable by biopsy |
| **Clinical-cytological-histological field** | Full spectrum from normal-appearing skin → subclinical AK → clinical AK → SCC |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **UVB-induced DNA damage** | Strong | Pyrimidine dimers; UV signature mutations in p53, NOTCH1, HRAS |
| **Chronic immunosuppression** | Strong | Organ transplant recipients have 65–100x increased SCC risk; AKs more numerous and aggressive |
| **Cumulative UV exposure** | Strong | Dose-response relationship; outdoor workers at highest risk |
| **Fair skin (Fitzpatrick I–II)** | Strong | Less melanin protection; highest prevalence |
| **Aging** | Strong | Decreased DNA repair capacity; immune senescence |
| **HPV infection** | Moderate | Beta-HPV types may contribute in immunosuppressed |
| **Oxidative stress** | Moderate | UV-generated ROS damage keratinocyte DNA |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Factor | Evidence | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Lifetime sun exposure** | Strong | Cumulative dose; latitude, altitude, outdoor occupation |
| **Sunburns** | Moderate | Especially blistering burns in childhood |
| **Tanning beds** | Strong | Significantly increase AK and SCC risk |
| **Immunosuppression** | Strong | Organ transplant, chemotherapy, HIV, chronic steroids |
| **Advanced age** | Strong | Prevalence increases with age; > 80% of elderly have AKs |
| **Fitzpatrick I–III** | Strong | Higher risk |
| **Male sex** | Moderate | Higher prevalence; more outdoor exposure |
| **Xeroderma pigmentosum** | Strong | DNA repair defect; extreme AK and skin cancer risk |
| **Prior skin cancer** | Strong | Marker of field cancerization; increased risk of future SCC |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Risk | Presentation | Treatment Considerations |
|-----------|------|--------------|------------------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–II** | Very high | Early onset (40s); numerous lesions; scalp (men), ears, dorsal hands | Aggressive treatment; frequent skin checks; field therapy |
| **Fitzpatrick III–IV** | Moderate | Later onset; fewer lesions; may be pigmented | Ensure diagnosis; don't miss pigmented AKs |
| **Fitzpatrick V–VI** | Lower but not zero | Less common; pigmented; may be misdiagnosed as seborrheic keratosis or melanoma | Lower threshold for biopsy; careful dermoscopic examination |

### Treatment Pathways

#### Field Therapy (For Multiple AKs/Field Cancerization)

| Treatment | Mechanism | Application | Duration | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|----------|-------|
| **5-Fluorouracil (5-FU) 0.5–5% cream** | Antimetabolite; inhibits DNA/RNA synthesis | Daily to affected area | 2–4 weeks | Classic field therapy; inflammatory response expected (erythema, crusting); 2–4 week "reaction" |
| **Imiquimod 2.5–5% cream** | TLR7 agonist; stimulates innate immunity | 2–3x weekly | 4–16 weeks | Strong local inflammatory response; effective for field therapy |
| **Diclofenac 3% gel** | NSAID; COX-2 inhibition | BID | 60–90 days | Less inflammatory than 5-FU or imiquimod; slower; good for mild AKs |
| **Tirbanibulin 1% ointment** | Microtubule inhibitor; novel mechanism | Once daily | 5 days | Shortest regimen; FDA-approved 2020; field-directed |
| **Ingenol mebutate 0.015–0.05% gel** | Protein kinase C activator; cell death + inflammation | Once daily | 2–3 days | Very short course; potent inflammatory response |

#### Lesion-Directed Therapy (For Individual AKs)

| Treatment | Indication | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| **Cryotherapy (liquid nitrogen)** | Few, discrete AKs; first-line for limited lesions | 5–10 second freeze; blistering expected; 85–90% clearance |
| **Curettage and electrodessication (C&E)** | Hypertrophic or resistant AKs | Local anesthesia; scrape + cauterize; good for thick lesions |
| **Shave excision** | Suspicious for SCC; diagnostic and therapeutic | Send to pathology |
| **Photodynamic therapy (PDT)** | Multiple AKs; field cancerization; cosmetically sensitive areas | Apply ALA or MAL → incubate → activate with blue or red light; excellent cosmetic results |

#### Comparison of Field-Directed Treatments

| Treatment | Efficacy | Inflammation | Duration | Cost | Convenience |
|-----------|----------|--------------|----------|------|-------------|
| **5-FU** | High | High | 2–4 weeks | Low | Moderate |
| **Imiquimod** | High | High | 4–16 weeks | High | Moderate |
| **Diclofenac** | Moderate | Low | 60–90 days | Moderate | High |
| **Tirbanibulin** | High | Moderate | 5 days | High | Very high |
| **Ingenol mebutate** | High | High | 2–3 days | High | High |

#### Follow-Up and Prevention

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Regular skin exams** | Every 6–12 months for AK patients; more frequent if immunosuppressed |
| **Photoprotection** | SPF 50+ daily; protective clothing; sun avoidance |
| **Self-examination** | Monthly; look for new or changing lesions |
| **Retinoids** | Topical or oral (isotretinoin/acitretin for high-risk) may reduce new AK formation |
| **Nicotinamide** | 500 mg BID oral; reduces AK and SCC in high-risk patients (ONTRAC trial) |

### Red Flags — Urgent Dermatologist Referral

- [ ] **Induration, thickening, or nodularity** — possible SCC progression
- [ ] **Ulceration or bleeding** — concerning for invasive SCC
- [ ] **Rapid growth** — SCC or amelanotic melanoma
- [ ] **Pigmented lesion with irregular features** — rule out melanoma
- [ ] **Treatment failure** after appropriate field therapy
- [ ] **Immunosuppressed patient with AKs** — higher SCC risk; aggressive management needed
- [ ] **Cutaneous horn** — underlying lesion may be SCC; biopsy required

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "AKs are just dry skin spots" | They are pre-malignant; require treatment and monitoring |
| "Not all AKs need treatment" | While some may spontaneously regress, risk of SCC progression warrants treatment, especially for multiple lesions |
| "If one is treated, others won't appear" | Field cancerization means new AKs will continue to develop; ongoing surveillance needed |
| "AKs only occur in very old people" | Can occur in 30s–40s with significant sun exposure; especially in immunosuppressed |
| "Tanning protects against AKs" | Tanning causes AKs; any UV exposure is cumulative and harmful |
| "Once treated, I don't need follow-up" | Regular skin checks essential for life; new AKs and skin cancers will develop |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Actinic Keratosis:**

1. **Risk Assessment:**
   - Age, Fitzpatrick type, sun exposure history
   - Number and distribution of lesions
   - Immunosuppression status
   - Prior skin cancer history
   - Current lesion characteristics

2. **Urgent Flags:**
   - Induration, ulceration, bleeding → immediate dermatology referral
   - Rapid change → urgent biopsy
   - Immunosuppressed + new lesion → expedited evaluation

3. **Treatment Guidance:**
   - **Few discrete lesions:** Cryotherapy referral
   - **Multiple lesions/field cancerization:** Field therapy education; discuss options with dermatologist
   - **Cosmetically sensitive areas (face):** PDT may be preferred
   - **Severe photodamage:** Consider oral nicotinamide prophylaxis

4. **Prevention Emphasis:**
   - Rigorous photoprotection education
   - Regular skin check reminders
   - Self-examination education
   - Hat and clothing recommendations

5. **Follow-Up:**
   - Dermatology appointment reminders (6–12 months)
   - Photo tracking for new or changing lesions
   - Treatment completion tracking for field therapy

6. **Safety Checks:**
   - Never provide false reassurance about suspicious lesions
   - Emphasize need for in-person dermatology evaluation
   - Immunosuppressed patients need specialized, aggressive management

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | — | 5-FU, imiquimod, diclofenac, tirbanibulin, ingenol mebutate — all contraindicated or limited data |
| **Procedures** | Cryotherapy (limited, small areas) | PDT, extensive procedures |
| **Prevention** | Physical sunscreen, protective clothing | Oral retinoids, nicotinamide (limited data) |

**Note:** AK treatment is generally deferred in pregnancy unless biopsy-proven SCC. Focus on photoprotection and surveillance.

## 20. Keratosis Pilaris

### Clinical Definition

Keratosis pilaris (KP) is a common, benign, autosomal-dominant disorder of keratinization characterized by follicular papules with surrounding erythema, resembling "gooseflesh" or "chicken skin." It results from hyperkeratotic plugging of hair follicles.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L85.8 (Other specified epidermal thickening) or L85.9 (Epidermal thickening, unspecified)
- **Synonyms:** Follicular keratosis, "chicken skin," "goose bumps"

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Morphology:** Multiple small, rough, follicular papules with central keratotic plug
- **Color:** Skin-colored, white, or red (erythematous papules)
- **Distribution:** Extensor upper arms (most common), thighs, buttocks, cheeks (children), trunk
- **Texture:** Sandpaper-like; rough to touch
- **Symptoms:** Usually asymptomatic; may have mild pruritus or cosmetic concern
- **Seasonal variation:** Often worse in winter (low humidity)
- **Age:** Onset typically in childhood/adolescence; may improve with age
- **Association:** Atopic dermatitis, ichthyosis vulgaris, filaggrin mutations

### Subtypes

| Subtype | Features | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| **Keratosis pilaris rubra** | Red, inflamed papules; background erythema | Most visible; cosmetic concern |
| **Keratosis pilaris alba** | White, non-inflamed papules; no erythema | Common; less noticeable |
| **Keratosis pilaris rubra faceii** | Erythematous patches on cheeks | Resembles rosacea or eczema |
| **Keratosis pilaris atrophicans** | Follicular papules with central atrophy or scarring | Includes ulerythema ophryogenes (brow atrophy) and atrophoderma vermiculata |
| **Keratosis follicularis spinulosa decalvans** | KP with scarring alopecia | Rare; X-linked or autosomal dominant |

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mild** | Few scattered papules; subtle; mainly tactile |
| **Moderate** | Numerous papules covering affected area; visible and palpable; some erythema |
| **Severe** | Confluent, widespread; prominent erythema; possible associated atrophy or scarring (atrophicans subtypes) |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Filaggrin (FLG) gene mutations** | Strong | Loss-of-function mutations in FLG strongly associated; impaired keratinization and barrier function |
| **Keratin plug formation** | Strong | Excess keratin accumulates in follicular opening; blocks hair growth |
| **Hair shaft abnormalities** | Moderate | Coiled or embedded hair shafts may contribute |
| **Genetic predisposition** | Strong | Autosomal dominant inheritance; 30–50% family history |
| **Atopic diathesis** | Strong | Strongly associated with atopic dermatitis and ichthyosis vulgaris |
| **Dry skin (xerosis)** | Strong | Low humidity worsens condition; impaired barrier increases scale |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Factor | Evidence | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Low humidity/winter** | Strong | Xerosis worsens follicular plugging |
| **Atopic dermatitis** | Strong | Co-occurs frequently; shared filaggrin mutations |
| **Ichthyosis vulgaris** | Strong | Filaggrin deficiency; overlapping pathophysiology |
| **Age** | Moderate | Peaks in childhood/adolescence; often improves with age |
| **Hormonal changes** | Limited | May worsen during puberty |
| **Tight clothing** | Limited | Friction may irritate |
| **Hot showers** | Limited | Strips skin oils; worsens dryness |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Presentation | Treatment Considerations |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|
| **Fitzpatrick I–II** | Red papules prominent (KP rubra); post-inflammatory erythema | Focus on exfoliation + anti-inflammatory |
| **Fitzpatrick III–IV** | Mixed white and red papules; PIH risk from irritation | Gentle approach; balance exfoliation with irritation prevention |
| **Fitzpatrick V–VI** | Papules may appear as dark brown or gray; follicular accentuation; PIH is major concern | Ultra-gentle exfoliation; PIH prevention paramount; may be mistaken for acne or folliculitis |

### Treatment Pathways

#### First-Line: Keratolytic Agents

| Ingredient | Concentration | Mechanism | Application |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|------------|
| **Urea** | 10–20% | Keratolytic; hydrates; breaks down keratin | Daily; well-tolerated |
| **Lactic acid** | 5–12% | AHA; exfoliates; increases moisture | Daily; may sting |
| **Glycolic acid** | 8–15% | AHA; exfoliates | Daily or every other day |
| **Salicylic acid** | 2–3% | BHA; lipophilic; penetrates follicles | Daily; good for oily skin |
| **Ammonium lactate** | 12% | AHA; prescription strength | BID; effective |

#### Retinoids (For Resistant Cases)

| Medication | Concentration | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-------|
| **Tretinoin** | 0.025–0.1% | Increases cell turnover; prevents plug formation; irritating |
| **Adapalene** | 0.1–0.3% | Less irritating than tretinoin; good for KP rubra |
| **Tazarotene** | 0.1% | Most potent; most irritating |

**Application:** Start 2–3x weekly, increase as tolerated. Apply to dry skin to reduce irritation.

#### Physical Exfoliation (Adjunctive)

| Method | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| **Gentle scrubbing with washcloth** | Avoid aggressive scrubbing; can cause irritation |
| **Dry brushing** | Before shower; stimulates circulation; exfoliates |
| **Chemical exfoliation preferred** | Less traumatic than physical |

#### Moisturization (Essential)

| Moisturizer Type | Ingredients | Notes |
|-----------------|-------------|-------|
| **Ceramide-based** | Ceramides, cholesterol, fatty acids | Barrier repair; essential for KP |
| **Petrolatum-based** | Petrolatum, mineral oil | Occlusive; locks in moisture |
| **Urea-containing** | 10–20% urea | Dual action: keratolytic + hydrating |
| **Lactic acid + ammonium lactate** | 12% ammonium lactate | Prescription; effective dual action |

#### Professional Procedures

| Procedure | Indication | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| **Chemical peels** | Resistant KP; series of 3–6 | Medium-depth AHA or Jessner's peels |
| **Microdermabrasion** | Physical exfoliation; temporary improvement | Multiple sessions needed |
| **Laser hair removal** | For KP with prominent hair component | May reduce follicular plugging; not primary treatment |
| **Pulse dye laser** | KP rubra with significant erythema | Reduces redness; limited efficacy for papules |

#### What NOT to Do

| Action | Why to Avoid |
|--------|-------------|
| **Aggressive scrubbing/picking** | Causes irritation, inflammation, PIH, and scarring |
| **High-strength actives initially** | Causes severe irritation; start low and slow |
| **Over-washing** | Strips skin barrier; worsens dryness |
| **Hot showers** | Dehydrates skin; lukewarm water preferred |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Folliculitis or infection** — pustules, warmth, pain; may need antibiotic treatment
- [ ] **Significant scarring or atrophy** — consider KP atrophicans; dermatology referral
- [ ] **Sudden onset in adulthood** — rare; rule out underlying condition
- [ ] **Severe psychological distress** — cosmetic concern can be significant; support and counseling
- [ ] **Treatment causing severe irritation** — adjust regimen; consider lower strengths or frequency

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "KP is acne" | Different condition; KP is follicular keratinization, not acne; treatments differ |
| "It can be cured" | No cure; chronic condition manageable with ongoing treatment |
| "Picking the bumps helps" | Picking causes scarring, PIH, and infection; never recommended |
| "It's caused by poor hygiene" | Genetic and barrier-related; not a cleanliness issue |
| "It will last forever" | Often improves significantly with age; many outgrow by 30s |
| "Expensive products are needed" | Simple keratolytics (urea, lactic acid, salicylic acid) are effective and affordable |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Keratosis Pilaris:**

1. **Assessment:**
   - Distribution and severity
   - Subtype (rubra vs. alba)
   - Associated atopic dermatitis or ichthyosis
   - Fitzpatrick type (PIH risk)
   - Seasonal pattern

2. **Treatment Ladder:**
   - **Mild:** Daily moisturizing + gentle AHA or urea lotion
   - **Moderate:** Add salicylic acid or lactic acid; introduce adapalene if needed
   - **Severe/Resistant:** Prescription ammonium lactate 12% or tretinoin; consider professional peels

3. **Routine Building:**
   - Morning: Moisturize (urea or ceramide-based)
   - Evening: Keratolytic (AHA/BHA or retinoid) + moisturizer
   - Bathing: Lukewarm water; gentle cleanser; pat dry; apply products within 3 minutes

4. **Expectation Setting:**
   - No cure; ongoing maintenance required
   - Improvement takes 4–8 weeks
   - Often improves with age
   - Winter flare-ups expected

5. **Safety Checks:**
   - Start low and slow with actives to prevent irritation
   - Pregnancy: avoid retinoids; urea, lactic acid, and salicylic acid (limited areas) generally safe
   - Darker skin: PIH risk from irritation; gentle approach

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Urea, lactic acid (low %), ammonium lactate, gentle moisturizers | Retinoids (tretinoin, adapalene, tazarotene) |
| **Exfoliants** | Gentle physical exfoliation, low-strength AHA | High-strength salicylic acid over large areas |

# Hair & Nail Conditions

## 21. Androgenic Alopecia

### Clinical Definition

Androgenic alopecia (AGA, androgenetic alopecia, male/female pattern hair loss) is the most common form of hair loss, characterized by progressive, patterned hair miniaturization driven by androgens and genetic predisposition. It affects approximately 50% of men by age 50 and up to 40% of women by age 70.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:**
  - L64.9 (Androgenic alopecia, unspecified)
  - L64.0 (Male pattern baldness)
  - L64.8 (Other androgenic alopecia)
  - L64.9 (Female pattern baldness)

### Diagnostic Criteria

#### Male Pattern Hair Loss (MPHL)

- **Pattern:** Bitemporal recession → vertex thinning → complete baldness (Hamilton-Norwood classification)
- **Onset:** Post-pubertal; peaks 20s–30s
- **Hair characteristics:** Miniaturization of terminal hairs to vellus-like hairs
- **Scalp:** Normal appearance; no inflammation or scarring
- **Family history:** Common; polygenic inheritance

#### Female Pattern Hair Loss (FPHL)

- **Pattern:** Diffuse thinning over crown with preserved frontal hairline (Ludwig classification); or Christmas tree pattern (Olsen); or Hamilton pattern (frontal)
- **Onset:** Perimenopause or postmenopause most common; can occur earlier
- **Hair characteristics:** Miniaturization; increased part width
- **Scalp:** Normal; no inflammation or scarring
- **Androgen excess signs:** Hirsutism, acne, irregular menses → evaluate for PCOS

### Classification

#### Hamilton-Norwood Scale (Men)

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **I** | No significant hair loss |
| **II** | Mild bitemporal recession |
| **III** | Deep temporal recession; vertex may be involved |
| **IV** | Advanced frontal loss + vertex thinning; band of hair separates |
| **V** | Larger areas of loss; narrower separating band |
| **VI** | Band disappears; only rim of hair remains |
| **VII** | Only horseshoe rim of hair remains |

#### Ludwig Scale (Women)

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **I** | Mild thinning on crown; perceptible on close examination |
| **II** | Moderate thinning; increased part width; noticeable |
| **III** | Severe thinning; diffuse crown involvement; "see-through" appearance |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Genetic predisposition** | Strong | Polygenic; heritability 80%; family history in both parents relevant |
| **Dihydrotestosterone (DHT)** | Strong | 5-alpha-reductase converts testosterone to DHT; DHT binds androgen receptors in follicle, miniaturizing hair |
| **Androgen receptor sensitivity** | Strong | Genetic variation in AR gene; determines follicle response to androgens |
| **Follicular miniaturization** | Strong | Terminal hairs (thick, pigmented) progressively become vellus-like (fine, short, non-pigmented) |
| **Inflammation** | Moderate | Perifollicular microinflammation may contribute |
| **Blood flow alterations** | Moderate | Reduced microcirculation to miniaturizing follicles |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Factor | Evidence | Notes |
|--------|----------|-------|
| **Age** | Strong | Prevalence increases with age |
| **Hormonal changes (women)** | Strong | Menopause, postpartum, discontinuation of OCPs |
| **PCOS** | Strong | Androgen excess causes or worsens FPHL |
| **Thyroid disease** | Moderate | Both hypo- and hyperthyroidism cause hair loss; may unmask or worsen AGA |
| **Iron deficiency** | Moderate | Ferritin < 40 ng/mL associated with hair loss; common in women |
| **Stress/illness** | Moderate | Telogen effluvium can unmask or accelerate AGA |
| **Medications** | Moderate | Retinoids, anticoagulants, beta-blockers, lithium, valproate |
| **Nutritional deficiency** | Moderate | Protein, zinc, biotin deficiency |
| **Smoking** | Moderate | Associated with accelerated hair loss |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Presentation | Treatment Considerations |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|
| **All types** | AGA affects all ethnicities | Treatment response similar across skin types |
| **Darker hair** | Miniaturization more visible against scalp | May seek treatment earlier |
| **Lighter hair** | Less visible thinning | May delay treatment seeking |
| **African descent** | Traction alopecia commonly co-occurs; central centrifugal cicatricial alopecia (CCCA) must be ruled out | Ensure diagnosis is AGA and not scarring alopecia |
| **Asian hair** | Typically straight, coarse; miniaturization may be less obvious | Same treatment approach |

### Treatment Pathways

#### FDA-Approved Treatments

| Treatment | Mechanism | Dose/Application | Efficacy | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|----------|-------|
| **Minoxidil (topical)** | KATP channel opener; increases blood flow; prolongs anagen; enlarges miniaturized follicles | Men: 5% foam or solution BID; Women: 2% BID or 5% foam once daily | 40% show regrowth; halts progression in most | First-line for men and women; requires ongoing use; initial shedding common; 4–6 months for visible results |
| **Finasteride (oral)** | 5-alpha-reductase inhibitor (Type II); reduces DHT by ~70% | Men: 1 mg daily | 66% show improvement; 83% halt progression | Men only; prescription; sexual side effects (low); PSA reduction; teratogenic to male fetus |
| **Dutasteride (oral)** | 5-alpha-reductase inhibitor (Type I and II); reduces DHT by > 90% | 0.5 mg daily (off-label for AGA) | More potent than finasteride; may help finasteride non-responders | Off-label; higher side effect risk; longer half-life |
| **Minoxidil (oral)** | Systemic vasodilation | 0.25–2.5 mg daily (off-label) | Emerging; promising for topical non-responders | Off-label; hypotension risk; hypertrichosis |

#### Adjunctive/Alternative Treatments

| Treatment | Evidence | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Ketoconazole 2% shampoo** | Moderate | Anti-androgen and anti-inflammatory; 2–3x weekly; adjunctive |
| **Spironolactone (women)** | Moderate | Anti-androgen; 50–200 mg daily; off-label for FPHL; monitor potassium |
| **Low-level laser therapy (LLLT)** | Moderate | FDA-cleared devices (Capillus, iRestore, HairMax); 3x weekly; 15–30 min; home use | Promotes hair growth; adjunctive |
| **Platelet-rich plasma (PRP)** | Moderate | Monthly injections x 3, then maintenance; stimulates growth factors | Expensive; variable protocols; promising |
| **Microneedling** | Moderate | Enhances minoxidil absorption; stimulates wound healing response | Adjunctive; weekly; 1.5 mm depth |
| **Hair transplantation** | Strong | Follicular unit extraction (FUE) or follicular unit transplantation (FUT) | Permanent; for stable AGA; requires adequate donor area |
| **Camouflage** | — | Hair fibers (Toppik, Caboki), scalp micropigmentation, wigs, hair systems | Immediate cosmetic improvement |

#### Supplements (Limited Evidence)

| Supplement | Evidence | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Biotin** | Limited | Only helpful if deficient; most people get adequate from diet |
| **Saw palmetto** | Limited | Weak 5-alpha-reductase inhibition; variable evidence |
| **Marine collagen peptides** | Limited | May improve hair thickness |
| **Iron** | Moderate | Supplement if ferritin < 40 ng/mL |
| **Zinc** | Limited | Supplement if deficient |
| **Vitamin D** | Limited | Supplement if deficient |
| **Selenium** | Limited | Supplement if deficient |

### Laboratory Workup (Especially for Women)

| Test | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| **TSH** | Rule out thyroid dysfunction |
| **Ferritin** | Iron deficiency common cause of hair loss in women |
| **CBC** | Anemia |
| **Testosterone, DHEAS** | Rule out androgen excess/PCOS |
| **17-hydroxyprogesterone** | Rule out late-onset congenital adrenal hyperplasia |
| **Vitamin D** | Common deficiency |
| **Zinc** | If deficiency suspected |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Scarring or inflammation on scalp** — rule out scarring alopecia (lichen planopilaris, CCCA, discoid lupus)
- [ ] **Rapid progression** — possible telogen effluvium or alopecia areata incognita
- [ ] **Patchy hair loss** — not AGA; consider alopecia areata, tinea capitis, trichotillomania
- [ ] **Systemic symptoms** — fatigue, weight change, menstrual irregularities; endocrine workup needed
- [ ] **Treatment failure after 12 months** — reconsider diagnosis; consider dutasteride or combination therapy
- [ ] **Significant psychological distress** — body dysmorphic disorder screening; counseling

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Hair loss comes from mother's father" | Polygenic; both parents' genetics contribute |
| "Wearing hats causes baldness" | No evidence; hats don't affect hair follicles |
| "Shaving makes hair grow back thicker" | Shaving doesn't change follicle or hair shaft diameter |
| "Stress causes permanent baldness" | Stress causes temporary telogen effluvium, not permanent AGA |
| "Topical minoxidil works immediately" | Takes 4–6 months to see results; initial shedding is common |
| "Finasteride is dangerous for all men" | Side effects are uncommon (2–4%); most are reversible; discuss risks/benefits |
| "Once you're bald, treatment won't help" | Hair transplantation can restore hair even in advanced cases; medical therapy may still halt progression |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Androgenic Alopecia:**

1. **Assessment:**
   - Pattern and distribution (Hamilton-Norwood or Ludwig classification)
   - Duration and progression rate
   - Family history
   - Associated symptoms (hirsutism, acne, menstrual irregularities in women)
   - Current treatments and response
   - Scalp examination for inflammation or scarring

2. **Diagnosis Confirmation:**
   - Distinguish from telogen effluvium, alopecia areata, scarring alopecia
   - Flag scarring signs for urgent dermatology referral
   - Recommend laboratory workup for women

3. **Treatment Planning:**
   - **Men (Mild-Moderate):** Minoxidil 5% + ketoconazole shampoo; consider finasteride 1 mg
   - **Men (Moderate-Advanced):** Finasteride 1 mg + minoxidil 5%; add LLLT or PRP; consider hair transplant
   - **Women (Mild-Moderate):** Minoxidil 2–5%; spironolactone if signs of androgen excess; ketoconazole shampoo
   - **Women (Moderate-Advanced):** Spironolactone 100–200 mg + minoxidil; dermatology referral; check labs

4. **Expectation Setting:**
   - Results take 6–12 months
   - Treatment must be continued indefinitely to maintain results
   - Goal: halt progression + partial regrowth; full restoration unlikely in advanced cases
   - Initial minoxidil shedding is normal (shedding telogen hairs to make way for new growth)

5. **Follow-Up:**
   - Photo tracking every 3 months
   - Assess adherence and side effects
   - Adjust treatment at 12 months if inadequate response
   - Annual lab monitoring for systemic therapies

6. **Safety Checks:**
   - Pregnancy: absolutely avoid finasteride/dutasteride; minoxidil with caution; spironolactone contraindicated
   - Women of childbearing age: contraception mandatory with finasteride or spironolactone
   - Cardiovascular: monitor blood pressure with minoxidil
   - Mental health: screen for body image concerns

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid (Absolute) |
|----------|------|------------------|
| **Topical** | Minoxidil (limited data; some use cautiously) | Finasteride, dutasteride (systemic absorption) |
| **Oral** | — | Finasteride, dutasteride, spironolactone, oral minoxidil |
| **Procedures** | — | PRP, hair transplant |
| **Shampoos** | Ketoconazole | — |

**Note:** Hair loss often improves during pregnancy (estrogen prolongs anagen); postpartum telogen effluvium is common and usually resolves within 6–12 months.

## 22. Alopecia Areata

### Clinical Definition

Alopecia areata (AA) is a chronic, organ-specific autoimmune disease characterized by non-scarring hair loss due to T-cell-mediated attack on hair follicles. It affects approximately 2% of the population and can range from small patches to total body hair loss.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L63.9 (Alopecia areata, unspecified)
- **Subclassification codes:**
  - L63.0 — Alopecia (capitis) totalis
  - L63.1 — Alopecia universalis
  - L63.2 — Ophiasis
  - L63.8 — Other alopecia areata
  - L63.9 — Alopecia areata, unspecified

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Sudden onset:** Rapid hair loss over days to weeks
- **Well-circumscribed patches:** Round or oval bald patches; smooth, non-inflamed scalp
- **Exclamation mark hairs:** Short, broken hairs narrower at base (pathognomonic)
- **Nail changes:** Pitting, trachyonychia, onychomadesis, red lunulae
- **Distribution:** Scalp (most common); beard; eyebrows; eyelashes; body hair
- **Non-scarring:** Follicular openings preserved; hair can regrow
- **Associated conditions:** Atopic dermatitis, thyroid disease, vitiligo, psoriasis
- **Pull test:** Positive at periphery of active patch

### Severity Classification (SALT Score)

**SALT (Severity of Alopecia Tool):** Percentage of scalp hair loss

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **S0** | No hair loss |
| **S1** | < 25% loss |
| **S2** | 25–49% loss |
| **S3** | 50–74% loss |
| **S4** | 75–99% loss |
| **S5** | 100% loss (alopecia totalis) |

**Extent variants:**

| Variant | Description | Prognosis |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| **Patchy AA** | One or few patches; most common | Good; spontaneous regrowth common |
| **Alopecia totalis (AT)** | Complete scalp hair loss | Poorer; less likely to fully regrow |
| **Alopecia universalis (AU)** | Complete loss of all body hair | Poorest prognosis |
| **Ophiasis** | Band-like pattern at periphery (temporal, occipital) | More resistant to treatment |
| **Diffuse AA** | Widespread thinning without distinct patches | Difficult to diagnose; mimics telogen effluvium |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Autoimmune T-cell attack** | Strong | CD8+ NKG2D+ T-cells target anagen hair follicles; IFN-gamma and IL-15 signaling central |
| **Genetic predisposition** | Strong | Polygenic; family history in 10–20%; HLA associations (HLA-DQB1*03) |
| **Immune privilege collapse** | Strong | Hair follicle normally has immune privilege; collapse allows autoimmune attack |
| **Stress trigger** | Moderate | Psychological stress may precipitate or worsen |
| **Viral trigger** | Emerging | Possible role in initiating autoimmunity |
| **Thyroid autoimmunity** | Moderate | Increased prevalence of Hashimoto's thyroiditis |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Trigger | Evidence | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| **Psychological stress** | Moderate | May trigger or exacerbate |
| **Physical trauma** | Limited | Koebner phenomenon; trauma to scalp may induce new patches |
| **Viral illness** | Limited | May precede onset |
| **Pregnancy/postpartum** | Moderate | Hormonal changes may trigger |
| **Seasonal** | Limited | Some evidence for autumn/winter onset |
| **Atopy** | Moderate | Higher prevalence in atopic individuals |
| **Thyroid disease** | Moderate | Screen for autoimmune thyroid disease |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Presentation | Treatment Considerations |
|-----------|--------------|------------------------|
| **All types** | Affects all ethnicities equally | Response to treatment similar across types |
| **Darker skin** | Patches may appear more prominent against dark scalp | Psychological impact may be significant; support resources important |
| **African descent** | Must distinguish from central centrifugal cicatricial alopecia (CCCA) and traction alopecia | Scarring alopecia requires different, urgent treatment |
| **Asian descent** | Similar presentation | Same treatment approach |

### Treatment Pathways

#### First-Line: Intralesional and Topical Corticosteroids

| Treatment | Application | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **Triamcinolone acetonide (IL)** | 2.5–10 mg/mL injected into patch periphery; 0.1 mL per site; spaced 1 cm apart | First-line for limited patchy AA; 4–6 week intervals; hair regrowth in 4–8 weeks |
| **Topical clobetasol propionate 0.05%** | Foam, solution, or ointment BID under occlusion | For patchy AA; limited scalp involvement |
| **Topical betamethasone valerate** | BID | Less potent alternative |

#### Topical Immunotherapy (For Extensive or Recalcitrant AA)

| Treatment | Mechanism | Application | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|-------|
| **Diphenylcyclopropenone (DPCP)** | Contact sensitizer; induces allergic contact dermatitis; modulates local immunity | Applied weekly by physician; initial sensitization | 30–60% response for patchy AA; lower for AT/AU; requires specialized center |
| **Squaric acid dibutylester (SADBE)** | Similar to DPCP | Similar protocol | Similar efficacy; availability varies |
| **Dinitrochlorobenzene (DNCB)** | Similar mechanism | Similar protocol | Less commonly used due to mutagenicity concerns |

#### Systemic Therapies (For Moderate-Severe AA)

| Treatment | Dose | Efficacy | Notes |
|-----------|------|----------|-------|
| **Oral corticosteroids** | Prednisone 0.5 mg/kg tapered over 6–12 weeks | Rapid but temporary regrowth | High relapse rate; significant side effects; short courses only |
| **Methotrexate** | 15–25 mg weekly | Variable; 30–40% response | Often combined with corticosteroids; folate supplementation |
| **Cyclosporine** | 3–5 mg/kg/day | Variable | Rapid relapse on discontinuation; nephrotoxicity |
| **Oral JAK inhibitors** | Tofacitinib 5 mg BID; ruxolitinib 20 mg BID; baricitinib 2–4 mg daily | **Game-changer**; 50–70% significant regrowth | FDA-approved (baricitinib, ruxolitinib) for severe AA; requires monitoring; relapse common after stopping |

**JAK Inhibitors (Breakthrough Therapy):**

| Drug | Target | FDA Status | Key Trials |
|------|--------|------------|------------|
| **Baricitinib (Olumiant®)** | JAK1/2 | Approved (2022) for severe AA | BRAVE-AA1 and BRAVE-AA2; ~35% achieved 80% regrowth at 36 weeks |
| **Ruxolitinib (Jakafi®)** | JAK1/2 | Approved (2023) for severe AA | ~50% achieved 80% regrowth |
| **Tofacitinib (Xeljanz®)** | JAK1/3 | Off-label; widely studied | Multiple trials; 50–70% response in severe AA |
| **Deuruxolitinib (Leqselvi®)** | JAK1/2 | Approved (2024) | THRIVE-AA trials; oral option |

#### Biologics and Emerging Therapies

| Treatment | Mechanism | Status |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Dupilumab** | IL-4Rα antagonist | Case reports of AA occurring on dupilumab; not treatment |
| **Rituximab** | Anti-CD20 B-cell depletion | Limited evidence; not standard |
| **Emerging JAK inhibitors** | Topical and oral | Ongoing trials; more targeted agents |

#### Supportive and Cosmetic

| Option | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Camouflage** | Hair fibers, scalp micropigmentation, wigs, toppers |
| **Psychological support** | Counseling; support groups (NAAF); body image therapy |
| **Nail care** | For associated nail changes; dermatology referral if severe |

#### Adjunctive Measures

| Measure | Rationale |
|---------|-----------|
| **Minoxidil 5%** | May accelerate regrowth once inflammation controlled; adjunctive |
| **Anthralin** | Irritant; limited evidence; may help patchy AA |
| **Phototherapy (PUVA, excimer)** | Limited evidence; not first-line |
| **Platelet-rich plasma (PRP)** | Emerging; mixed evidence |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Alopecia totalis/universalis** — severe; requires systemic therapy; psychological support critical
- [ ] **Rapid progression** — urgent treatment needed to prevent extensive loss
- [ ] **Associated autoimmune disease** — screen thyroid (TSH), vitiligo, atopy
- [ ] **Significant psychological distress** — depression, anxiety, social withdrawal; mental health referral
- [ ] **Signs of scarring** — not AA; possible lichen planopilaris or CCCA; urgent dermatology
- [ ] **Children with extensive AA** — family and school support needed; treatment options more limited

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "AA is caused by stress alone" | Stress may trigger but is not the sole cause; autoimmune mechanism is primary |
| "AA is contagious" | Not contagious; autoimmune |
| "Hair will never grow back" | Most patchy AA regrows spontaneously; even AT/AU can regrow unpredictably |
| "There's no effective treatment" | JAK inhibitors have revolutionized treatment for severe AA; many options exist |
| "Shaving or cutting hair stimulates growth" | Doesn't affect autoimmune process |
| "Diet can cure AA" | No specific diet cures AA; general nutrition supports overall health |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Alopecia Areata:**

1. **Initial Assessment:**
   - Pattern and extent (SALT score)
   - Duration and progression
   - Nail changes
   - Associated autoimmune conditions
   - Psychological impact
   - Prior treatments and response

2. **Urgency Stratification:**
   - **Patchy, limited:** Dermatology referral for intralesional steroids
   - **Extensive/rapidly progressive:** Urgent dermatology; consider oral JAK inhibitors
   - **AT/AU:** Specialized care; psychological support; JAK inhibitor evaluation

3. **Treatment Education:**
   - Explain autoimmune nature
   - Discuss JAK inhibitor options for severe disease
   - Set realistic expectations (treatments improve but may not cure)
   - Discuss relapse possibility

4. **Support Resources:**
   - Connect to NAAF (National Alopecia Areata Foundation)
   - Wig and hairpiece resources
   - Scalp micropigmentation information
   - Mental health support

5. **Follow-Up:**
   - Photo tracking monthly
   - Treatment response assessment at 12 weeks
   - Side effect monitoring for systemic therapies

6. **Safety Checks:**
   - Screen for thyroid disease (TSH recommendation)
   - Pregnancy: JAK inhibitors contraindicated; corticosteroids with caution
   - Immunization status before JAK inhibitors (live vaccines)
   - TB screening before biologics/JAK inhibitors
   - Monitor for infections on immunosuppressants

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Topical** | Limited; corticosteroids (small areas) | Immunotherapy (DPCP, SADBE) |
| **Intralesional** | Small volume corticosteroids | Large volume systemic absorption |
| **Systemic** | — | Oral steroids (if possible), JAK inhibitors, methotrexate, cyclosporine |
| **Cosmetic** | Wigs, fibers, scalp micropigmentation | — |

**Note:** AA may improve, worsen, or remain unchanged during pregnancy; unpredictable.

## 23. Telogen Effluvium

### Clinical Definition

Telogen effluvium (TE) is a non-scarring, diffuse hair loss condition characterized by excessive shedding of telogen (resting) phase hairs following a systemic stressor. It is the second most common form of hair loss and represents a transient disruption of the normal hair cycle.

- **ICD-10-CM Code:** L65.0 (Telogen effluvium)
- **Synonyms:** Diffuse alopecia, stress-related hair loss, postpartum hair loss

### Diagnostic Criteria

- **Diffuse shedding:** Increased daily hair fall; hair collected in shower, brush, pillow
- **Onset:** 2–3 months after triggering event (characteristic lag phase)
- **Distribution:** Diffuse; entire scalp; bitemporal recession may occur
- **Hair characteristics:** Shed hairs have club-shaped (telogen) bulbs
- **Scalp:** Normal; no inflammation, scaling, or scarring
- **Pull test:** Positive (easily extracted telogen hairs)
- **Trichogram:** > 25% telogen hairs (normal is < 10–15%)
- **Duration:** Usually self-limited; resolves within 6–12 months of trigger removal

### Grading Scale

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Mild** | Noticeable increased shedding; no visible scalp thinning |
| **Moderate** | Visible decrease in hair volume; scalp may be visible in bright light |
| **Severe** | Significant thinning; scalp clearly visible; may resemble AGA |

### Root Causes

| Mechanism | Evidence | Details |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Stress-induced anagen arrest** | Strong | Significant stressor triggers large number of anagen hairs to enter telogen prematurely |
| **Synchronized hair cycle** | Strong | Normally random cycling becomes synchronized; hairs enter telogen together and shed together 2–3 months later |
| **Follicle metabolic disruption** | Strong | Stressors (nutritional, hormonal, inflammatory) disrupt follicle energy metabolism |
| **Cytokine-mediated** | Moderate | Pro-inflammatory cytokines may signal follicle to enter telogen |

### Contributing/Trigger Factors

| Trigger | Evidence | Time to Onset | Notes |
|---------|----------|---------------|-------|
| **Childbirth (postpartum)** | Strong | 1–4 months postpartum | Most common cause in women; "postpartum shedding"; usually resolves by 6–12 months |
| **Severe illness/fever** | Strong | 2–3 months after | Major surgery, COVID-19, sepsis, high fever |
| **Psychological stress** | Moderate | 2–3 months after | Bereavement, divorce, major life events |
| **Rapid weight loss/dieting** | Strong | 2–3 months after | Crash diets, bariatric surgery, eating disorders |
| **Medications** | Strong | 2–3 months after starting | Retinoids, anticoagulants, beta-blockers, lithium, valproate, ACE inhibitors, SSRIs |
| **Iron deficiency** | Strong | Chronic or acute | Ferritin < 40 ng/mL associated with TE |
| **Thyroid dysfunction** | Strong | Variable | Both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism |
| **Protein deficiency** | Moderate | Chronic | Kwashiorkor; crash diets; malabsorption |
| **Zinc deficiency** | Moderate | Chronic | Acrodermatitis enteropathica; malnutrition |
| **Vitamin D deficiency** | Moderate | Chronic | Common; supplementation may help |
| **Selenium deficiency or excess** | Moderate | Chronic | Both can cause hair loss |
| **Hormonal changes** | Moderate | Variable | Stopping/starting OCPs, HRT changes |
| **Seasonal shedding** | Limited | Autumn | Some people shed more in fall |
| **Anesthesia/surgery** | Moderate | 2–3 months post-op | Major surgery with general anesthesia |
| **Chronic disease** | Moderate | Variable | Autoimmune disease, chronic infection, malignancy |

### Fitzpatrick Considerations

| Skin Type | Presentation | Notes |
|-----------|--------------|-------|
| **All types** | Affects all equally | Underlying trigger assessment is key |
| **Darker skin** | May be more noticeable due to contrast with dark hair | Support and reassurance important |
| **African descent** | Must distinguish from traction alopecia and CCCA; TE is non-scarring | Traction alopecia is preventable but can become scarring; urgent education needed |

### Treatment Pathways

#### Primary Treatment: Identify and Remove Trigger

| Trigger | Investigation | Management |
|---------|---------------|------------|
| **Postpartum** | Clinical diagnosis | Reassurance; self-limiting; 6–12 months |
| **Iron deficiency** | Ferritin, CBC | Iron supplementation (ferrous sulfate 325 mg or elemental iron 60–65 mg daily) |
| **Thyroid disease** | TSH, free T4 | Treat hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism |
| **Medication-induced** | Medication review | Discontinue or switch offending medication if possible |
| **Nutritional deficiency** | Comprehensive metabolic panel, vitamin D, zinc, protein | Dietary optimization; targeted supplementation |
| **Stress-related** | Clinical assessment | Stress management; sleep optimization; counseling |
| **Rapid weight loss** | Dietary history | Balanced nutrition; gradual weight loss; protein intake |
| **Chronic disease** | Appropriate workup | Treat underlying condition |

#### Supportive Treatments

| Treatment | Evidence | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Minoxidil 5%** | Moderate | May accelerate recovery; not curative; use if shedding persists > 6 months |
| **Nutritional supplements** | Limited | Biotin (if deficient), zinc, vitamin D, iron as indicated |
| **Low-level laser therapy (LLLT)** | Limited | May support regrowth; adjunctive |
| **Platelet-rich plasma (PRP)** | Limited | Emerging; mixed evidence |
| **Stress reduction** | Moderate | CBT, meditation, adequate sleep, exercise |

#### What NOT to Do

| Action | Why to Avoid |
|--------|-------------|
| **High-dose biotin without deficiency** | No benefit; can interfere with lab tests |
| **Aggressive treatments for AGA** | TE is not AGA; finasteride/spironolactone not indicated unless true AGA coexists |
| **Topical or oral steroids** | Not indicated for TE unless associated inflammatory condition |
| **Crash dieting during recovery** | Perpetuates or worsens TE |

### Red Flags

- [ ] **Shedding persisting > 12 months** — consider chronic TE or unmasked AGA ("telogen effluvium incognita")
- [ ] **Patchy hair loss** — not typical for TE; consider alopecia areata or tinea capitis
- [ ] **Scarring or inflammation** — not TE; urgent dermatology for scarring alopecia
- [ ] **Systemic symptoms** — fatigue, weight loss, fever; underlying disease workup needed
- [ ] **Significant anemia** — requires investigation and treatment
- [ ] **Psychological distress** — anxiety about hair loss can become severe; support needed

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "TE causes permanent baldness" | TE is self-limited; hair regrows once trigger is removed |
| "Washing hair less reduces shedding" | Shedding is cyclical; washing just dislodges already-loose hairs |
| "Shaving hair makes it grow back thicker" | Shaving doesn't change hair shaft diameter or follicle |
| "Stress from hair loss causes more hair loss" | While possible, the primary stressor has usually already occurred; reassurance and trigger management are key |
| "All hair loss in women is hormonal" | TE is very common in women; not all hair loss is AGA or hormonal |
| "Supplements alone will cure TE" | Only if deficiency is the trigger; most important step is identifying and addressing the specific cause |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Telogen Effluvium:**

1. **Diagnostic Screening:**
   - Timeline: When did shedding start? What happened 2–3 months prior?
   - Triggers: Childbirth, illness, surgery, stress, weight loss, medication changes
   - Pattern: Diffuse vs. patchy
   - Associated symptoms: Fatigue, weight change, menstrual changes

2. **Trigger Identification:**
   - Medication review
   - Nutritional assessment
   - Stress and lifestyle review
   - Medical history (thyroid, anemia, chronic disease)
   - Pregnancy/postpartum status

3. **Laboratory Recommendations:**
   - Ferritin, CBC, TSH (minimum workup)
   - Vitamin D, zinc if indicated
   - Comprehensive metabolic panel if systemic symptoms

4. **Treatment:**
   - **Primary:** Address identified trigger
   - **Supportive:** Reassurance, minoxidil if persistent, nutritional optimization
   - **Timeline:** Set expectation of 6–12 month recovery

5. **Differential Diagnosis:**
   - Distinguish from AGA (patterned thinning, progressive)
   - Distinguish from alopecia areata (patchy, exclamation hairs)
   - Distinguish from chronic TE (shedding > 6 months with no trigger)
   - Flag for dermatology if diagnosis uncertain

6. **Expectation Setting:**
   - Shedding will decrease before regrowth is visible
   - New growth is fine and short initially ("baby hairs")
   - Full recovery takes 6–12 months
   - Some patients may have unmasked AGA requiring ongoing treatment

7. **Safety Checks:**
   - Screen for eating disorders (rapid weight loss)
   - Pregnancy: common cause; reassure and monitor
   - Mental health: hair loss can trigger anxiety and depression

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Category | Safe | Avoid |
|----------|------|-------|
| **Postpartum TE** | Reassurance; time; nutrition | Minoxidil if breastfeeding (limited data; some caution) |
| **Iron** | Supplement if deficient | — |
| **Thyroid** | Treat if abnormal | — |
| **Nutrition** | Balanced diet; prenatal vitamins | Crash dieting |

**Note:** Postpartum TE is extremely common (up to 50% of women); reassurance and time are primary treatments.

## 24. Nail Changes

### Clinical Definition

Nail changes are alterations in nail plate, nail bed, or periungual tissue that may reflect localized nail disease, systemic illness, medication effects, or genetic conditions. The nails are windows to systemic health, and careful examination can reveal important diagnostic clues.

- **ICD-10-CM Codes:** By specific condition (see subsections below)

### Major Nail Signs — Clinical Profiles

---

#### A. Clubbing (Digital Clubbing, Hippocratic Fingers)

**ICD-10:** R68.3 (Clubbing of fingers)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Bulbous enlargement of distal phalanx with increased Lovibond angle (> 180°) and sponginess of nail bed |
| **Mechanism** | Increased connective tissue in nail bed from platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF) and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF); associated with chronic hypoxia |
| **Associations** | **Pulmonary:** Lung cancer (most common), bronchiectasis, TB, cystic fibrosis, IPF, empyema **Cardiac:** Cyanotic heart disease, infective endocarditis **GI:** Cirrhosis, IBD, celiac disease **Other:** Thyrotoxicosis (thyroid acropachy), familial |
| **Clinical test** | Profile sign: loss of normal 160° angle between nail and cuticle; Schamroth window test: opposing nails no longer form diamond window |
| **Red flags** | New-onset clubbing in adult → urgent chest imaging to rule out lung cancer |

---

#### B. Koilonychia (Spoon Nails)

**ICD-10:** L60.3 (Nail dystrophy)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Concave, spoon-shaped nail plate that can hold a drop of water |
| **Mechanism** | Iron deficiency → impaired nail matrix keratinization; may also relate to chronic hypoxia or trauma |
| **Associations** | **Iron deficiency anemia** (most common), hemochromatosis (paradoxical), Raynaud's disease, SLE, hypothyroidism, trauma, occupational exposure to petroleum products |
| **Location** | Fingernails (especially thumbnails); toenails less commonly affected |
| **Red flags** | New koilonychia → iron studies (ferritin, TIBC, CBC); may be early sign of iron deficiency before anemia develops |

---

#### C. Beau's Lines

**ICD-10:** L60.4 (Beau's lines)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Transverse grooves or depressions across nail plate; represent temporary arrest of nail matrix growth |
| **Mechanism** | Any severe systemic stressor temporarily halts nail matrix proliferation |
| **Associations** | **Acute illness:** High fever, sepsis, COVID-19, myocardial infarction, severe infection **Surgery/trauma:** General anesthesia, major surgery **Chemotherapy:** Cytotoxic agents **Nutritional:** Severe protein deficiency, zinc deficiency **Systemic disease:** Pemphigus, Raynaud's, peripheral ischemia **Local trauma:** Manicure injury, pressure on proximal nail fold |
| **Clinical significance** | Timing: measure distance from cuticle to line; nails grow 3 mm/month (fingernails); line at 3 mm = event 1 month ago. Can date systemic illness |
| **Red flags** | Multiple parallel lines → repeated severe stressors; single deep line → major acute event |

---

#### D. Melanonychia (Nail Pigmentation)

**ICD-10:** L60.8 (Other nail disorders)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Brown-to-black longitudinal or diffuse pigmentation of nail plate from melanin deposition |
| **Types** | **Longitudinal melanonychia (LM):** Brown-black streak from proximal to distal nail plate **Diffuse melanonychia:** Entire nail pigmented |
| **Mechanism** | **Melanocytic activation:** Increased melanin in normal melanocytes (benign) **Melanocytic hyperplasia:** Increased melanocyte number (nevus, melanoma) |
| **Causes** | **Benign:** Racial (Fitzpatrick V–VI; up to 100% have LM by age 50), trauma, drugs (chemotherapy, antimalarials, minocycline, zidovudine), pregnancy, Addison's disease, onychomycosis, psoriasis **Malignant:** Subungual melanoma |
| **ABCDEF rule for subungual melanoma** | **A** — Age (peak 50–70; rare < 20) **B** — Brown-black band width > 3 mm **C** — Change in pigment band or lack of change after treatment **D** — Digit involved (thumb > great toe > index finger) **E** — Extension of pigment to proximal/lateral nail fold (Hutchinson sign) **F** — Family/personal history of melanoma |
| **Red flags** | Hutchinson sign, width > 3 mm, irregular pigment, progressive darkening, single nail in adult, dystrophic nail → urgent dermatology/biopsy |

---

#### E. Onycholysis

**ICD-10:** L60.1 (Onycholysis)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Distal separation of nail plate from nail bed, creating white or yellow opaque area |
| **Mechanism** | Disruption of nail plate–bed adhesion |
| **Causes** | **Local:** Trauma, manicure, psoriasis, onychomycosis, contact irritants (nail polish, cleaners) **Systemic:** Thyroid disease (especially hyperthyroidism), drugs (tetracyclines + sun exposure, psoralens, chemotherapy), iron deficiency, pregnancy |
| **Secondary infection** | Pseudomonas colonization → green-black discoloration |
| **Management** | Keep nails short; avoid irritants; treat underlying cause; allow nail to grow out (6–9 months for fingernails) |
| **Red flags** | Unexplained onycholysis + systemic symptoms → thyroid function tests, medication review |

---

#### F. Pitting

**ICD-10:** L60.8 (Other nail disorders)

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Small punctate depressions in nail plate surface |
| **Mechanism** | Focal areas of abnormal keratinization in nail matrix |
| **Associations** | **Psoriasis** (most common; deep, irregular pits), **alopecia areata** (fine, geometric, superficial pits), **eczema** (shallow, irregular), **lichen planus**, **Reiter's syndrome** |
| **Pattern significance** | Random deep pits → psoriasis; fine geometric pattern → alopecia areata |

---

#### G. Leukonychia (White Nails)

**ICD-10:** L60.8 (Other nail disorders)

| Type | Description | Associations |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| **Punctate** | Small white spots | Trauma (most common); benign |
| **Striate** | White longitudinal lines | Darier disease, Hailey-Hailey, trauma |
| **Diffuse (Terry nails)** | Ground-glass white with distal pink/brown band | Cirrhosis, CHF, diabetes, aging |
| **Muehrcke lines** | Paired white transverse bands | Hypoalbuminemia, chemotherapy |
| **Half-and-half (Lindsay nails)** | Proximal half white, distal half red-brown | Chronic kidney disease |

---

### Comprehensive Nail Assessment

| Component | What to Examine |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Nail plate** | Color, shape, thickness, surface (ridges, pits, grooves), attachments |
| **Nail bed** | Color, capillary refill, attachment to plate |
| **Nail folds** | Erythema, swelling, cuticle integrity |
| **Periungual tissue** | Pigment extension, masses, inflammation |
| **All 20 nails** | Compare for symmetry and pattern |

### Red Flags — Urgent Referral

- [ ] **Hutchinson sign** (pigment extending to periungual skin) — subungual melanoma until proven otherwise
- [ ] **New single-nail melanonychia in adult** — biopsy indicated
- [ ] **Rapidly changing pigmented nail lesion** — urgent dermatology
- [ ] **Clubbing with respiratory symptoms or weight loss** — urgent chest imaging
- [ ] **Nail dystrophy with systemic symptoms** — underlying disease workup
- [ ] **Paronychia with immune compromise** — possible deep infection; urgent care
- [ ] **Blue lunula** — argyria, Wilson's disease, hemochromatosis
- [ ] **Yellow nail syndrome** — lymphedema, bronchiectasis, pleural effusion; triad

### Common Misconceptions

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "White spots mean calcium deficiency" | Usually traumatic; not related to calcium |
| "Nail changes are always cosmetic" | Often indicate systemic disease; important diagnostic clues |
| "Nail fungus is the most common cause of nail dystrophy" | Psoriasis is equally common; diagnosis requires KOH/culture |
| "Melanonychia is always melanoma" | Most melanonychia in darker skin is benign; ABCDEF criteria guide concern |
| "Nails grow at the same rate year-round" | Faster in summer, during pregnancy, and on dominant hand |
| "You can't get skin cancer under nails" | Subungual melanoma exists and is often diagnosed late |

### SKINgenius Recommendation Logic

**App Behavior for Nail Changes:**

1. **Pattern Recognition:**
   - Ask about color changes, shape changes, separation, pain, growth changes
   - Photo documentation of all nails
   - Timeline of changes
   - Associated symptoms (skin, systemic)
   - Medication history

2. **Differential Generation:**
   - Pigmented band → ABCDEF assessment for melanoma risk
   - Spoon shape → iron deficiency screen
   - Clubbing → respiratory/cardiac symptom screen
   - Pitting → psoriasis vs. alopecia areata context
   - Separation → trauma vs. thyroid vs. fungus

3. **Urgency Stratification:**
   - **Emergency:** Hutchinson sign, rapidly changing pigmented lesion, clubbing with systemic symptoms
   - **Urgent:** New single-nail melanonychia in adult, unexplained nail dystrophy with systemic symptoms
   - **Routine:** Traumatic changes, known psoriasis nail involvement, benign variants

4. **Referral Guidance:**
   - Suspicious pigmentation → dermatology (possible nail unit biopsy)
   - Clubbing with symptoms → primary care + chest imaging
   - Spoon nails → iron studies
   - Onycholysis → thyroid tests, medication review
   - Beau's lines → recent illness/surgery review

5. **Education:**
   - Normal nail growth rate (fingernails 3 mm/month; toenails 1 mm/month)
   - Trauma is common cause of white spots
   - Nail changes take months to resolve due to slow growth
   - Never ignore new single-nail pigmentation in adults

6. **Safety Checks:**
   - Never dismiss nail changes as "just cosmetic"
   - ABCDEF rule education for melanonychia
   - Pregnancy: many nail changes are normal (brittleness, faster growth)
   - Diabetes: increased onychomycosis risk; foot care critical

### Pregnancy Considerations

| Change | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Faster nail growth** | Increased blood flow and hormones |
| **Brittleness** | Common; temporary |
| **Onycholysis** | Can occur; usually resolves postpartum |
| **Melanonychia** | Physiologic pigmentation may increase; usually benign |
| **Treatment** | Defer elective nail procedures; treat infections; gentle care |
