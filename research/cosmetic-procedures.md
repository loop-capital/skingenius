### 1A. BOTULINUM TOXIN (Botox®, Dysport®, Xeomin®, Jeuveau®)

#### Mechanism of Action
Botulinum toxin (BoNT) is a neurotoxin produced by *Clostridium botulinum*. It consists of a heavy chain (100 kDa) and a light chain (50 kDa) linked by a disulfide bond. The mechanism involves:

1. **Binding:** The heavy chain binds to polysialoganglioside receptors (specifically SV2 and synaptotagmin) on the presynaptic cholinergic nerve terminal.
2. **Internalization:** Receptor-mediated endocytosis brings the toxin into the nerve terminal.
3. **Cleavage:** The light chain, a zinc-dependent endopeptidase, cleaves specific SNARE (SNAP-25, synaptobrevin/VAMP, syntaxin) proteins.
4. **Blockade:** SNARE protein cleavage prevents vesicle docking and fusion, blocking acetylcholine release into the neuromuscular junction.
5. **Muscle relaxation:** Without acetylcholine signaling, the target muscle cannot contract, reducing dynamic wrinkles caused by repetitive facial expressions.

**Clinical effect onset:** 24-72 hours (full effect by 7-14 days)  
**Duration:** 3-4 months (individual variation: 2-6 months)

#### Indications
- **Glabellar lines** ("11s" between brows) — FDA-approved
- **Forehead lines** — FDA-approved
- **Crow's feet** (lateral canthal lines) — FDA-approved
- **Bunny lines** (nasalis muscle)
- **Gummy smile** (excessive gingival show)
- **Lip flip** (subtle upper lip eversion)
- **Marionette lines** (DAO muscle relaxation)
- **Masseter hypertrophy** (jaw slimming, bruxism)
- **Hyperhidrosis** (excessive sweating) — FDA-approved for axillary
- **Migraine prophylaxis** — FDA-approved (chronic migraine)
- **Cervical dystonia, blepharospasm, strabismus**

#### Contraindications
- **Absolute:** Known hypersensitivity to botulinum toxin or albumin, infection at injection site, myasthenia gravis, Eaton-Lambert syndrome, amyotrophic lateral sclerosis
- **Relative:** Pregnancy, breastfeeding, age <18, concurrent aminoglycoside antibiotics or other neuromuscular junction blockers, blood thinners (increased bruising risk), unrealistic expectations

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Safe for all skin types (I-VI)**
- No melanin-targeting mechanism; does not cause post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) or hypopigmentation
- Skin of color patients show equivalent efficacy and safety profiles

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Onset:** 24-72 hours
- **Peak effect:** 7-14 days
- **Duration:** 3-4 months (masseter treatments may last 6+ months)
- **Recommended interval:** Every 3-4 months for maintenance

#### Downtime
- **Minimal to none**
- Small bumps at injection sites resolve within 15-30 minutes
- Possible minor bruising (1-3 days)
- No activity restrictions beyond avoiding lying flat for 4 hours, strenuous exercise for 24 hours, and facial massage for 24 hours

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Per unit:** $10-25 (geographic variation)
- **Per area:** $300-600
- **Full face (3 areas):** $900-1,800
- **Hyperhidrosis:** $1,000-1,500
- **Masseter:** $400-800

#### Evidence Level: **A**
Multiple FDA approvals, decades of clinical use, extensive RCTs and meta-analyses. One of the most-studied cosmetic procedures.

#### Risks/Side Effects
**Common (1-10%):**
- Injection site pain, bruising, edema
- Eyebrow asymmetry or drooping (ptosis) — 1-2%
- Headache (first 24-48 hours)
- "Frozen" or unnatural expression (dose-dependent)
- Flu-like symptoms (rare with cosmetic doses)

**Uncommon (<1%):**
- Eyelid ptosis (if frontalis over-treated or diffusion to levator palpebrae)
- Diplopia (double vision)
- Asymmetric smile
- Dry eye or excessive tearing

**Rare but serious:**
- Systemic toxin spread (distant muscle weakness, difficulty swallowing/breathing) — extremely rare at cosmetic doses
- Allergic reaction/anaphylaxis

**SKINgenius safety note:** Always verify practitioner credentials. Complications increase with unqualified injectors.

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Dermal fillers:** "Liquid facelift" — BoNT for dynamic wrinkles, fillers for static volume loss
- **+ Laser/IPL:** Wait 2 weeks between BoNT and laser to avoid toxin diffusion from heat/inflammation
- **+ Microneedling:** Can be done same day (microneedling first, then BoNT)
- **+ Chemical peels:** Wait 1-2 weeks between treatments

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient has dynamic wrinkles (visible with expression, not at rest), wants non-surgical prevention/treatment of aging signs, has realistic expectations
- **Refer to dermatologist/plastic surgeon when:** Considering high-volume or off-label use, has neuromuscular conditions, requires complex facial analysis for asymmetry correction, or seeks permanent solutions
- **First-line for:** Glabellar lines, crow's feet, forehead rhytides in patients aged 25-65

### 1B. DERMAL FILLERS

#### Mechanism of Action
Dermal fillers restore volume, smooth wrinkles, and enhance facial contours through physical tissue expansion and/or biostimulation. Categories by composition:

**Hyaluronic Acid (HA) Fillers (Juvederm®, Restylane®, Belotero®, Revanesse®):**
- HA is a naturally occurring glycosaminoglycan (GAG) in the dermis
- Attracts and binds water (up to 1,000x its weight in water)
- Provides immediate volume through physical displacement
- Cross-linking (with BDDE) determines gel hardness, longevity, and lift capacity
- **Non-animal stabilized HA (NASHA):** Restylane — firmer, more structured
- **Hylacross:** Juvederm Ultra — smoother, more cohesive
- **Vycross:** Juvederm Voluma/Vollure/Volbella — balanced cohesivity and duration
- **Reversible with hyaluronidase** (Hylenex, Vitrase)

**Calcium Hydroxylapatite (CaHA) — Radiesse®:**
- Microspheres (25-45 μm) of CaHA in carboxymethylcellulose gel carrier
- Gel provides immediate filling; CaHA stimulates neocollagenesis over 3-6 months
- Biodegradable; metabolized to calcium and phosphate
- **Not reversible** with hyaluronidase

**Poly-L-Lactic Acid (PLLA) — Sculptra®:**
- Biocompatible, biodegradable synthetic polymer
- Microparticles (40-63 μm) stimulate macrophage response and fibroblast activation
- Progressive collagen synthesis over 6-12 weeks
- Requires reconstitution (5-9 mL sterile water) and massage post-injection
- **Not a filler** in the traditional sense — a biostimulator

**Polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) — Bellafill®:**
- 20% PMMA microspheres (30-50 μm) in 80% bovine collagen carrier
- Collagen provides immediate volume; PMMA provides permanent structural scaffold
- Stimulates long-term collagen deposition around microspheres
- **Permanent/very long-lasting** (5+ years)
- Requires skin testing for bovine collagen allergy

**Autologous Fat Transfer:**
- Liposuction harvest → purification → reinjection
- Living adipocytes that can survive long-term (30-70% retention)
- Requires overcorrection (typically 30-50% more than desired)
- Variable resorption; may need multiple sessions

#### Indications by Filler Type

| Filler Type | Best For | Avoid For |
|-------------|----------|-----------|
| HA (soft) | Lips, tear troughs, fine lines | Areas requiring significant structural support |
| HA (firm) | Cheeks, jawline, chin, nasolabial folds | Superficial injection (Tyndall effect risk) |
| CaHA | Nasolabial folds, cheeks, hands | Lips (nodule risk), tear troughs |
| PLLA | General facial volume loss, HIV lipoatrophy | Isolated deep wrinkles, immediate results needed |
| PMMA | Deep nasolabial folds, acne scars | First-time filler patients, lips, thin skin areas |
| Fat | Large volume restoration, natural augmentation | Patients unwilling to undergo liposuction |

#### Contraindications
- **Absolute:** Severe allergy/anaphylaxis history, bleeding disorders, active infection/inflammation at injection site, autoimmune connective tissue diseases (relative), keloid formation history (consider carefully)
- **HA-specific:** Hypersensitivity to HA, gram-positive bacterial proteins (some products), lidocaine (if using formulated product)
- **CaHA:** Avoid in thin-skinned areas, lips
- **PLLA:** Avoid in areas with active inflammation, recent radiation
- **PMMA:** Must complete negative skin test 4 weeks prior; avoid if positive for bovine collagen allergy
- **Fat transfer:** Insufficient donor fat, unrealistic expectations about resorption

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Generally safe for all skin types**
- **Higher risk in darker skin (IV-VI):** Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) from injection trauma or vascular compromise
- **Critical:** Proper technique to avoid vascular occlusion (risk of skin necrosis and blindness) is independent of skin type but must be emphasized in all patients
- **Tyndall effect** (blueish tint from superficial HA placement) more visible on lighter skin but can occur in all types

#### Expected Results Timeline

| Filler Type | Immediate Effect | Full Effect | Duration |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------|----------|
| HA (soft) | Yes | Immediate | 6-12 months |
| HA (firm/Vycross) | Yes | Immediate | 12-24 months |
| CaHA | Yes | 3-6 months | 12-18 months |
| PLLA | Minimal | 6-12 weeks | 24+ months |
| PMMA | Yes (collagen carrier) | 3 months | 5+ years |
| Fat | Yes (overcorrected) | 3-6 months | Variable (years if survived) |

#### Downtime
- **Minimal:** Most patients resume normal activities immediately
- **Bruising:** 3-7 days (more common with deep injections, blood thinners)
- **Swelling:** 2-7 days (lips may swell for 1-2 weeks)
- **Avoid:** Strenuous exercise 24 hours, excessive heat/sun 48 hours, facial massage 1-2 weeks (unless instructed for PLLA)

#### Cost Range (US)
- **HA fillers:** $600-1,200 per syringe (1 mL); most areas need 1-3 syringes
- **CaHA (Radiesse):** $800-1,500 per syringe (1.5 mL)
- **PLLA (Sculptra):** $800-1,500 per vial; typically 2-4 vials for full face
- **PMMA (Bellafill):** $1,500-2,500 per syringe
- **Fat transfer:** $3,000-8,000 (includes liposuction, anesthesia)

#### Evidence Level: **A** (HA, CaHA), **B** (PLLA), **B** (PMMA — fewer long-term studies)

#### Risks/Side Effects

**Common (expected):**
- Injection site pain, bruising, swelling, erythema
- Temporary numbness or tingling
- Asymmetry (often resolves with settling)

**Uncommon but serious:**
- **Vascular occlusion:** Filler injected into or compressing an artery → tissue ischemia → necrosis. Can cause **blindness** if retrograde embolization to retinal artery. **Medical emergency.** Incidence: ~0.001-0.01% but catastrophic.
  - Recognition: Immediate blanching, livedo pattern, severe pain
  - Treatment: Hyaluronidase (for HA), aspirin, nitroglycerin paste, warm compresses, hyperbaric oxygen if available
- **Infection:** Bacterial (S. aureus, streptococci), biofilm formation — rare but requires antibiotics and possible drainage
- **Nodule/granuloma formation:** Immune reaction to filler; incidence 0.01-1%
  - Treatment: Intralesional steroids, 5-FU, hyaluronidase (HA only), surgical excision
- **Tyndall effect:** Blue discoloration from superficial HA placement
- **Migration:** Filler displacement from injection site

**Delayed complications (weeks to years):**
- Delayed inflammatory nodules (especially with PLLA, CaHA, permanent fillers)
- Allergic reactions
- Foreign body granulomas

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Botulinum toxin:** Classic combination — "liquid facelift"; toxins for dynamic lines, fillers for static wrinkles/volume
- **+ Thread lifts:** Fillers for volume, threads for lifting vector
- **+ Laser/IPL:** Wait 2-4 weeks between filler and energy-based treatments
- **+ Microneedling/RF microneedling:** Can enhance collagen response; separate by 2-4 weeks

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient has static wrinkles (present at rest), volume loss in cheeks/temples/lips, desires non-surgical facial rejuvenation
- **First-line:** HA fillers for most applications due to reversibility and safety profile
- **Refer to specialist when:** Complex facial analysis needed, permanent fillers considered, significant volume loss requiring surgical consultation, history of complications
- **Absolute red flag:** Any provider who cannot explain vascular occlusion risk or does not have hyaluronidase on-site for HA fillers

### 1C. BIOSTIMULATORS

#### Mechanism of Action
Biostimulators differ from traditional fillers by primarily inducing endogenous collagen, elastin, and extracellular matrix production rather than providing immediate physical volume:

**Poly-L-Lactic Acid (PLLA) — Sculptra®:**
- PLLA microparticles (40-63 μm) injected into deep dermis/subcutis
- Macrophages phagocytose particles → inflammatory cascade → fibroblast recruitment
- Progressive Type I and Type III collagen neosynthesis over 6-12 weeks
- Results develop gradually over 2-3 sessions spaced 4-6 weeks apart
- Metabolized to lactic acid monomers → CO₂ and water

**Calcium Hydroxylapatite (CaHA) — Radiesse®:**
- CaHA microspheres act as scaffold for collagen deposition
- Gel carrier (carboxymethylcellulose) provides immediate volume (3-6 months)
- Microspheres stimulate fibroblasts to produce new collagen
- Completely biodegraded to calcium and phosphate ions over 12-18 months

**Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP) / Platelet-Rich Fibrin (PRF):**
- See Section 4A for detailed mechanism
- Growth factors (PDGF, TGF-β, VEGF, IGF, FGF) stimulate collagen synthesis, angiogenesis, and cell proliferation
- Used as standalone or mixed with other biostimulators

#### Indications
- Generalized facial volume loss (not isolated wrinkles)
- HIV-associated lipoatrophy (PLLA specifically FDA-approved)
- Skin laxity and crepiness
- Hand rejuvenation (Radiesse FDA-approved)
- Décolletage aging
- Acne scar improvement (as adjunct)
- Cellulite improvement (off-label, emerging)

#### Contraindications
- Active infection or inflammation at treatment site
- Autoimmune connective tissue disease (relative)
- Keloid/scar formation history
- Pregnancy, breastfeeding
- For PLLA: Recent radiation therapy to area
- For CaHA: Thin skin areas, lips (nodule risk)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Safe for all skin types**
- **PIH risk** from injection trauma higher in darker skin — use smallest gauge needles, minimize passes
- Post-procedure sun protection critical for Fitzpatrick IV-VI
- PLLA nodules may be more visible on darker skin

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Onset:** Minimal immediate effect (except CaHA gel carrier)
- **Visible improvement:** 6-12 weeks after first session
- **Optimal results:** After 2-4 sessions spaced 4-6 weeks apart
- **Duration:** 24+ months (PLLA), 12-18 months (CaHA)

#### Downtime
- **Minimal:** Similar to fillers
- **Swelling:** 2-7 days
- **Bruising:** 3-7 days
- **Subcutaneous nodules:** If product not properly reconstituted or massaged (PLLA)
- **Post-PLLA:** Requires 5-5-5 massage protocol (5 minutes, 5 times daily, 5 days)

#### Cost Range (US)
- **PLLA (Sculptra):** $800-1,500 per vial; 2-4 vials for full facial treatment series
- **CaHA (Radiesse):** $800-1,500 per syringe
- **Series cost:** $2,000-5,000 for complete biostimulator regimen

#### Evidence Level: **A** (PLLA for HIV lipoatrophy), **B** (general cosmetic indications), **B** (CaHA)

#### Risks/Side Effects
- Injection site reactions (pain, bruising, swelling)
- Nodule formation (PLLA: 6-13% if not massaged; CaHA: 0.5-2%)
- Granuloma formation (rare: <1%)
- Asymmetry
- Overcorrection (difficult with slow-onset products)
- Vascular occlusion (rare, as with all injectables)

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Microneedling:** Enhanced collagen induction
- **+ Laser/RF:** Stimulate different collagen remodeling pathways
- **+ HA fillers:** Biostimulators for global volume, HA for targeted areas
- **+ PRP/PRF:** Growth factors may enhance biostimulator response

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient wants gradual, natural-looking improvement; has global volume loss rather than isolated wrinkles; is willing to wait for results
- **Best for:** Patients aged 40-60 with moderate facial volume loss
- **Not ideal for:** Patients wanting immediate results, those who cannot commit to multiple sessions
- **Refer when:** Patient has history of keloid/scar formation, autoimmune disease, or needs complex facial analysis

### 2A. LASER RESURFACING

#### Mechanism of Action
Laser resurfacing uses focused light energy to remove damaged skin layers and stimulate collagen remodeling. "Laser" stands for Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation.

**Tissue interaction principles:**
- **Selective photothermolysis:** Target chromophore (water, hemoglobin, melanin) absorbs specific wavelength → thermal damage confined to target
- **Ablative lasers:** Vaporize tissue (water as chromophore) — immediate tissue removal + thermal coagulation zone stimulates healing cascade
- **Non-ablative lasers:** Heat dermis without epidermal destruction → collagen denaturation and neocollagenesis

**Ablative Lasers:**

**CO₂ Laser (10,600 nm):**
- Water chromophore absorption peak
- Full ablation removes epidermis and variable depth of dermis
- Creates immediate tissue vaporization + residual thermal damage (RTD) zone of 50-150 μm
- Traditional CO₂: Excellent results, significant downtime (2-4 weeks)
- Fractional CO₂: Creates microthermal zones (MTZs) of ablation surrounded by intact tissue → faster healing, less downtime, slightly less dramatic results

**Erbium:YAG (Er:YAG, 2,940 nm):**
- 10-16x higher water absorption than CO₂
- More precise tissue ablation with less thermal damage (<5 μm RTD vs 50-150 μm for CO₂)
- Less hemostasis than CO₂ (more bleeding)
- Heals faster than traditional CO₂ (7-10 days vs 2-4 weeks)
- Better for superficial resurfacing and darker skin types

**Non-Ablative Lasers:**

**Fractional Non-Ablative (1,540-1,550 nm Erbium:glass, 1,440 nm):**
- Deliver heat in fractionated pattern through intact epidermis
- MTZs in dermis stimulate collagen without epidermal removal
- Minimal downtime (1-3 days of redness)
- Requires multiple sessions (3-6) for significant improvement

**Pulsed Dye Laser (PDL, 585-595 nm):**
- Hemoglobin chromophore
- Targets vascular lesions, redness, rosacea
- Also stimulates collagen through thermal injury

#### Indications

| Condition | Best Laser | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| Deep wrinkles | Ablative CO₂/Er:YAG | Most dramatic results |
| Moderate wrinkles | Fractional CO₂ | Balance of efficacy/downtime |
| Fine lines | Fractional non-ablative | Multiple sessions needed |
| Acne scars | Fractional CO₂/Er:YAG | Significant improvement possible |
| Surgical scars | Fractional lasers | Multiple sessions |
| Actinic keratoses | Ablative or fractionated | Often combined with PDT |
| Solar lentigines | Q-switched lasers, IPL | Pigment-specific |
| Skin laxity | Ablative or RF | Modest tightening |
| Rhinophyma | CO₂ laser | Reshaping |

#### Contraindications
- Active infection (bacterial, viral — especially HSV), open wounds
- Recent isotretinoin use (within 6-12 months for ablative) — impaired healing, scarring risk
- Pregnancy
- Keloid/scar formation history
- Active inflammatory skin conditions (eczema, psoriasis, dermatitis)
- Connective tissue disease (relative)
- Recent radiation therapy to area
- Fitzpatrick VI with ablative lasers (high hypopigmentation risk)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety

| Skin Type | Ablative CO₂ | Fractional CO₂ | Non-Ablative Fractional | Er:YAG |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|------------------------|--------|
| I-II | Excellent | Excellent | Excellent | Excellent |
| III-IV | Caution | Good with caution | Good | Better choice |
| V-VI | Avoid | High risk | Moderate risk | Best ablative option |

- **Hypopigmentation risk:** Up to 20% with ablative CO₂ in darker skin; rare with fractional approaches
- **Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH):** 10-30% in Fitzpatrick IV-VI after ablative lasers; 5-10% with fractional
- **Pre-treatment:** Hydroquinone 4% + tretinoin + sunscreen for 4-6 weeks pre-op in darker skin
- **Post-treatment:** Strict sun protection, hydroquinone if PIH develops

#### Expected Results Timeline

| Laser Type | Visible Improvement | Optimal Results | Duration |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|----------|
| Ablative CO₂ | 2-4 weeks | 3-6 months | 5-10+ years |
| Fractional CO₂ | 1-2 weeks | 3-6 months | 3-5 years |
| Er:YAG | 1-2 weeks | 3 months | 2-5 years |
| Non-ablative fractional | After each session | 6 months post-series | 2-3 years |

#### Downtime

| Laser Type | Redness/Swelling | Peeling/Healing | Makeup | Full Recovery |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|--------|---------------|
| Ablative CO₂ | 2-4 weeks | 7-14 days | 2-4 weeks | 3-6 months |
| Fractional CO₂ | 3-7 days | 3-5 days | 5-7 days | 1-2 months |
| Er:YAG | 5-10 days | 3-7 days | 7-10 days | 1-3 months |
| Non-ablative fractional | 1-3 days | Minimal | 24-48 hours | 1 week |

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Ablative CO₂ (full face):** $3,000-6,000
- **Fractional CO₂ (full face):** $1,500-3,500
- **Er:YAG:** $2,000-4,000
- **Non-ablative fractional series:** $1,000-2,500 per session; $3,000-7,500 for series

#### Evidence Level: **A**
Decades of clinical data, multiple RCTs, established as gold standard for skin resurfacing.

#### Risks/Side Effects

**Expected (all types):**
- Erythema, edema, warmth
- Crusting, peeling
- Sensitivity

**Common complications:**
- Prolonged erythema (weeks to months)
- PIH (especially darker skin)
- Hypopigmentation (especially ablative in darker skin)
- Milia formation
- Acne flare
- Contact dermatitis from post-procedure products

**Serious complications:**
- Infection (bacterial, viral HSV reactivation, fungal)
- Scarring (rare with proper technique)
- Ectropion (if periorbital over-treated)
- Prolonged healing (>3 months)

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Botulinum toxin:** Wait 2-4 weeks post-laser before toxin (to avoid diffusion); or do toxin 1-2 weeks pre-laser
- **+ Fillers:** Wait 4-6 weeks post-laser
- **+ Chemical peels:** Not typically combined; laser is more aggressive alternative
- **+ PRP:** Applied immediately post-ablative laser to enhance healing ("vampire facial" concept) — evidence mixed
- **+ Topical retinoids:** Resume 2-4 weeks post-healing for maintenance

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Significant photoaging, deep wrinkles, acne scarring, patient can accommodate downtime
- **First-line:** Fractional CO₂ or Er:YAG for most patients (balance of efficacy/safety)
- **Avoid:** Fitzpatrick V-VI for ablative procedures; recent isotretinoin users; patients with unrealistic expectations about downtime
- **Always:** Pre-treatment with antiviral prophylaxis (valacyclovir) for ablative procedures due to HSV reactivation risk
- **Refer:** Any ablative procedure should be performed by experienced laser surgeon; complications increase dramatically with inexperienced providers

### 2B. IPL (INTENSE PULSED LIGHT)

#### Mechanism of Action
IPL uses a broad spectrum of light (typically 500-1,200 nm) filtered to target specific chromophores:

**Key differences from lasers:**
- **Broad spectrum vs. single wavelength:** IPL emits multiple wavelengths; lasers emit one
- **Non-coherent vs. coherent light:** IPL is polychromatic; laser is monochromatic
- **Larger spot size:** Treats broader areas faster
- **Less specific:** May affect surrounding tissue more than targeted chromophore

**Tissue interactions:**
- **Hemoglobin (vascular lesions):** 500-600 nm absorption → vessel coagulation
- **Melanin (pigment):** 600-1,200 nm absorption → pigment fragmentation
- **Water (collagen heating):** Longer wavelengths → dermal heating → collagen remodeling

**Cut-off filters:** Used to select appropriate wavelength range for target:
- 515 nm filter: Pigmented lesions, vascular lesions (light skin)
- 560-590 nm: General photorejuvenation
- 615-755 nm: Hair removal, deeper vascular lesions

#### Indications
- **Vascular lesions:** Telangiectasias, rosacea, facial redness, spider veins
- **Pigmented lesions:** Solar lentigines (age spots), ephelides (freckles), melasma (caution)
- **Photorejuvenation:** Overall skin tone, texture, fine lines
- **Hair reduction:** Multiple sessions required
- **Acne:** Blue light component kills *P. acnes* (limited evidence)

#### Contraindications
- Recent tanning or sun exposure (increased melanin competes with target)
- Pregnancy
- Active infection
- Photosensitizing medications (tetracyclines, isotretinoin, amiodarone, etc.)
- Active cold sore history (HSV prophylaxis recommended)
- Fitzpatrick V-VI (see below)
- Unrealistic expectations

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Best for:** Fitzpatrick I-III
- **Caution for:** Fitzpatrick IV
- **Avoid/high risk:** Fitzpatrick V-VI
  - Melanin in epidermis absorbs broad IPL spectrum → thermal injury to epidermis → PIH, blistering, scarring
  - If used in darker skin: Longer wavelengths (690+ nm), lower fluences, test spots mandatory
- **Paradoxical hypertrichosis:** Rare increase in hair growth, more common in darker skin types

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Vascular lesions:** 2-4 sessions; improvement visible after 1st session
- **Pigment:** 2-3 sessions; darkening then sloughing over 1-2 weeks
- **Photorejuvenation:** 3-6 sessions; gradual improvement
- **Hair reduction:** 6-10 sessions; maintenance required
- **Duration:** Results for pigment/vascular: months to years with sun protection; hair reduction: permanent reduction but not elimination

#### Downtime
- **Minimal:** "Lunchtime procedure"
- **Immediate:** Mild redness, warmth (1-24 hours)
- **Pigment:** Darkening of spots for 3-7 days before sloughing
- **Avoid:** Sun exposure, heat, strenuous exercise for 24-48 hours

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Per session (face):** $300-600
- **Series (3-6 sessions):** $1,500-3,500
- **Package pricing:** Often available

#### Evidence Level: **A** (vascular, pigment), **B** (photorejuvenation), **B** (hair reduction)

#### Risks/Side Effects
**Common:**
- Pain/discomfort during treatment ("rubber band snap")
- Erythema, edema (hours to days)
- Pigment darkening then lightening
- Mild crusting

**Uncommon:**
- Blistering
- PIH (especially darker skin)
- Hypopigmentation
- Scarring (rare)
- Paradoxical hair growth

**Rare but serious:**
- Burns
- Eye injury (if eye protection inadequate)
- Skin texture changes

#### Comparison: IPL vs. Laser

| Factor | IPL | Laser |
|--------|-----|-------|
| Light type | Broad spectrum | Single wavelength |
| Specificity | Less specific | Highly specific |
| Treatment time | Faster (large areas) | Slower (precise) |
| Downtime | Minimal | Variable |
| Fitzpatrick safety | I-III best | More options for darker skin |
| Cost per session | Lower | Higher |
| Number of sessions | More | Fewer |
| Best for | Multiple concerns at once, budget | Specific concerns, deeper issues |

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Chemical peels:** Alternate sessions (IPL one month, peel next)
- **+ Botulinum toxin:** Can be done same day (toxin first, then IPL)
- **+ Fillers:** Wait 2 weeks between treatments
- **+ Retinoids:** Discontinue 1 week pre/post IPL; resume after healing

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient has multiple concerns (redness + pigment), Fitzpatrick I-III, wants minimal downtime
- **First-line for:** Rosacea, facial telangiectasias, solar lentigines in light skin
- **Avoid:** Fitzpatrick V-VI (use Nd:YAG 1,064 nm laser instead for vascular); recent tanners; melasma (can worsen)
- **Critical:** Strict pre- and post-treatment sun protection; treatment of tanned skin significantly increases complication risk

### 2C. RADIOFREQUENCY (RF)

#### Mechanism of Action
RF devices deliver electrical energy that creates **ohmic heating** in tissues:

**Physics:**
- Alternating current (0.3-40 MHz) causes ions in tissue to oscillate
- Friction from ion movement generates heat
- Heat is produced in proportion to tissue resistance (impedance)
- Deeper penetration than many lasers; not dependent on chromophore

**Biological effects:**
- **Immediate:** Collagen fibers denature and contract (60-65°C) → immediate tightening
- **Short-term:** Inflammatory cascade → neocollagenesis
- **Long-term:** Dermal remodeling, new collagen and elastin synthesis over 3-6 months

**Types of RF delivery:**

1. **Monopolar RF (Thermage®):**
   - Single electrode on skin, grounding pad elsewhere on body
   - Current flows deep through tissue → volumetric heating
   - Deepest penetration; can reach subcutaneous fat
   - Requires cooling to protect epidermis
   - Painful; may require anesthesia

2. **Bipolar RF:**
   - Two electrodes close together on skin surface
   - Current flows between electrodes → superficial heating
   - Less penetration; safer for thinner skin areas
   - Often combined with other energies

3. **Fractional RF (Morpheus8®, Profound®):**
   - Microneedles deliver RF energy at precise depths (0.5-7 mm)
   - Combines mechanical microneedling with thermal RF energy
   - Creates fractionated thermal zones at controlled depths
   - Can target specific layers (dermis, subcutis)
   - Significant improvement in skin laxity, acne scars, wrinkles

4. **Tripolar/Multipolar RF:**
   - Multiple electrodes create more controlled current paths
   - Improved safety and comfort
   - Used in many at-home devices (limited efficacy)

5. **Combination systems (Forma®, Evolve®):**
   - RF + vacuum, RF + infrared, or RF + PEMF
   - Enhanced lymphatic drainage and tissue manipulation

#### Indications
- **Skin laxity:** Jowls, neck, brow, abdomen, arms, thighs
- **Wrinkles and fine lines**
- **Acne scars:** Especially fractional RF (Morpheus8)
- **Cellulite:** Combined with vacuum/subdermal approaches
- **Body contouring:** Subcutaneous fat reduction (at higher energies)
- **Hyperhidrosis:** RF thermolysis of sweat glands

#### Contraindications
- Active infection, open wounds
- Pacemaker or implanted electrical device (RF can interfere)
- Metal implants in treatment area
- Pregnancy
- Autoimmune connective tissue disease (relative)
- History of keloid/scar formation (relative)
- Recent isotretinoin (for aggressive fractional RF)
- Severe cardiac disease

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Safe for ALL skin types (I-VI)** — major advantage over lasers
- No melanin chromophore dependence → no PIH or hypopigmentation risk from thermal injury to melanocytes
- Fractional RF microneedling may cause temporary PIH in darker skin from needle trauma (rare)
- **Preferred energy device for skin of color**

#### Expected Results Timeline

| Device Type | Immediate Effect | Visible Improvement | Optimal Results | Duration |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|-----------------|----------|
| Monopolar (Thermage) | Minimal | 2-3 months | 6 months | 1-2 years |
| Fractional RF (Morpheus8) | Mild tightening | 2-4 weeks | 3 months | 1-2 years |
| Bipolar/multipolar | Minimal | 4-8 weeks | 3 months | 6-12 months |

- **Sessions:** 1 (Thermage) to 3-6 (bipolar); 3-4 (Morpheus8)
- **Maintenance:** Annual or as needed

#### Downtime

| Device Type | Redness | Swelling | Activity Restriction |
|-------------|---------|----------|----------------------|
| Monopolar | 1-2 days | Mild | None |
| Fractional RF | 2-5 days | 1-3 days | 24-48 hours |
| Bipolar | None-minimal | None | None |

- **Morpheus8:** Pinpoint bleeding, redness, swelling for 2-5 days; visible micro-injuries for 1-2 days

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Thermage (full face):** $2,000-4,000
- **Morpheus8 (full face):** $800-1,500 per session; $2,400-4,500 for series
- **Bipolar/multipolar series:** $1,500-3,000
- **Body treatments:** $1,500-4,000 per area

#### Evidence Level: **B** (monopolar), **B** (fractional RF), **C** (bipolar/multipolar alone)

#### Risks/Side Effects
**Common:**
- Pain during treatment (monopolar especially)
- Erythema, edema
- Warmth sensation
- Temporary numbness

**Uncommon:**
- Burns (improper technique or equipment malfunction)
- Blistering
- PIH (rare, mainly from microneedle trauma in darker skin)
- Subcutaneous fat atrophy (over-treatment with deep RF)
- Scarring (rare)

**Rare but serious:**
- Nerve injury
- Asymmetry
- Volume loss (deep RF in face)

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Fillers:** Wait 2-4 weeks between treatments
- **+ Botulinum toxin:** Wait 2 weeks
- **+ Microneedling:** Fractional RF is essentially advanced microneedling + heat
- **+ Fat reduction (CoolSculpting, etc.):** RF for skin tightening post-fat reduction
- **+ Laser:** Not typically same session; alternate modalities

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient has skin laxity (mild to moderate), wants non-surgical tightening, has darker skin (Fitzpatrick IV-VI), or wants minimal downtime
- **First-line for:** Skin of color patients seeking tightening; patients wanting "preventive" collagen stimulation
- **Best device:** Morpheus8 for comprehensive results (scars, laxity, texture); Thermage for single-treatment convenience
- **Realistic expectations:** 20-30% improvement in laxity; not a facelift substitute
- **Refer:** Patients with severe laxity needing surgical evaluation; patients with implanted devices that may interfere

### 2D. ULTRASOUND (Ultherapy®, Sofwave®)

#### Mechanism of Action
High-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU) delivers precise thermal energy to specific tissue depths:

**Physics:**
- Ultrasound waves (4-10 MHz for imaging; 4-7 MHz for treatment) penetrate tissue
- At focal point, acoustic energy converts to heat (60-70°C)
- Creates thermal coagulation points (TCPs) at precise depths without damaging intervening tissue
- Depth control: 1.5 mm (dermis), 3.0 mm (deep dermis/SMAS), 4.5 mm (SMAS/fascial layer)

**Biological response:**
- **Immediate:** Collagen denaturation and contraction
- **Short-term:** Wound healing cascade, inflammatory response
- **Long-term:** Neocollagenesis, elastin remodeling over 3-6 months
- **SMAS layer targeting:** Unique ability to non-surgically affect superficial muscular aponeurotic system (foundation for facelift surgery)

**Device variations:**
- **Ultherapy®:** Microfocused ultrasound with visualization (MFU-V); real-time ultrasound imaging to see tissue layers
- **Sofwave®:** Parallel beam technology; 7 parallel beams at 1.5 mm depth; larger treatment zones; less pain
- **Other devices:** Various HIFU systems with different frequencies and focal depths

#### Indications
- **Brow lifting:** Non-surgical brow elevation (FDA-approved)
- **Neck and submental tightening:** "Turkey neck" improvement
- **Jawline definition:** Mild to moderate jowling
- **Décolletage wrinkles:** Chest lines
- **General facial laxity:** Overall skin tightening
- **Body:** Arms, abdomen, thighs (off-label, variable results)

#### Contraindications
- Open wounds, active infection
- Severe or cystic acne in treatment area
- Metal implants in treatment area
- Pregnancy, breastfeeding
- Pacemakers or implanted electrical devices
- Unrealistic expectations

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Safe for ALL skin types (I-VI)**
- No melanin dependence → no risk of PIH or hypopigmentation
- Ultrasound passes through melanin-rich epidermis without absorption
- **Excellent option for skin of color**
- Rarely, temporary edema or nerve irritation can occur in any skin type

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Immediate:** Minimal visible change (some subtle tightening from collagen contraction)
- **Visible improvement:** 2-3 months
- **Optimal results:** 3-6 months
- **Duration:** 12-18 months (individual variation)
- **Sessions:** 1 treatment typically; some patients benefit from second treatment at 6 months

#### Downtime
- **Essentially none**
- Mild redness: 1-2 hours
- Rare: Swelling, tingling, tenderness for 1-7 days
- Transient bruising (rare)
- No activity restrictions

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Full face and neck:** $3,000-5,500
- **Partial face (brow/lower face):** $1,500-3,000
- **Décolletage:** $1,000-2,000
- **Package (face + neck + chest):** $4,000-7,000

#### Evidence Level: **B**
FDA-cleared with clinical trials, but evidence less robust than some other modalities. Some debate about degree of improvement.

#### Risks/Side Effects
**Common:**
- Pain during treatment (significant for Ultherapy; less with Sofwave)
- Temporary redness, swelling
- Numbness or tingling (resolves in days to weeks)
- Tenderness to touch

**Uncommon:**
- Bruising
- Temporary nerve palsy (marginal mandibular branch — resolves in weeks to months)
- Prolonged erythema
- Subcutaneous nodules

**Rare but serious:**
- Burns
- Scarring
- Fat atrophy (if treated too deeply in areas with minimal SMAS)
- Persistent nerve injury

#### Comparison: Ultrasound vs. RF

| Factor | Ultrasound (HIFU) | Radiofrequency |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| Depth reached | SMAS (4.5 mm) | Dermis to subcutis (variable) |
| Precision | Very precise focal points | Broader heating zones |
| Pain level | Higher (Ultherapy) | Moderate to high |
| Skin type safety | All | All |
| Downtime | None | Minimal |
| Cost | Higher | Moderate to high |
| Evidence for lifting | Moderate | Moderate |
| Best for | Brow, neck, jawline | Overall laxity, body |

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Botulinum toxin:** Wait 2 weeks between treatments
- **+ Fillers:** Wait 4-6 weeks
- **+ RF:** Can be complementary; not same session typically
- **+ Thread lifts:** Ultrasound first for deep lifting, threads for immediate support

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient has mild to moderate skin laxity (brow, neck, jawline), wants non-surgical option with zero downtime, has darker skin
- **Best for:** Brow ptosis, early jowling, neck laxity in patients 35-55
- **Realistic expectations:** 20-30% improvement; not a facelift substitute; results subtle and gradual
- **Consider:** Sofwave for reduced pain vs. Ultherapy; patient pain tolerance matters
- **Refer:** Patients with significant laxity needing surgical evaluation; those expecting dramatic immediate results

### 2E. LED/LOW-LEVEL LIGHT THERAPY (LLLT)

#### Mechanism of Action
LED therapy uses specific wavelengths of light to trigger photochemical reactions in cells without thermal damage:

**Photobiomodulation principles:**
- Light absorbed by chromophores in mitochondria (cytochrome c oxidase)
- Increases ATP production, reduces oxidative stress, modulates inflammation
- Non-thermal, non-ablative, non-invasive

**Wavelengths and effects:**

| Wavelength | Color | Depth | Primary Effects |
|-----------|-------|-------|----------------|
| 415 nm | Blue | 1 mm | *P. acnes* destruction (porphyrin activation), anti-inflammatory |
| 630-660 nm | Red | 4-5 mm | Increased ATP, collagen stimulation, anti-inflammatory, improved circulation |
| 830 nm | Near-infrared (NIR) | 5-10 mm | Deep tissue repair, enhanced collagen, reduced inflammation, pain relief |
| 590 nm | Yellow/Amber | 1-2 mm | Redness reduction, lymphatic drainage |
| 525 nm | Green | 1-2 mm | Pigment modulation (limited evidence), anti-inflammatory |

**Cellular mechanisms:**
- **Red/NIR:** Activates cytochrome c oxidase → ↑ ATP → ↑ cellular metabolism → ↑ fibroblast activity → ↑ collagen, elastin
- **Blue:** Activates bacterial porphyrins → reactive oxygen species → bacterial death
- **Anti-inflammatory:** Reduces pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α), increases anti-inflammatory mediators

#### Indications
- **Acne:** Blue light (415 nm) for active acne; red for inflammation and healing
- **Anti-aging/photorejuvenation:** Red + NIR for collagen stimulation
- **Wound healing:** Red + NIR to accelerate tissue repair
- **Post-procedure:** Reduce inflammation, speed healing after lasers, peels, surgery
- **Rosacea/Redness:** Red + yellow to reduce inflammation and vascular reactivity
- **Hair growth:** Red + NIR for androgenetic alopecia (low-level laser therapy — LLLT)
- **Pain relief:** NIR for musculoskeletal conditions

#### Contraindications
- **Photosensitivity disorders:** Lupus, porphyria, albinism, xeroderma pigmentosum
- **Photosensitizing medications:** Isotretinoin, tetracyclines, psoralens, amiodarone, etc.
- **Active skin cancer or precancerous lesions** (avoid stimulation)
- **Pregnancy:** Caution (limited safety data)
- **Epilepsy:** Flickering lights may trigger seizures in susceptible individuals
- **Eye disease:** Protect eyes during treatment; avoid if active retinal disease

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Safe for ALL skin types (I-VI)**
- No thermal injury → no risk of PIH or hypopigmentation
- Non-invasive, no tissue damage
- Ideal for sensitive skin and skin of color

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Acne:** Improvement in 2-4 weeks with consistent use (3-5x/week)
- **Anti-aging:** 8-12 weeks for visible improvement; requires ongoing maintenance
- **Wound healing:** Accelerated by 25-50%
- **Hair growth:** 3-6 months to see improvement
- **Duration:** Results require maintenance (2-3x/week ongoing)
- **Sessions:** Professional: 20-30 minutes, 2-3x/week for 4-6 weeks; At-home: Daily or every other day

#### Downtime
- **None whatsoever**
- Can be done during lunch break
- No restrictions after treatment
- May experience mild warmth or relaxation

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Professional single session:** $50-150
- **Professional package (6-12 sessions):** $500-2,000
- **At-home LED masks:** $200-600
- **At-home panels/devices:** $300-2,000
- **Medical-grade professional panels:** $10,000-50,000

#### Evidence Level: **B** (acne, wound healing), **C** (anti-aging), **C** (hair growth)
Growing evidence but many studies are small or industry-sponsored.

#### Risks/Side Effects
- **Extremely safe** — no known serious side effects
- Mild warmth during treatment
- Temporary redness (rare)
- Dryness (rare)
- Eye strain if proper protection not used
- **No thermal damage, no cancer risk** (non-ionizing radiation)

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Almost any procedure:** Excellent adjunct to reduce inflammation and speed healing
- **+ Topical skincare:** Enhances absorption and efficacy of serums
- **+ Retinoids:** Reduces irritation from retinoid use
- **+ Chemical peels:** Pre- and post-peel LED reduces downtime
- **+ Microneedling:** Post-microneedling LED enhances collagen response
- **+ Botulinum toxin/fillers:** Can be done same day

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient wants gentle, non-invasive maintenance; has acne, rosacea, or sensitivity; seeking post-procedure healing acceleration
- **First-line for:** Acne (especially mild to moderate, or as adjunct to topicals); post-procedure healing; patients seeking "preventive" anti-aging
- **Best approach:** Combine red + NIR for anti-aging; blue + red for acne
- **Caution:** Home devices vary widely in quality; medical-grade panels are stronger and more effective than most consumer masks
- **Realistic expectations:** Modest improvement; best as maintenance or adjunct, not standalone for significant concerns
- **Note:** Consistency is key — sporadic use yields minimal results

## 3. MECHANICAL PROCEDURES

### 3A. CHEMICAL PEELS

#### Mechanism of Action
Chemical peels use acidic solutions to cause controlled epidermal and/or dermal injury, triggering wound healing and skin renewal:

**Mechanism by depth:**

**Superficial Peels (epidermis to upper papillary dermis):**
- **Alpha-hydroxy acids (AHAs):** Glycolic acid (smallest, most penetrating), lactic acid, mandelic acid, citric acid, tartaric acid
  - Disrupt corneocyte cohesion by chelating calcium ions → desquamation
  - Stimulate epidermal turnover
  - Increase dermal glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)
  - Glycolic: 20-70% (professional); lactic: 10-30%

- **Beta-hydroxy acid (BHA):** Salicylic acid (lipid-soluble)
  - Penetrates sebaceous follicles → keratolytic, comedolytic
  - Anti-inflammatory (aspirin derivative)
  - Concentration: 20-30% (professional)

- **Jessner's solution:** Resorcinol 14% + salicylic acid 14% + lactic acid 14% in ethanol
  - Keratolytic, breaks up intracellular bridges
  - Often used as pre-treatment before TCA or as standalone superficial peel

- **Retinoic acid (vitamin A) peel:** 
  - Increases cell turnover, stimulates collagen
  - Often used as adjunct or in combination peels

**Medium Peels (papillary to upper reticular dermis):**
- **Trichloroacetic acid (TCA):** 35-50%
  - Protein denaturation, coagulative necrosis of epidermis and superficial dermis
  - Keratocoagulant effect visible as "frosting"
  - Stimulates significant collagen remodeling
  - Can be combined with Jessner's for enhanced penetration

**Deep Peels (mid-reticular dermis):**
- **Phenol (carbolic acid):** 88% (often with croton oil, septisol, water)
  - Causes epidermal/dermal necrosis to depth determined by concentration and application
  - Cardiotoxicity risk (must be performed with cardiac monitoring)
  - Hepatotoxicity, renal toxicity at high doses
  - Permanent hypopigmentation common
  - Dramatic results but highest risk
  - Typically 1-2 applications in lifetime

**Wound healing cascade post-peel:**
1. **Inflammation** (0-3 days): Neutrophil infiltration, cytokine release
2. **Proliferation** (3-14 days): Keratinocyte migration, fibroblast activation
3. **Remodeling** (weeks to months): Collagen synthesis, elastin remodeling, matrix reorganization

#### Indications by Peel Depth

| Concern | Superficial | Medium | Deep |
|---------|-------------|--------|------|
| Fine lines | Mild improvement | Good | Excellent |
| Acne | Excellent (salicylic) | Good | Not first-line |
| Acne scars | Mild | Moderate | Significant |
| Pigment (melasma) | Good (gentle AHAs) | Caution | Avoid |
| Sun damage | Mild | Good | Excellent |
| Texture/smoothness | Good | Excellent | Excellent |
| Actinic keratoses | Adjunct | Good | Excellent |
| Skin laxity | Minimal | Mild | Moderate |

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Active infection (bacterial, viral — HSV), open wounds
- Recent isotretinoin use (within 6-12 months for medium/deep)
- Pregnancy (especially salicylic acid — teratogenic; TCA and phenol also avoided)
- History of keloid/scar formation (medium/deep)
- Active eczema, psoriasis, dermatitis in treatment area
- Immunosuppression (impaired healing)

**Relative:**
- Darker skin types (medium/deep peels)
- History of PIH
- Photosensitivity disorders
- Poor wound healing (diabetes, smoking)
- Recent radiation therapy
- Unrealistic expectations

#### Fitzpatrick Safety

| Peel Type | Fitzpatrick I-III | Fitzpatrick IV | Fitzpatrick V-VI |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Superficial (AHA/BHA) | Excellent | Good with caution | Good with caution, pre-treat |
| Medium (TCA) | Good | High PIH risk | Avoid |
| Deep (Phenol) | Good with risks | Avoid | Avoid |

- **Pre-treatment for darker skin (IV-VI):** Hydroquinone 4% + tretinoin 0.05% + sunscreen for 4-6 weeks pre-peel reduces PIH risk
- **Post-treatment:** Strict sun protection, hydroquinone if PIH develops
- **Salicylic acid:** Generally safer for darker skin than TCA; self-neutralizing
- **Mandelic acid:** Large molecule, slow penetration — safest AHA for darker skin

#### Expected Results Timeline

| Peel Depth | Visible Improvement | Optimal Results | Duration |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|----------|
| Superficial | After 1-2 sessions | After series (4-6) | Maintain with monthly peels |
| Medium | 1-2 weeks | 2-3 months | 1-3 years |
| Deep | 2-4 weeks | 6-12 months | 5-10+ years |

#### Downtime

| Peel Depth | Redness | Peeling | Healing | Makeup | Sun Exposure |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|--------|--------------|
| Superficial | 1-2 days | Minimal/flaking | 1-3 days | Next day | Avoid 1 week |
| Medium | 3-7 days | 5-10 days | 7-14 days | 7-10 days | Avoid 2-4 weeks |
| Deep | 2-4 weeks | 7-14 days | 2-4 weeks | 2-4 weeks | Avoid 3-6 months |

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Superficial peel:** $100-300 per session
- **Superficial series (4-6):** $400-1,500
- **Medium peel:** $500-1,500
- **Deep peel (phenol):** $2,500-6,000
- **Combination peels:** $300-1,000

#### Evidence Level: **A** (superficial peels for acne, photoaging), **B** (medium peels), **B** (deep peels — fewer modern studies due to laser competition)

#### Risks/Side Effects

**Expected (all peels):**
- Stinging, burning during application
- Erythema, edema
- Peeling, flaking, dryness

**Common complications:**
- Prolonged erythema (>1 month)
- PIH (especially medium/deep in darker skin)
- Hypopigmentation (especially phenol)
- Milia, acne flare
- Contact dermatitis from post-peel products
- Infection (bacterial, HSV reactivation)

**Serious complications:**
- Scarring (medium/deep if improperly performed or poor healing)
- Cardiac arrhythmia (phenol — requires cardiac monitoring)
- Systemic toxicity (phenol — hepatic/renal)
- Persistent lines of demarcation (phenol, deep TCA)
- Ectropion (periorbital deep peels)

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Botulinum toxin:** Wait 1-2 weeks post-peel; or do toxin 2 weeks pre-peel
- **+ IPL/laser:** Alternate modalities; do not combine same day
- **+ Microneedling:** Wait 2-4 weeks between treatments
- **+ Retinoids:** Discontinue 1 week pre-peel (superficial) or longer (medium/deep); resume 2-4 weeks post-healing
- **+ LED:** Can enhance healing post-peel

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient wants improved texture, mild photoaging, acne; willing to commit to series
- **First-line for:** Acne (salicylic acid), mild photoaging (glycolic/lactic), maintenance
- **Best peel by skin type:**
  - Oily/acne: Salicylic acid
  - Sensitive/dry: Lactic acid
  - Darker skin: Mandelic acid, gentle salicylic
  - Significant photoaging: TCA medium (if appropriate skin type)
- **Avoid:** Medium/deep peels in Fitzpatrick V-VI; phenol in most patients (laser is safer alternative)
- **Critical:** Pre-treatment with retinoids and hydroquinone for darker skin; strict post-peel sun protection for all

### 3B. MICRONEEDLING

#### Mechanism of Action
Microneedling (collagen induction therapy, CIT) creates controlled micro-injuries to stimulate wound healing:

**Physical mechanism:**
- Fine needles (0.25-3.0 mm length) create thousands of micro-channels in epidermis and/or dermis
- Needle penetration triggers dermal wound healing cascade without significant epidermal destruction
- Channels close within minutes to hours

**Biological response:**
1. **Inflammation (Days 1-3):** Platelet activation → release of PDGF, TGF-α, TGF-β, VEGF → neutrophil and macrophage recruitment
2. **Proliferation (Days 3-14):** Fibroblast activation → Type III collagen, elastin, GAG synthesis; keratinocyte migration
3. **Remodeling (Days 14-365+):** Type III collagen replaced by Type I; collagen maturation and cross-linking; continued dermal remodeling up to 12 months

**Growth factors released:**
- PDGF: Fibroblast chemotaxis and proliferation
- TGF-β3: Scarless wound healing (fetal-like repair)
- VEGF: Angiogenesis
- FGF: Cell proliferation and differentiation
- EGF: Epithelialization

**Enhanced delivery:**
- Micro-channels allow topical products to penetrate deeper ("dermal drug delivery")
- Often combined with growth factors, PRP, or exosomes for enhanced results

#### Indications
- **Acne scars:** Excellent for atrophic, rolling, and boxcar scars (less effective for icepick)
- **Fine lines and wrinkles:** Mild to moderate photoaging
- **Skin texture/smoothness:** Enlarged pores, rough texture
- **Stretch marks:** Both red (striae rubrae) and white (striae albae)
- **Hyperpigmentation:** PIH, melasma (gentle approach)
- **Hair loss:** Androgenetic alopecia, alopecia areata (with growth factors)
- **Surgical scars:** Improvement in texture and appearance

#### Contraindications
- Active infection, open wounds
- Active acne (inflammatory/cystic) — risk of spreading bacteria
- Keloid/scar formation history (relative — debate exists; some studies show safe use)
- Blood clotting disorders, anticoagulant use (increased bruising)
- Active eczema, psoriasis, dermatitis
- Pregnancy (relative — minimal systemic absorption but caution)
- Recent radiation therapy
- Unrealistic expectations

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Generally safe for ALL skin types (I-VI)**
- Lower PIH risk than lasers because epidermis remains largely intact
- **Safer than lasers for darker skin** when treating acne scars and pigment
- Risk of PIH from needle trauma in Fitzpatrick V-VI (lower than ablative lasers)
- Pre- and post-treatment with tyrosinase inhibitors (hydroquinone, azelaic acid, vitamin C) recommended for darker skin

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Initial improvement:** 2-4 weeks (skin texture)
- **Visible collagen remodeling:** 6-8 weeks
- **Optimal results:** 3-6 months after series
- **Sessions:** 3-6 treatments spaced 4-6 weeks apart
- **Duration:** 1-2 years; maintenance every 6-12 months

#### Downtime
- **Redness:** 1-3 days (mild sunburn appearance)
- **Swelling:** 1-2 days
- **Dryness, flaking:** 2-5 days
- **Pinpoint bleeding:** During and immediately after treatment
- **Activity:** Avoid makeup 24 hours, sun exposure 1 week, strenuous exercise 24 hours
- **Compared to laser:** Significantly less downtime than ablative; comparable to non-ablative fractional

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Microneedling alone:** $200-500 per session
- **Microneedling + PRP:** $500-1,500 per session
- **Series (3-6 sessions):** $600-3,000
- **RF microneedling:** $800-1,500 per session (see Section 2C)

#### Evidence Level: **A** (acne scars), **B** (photoaging), **B** (striae), **C** (hair loss)

#### Risks/Side Effects
**Common:**
- Pain/discomfort during treatment (numbing cream used)
- Erythema, edema
- Dryness, flaking
- Pinpoint bleeding
- Temporary sensitivity

**Uncommon:**
- Bruising
- PIH (darker skin)
- Infection (if improper technique or aftercare)
- Milia

**Rare:**
- Scarring (if too aggressive or poor technique)
- Granuloma formation (if combined with inappropriate topicals)
- Allergic reaction to topical products used during/after

#### Comparison: Microneedling vs. RF Microneedling

| Factor | Traditional Microneedling | RF Microneedling (Morpheus8) |
|--------|---------------------------|------------------------------|

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient wants improved texture, acne scars, mild photoaging; has darker skin (safer than lasers); wants minimal downtime

- **Avoid:** Active inflammatory acne (treat acne first); keloid history (caution); patients seeking immediate dramatic results

### 3C. MICRODERMABRASION

#### Mechanism of Action
Microdermabrasion is a mechanical exfoliation technique:

**Methods:**
1. **Crystal microdermabrasion:** Aluminum oxide (corundum) or sodium bicarbonate crystals sprayed onto skin at high velocity, then vacuumed away along with abraded skin cells
2. **Diamond-tip microdermabrasion:** Diamond-encrusted tip abrades skin while vacuum suction removes debris; no loose crystals
3. **Hydradermabrasion (SilkPeel®):** Diamond tip with simultaneous serum infusion

**Depth:** Stratum corneum only (superficial epidermis, ~10-15 μm)

**Biological effects:**
- Immediate removal of dead skin cells
- Mild stimulation of epidermal turnover
- Enhanced topical product penetration
- Lymphatic drainage from vacuum suction
- Minimal collagen stimulation (superficial only)

#### Indications
- **Mild photoaging:** Early fine lines, dull skin
- **Uneven texture:** Roughness, enlarged pores (minimal effect)
- **Hyperkeratosis:** Thick, rough skin
- **Mild acne/comedones:** Extraction aid
- **Melasma:** As adjunct to lighten (limited standalone efficacy)
- **Preparation for other procedures:** Skin priming
- **Maintenance:** Between more aggressive treatments

#### Contraindications
- Active infection, open wounds
- Active inflammatory acne (can spread bacteria)
- Eczema, psoriasis, dermatitis
- Rosacea (can worsen — relative contraindication)
- Fragile capillaries (relative)
- Recent chemical peel or laser (wait until healed)
- Isotretinoin use (increased fragility)
- Pregnancy (relative)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Safe for ALL skin types**
- Very superficial → minimal PIH risk
- However, aggressive treatment can cause petechiae and PIH in darker skin
- Gentle approach recommended for Fitzpatrick IV-VI

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Immediate:** Smoother, brighter skin
- **Duration:** Short-term (days to 1 week); requires maintenance
- **Sessions:** Weekly to monthly for cumulative effect

#### Downtime
- **None**
- Mild redness: 30 minutes to 2 hours
- Sensitivity: Same day
- Can wear makeup immediately

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Single session:** $75-200
- **Package (6-10):** $400-1,500
- **Often included in facials:** $100-300 combined

#### Evidence Level: **C**
Limited high-quality evidence; primarily anecdotal and small studies. Considered maintenance rather than transformative treatment.

#### Risks/Side Effects
- Mild irritation, redness
- Dryness
- Petechiae (small broken capillaries from vacuum)
- PIH (rare, aggressive treatment)
- Crystal inhalation (with older crystal systems)
- Infection (rare)

#### Comparison: Microdermabrasion vs. Chemical Peel

| Factor | Microdermabrasion | Superficial Chemical Peel |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| Mechanism | Mechanical abrasion | Chemical exfoliation |
| Depth | Stratum corneum | Stratum corneum to epidermis |
| Downtime | None | 1-2 days |
| Results | Immediate but brief | More sustained |
| Collagen stimulation | Minimal | Mild |
| Cost | Lower | Slightly higher |
| Skin types | All | Most (caution darker skin) |
| Evidence | Limited | Moderate |

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Chemical peels:** Enhanced exfoliation (superficial only)
- **+ IPL:** Can be done same day (microderm first, then IPL)
- **+ LED:** Same day for enhanced results
- **+ Facials:** Standard combination
- **+ Topical skincare:** Enhanced product penetration post-treatment

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient wants gentle exfoliation, immediate brightness, maintenance between other treatments
- **Not a standalone solution for:** Significant aging, deep wrinkles, acne scars, significant pigment

### 4A. PRP / PRF (Platelet-Rich Plasma / Platelet-Rich Fibrin)

#### Mechanism of Action

**PRP (Platelet-Rich Plasma):**
1. Blood draw (typically 10-60 mL)
2. Centrifugation separates blood components:
   - Bottom: Red blood cells
   - Middle: Buffy coat (platelets and white blood cells) — PRP layer
   - Top: Platelet-poor plasma (PPP)
3. PRP contains 3-5x baseline platelet concentration
4. Activated with calcium chloride or thrombin to release growth factors
5. Injected or applied topically

**PRF (Platelet-Rich Fibrin) — next generation:**
- Single centrifugation without anticoagulants
- Forms fibrin matrix that traps platelets and leukocytes
- Slower, sustained growth factor release (hours to days vs. minutes for PRP)
- No additives needed
- Contains mesenchymal stem cells, leukocytes, and higher concentration of growth factors

**Growth factors released:**
- **PDGF** (Platelet-Derived Growth Factor): Cell proliferation, angiogenesis
- **TGF-β** (Transforming Growth Factor-beta): Collagen synthesis, cell differentiation
- **VEGF** (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor): Angiogenesis, tissue perfusion
- **IGF** (Insulin-like Growth Factor): Cell growth and differentiation
- **EGF** (Epidermal Growth Factor): Epithelial cell proliferation
- **FGF** (Fibroblast Growth Factor): Fibroblast activation

**Applications:**
- **Facial rejuvenation ("Vampire Facial"):** Microneedling or injection with PRP/PRF
- **Hair restoration:** Scalp injections for androgenetic alopecia
- **Acne scars:** Combined with microneedling or subcision
- **Wound healing:** Chronic wounds, surgical recovery
- **Orthopedic:** Joint and tendon healing (outside cosmetic scope)

#### Indications
- Facial rejuvenation (fine lines, texture, tone)
- Hair loss (androgenetic alopecia, alopecia areata)
- Acne scar improvement (as adjunct)
- Under-eye hollows/dark circles (tear trough PRP)
- Décolletage rejuvenation
- Hand rejuvenation
- Accelerated healing post-procedure

#### Contraindications
- Blood clotting disorders, anticoagulant therapy
- Platelet dysfunction syndromes
- Thrombocytopenia (low platelet count)
- Active infection
- Cancer (especially hematologic) — relative
- Pregnancy, breastfeeding
- Recent fever or illness
- Severe anemia
- Autoimmune disease (relative)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Safe for ALL skin types**
- Autologous (patient's own blood) → no allergic reaction
- No thermal injury → no PIH risk
- Excellent for skin of color

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Facial rejuvenation:** Improvement in 2-4 weeks; optimal at 3 months
- **Hair:** 3-6 months to see new growth; maintenance required
- **Sessions:** 3-4 treatments spaced 4-6 weeks; maintenance every 6-12 months
- **Duration:** 1-2 years (variable)

#### Downtime
- **Minimal:** Redness, swelling at injection sites (1-3 days)
- **With microneedling:** Same downtime as microneedling (1-3 days)
- No activity restrictions

#### Cost Range (US)
- **PRP facial (with microneedling):** $500-1,500
- **PRP hair restoration:** $1,500-3,500 per series
- **PRP series (3-4):** $2,000-5,000
- **PRF:** Slightly higher than PRP; $600-1,800 per session

#### Evidence Level: **B** (hair loss), **C** (facial rejuvenation), **C** (acne scars)
Growing evidence but many studies are small, lack controls, or have methodological limitations.

#### Risks/Side Effects
- Pain at injection sites
- Bruising, swelling
- Infection (rare with proper technique)
- No risk of allergic reaction (autologous)
- Minimal side effect profile overall

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Microneedling:** Standard combination ("vampire facial")
- **+ Laser:** Applied post-laser to enhance healing
- **+ Hair transplant:** PRP to grafts and recipient sites
- **+ Fillers:** PRP may enhance filler longevity (emerging evidence)

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient wants natural, autologous approach; has hair loss; wants to enhance other procedures
- **Best evidence for:** Hair restoration (moderate evidence)
- **Caution:** Facial rejuvenation evidence is promising but not robust; set realistic expectations
- **Choose PRF over PRP:** Sustained growth factor release, no additives
- **Refer:** Complex hair loss cases requiring dermatology evaluation; patients expecting dramatic results

### 4B. EXOSOMES

#### Mechanism of Action
Exosomes are extracellular vesicles (30-150 nm) secreted by cells that act as intercellular messengers:

**Biology:**
- Derived from mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs), fibroblasts, platelets, or other cell types
- Contain proteins, lipids, mRNA, microRNA, and growth factors
- Fuse with recipient cells → deliver cargo → modulate cellular behavior
- **Do not contain DNA** → cannot replicate or form tumors

**Proposed mechanisms in skincare:**
- Stimulate fibroblast proliferation and collagen synthesis
- Modulate inflammation (anti-inflammatory cytokines)
- Promote angiogenesis
- Enhance wound healing
- Regulate melanogenesis (potential for pigment disorders)
- Transfer "youth signals" from young cells to aging cells

**Sources in cosmetic products:**
- Plant-derived exosomes (aloe, rose, etc.) — limited evidence
- Human-derived: Stem cell cultures, platelets
- Animal-derived: Some products use non-human sources

#### Indications (Emerging)
- Anti-aging/facial rejuvenation
- Hair restoration
- Wound healing enhancement
- Scar improvement
- Pigmentation disorders (very early research)
- Post-procedure recovery

#### Contraindications
- Not well-established (emerging technology)
- Caution with immunocompromised patients
- Allergies to source materials
- Pregnancy, breastfeeding (insufficient safety data)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- Theoretical safety for all skin types
- No thermal mechanism → no PIH risk
- Long-term safety data lacking across all skin types

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Unknown/variable:** Technology too new for established timelines
- Manufacturers claim: Improvement in 4-8 weeks
- Duration: Unclear

#### Downtime
- **Minimal to none** (when applied topically)
- When combined with microneedling: Same downtime as microneedling

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Topical exosome serums:** $200-600
- **Professional exosome treatments:** $500-2,000 per session
- **Highly variable:** Market not standardized

#### Evidence Level: **D** (cosmetic applications)
- Robust scientific evidence for exosomes in research settings
- **Limited clinical trial evidence** for cosmetic outcomes
- Most studies are in vitro, animal models, or small uncontrolled human studies
- Regulatory status unclear in many jurisdictions

#### Risks/Side Effects
- **Unknown long-term safety:** Limited longitudinal data
- **Regulatory concerns:** FDA has not approved exosome products for cosmetic use; some products may be marketed illegally as drugs
- **Source variability:** Product quality and exosome concentration vary widely
- **Contamination risk:** If not properly manufactured
- **Allergic reactions:** Possible depending on source
- **Ethical concerns:** Human-derived exosomes raise sourcing questions

#### Regulatory Status
- **FDA:** Has not approved any exosome products for cosmetic use
- **Warning:** FDA has issued warnings to companies making unsubstantiated claims
- **Classification:** May be regulated as drugs, not cosmetics, if therapeutic claims made
- **Caution:** Patients should be wary of products making dramatic claims

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Status: WATCH AND WAIT**
- **Not currently recommending:** Insufficient clinical evidence for cosmetic outcomes
- **Interesting potential:** Scientific rationale is sound; research advancing rapidly
- **If patients ask:** Explain that while science is promising, clinical evidence for cosmetic results is currently limited
- **Red flags:** Products making cure-all claims, unregulated sources, exorbitant pricing without evidence
- **Future:** May become valuable adjunct; monitor literature closely

### 4C. CRYOTHERAPY

#### Mechanism of Action

**Local Cryotherapy (targeted skin treatment):**
- Application of extreme cold (-100°C to -196°C) via liquid nitrogen, nitrous oxide, or argon gas
- Causes intracellular ice crystal formation → cell membrane rupture → cell death
- Vascular damage → thrombosis → ischemic necrosis
- Inflammation → wound healing response

**Mechanisms by application:**
- **Cryosurgery:** Destroys abnormal tissue (lesions, warts, precancers)
- **Cryofacials:** Cold nitrogen vapor applied to face; vasoconstriction followed by vasodilation → improved circulation, reduced inflammation, temporary pore tightening
- **Cryotherapy chambers (whole-body):** Extreme cold exposure → systemic anti-inflammatory effects, endorphin release, metabolic changes

**Whole-body cryotherapy (WBC):**
- 2-4 minutes in chamber at -110°C to -140°C
- Triggers "fight or flight" response → norepinephrine release, anti-inflammatory cytokines
- Claims for skin: Reduced inflammation, improved eczema/psoriasis, collagen stimulation (limited evidence)

#### Indications

**Medical/Dermatological:**
- Actinic keratoses
- Warts, molluscum contagiosum
- Seborrheic keratoses
- Skin tags
- Lentigines (cryotherapy can lighten)
- Basal cell carcinoma (superficial types)
- Keloids (intralesional cryotherapy)

**Cosmetic:**
- **Cryofacials:** Temporary pore tightening, reduced puffiness, improved circulation
- **WBC:** Claims for anti-aging, collagen stimulation, skin conditions

#### Contraindications
- Cold urticaria or cold intolerance
- Raynaud's phenomenon
- Cryoglobulinemia
- Paroxysmal cold hemoglobinuria
- Open wounds (local)
- Pregnancy (WBC — insufficient safety data)
- Severe cardiovascular disease (WBC)
- Uncontrolled hypertension (WBC)
- Nerve disorders (risk of neuropathy)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Local cryotherapy:** Safe for all skin types
- **Risk:** Post-inflammatory pigment changes (hypo- or hyperpigmentation) in darker skin after aggressive cryotherapy for lesions
- **Cryofacials:** Generally safe for all skin types
- **WBC:** No specific skin type risk identified

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Lesion destruction:** 1-3 weeks for crusting and healing
- **Cryofacial:** Immediate temporary effects (hours)
- **WBC:** Claims of improved skin are anecdotal; no established timeline

#### Downtime
- **Local cryosurgery:** Blistering, crusting 1-2 weeks; possible scarring
- **Cryofacial:** None
- **WBC:** None

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Cryosurgery (medical):** $100-500 per session (often covered by insurance for medical indications)
- **Cryofacial:** $50-150 per session
- **WBC:** $50-100 per session; packages available

#### Evidence Level: **A** (medical indications), **D** (cosmetic skin claims)

#### Risks/Side Effects
- Pain, blistering, edema
- Infection
- Scarring (rare with proper technique)
- Hypopigmentation (common, especially darker skin)
- Hyperpigmentation (less common)
- Nerve damage (if over nerves)
- Hair loss (if over hair-bearing areas)

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Medical indications (lesions, AKs):** Well-established; refer to dermatologist
- **Cosmetic cryotherapy (cryofacials, WBC for skin):** Evidence insufficient to recommend for anti-aging or collagen stimulation
- **Temporary effects only:** Cryofacials provide transient vasoconstriction → temporary tightening; no lasting collagen benefit demonstrated
- **WBC for skin:** Not recommended based on current evidence

### 4E. PHOTODYNAMIC THERAPY (PDT)

#### Mechanism of Action
PDT uses a photosensitizing agent activated by light to destroy target cells:

**Three components:**
1. **Photosensitizer:** Applied topically (aminolevulinic acid — ALA, or methyl aminolevulinate — MAL)
2. **Light activation:** Blue light (417 nm), red light (630 nm), IPL, or PDL
3. **Oxygen:** Required for photodynamic reaction

**Mechanism:**
- Photosensitizer accumulates in target cells (rapidly dividing cells, sebaceous glands, cancer cells)
- Light activation → photosensitizer excited to triplet state
- Energy transfer to oxygen → reactive oxygen species (ROS)
- ROS → cell membrane damage, mitochondrial injury, apoptosis
- Inflammation → immune response → destruction of abnormal cells

#### Indications
- **Actinic keratoses (AKs):** FDA-approved; field treatment for multiple AKs
- **Acne:** Reduces sebaceous gland activity and *P. acnes*
- **Photorejuvenation:** Improves texture, tone, solar lentigines
- **Superficial skin cancers:** Basal cell carcinoma (superficial), Bowen's disease
- **Hidradenitis suppurativa:** Emerging use
- **Prevention:** Reduces development of new AKs in treated field

#### Contraindications
- Porphyria, lupus, other photosensitivity disorders
- Photosensitizing medications
- Pregnancy, breastfeeding
- Known allergy to photosensitizer components
- Active infection
- Cold sore history (HSV prophylaxis needed)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Safe for most skin types**
- **Challenge in darker skin:** Photosensitizer may cause temporary hyperpigmentation; light activation must be carefully calibrated to avoid thermal injury to melanin-rich epidermis
- **IPL/PDL activation:** Higher risk in darker skin; red light or blue light generally safer
- **Pre-treatment:** Hydroquinone may reduce PIH risk

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **AKs:** 2-4 weeks for crusting and healing; clearance rate 70-90%
- **Acne:** Improvement in 2-4 weeks; multiple sessions needed
- **Photorejuvenation:** Gradual over 4-8 weeks
- **Sessions:** 1-3 for AKs; 2-4 for acne; 1-2 for photorejuvenation

#### Downtime
- **Photosensitivity:** 48 hours post-treatment (strict sun avoidance)
- **Redness, swelling:** 2-7 days
- **Crusting, peeling:** 3-14 days (ALA PDT more than MAL)
- **Discomfort:** Burning/stinging during and 24-48 hours post

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Single session:** $500-1,500
- **Actinic keratoses:** Often covered by insurance
- **Cosmetic PDT:** $800-2,000 per session

#### Evidence Level: **A** (actinic keratoses), **B** (acne), **B** (photorejuvenation)

#### Risks/Side Effects
- Pain/burning during and after treatment
- Erythema, edema, crusting
- PIH (darker skin)
- Blistering, ulceration (rare)
- Infection
- Scarring (rare)
- **Severe photosensitivity:** Must avoid sun for 48 hours (can cause severe burns)

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Cryotherapy:** For thick AKs before PDT
- **+ 5-FU or imiquimod:** Alternating or sequential for field cancerization
- **+ Laser/IPL:** IPL can be activating light source
- **+ Retinoids:** Maintenance post-PDT

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Strongly recommend for:** Actinic keratoses (field treatment superior to spot treatment)
- **Consider for:** Moderate to severe acne refractory to topicals; photodamage with AKs
- **Prevention value:** Treating field of cancerization reduces future skin cancer risk
- **Refer:** All patients with AKs should see dermatologist for PDT evaluation
- **Cosmetic PDT:** Less compelling than other photorejuvenation options unless AKs also present

### 4F. THREAD LIFTS

#### Mechanism of Action
Thread lifts use dissolvable or permanent sutures to mechanically lift and support sagging tissue:

**Thread types:**

**PDO (Polydioxanone) threads:**
- Synthetic absorbable polymer (same as surgical sutures)
- Dissolves in 6-9 months via hydrolysis
- Stimulates collagen around thread as foreign body response
- Types: Mono (smooth), screw/tornado, cog/barbed

**PLLA (Poly-L-Lactic Acid) threads:**
- Longer-lasting than PDO (12-18 months)
- Biostimulatory effect (same as Sculptra)
- Stimulates collagen production

**PCL (Polycaprolactone) threads:**
- Longest-lasting absorbable threads (24+ months)
- Similar collagen stimulation

**Permanent threads:**
- Polypropylene (Prolene) or other non-absorbable materials
- Long-term support but higher complication risk
- Rarely used today due to safety concerns

**Mechanism of action:**
1. **Mechanical lift:** Barbed/cogged threads anchor to tissue and provide immediate lifting vector
2. **Collagen stimulation:** Foreign body response → fibroblast activation → Type I and III collagen
3. **Tissue adhesion:** Scar tissue formation around threads maintains lift after absorption
4. **Vascularization:** Improved blood supply to treated areas

#### Indications
- **Mild to moderate facial laxity:** Jowls, nasolabial folds, marionette lines
- **Brow ptosis:** Lateral brow lift
- **Neck laxity:** Submental area
- **Mid-face lift:** Cheek elevation
- **Body:** Arms, abdomen, knees (off-label, limited evidence)

#### Contraindications
- Active infection, open wounds
- Autoimmune connective tissue disease
- Keloid/scar formation history
- Blood clotting disorders
- Unrealistic expectations (not a facelift substitute)
- Very thin skin (thread visibility)
- Severe laxity (surgical candidate)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **Safe for ALL skin types**
- No thermal injury → no PIH risk
- Entry points may cause temporary PIH in darker skin
- Excellent option for skin of color seeking lifting

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Immediate:** 30-50% of final lift visible (mechanical)
- **Optimal:** 3-6 months (as collagen forms)
- **Duration:** 12-24 months depending on thread type
- **Maintenance:** Annual or as needed

#### Downtime
- **Minimal:** 2-7 days
- **Swelling, bruising:** 3-7 days
- **Tenderness:** 1-2 weeks
- **Dimpling, bunching:** May occur temporarily (1-2 weeks)
- **Avoid:** Excessive facial movement, massage, sleeping on face for 1-2 weeks

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Lower face lift:** $1,500-4,500
- **Full face:** $3,000-6,000
- **Brow lift:** $1,000-2,500
- **Neck:** $1,500-3,500

#### Evidence Level: **C**
Limited high-quality evidence; many studies are small, retrospective, or have short follow-up. Safety data improving but long-term outcomes not well-established.

#### Risks/Side Effects

**Common:**
- Bruising, swelling
- Tenderness, tightness
- Temporary dimpling, asymmetry
- Thread visibility under thin skin

**Uncommon:**
- Infection
- Thread migration or extrusion
- Asymmetry
- Persistent dimpling
- Nerve irritation (temporary)

**Rare but serious:**
- **Vascular occlusion:** Thread placed into vessel → embolization → tissue necrosis, blindness (rare but catastrophic, similar to filler vascular events)
- **Infection requiring removal**
- **Chronic foreign body reaction**
- **Scarring**
- **Facial nerve injury**

#### Comparison: Thread Lift vs. Surgical Facelift

| Factor | Thread Lift | Surgical Facelift |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Invasiveness | Minimally invasive | Surgery |
| Anesthesia | Local | General or deep sedation |
| Duration | 30-60 minutes | 3-6 hours |
| Downtime | 2-7 days | 2-4 weeks |
| Results | Subtle, natural | Dramatic |
| Longevity | 12-24 months | 7-15 years |
| Cost | $1,500-6,000 | $8,000-20,000 |
| Scarring | Minimal entry points | Incision scars |
| Risk profile | Lower | Higher (surgical risks) |

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Fillers:** Threads for lift, fillers for volume
- **+ Botulinum toxin:** Threads for lift, toxin for dynamic lines
- **+ Ultrasound/RF:** Energy for collagen, threads for mechanical support
- **+ PRP:** May enhance collagen response around threads

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient has mild to moderate laxity, wants subtle natural lift without surgery, understands temporary nature
- **Best for:** Early jowling (30s-50s), brow ptosis, patients not ready for surgery
- **Not for:** Severe laxity, patients expecting facelift results, very thin skin
- **Critical:** Provider experience matters enormously; vascular occlusion risk requires anatomical knowledge
- **Refer:** Patients with significant laxity needing surgical evaluation; patients with unrealistic expectations

## 5. SEMI-PERMANENT MAKEUP (SPMU)

---

### Overview
Semi-permanent makeup (SPMU), also called cosmetic tattooing or micropigmentation, involves implanting pigment into the dermis to enhance facial features. Unlike traditional tattoos (deeper dermis, different pigments), SPMU is more superficial and uses pigments designed to fade over time.

**Key differences from body tattoos:**
- Depth: SPMU in upper dermis (more superficial)
- Pigments: Iron oxide-based (SPMU) vs. carbon-based (traditional)
- Longevity: 1-3 years (SPMU) vs. permanent (traditional)
- Fading: SPMU designed to fade naturally; traditional tattoos may blur but remain

---

### 5A. MICROBLADING

#### Mechanism of Action
Microblading is a manual technique for eyebrow enhancement:

**Technique:**
- Handheld tool with 10-16 fine needles arranged in blade formation
- Manual strokes create fine, hair-like incisions in epidermis/upper dermis
- Pigment implanted into incisions
- Heals with "hair stroke" appearance

**Depth:** 0.08-0.15 mm (epidermis to superficial dermis)

**Pigments:** Iron oxide-based; organic or inorganic; designed to fade naturally

#### Indications
- Sparse, thin, or over-plucked eyebrows
- Asymmetrical brows
- Alopecia or hair loss (medical or cosmetic)
- Desire for natural, hair-stroke brow appearance
- Active individuals wanting "wake up and go" brows

#### Contraindications
- **Pregnancy, breastfeeding**
- Active skin infection, eczema, psoriasis in brow area
- Keloid/scar formation history
- Blood clotting disorders, anticoagulant use
- Diabetes (impaired healing — relative)
- Recent Botox in brow area (wait 2 weeks)
- Recent chemical peel or laser (wait until healed)
- **Accutane/isotretinoin use:** Wait 6-12 months
- **Chemotherapy/radiation:** Wait until treatment complete and healed

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **All skin types can undergo microblading**
- **Challenges in darker skin:**
  - Pigment color selection critical for natural appearance
  - Ashy/gray undertones can develop as pigment fades
  - PIH from procedure trauma (rare but possible)
- **Oily skin:** Pigment may not retain well; may blur (see below)

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Immediately after:** Dark, bold appearance (will fade 30-50%)
- **Healing:** 7-14 days for surface healing; 4-6 weeks for complete dermal healing
- **True color:** Visible at 4-6 weeks
- **Touch-up:** Required at 4-8 weeks (included in initial price)
- **Longevity:** 12-18 months (varies by skin type, lifestyle, aftercare)

#### Downtime
- **Day 1-2:** Dark, swollen brows
- **Day 3-7:** Scabbing, flaking (CRITICAL: Do not pick)
- **Day 7-14:** "Ghosting" phase — may appear too light (normal)
- **Weeks 4-6:** True color emerges
- **Restrictions:** No makeup on brows for 10-14 days; no swimming, saunas, excessive sweating for 7-10 days

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Initial session:** $400-800
- **Touch-up (4-8 weeks):** Usually included
- **Annual touch-up:** $200-400

#### Evidence Level: **C** (cosmetic procedure; limited clinical trials)

#### Risks/Side Effects
**Common:**
- Swelling, redness
- Tenderness
- Scabbing, flaking
- Itching during healing

**Uncommon:**
- Infection (bacterial, viral — HSV reactivation)
- Allergic reaction to pigment
- Asymmetry
- Color change (oxidation, fading to undesirable tone)

**Serious:**
- **Scarring:** From improper depth or picking scabs
- **Pigment migration:** Blurring of strokes (especially oily skin)
- **Granulomatous reaction:** To pigment
- **Koebner phenomenon:** Psoriasis or vitiligo triggered in brow area

**SKINgenius specific concerns:**
- **Retinoid use:** Must discontinue tretinoin/retinol 2-4 weeks pre-procedure and 4-6 weeks post (causes pigment migration and poor retention)
- **Oily skin:** Microblading may not last; nano-blading or powder brows better options

#### Combination Therapy
- **+ Botulinum toxin:** Wait 2 weeks before or after
- **+ Brow lamination:** Wait until fully healed (6+ weeks)
- **+ Chemical peels/laser:** Avoid brow area; if treating surrounding skin, protect brows

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient wants natural, hair-stroke brows; has normal to dry skin; willing to commit to touch-ups
- **Best for:** Fitzpatrick I-III with normal/dry skin; sparse brows needing definition
- **Avoid or caution:**
  - Oily skin (pigment retention poor, strokes blur)
  - Fitzpatrick V-VI (may appear ashy; consider nano-blading or powder brows)
  - Keloid history
  - Active skin conditions
- **Critical:** Choose experienced artist; review healed (not fresh) portfolio photos

---

### 5B. NANO-BLADING

#### Mechanism of Action
Nano-blading is a digital/machine method of creating hair-stroke brows:

**Technique:**
- Digital PMU machine with single nano needle (0.18-0.25 mm)
- Machine creates precise punctures rather than blade incisions
- Less traumatic than manual blading
- More controlled pigment implantation

**Advantages over microblading:**
- Finer, more precise strokes
- Less trauma to skin
- Better for oily and sensitive skin
- Longer-lasting results
- Less scabbing
- Can create more detailed, crisp strokes

#### Indications
- Same as microblading
- Particularly good for: Oily skin, sensitive skin, mature skin, those wanting crisper results

#### Contraindications
Same as microblading (Section 5A)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **All skin types**
- Better option than microblading for Fitzpatrick IV-VI (less trauma → less risk of pigment ashing or PIH)
- Still requires skilled color matching for darker skin

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Similar to microblading:** 12-24 months (slightly longer lasting than microblading)
- **Healing:** Slightly faster than microblading (less trauma)
- **Touch-up:** At 4-8 weeks

#### Downtime
- **Slightly less than microblading**
- Less scabbing due to puncture vs. incision technique
- Same aftercare restrictions

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Initial:** $500-900 (slightly higher than microblading)
- **Touch-ups:** $250-450

#### Evidence Level: **C**

#### Risks/Side Effects
- Similar to microblading but lower incidence due to less trauma
- Less risk of scarring
- Less pigment migration in oily skin

#### Comparison: Nano-blading vs. Microblading

| Factor | Microblading | Nano-blading |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Tool | Manual blade | Digital machine |
| Needle | Multiple needles in blade | Single nano needle |
| Trauma | More (incision) | Less (puncture) |
| Precision | Good | Excellent |
| Skin types | Normal-dry best | All including oily |
| Longevity | 12-18 months | 18-24 months |
| Healing | 7-14 days | 5-10 days |
| Scabbing | More | Less |
| Cost | $400-800 | $500-900 |

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Prefer over microblading for:** Oily skin, sensitive skin, mature skin, Fitzpatrick IV-VI
- **Results:** Crisper, longer-lasting hair strokes
- **If choosing between the two:** Nano-blading is generally superior technique with fewer risks
- **Still requires:** Skilled artist, proper aftercare, touch-up

### 5C. POWDER / OMBRE BROWS

#### Mechanism of Action
Powder/ombre brows create a shaded, makeup-like brow appearance:

**Technique:**
- Digital PMU machine implants pigment in stippling/dotting motion
- Creates gradient effect (lighter at inner brow, darker at tail) — "ombre"
- Or uniform powder appearance throughout
- Pigment implanted more superficially and uniformly than hair strokes

**Results:**
- Resembles brow powder or pomade makeup
- Soft, shaded appearance rather than individual hairs
- Can be combined with hair strokes ("combo brows") for natural + defined look

#### Indications
- **Oily skin:** Pigment retention better than microblading
- **Dense natural brows wanting enhancement:** Fills gaps, adds definition
- **Makeup lovers:** Want "done" brow look
- **Mature skin:** Less trauma than microblading
- **Previous microblading that blurred:** Can be covered/corrected
- **Alopecia:** Full brow reconstruction

#### Contraindications
Same as microblading (Section 5A)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **All skin types**
- **Better option for Fitzpatrick IV-VI than microblading**
- Shading technique less likely to cause ashy appearance
- Color selection still critical

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Longevity:** 18-36 months (longer than microblading)
- **Healing:** 7-14 days
- **Touch-up:** 4-8 weeks
- **Annual refresh:** Every 12-24 months

#### Downtime
- Similar to microblading but potentially less scabbing
- Same aftercare requirements

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Initial:** $400-800
- **Touch-ups:** $200-400

#### Evidence Level: **C**

#### Risks/Side Effects
- Similar to microblading but lower risk of scarring
- Color may heal unevenly (patchy)
- Can look too "harsh" if artist inexperienced

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Best option for:** Oily skin, Fitzpatrick IV-VI, patients wanting defined "makeup" look
- **Combo brows:** Hair strokes at inner brow + powder/ombre toward tail — most versatile technique
- **Most universally suitable SPMU technique:** Works across skin types and preferences

### 5D. EYELINER TATTOO

#### Mechanism of Action
Permanent pigment implanted along lash line to enhance eye definition:

**Techniques:**
1. **Lash line enhancement:** Pigment between lashes only (most natural)
2. **Thin eyeliner:** Small line above lashes
3. **Winged eyeliner:** Extended line with wing (more dramatic)
4. **Lower lash line:** Can be done but higher risk

**Depth:** 0.3-0.5 mm (deeper than brows due to thinner eyelid skin structure)

#### Indications
- Sparse or light lashes
- Desire for "wake up and go" eye definition
- Active lifestyle (swimming, sports)
- Allergies to conventional eye makeup
- Vision impairment making makeup application difficult
- Alopecia affecting lashes

#### ContraindicATIONS
- **Pregnancy, breastfeeding**
- Active eye infection, conjunctivitis, blepharitis
- Dry eye syndrome (may worsen)
- Glaucoma (relative — pressure on eye)
- Recent eye surgery (LASIK, cataract — wait 6+ months)
- **Keloid/scar formation history**
- Blood clotting disorders
- Diabetes (healing concerns)
- **Trigeminal neuralgia or other facial pain syndromes**
- **History of corneal abrasion or eye injury**
- **Use of eyelash growth serums (Latisse, etc.):** Discontinue 2+ weeks prior

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **All skin types**
- **Special considerations for darker skin:**
  - Pigment may appear more intense on darker skin
  - Blue-black pigments can turn blue-gray as they fade
  - Warm brown pigments often better for Fitzpatrick IV-VI
- **Thin eyelid skin:** All skin types have thin eyelid skin; technique must be precise

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Immediately after:** Dark, swollen
- **Healing:** 5-10 days (surface); 4-6 weeks (true color)
- **Touch-up:** 4-8 weeks
- **Longevity:** 1-3 years (varies significantly)
- **Fading:** Faster on oilier skin, with sun exposure, with retinoid use

#### Downtime
- **Swelling:** 1-3 days (often significant day 1-2)
- **Redness, tenderness:** 3-7 days
- **Scabbing/flaking:** 5-10 days
- **Restrictions:** No eye makeup for 7-10 days; no contact lenses for 3-5 days; no swimming/saunas for 10-14 days
- **Important:** Eyes may be swollen shut temporarily (normal, resolves in 24-48 hours)

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Upper lash line:** $300-600
- **Upper + lower:** $500-900
- **With wing/shadow:** $600-1,200
- **Touch-up:** $150-400

#### Evidence Level: **C**

#### Risks/Side Effects
**Common:**
- Significant swelling (especially day 1-2)
- Tenderness
- Scabbing, flaking
- Temporary blurred vision from swelling
- Watery eyes

**Uncommon:**
- Infection (bacterial, viral — HSV reactivation)
- Allergic reaction to pigment
- Migration of pigment (blurred line)
- Uneven application
- Color change (fading to blue/gray with black pigments)

**Serious:**
- **Corneal abrasion or damage:** From improper technique
- **Vision impairment:** Temporary from swelling; rare permanent
- **MRI concerns:** Iron oxide pigments can cause burning sensation during MRI (rare, but patients should inform MRI technicians)
- **Granulomatous reaction**
- **Scarring**
- **Difficulty with future eye surgeries:** Pigment may interfere

#### MRI Concerns with Iron Oxide Pigments
- **Issue:** Ferromagnetic properties of iron oxide can interact with MRI magnetic field
- **Risk:** Burning sensation, tissue distortion
- **Mitigation:** Inform MRI technologist of permanent makeup; some facilities may refuse MRI or require specific protocols
- **Alternative:** Some artists now use organic non-iron oxide pigments specifically for eyeliner

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient has realistic expectations, understands risks, wants subtle enhancement
- **Best for:** Lash line enhancement (most natural and lowest risk)
- **Caution with:** Winged eyeliner (fashion changes; difficult to remove); lower lash line (higher risk)
- **Critical:** Choose artist with specific eyeliner experience (not just brow artist); ophthalmic safety knowledge essential
- **Strong recommendation:** Lash line enhancement only; avoid dramatic permanent styles

### 5E. LIP BLUSHING

#### Mechanism of Action
Semi-permanent pigment implanted into lips to enhance color and definition:

**Techniques:**
1. **Lip liner:** Pigment along vermilion border only
2. **Lip blush:** Pigment throughout lip body for overall color
3. **Full lip color:** More opaque, lipstick-like appearance
4. **Ombré lips:** Lighter center, darker edges

**Depth:** 0.3-0.8 mm (varies by lip area)

#### Indications
- Pale or uneven lip color
- Loss of vermilion border definition (aging)
- Desire for "just-bitten" natural lip tint
- Active lifestyle wanting low-maintenance lip color
- Allergies to conventional lip products
- Scarring or asymmetry correction

#### Contraindications
- **Pregnancy, breastfeeding**
- **Active cold sores (HSV-1):** ABSOLUTE — will trigger severe outbreak
- **History of cold sores:** REQUIRES antiviral prophylaxis
- Active infection, eczema, psoriasis on lips
- **Diabetes:** Higher infection risk
- Blood clotting disorders
- **Recent lip fillers:** Wait 4+ weeks
- **Accutane/isotretinoin:** Wait 6-12 months
- **Autoimmune conditions:** Relative (healing concerns)

#### CRITICAL: HSV-1 Prophylaxis
**For ALL patients with ANY history of cold sores:**
- **Valacyclovir 500 mg BID** (or acyclovir 400 mg TID) starting **2 days before procedure**
- Continue for **3-5 days post-procedure**
- **Without prophylaxis:** 80-90% chance of severe herpetic outbreak → scarring, pigment loss, significant complications
- **Even without known history:** Consider prophylaxis (many carriers are asymptomatic)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **All skin types can undergo lip blushing**
- **CRITICAL considerations for melanin-rich lips (Fitzpatrick IV-VI):**
  - **Hyperpigmentation risk:** Lip blushing on dark lips can cause further hyperpigmentation or uneven color
  - **Color selection:** Must neutralize natural dark tones first (color theory: use orange/peach corrector before target color)
  - **Results:** May not achieve desired "pink" color on very dark lips; realistic expectations essential
  - **Keloid risk:** Higher in darker skin (though rare on lips)
  - **Artist skill:** Must have experience with skin of color

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Immediately after:** Dark, swollen ("duck lips" appearance)
- **Day 3-7:** Heavy scabbing, peeling
- **Week 2:** Color appears very light ("ghosting" — normal)
- **Weeks 4-6:** True color emerges
- **Touch-up:** 6-8 weeks (longer wait than brows due to lip healing)
- **Longevity:** 1-3 years (faster fading than brows due to lip cell turnover and oral environment)

#### Downtime
- **Swelling:** 1-3 days (significant day 1-2)
- **Scabbing, peeling:** 5-10 days
- **Restrictions:**
  - No kissing for 10-14 days (bacterial transfer)
  - No spicy, salty, acidic foods for 7-10 days
  - No straws (stretching)
  - No swimming/saunas for 10-14 days
  - No lipstick for 10-14 days
  - Use straw for drinking (avoid direct contact)

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Initial:** $400-900
- **Touch-up:** $200-450
- **Color correction (dark lips):** Additional $100-300

#### Evidence Level: **C**

#### Risks/Side Effects
**Common:**
- Significant swelling
- Tenderness
- Scabbing, flaking
- Dryness
- Temporary uneven color

**Uncommon:**
- Infection (bacterial, fungal — oral environment)
- Allergic reaction to pigment
- Cold sore outbreak (if no prophylaxis)
- Asymmetry
- Color healing unevenly

**Serious:**
- **Severe HSV outbreak:** Scarring, permanent pigment loss
- **Scarring:** From picking or poor technique
- **Pigment migration:** Blurred lip line
- **Granulomatous reaction**
- **Koebner phenomenon:** If psoriasis triggered

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient understands HSV risk and commits to prophylaxis; has realistic color expectations; willing to follow strict aftercare
- **Best for:** Fitzpatrick I-III wanting natural lip tint; defined vermilion border
- **Caution for:** Fitzpatrick IV-VI (may not achieve desired color; hyperpigmentation risk)
- **Absolute requirement:** HSV prophylaxis for all patients with any history of cold sores
- **Color correction:** Dark lips require neutralization before target color; additional sessions needed
- **Post-procedure:** Emollient care essential; avoid petroleum-based products (can affect pigment)

### 5F. FRECKLE TATTOOING

#### Mechanism of Action
Cosmetic tattooing to create artificial freckles:

**Technique:**
- Manual or machine application of pigment in dot pattern

- Pigment implanted superficially for semi-permanent result

#### Indications
- Aesthetic preference for freckled appearance

#### Contraindications

#### Expected Results Timeline

#### Cost Range (US)
- **$150-400**

#### Evidence Level: **D**

#### Risks/Side Effects

- **Infection**

### 5H. SCALP MICROPIGMENTATION (SMP)

#### Mechanism of Action
Cosmetic tattooing to simulate hair follicles on scalp:

**Technique:**
- Specialized machine implants tiny pigment dots at superficial depth
- Creates illusion of closely-shaved hair or density in thinning areas
- Thousands of micro-dots create "buzz cut" appearance or fill in thinning

**Depth:** Very superficial (epidermis) to avoid ink spreading

**Pigments:** Specialized SMP pigments (carbon-based, designed not to blur)

#### Indications
- Male pattern baldness (shaved head look)
- Female pattern hair loss (density illusion)
- Scalp scar camouflage (FUT/FUE transplant scars, surgical scars)
- Alopecia areata, alopecia totalis
- Hair transplant enhancement

#### Contraindications
- Active scalp infection, psoriasis, seborrheic dermatitis
- Unstable hair loss (still actively shedding — may need to treat ongoing loss first)
- Keloid/scar formation history (relative)
- Pregnancy, breastfeeding
- Chemotherapy (wait until treatment complete)

#### Fitzpatrick Safety
- **All skin types**
- **Color matching critical:** Must match hair follicle color for natural appearance
- **On darker skin:** Use darker pigments; ensure artist experienced with skin of color

#### Expected Results Timeline
- **Sessions:** 3-4 sessions spaced 7-14 days apart
- **Healing:** 5-7 days per session
- **Longevity:** 3-5 years (fades gradually)
- **Touch-ups:** Every 3-5 years

#### Downtime
- **Minimal:** Redness 1-3 days
- Scabbing 3-7 days
- **Restrictions:** No swimming, heavy sweating, sun exposure for 7-10 days
- Can wear hats immediately

#### Cost Range (US)
- **Hairline only:** $1,500-3,000
- **Full scalp:** $3,000-6,000
- **Scar camouflage:** $1,200-3,500
- **Touch-ups:** $500-2,000

#### Evidence Level: **C**

#### Risks/Side Effects
- Infection
- Color change (blue/gray if wrong pigment depth or type)
- Dot spread/blurring (if too deep)
- Unnatural appearance (if artist inexperienced)
- Allergic reaction
- **Regret:** Difficult to remove; appearance is "shaved head" look

#### SKINgenius Recommendation
- **Recommend when:** Patient understands "shaved head" aesthetic; wants non-surgical solution; has realistic expectations
- **Best for:** Men with significant baldness wanting buzz-cut look; scar camouflage
- **Caution:** Women wanting "density" — results can appear as dots under bright light; may not achieve desired natural look
- **Critical:** Choose SMP specialist (not standard tattoo artist); review healed results portfolio
- **Before SMP:** Stabilize hair loss with medical treatments (finasteride, minoxidil) if possible

### 6B. SKIN OF COLOR CONSIDERATIONS (Fitzpatrick IV-VI)

#### General Principles
- **Higher PIH risk:** Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation is the most common complication in darker skin
- **Melanocyte reactivity:** Melanocytes in darker skin are more easily triggered by inflammation or trauma
- **Keloid risk:** Higher incidence in Fitzpatrick IV-VI, especially African, Asian, and Hispanic descent
- **Hypopigmentation risk:** Some procedures (ablative lasers, phenol peels) can cause permanent lightening
- **Slower healing:** May require longer intervals between treatments
- **Sun protection:** Critical for all post-procedure care; melanin provides SPF ~3-13 but does not prevent UV-induced PIH

#### Pre-Treatment Protocol for Fitzpatrick IV-VI
1. **Hydroquinone 4%** (or alternative: azelaic acid 20%, kojic acid, arbutin, vitamin C) — 4-6 weeks pre-procedure
2. **Tretinoin 0.05%** — increases cell turnover, reduces PIH risk
3. **Sunscreen SPF 30-50** — daily, non-negotiable
4. **Avoid:** Recent sun exposure, tanning, self-tanner 2 weeks pre-procedure

#### Post-Treatment Protocol for Fitzpatrick IV-VI
1. **Continue hydroquinone** (if not contraindicated by procedure) starting 1-2 weeks post-healing
2. **Strict sun protection:** SPF 30-50+, hats, shade
3. **Gentle skincare:** Avoid irritants until fully healed
4. **Do not pick:** At scabs or peeling skin
5. **Monitor for PIH:** Treat early if develops

### 6C. POST-PROCEDURE SKINCARE

#### General Post-Procedure Principles
1. **Gentle cleansing:** Non-foaming, pH-balanced cleanser
2. **Hydration:** Hyaluronic acid, ceramides, glycerin-based moisturizers
3. **Barrier repair:** Ceramides, cholesterol, fatty acids
4. **Sun protection:** SPF 30-50+, broad spectrum, reapply every 2 hours
5. **Avoid irritants:** Retinoids, acids, scrubs until fully healed

#### Procedure-Specific Post-Care

| Procedure | Immediate (0-3 days) | Early (3-14 days) | Maintenance |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Botulinum toxin** | Gentle cleansing; avoid pressure | Resume normal skincare | Continue regular routine; no special requirements |
| **Fillers** | Ice packs; avoid pressure | Gentle; avoid massage (unless PLLA) | Resume normal skincare after 1 week |
| **Ablative laser** | Occlusive ointment (Aquaphor, Vaseline) | Gentle cleansing + healing ointment; no actives | Resume retinoids 4-6 weeks post; strict sun protection |
| **Non-ablative laser/IPL** | Cooling; gentle | Gentle moisturizer + SPF | Resume actives 1 week post |
| **RF** | Cooling gel; gentle | Gentle; no heat exposure | Resume normal routine 3-5 days post |
| **Microneedling** | Hyaluronic acid serum; no makeup | Gentle; growth factors/peptides OK | Resume retinoids 1 week post; vitamin C OK |
| **Chemical peel** | Post-peel kit (if provided) | Gentle; no picking | Resume actives per provider guidance (1-4 weeks) |
| **SPMU** | Aftercare ointment (provided) | Keep clean and moist; no picking | Avoid retinoids on tattooed area long-term |

#### Ingredients to AVOID Post-Procedure
- **Retinoids:** Until cleared by provider (1-6 weeks depending on procedure)
- **AHAs/BHAs:** Until healed
- **Vitamin C (L-Ascorbic acid):** May sting; wait until barrier healed
- **Benzoyl peroxide:** Drying, irritating
- **Physical scrubs:** Until fully healed
- **Fragrance/essential oils:** Potential irritants

#### Ingredients to USE Post-Procedure
- **Hyaluronic acid:** Hydration without irritation
- **Ceramides:** Barrier repair
- **Growth factors/peptides:** Enhance healing (evidence variable)
- **Centella asiatica (cica):** Anti-inflammatory, wound healing
- **Panthenol (B5):** Soothing, healing
- **Allantoin:** Healing, soothing
- **Zinc oxide sunscreen:** Physical protection without chemical irritation

#### Recommended Treatment Sequences

**Comprehensive Facial Rejuvenation (Non-Surgical):**
1. **Month 1:** Botulinum toxin + fillers (volumizing)
2. **Month 2:** IPL or laser for pigment/vascular
3. **Month 3:** Microneedling with PRP (texture, scars)
4. **Month 4:** Chemical peel (maintenance, glow)
5. **Month 6:** Repeat toxins; assess filler maintenance
6. **Ongoing:** Monthly HydraFacial or similar maintenance

**Acne Scar Revision:**
1. **Month 1:** Subcision (if indicated) + PRP
2. **Month 2:** Fractional laser or RF microneedling
3. **Month 3:** Microneedling with growth factors
4. **Month 4:** Chemical peel
5. **Repeat:** Series of 3-6 sessions

**Skin of Color Rejuvenation:**
1. **Pre-treatment:** 4-6 weeks hydroquinone + tretinoin + SPF
2. **Month 1:** RF microneedling (safest energy device)
3. **Month 2:** Gentle chemical peel (mandelic or salicylic)
4. **Month 3:** Repeat RF microneedling
5. **Month 4:** Microneedling with PRP
6. **Maintenance:** LED therapy, gentle peels, strict sun protection

#### General Rules
- **Space aggressive procedures:** Never combine ablative laser + deep peel + aggressive microneedling
- **Layer gradually:** Start with less invasive, increase intensity as skin tolerates
- **Seasonal considerations:** Plan lasers and peels in fall/winter (less sun); maintain in spring/summer
- **Healing first:** Always let skin fully heal before next procedure
- **Budget realistically:** Comprehensive rejuvenation requires investment over time

### 6E. SPMU-SPECIFIC PRE & POST CARE

#### Pre-Procedure Preparation

**4 Weeks Before:**
- Discontinue tretinoin, retinol, and other retinoids on treatment area
- Discontinue AHAs/BHAs on treatment area
- Stop eyebrow growth serums (if eyeliner or brow procedure)
- Stop lash growth serums (if eyeliner)

**2 Weeks Before:**
- No Botox or fillers in treatment area
- No waxing, threading, or tweezing (brows)
- No chemical peels or laser on treatment area
- Begin antiviral prophylaxis if HSV history (lips)

**1 Week Before:**
- Avoid alcohol (increases bleeding/bruising)
- Avoid blood thinners if medically appropriate (consult physician)
- Stay hydrated
- No sunburn or tanned skin

**48 Hours Before:**
- No caffeine (reduces sensitivity)
- Get good sleep
- Wash hair (if scalp procedure; can't wash immediately after)

**Day Of:**
- Eat a good meal
- Avoid caffeine
- No makeup on treatment area
- Bring reference photos

#### Post-Procedure Care (All SPMU)

**Days 1-3:**
- Gently blot with clean tissue to remove lymph fluid (prevents heavy scabbing)
- Apply provided aftercare ointment thinly 2-3x daily
- Do not touch with dirty hands
- Sleep on clean pillowcase (back sleeping preferred)

**Days 3-10:**
- Continue aftercare ointment
- Do not pick, scratch, or rub scabs
- Let scabs fall off naturally
- Keep area clean and dry (except ointment)

**Days 10-14:**
- May switch to gentle moisturizer (if cleared by artist)
- Continue avoiding makeup on area
- Still no swimming, saunas, steam rooms

**Weeks 2-4:**
- Resume gentle skincare (no retinoids yet)
- Continue sun protection
- Color may appear uneven ("ghosting" — normal)

**Long-term:**
- **AVOID retinoids, AHAs, BHAs on tattooed area** — causes pigment fading
- Always use SPF on tattooed areas (sun fades pigment)
- Annual touch-ups recommended

#### Pigment Fading by Skin Type

| Skin Type | Brow Fading | Lip Fading | Eyeliner Fading |
|-----------|-------------|------------|-----------------|
| I-II | 12-18 months | 12-18 months | 18-24 months |
| III-IV | 12-18 months | 12-18 months | 18-24 months |
| V-VI | 8-14 months | 8-14 months | 12-18 months |

**Oily skin considerations:**
- Oily skin fades pigment faster (sebum breaks down pigment)
- Microblading strokes blur in oily skin ("feathering")
- **Solution:** Nano-blading or powder/ombre techniques preferred for oily skin
- More frequent touch-ups needed

#### MRI Concerns with Iron Oxide Pigments

**Issue:** Iron oxide pigments in SPMU can interact with MRI magnetic fields.

**Risk:**
- Burning sensation during MRI
- Image artifact (distortion) near tattoo
- Very rare: tissue heating

**Recommendations:**
- Inform MRI technologist of all permanent makeup
- Some facilities may refuse MRI or require specific protocols
- Consider non-iron oxide pigments if frequent MRIs anticipated
- Eyeliner has highest risk due to proximity to eyes

**Alternative pigments:** Some artists offer organic, non-iron oxide pigments specifically for patients who may need MRIs.

## CONCLUSION & SKINgenius CLINICAL GUIDANCE

### When to Recommend
- **Botulinum toxin:** First-line for dynamic wrinkles; excellent safety profile
- **HA fillers:** First-line for static wrinkles and volume loss (reversible)
- **RF microneedling:** Gold standard for skin of color and comprehensive rejuvenation
- **Microneedling + PRP:** Excellent for acne scars across all skin types
- **Chemical peels:** Superficial peels for maintenance; medium peels for appropriate candidates
- **LED therapy:** Safe adjunct for acne and post-procedure healing
- **SPMU:** Powder/ombre brows for most patients; nano-blading for hair-stroke preference; lip blushing only with HSV prophylaxis

### When to Refer
- **Suspicious lesions:** Any changing mole, non-healing sore, or suspected skin cancer → Dermatologist immediately
- **Severe laxity:** Surgical consultation for facelift, neck lift
- **Complications:** Vascular occlusion, severe infection, significant scarring → Emergency or specialist care
- **Complex conditions:** Keloid history, autoimmune disease, bleeding disorders → Specialist evaluation
- **Unrealistic expectations:** Patient education; may need psychological support

### Red Flags for Providers
1. Cannot explain complications or does not have reversal agents (hyaluronidase for HA fillers)
2. No medical supervision for invasive procedures
3. Offers permanent fillers without discussing risks
4. Performs lasers/peels without skin type assessment
5. No informed consent process
6. Pressure to purchase packages or upsell
7. "Too good to be true" pricing (often indicates counterfeit products)
